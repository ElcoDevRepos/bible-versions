And if it is by grace
This phrase emphasizes the foundational Christian doctrine of salvation by grace. Grace, in the biblical context, refers to the unmerited favor of God towards humanity. This concept is rooted in the Old Testament, where God's grace is evident in His covenant with Israel despite their repeated failures (Exodus 34:6-7). In the New Testament, grace is fully realized through Jesus Christ, who embodies God's grace (John 1:14, 17). The Apostle Paul, the author of Romans, consistently teaches that grace is the basis of salvation, not human effort or merit (Ephesians 2:8-9).

then it is no longer by works
This phrase contrasts grace with works, underscoring that human efforts cannot earn salvation. The Jewish law, which included various rituals and moral codes, was often seen as a means to righteousness. However, Paul argues that the law reveals sin but cannot save from it (Romans 3:20). This distinction is crucial in understanding the transition from the Old Covenant, based on adherence to the law, to the New Covenant, centered on faith in Christ (Galatians 3:10-14). The historical context of first-century Judaism, with its emphasis on the law, highlights the radical nature of Paul's message.

Otherwise, grace would no longer be grace
This phrase reinforces the purity and sufficiency of grace. If works contributed to salvation, grace would lose its essence as a free gift. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative that salvation is God's initiative, from the promise to Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) to its fulfillment in Christ (Galatians 3:16-18). Theologically, this underscores the doctrine of justification by faith alone, a cornerstone of Christian belief. The concept of grace as a gift is echoed in other scriptures, such as Titus 3:5-7, which emphasizes that salvation is not due to righteous deeds but according to God's mercy.

3. Israel
The nation chosen by God, central to Paul's discussion in Romans 11, as he explains God's plan for Israel and the Gentiles.
Teaching Points
Understanding Grace
Grace is unmerited favor from God. It cannot coexist with works as a means of earning salvation. Recognize that grace is a gift, not a transaction.

The Nature of Works
While good works are a natural outcome of faith, they are not the basis for salvation. Works should be seen as a response to grace, not a means to obtain it.

Living in Grace
Embrace a life that reflects gratitude for God's grace. This involves humility, recognizing that our standing with God is not based on our efforts.

Avoiding Legalism
Be cautious of falling into the trap of legalism, where one might believe that adherence to rules or rituals can earn God's favor.

Grace in Community
Encourage others in your faith community to rely on grace, fostering an environment where grace is extended to others as it has been received.

What Does Romans 11:6 Mean
And if it is by grace

“Grace” is God’s free, undeserved favor. Paul reminds us that everything about our rescue in Christ starts with what God does, not what we do (Ephesians 2:8-9; Titus 3:5).

• Grace flows from God’s initiative—He “chose us in Him before the foundation of the world” (Ephesians 1:4).

• Grace is given, not bartered; Romans 3:24 calls believers “justified freely by His grace.”

• Because grace is God’s gift, it is dependable; Hebrews 4:16 invites us to “approach the throne of grace with confidence.”

then it is no longer by works

Paul draws a sharp line: if salvation rests on grace, it cannot at the same time rest on human effort.

• Works of the law cannot justify anyone (Galatians 2:16); they merely reveal our need (Romans 3:20).

• Trying to earn favor turns the gospel into wages instead of a gift (Romans 4:4-5).

• Even our best deeds are stained by sin (Isaiah 64:6), so piling them up can never bridge the gap between us and God.

Otherwise, grace would no longer be grace

Mixing works with grace destroys grace altogether.

• Adding human merit cancels Christ’s work—“You have been severed from Christ…you who are trying to be justified by law” (Galatians 5:4).

• God “saved us and called us…not because of our works but by His own purpose and grace” (2 Timothy 1:9).

• Grace remains pure only when Christ’s finished work stands alone: “It is finished” (John 19:30).

summary

Romans 11:6 declares that salvation is entirely God’s gift. Grace and works cannot be blended; the moment human effort enters, grace ceases to be what it is. Our confidence, joy, and assurance rest solely in the unearned, unchanging favor God pours out through Jesus Christ.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) And if by grace.--The true text of this verse differs considerably from that which is translated in the Authorised version, "But if by grace, then is it no more of works, otherwise grace is no more seen to be grace."

The preservation of the remnant cannot be due to grace and works at the same time; it must be due to one or the other.
