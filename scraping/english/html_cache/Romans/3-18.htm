There is no fear of God
This phrase highlights a fundamental issue in humanity's relationship with God. The "fear of God" in biblical terms often refers to a profound respect, reverence, and awe for the Almighty. In the Old Testament, the fear of God is the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 9:10) and is associated with obedience and righteousness. The absence of this fear indicates a spiritual and moral deficiency. Historically, Israel was repeatedly warned against losing this fear, which often led to idolatry and disobedience (Deuteronomy 6:13-15). Theologically, this lack of fear is a root cause of sin, as it reflects a heart that does not acknowledge God's authority or holiness.

before their eyes
The phrase "before their eyes" suggests a deliberate choice to ignore or reject God's presence and authority. In biblical literature, the eyes often symbolize perception and understanding. When individuals or nations fail to see God "before their eyes," it indicates a willful blindness or ignorance. This concept is echoed in other scriptures, such as Psalm 36:1, which states, "Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart; there is no fear of God before his eyes." This blindness can be seen in the historical context of Israel's repeated cycles of sin and repentance, where they often turned away from God despite His clear revelations and interventions. In a broader sense, this phrase underscores humanity's tendency to prioritize worldly desires over divine truth, leading to moral and spiritual decay.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of the Book of Romans, Paul is addressing the Roman church, emphasizing the universal sinfulness of humanity and the need for God's righteousness.

2. The Roman Church
The recipients of the letter, consisting of both Jewish and Gentile believers, who are being taught about the righteousness that comes from faith.

3. The Human Condition
Paul is describing the state of humanity apart from God, highlighting the lack of reverence and fear of God as a fundamental issue.
Teaching Points
The Importance of the Fear of God
The fear of God is foundational to wisdom and understanding. Without it, individuals are prone to moral and spiritual blindness.

The Universality of Sin
Paul’s statement underscores the universal nature of sin. Every person, regardless of background, is in need of redemption.

The Need for Reverence
Cultivating a reverent fear of God is essential for a healthy spiritual life. It leads to obedience and a deeper relationship with God.

Self-Examination
Believers are encouraged to examine their own lives to ensure that the fear of God is present and active in their daily walk.

Evangelism and Witness
Understanding the lack of fear of God in the world should motivate believers to share the Gospel, which brings the knowledge of God and His righteousness.

What Does Romans 3:18 Mean
There is

Paul begins with a fact, not an opinion: “There is.” The phrase sounds almost like a courtroom verdict, declaring the condition of the unredeemed human heart.

• Scripture consistently states this reality; see Psalm 14:3, “All have turned away…”

• Romans 3:10–11, leading up to this verse, piles up similar declarations: “There is no one righteous… there is no one who seeks God.”

Here, the apostle is underscoring the universality of sin. No class, culture, or era escapes the charge.

no fear

The core issue is not ignorance but absence of reverence. Fear in biblical terms is a wholesome awe that moves a person to obey and worship.

• Proverbs 1:7, “The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge.”

• Exodus 20:20 shows fear’s positive role: it keeps people from sinning.

• When that fear evaporates, moral brakes fail (Judges 17:6).

Sin flourishes when God’s awe is replaced by self-sufficiency or indifference.

of God

This fear is directed toward the living, holy God—His character, His authority, His coming judgment.

• Hebrews 10:31 reminds us, “It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God.”

• Jesus warns in Matthew 10:28 to fear Him “who can destroy both soul and body in hell.”

• Psalm 33:8 calls all the earth to “fear the LORD; let all the inhabitants of the world stand in awe of Him.”

Without acknowledging who God is, fear degenerates into mere fright or is dismissed altogether.

before their eyes

The problem is perception. People see but do not see; God’s presence is not in their field of vision.

• 2 Corinthians 4:4 speaks of minds blinded by the god of this age.

• Isaiah 64:7 laments, “No one… stirs himself to lay hold of You; for You have hidden Your face from us.”

• Romans 1:21 notes, “Although they knew God, they neither glorified Him… but their thinking became futile.”

When God is not “before the eyes,” choices follow suit—leading to unchecked sin.

summary

Romans 3:18 caps Paul’s indictment by exposing the root of human rebellion: a life emptied of reverent awe for a holy God. Where that awe is absent, sin reigns, perception darkens, and judgment looms. The gospel’s hope is to restore true fear—and joyful worship—by revealing God through Christ, turning blind eyes to see and hearts to bow.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(18) The fear of God, which is properly a subjective feeling, is here projected, as it were, and regarded as an external rule of life.
