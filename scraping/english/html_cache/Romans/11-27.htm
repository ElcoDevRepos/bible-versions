And this is My covenant with them
This phrase refers to God's promise to Israel, highlighting the unbreakable nature of His covenant. The covenant mentioned here is rooted in the promises made to the patriarchs, such as Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and is further elaborated in the prophetic writings. In Jeremiah 31:31-34, God speaks of a new covenant with the house of Israel, emphasizing a relationship based on internal transformation rather than external adherence to the law. This covenant is also linked to the promise of restoration and blessing for Israel, as seen in Ezekiel 36:24-28, where God promises to gather His people and cleanse them.

when I take away their sins.
This phrase underscores the ultimate purpose of God's covenant: the removal of sin. The taking away of sins is a central theme in the New Testament, fulfilled through the atoning work of Jesus Christ. In Isaiah 53:5-6, the suffering servant is described as bearing the iniquities of many, a prophecy fulfilled in Christ's sacrificial death. The concept of sin removal is also echoed in Hebrews 8:12, where God promises to remember sins no more under the new covenant. This act of divine forgiveness is not only a future hope for Israel but also a present reality for all who believe in Christ, as seen in 1 John 1:9, which assures believers of forgiveness and cleansing from all unrighteousness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of the Book of Romans, Paul is addressing the church in Rome, composed of both Jewish and Gentile believers. He is explaining God's plan for Israel and the Gentiles.

2. Israel
The nation chosen by God, central to the discussion in Romans 11. Paul speaks of Israel's future redemption and the fulfillment of God's covenant promises.

3. The Covenant
Refers to God's promises to Israel, particularly the New Covenant, which includes the forgiveness of sins and is fulfilled through Jesus Christ.

4. Jesus Christ
The mediator of the New Covenant, through whom the forgiveness of sins is made possible.

5. The Gentiles
Non-Jewish people who have been grafted into the promises of God through faith in Jesus Christ, as discussed in the broader context of Romans 11.
Teaching Points
Understanding God's Faithfulness
God's covenant with Israel demonstrates His unwavering faithfulness. Despite Israel's unbelief, God remains committed to His promises.

The Role of Jesus in the New Covenant
Jesus is central to the fulfillment of God's covenant promises. Through His sacrifice, the forgiveness of sins is made available to all who believe.

The Inclusion of the Gentiles
The inclusion of the Gentiles into God's covenant plan shows the expansiveness of God's grace and the unity of believers in Christ.

The Assurance of Forgiveness
Believers can have confidence in the complete forgiveness of sins through the New Covenant, which is a promise from God.

Living in Light of the Covenant
Understanding our place in God's covenant should inspire us to live lives that reflect His grace and truth, sharing the message of redemption with others.

What Does Romans 11:27 Mean
And this is My covenant

• Paul quotes Isaiah 59:21 to remind his readers that God has pledged Himself to act.

• A covenant is God’s binding promise, not a mere human contract (Genesis 17:7; Jeremiah 31:31-34).

• Because God cannot lie (Numbers 23:19), the pledge guarantees fulfillment.

• The verse flows out of Romans 11:25-26, where “all Israel will be saved,” showing this covenant is the ground of that coming salvation.

• Hebrews 8:10-12 echoes the same promise, confirming that the “new covenant” rests on God’s initiative, secured by Christ’s shed blood (Luke 22:20).

with them

• “Them” points specifically to ethnic Israel, the very group Paul has been discussing (Romans 11:1-2; Jeremiah 31:36-37).

• God has not replaced Israel; He preserves a remnant now (Romans 11:5) and will restore the nation later (Ezekiel 37:21-28).

• Gentile believers are graciously grafted in (Romans 11:17), sharing spiritual blessings, but the covenant target remains Israel (Isaiah 61:8-9).

• This keeps the focus on God’s faithfulness: promises made to the patriarchs will be literally kept (Micah 7:20).

when I take away their sins.

• The climax of the covenant is total, once-for-all removal of sin—something only God can accomplish (Isaiah 43:25; Jeremiah 33:8).

• Christ’s atoning death achieved the objective basis for that cleansing (Isaiah 53:5-6; 1 Peter 2:24).

• Nationally, Israel will experience this cleansing at the Messiah’s return, when “they will look on Me whom they have pierced” (Zechariah 12:10; Romans 11:26).

• Individually, every believer—Jew or Gentile—already enjoys forgiveness and indwelling Spirit under this same covenant (Acts 3:19; Colossians 2:13), pointing to a future day when the whole nation will share it.

• The promise guarantees not only pardon but also transformation, as foretold in Ezekiel 36:25-27.

summary

Romans 11:27 anchors Israel’s future salvation in God’s unbreakable covenant. He Himself pledged, “And this is My covenant with them when I take away their sins.” The promise is certain because it rests on God’s character, delivered to Israel specifically, and fulfilled through the redemptive work of Jesus. What He promised, He will accomplish—pardoning sin, restoring His people, and showcasing His faithfulness before the whole world.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) The second part of the quotation, "For (rather, and), this is my covenant with them," &c., appears to be taken from the LXX. version of Isaiah 27:9. The connecting-links between the two are the removing of transgression from Jacob, and the form of the phrase, "This is my covenant with them." ("This is his blessing," Isaiah 27:9, LXX.)
