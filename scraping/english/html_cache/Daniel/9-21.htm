Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
while I was still praying
Daniel's prayer in this chapter is a profound intercession for his people, Israel, acknowledging their sins and seeking God's mercy. This reflects the importance of prayer in the life of a believer, emphasizing persistence and faithfulness in seeking God's will. Daniel's prayer is a model of humility and repentance, aligning with other biblical examples such as Nehemiah's prayer (Nehemiah 1:4-11) and the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13).

Gabriel, the man I had seen in the earlier vision
Gabriel is one of the few angels named in the Bible, known for delivering messages from God. His appearance to Daniel connects to the earlier vision in Daniel 8, where he provided understanding of the vision of the ram and the goat. Gabriel's role as a messenger is consistent with his later appearance to Zechariah (Luke 1:19) and Mary (Luke 1:26-27), announcing significant events in God's redemptive plan.

came to me in swift flight
The phrase "swift flight" suggests the urgency and importance of the message Gabriel brings. It underscores the immediacy of God's response to Daniel's prayer, highlighting the divine nature of angelic beings who operate beyond human limitations. This swift action reflects God's attentiveness to the prayers of His people, as seen in other scriptures like Isaiah 65:24, where God promises to answer before they call.

about the time of the evening sacrifice
The evening sacrifice, traditionally around 3 PM, was a significant time in Jewish worship, even though the temple was no longer standing during Daniel's time in Babylon. This reference indicates Daniel's continued devotion to God and adherence to the practices of his faith, despite being in exile. The timing also foreshadows the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who died at the ninth hour (Mark 15:34-37), fulfilling the sacrificial system and establishing a new covenant.

What Does Daniel 9:21 Mean
While I was still praying

Daniel records, “while I was still praying” (Daniel 9:21).

• God responds before the prayer even ends, echoing Isaiah 65:24—“Before they call, I will answer.”

• Daniel’s habit of earnest, disciplined prayer is already clear in Daniel 6:10, where he prayed three times a day despite the king’s edict.

• The immediacy of the answer underscores James 5:16: “The prayer of a righteous man has great power.” The Lord’s faithfulness to hear His people is literal and personal.

Gabriel, the man I had seen in the earlier vision

Gabriel is named only here and in Daniel 8:16; 9:22; Luke 1:19, 26.

• Described as “the man,” Gabriel appears in human form but is unmistakably an angelic messenger.

• His reappearance links the visions of chapters 8 and 9, assuring Daniel—and us—of continuity and reliability in God’s revelation.

• Hebrews 1:14 reminds us that angels are “ministering spirits sent to serve those who will inherit salvation,” underscoring their real involvement in God’s plan.

Came to me in swift flight

Gabriel arrives “in swift flight”, stressing speed and purpose.

• Psalm 103:20 pictures angels who “excel in strength, who do His word,” moving quickly to accomplish divine tasks.

• The phrase conveys urgency: God is not indifferent to humble repentance (Daniel 9:3–19) but acts decisively.

• Acts 12:7 shows a similar sudden angelic intervention for Peter, reinforcing that such visitations are literal, not symbolic.

About the time of the evening sacrifice

Although the temple lay in ruins, Daniel still reckons time by its worship schedule, about 3 p.m. (Exodus 29:39; 1 Kings 18:36).

• This detail roots the narrative in Israel’s covenant life; Daniel’s heart remains tied to God’s ordained worship even in exile.

• Ezra 9:4–5 mirrors this pattern of repentance “at the evening sacrifice,” highlighting shared devotion among the faithful remnant.

• The timing foreshadows the ultimate sacrifice: Jesus died at roughly this hour (Matthew 27:46–50), fulfilling the sacrificial system Daniel cherished.

summary

Daniel 9:21 shows a praying servant, an answering God, a swift angelic messenger, and a heart anchored in covenant worship. The verse assures us that God literally hears, responds, and moves heaven’s hosts to act, even when outward structures seem lost. Persistent prayer, reverence for God’s appointed means, and confidence in His prompt, powerful response remain our clear takeaways.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Being caused to fly swiftly.--A very difficult expression, occurring only here. The Authorised Version follows the LXX. and Theodotion. The rendering has been defended on the ground that the word translated "swiftly" comes from a root meaning "to fly." and is literally rendered by flight. Thus "caused to fly in flight" means "caused to fly swiftly." The marginal version "with weariness" finds supporters, and, if adopted, must be taken to refer to the bodily condition of Daniel (Daniel 8:17-27). The former translation is most in accordance with the context. The "flight" of angels is implied in Isaiah 6:2, and should not be regarded as an idea foreign to the Old Testament.

Touched me.--Literally, reached me. (Comp. this use of the word, Jonah 3:6.) The time of the evening sacrifice Isaiah 3 P.M., being the hour of evening prayer. (See Exodus 29:39; Numbers 28:4.) . . .
