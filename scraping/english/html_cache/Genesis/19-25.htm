Thus He destroyed these cities
This phrase refers to the divine judgment executed by God upon the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah. These cities were notorious for their wickedness and sin, as described earlier in Genesis 18:20-21. The destruction serves as a fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham to judge the cities if their sin was as grievous as reported. The destruction is often seen as a warning against immorality and a call to righteousness, echoed in later scriptures such as Jude 1:7, which references Sodom and Gomorrah as examples of divine judgment.

and the entire plain
The "plain" refers to the well-watered region of the Jordan Valley, which was initially chosen by Lot for its fertility (Genesis 13:10-11). This area was not only geographically significant but also economically prosperous, making its destruction a significant event. The obliteration of the entire plain underscores the totality of God's judgment, leaving no remnant of the sinful culture that had developed there.

including all the inhabitants of the cities
This phrase highlights the comprehensive nature of the judgment, affecting every person living in the cities. The inhabitants' destruction is a direct consequence of their participation in the pervasive sinfulness of the region. This serves as a sobering reminder of the seriousness of sin and the inevitability of divine justice, as seen in other biblical accounts of judgment, such as the flood in Genesis 6-8.

and everything that grew on the ground
The destruction extended to the natural environment, indicating a complete eradication of life and productivity in the area. This mirrors the curse upon the ground seen in Genesis 3:17-19, where sin leads to a disruption of the natural order. The obliteration of vegetation also symbolizes the barrenness that results from sin, contrasting with the life and abundance found in obedience to God, as seen in Deuteronomy 28:1-14.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Sodom and Gomorrah
These were two cities known for their wickedness and sinfulness. Their destruction serves as a significant event in biblical history, symbolizing God's judgment against sin.

2. The Plain
This refers to the surrounding area of Sodom and Gomorrah, which was also destroyed. It highlights the extent of God's judgment, affecting not just the cities but the entire region.

3. Inhabitants of the Cities
The people living in Sodom and Gomorrah who were judged and destroyed due to their sinful ways.

4. Lot and His Family
Although not mentioned in this specific verse, Lot and his family were the only ones spared from the destruction, emphasizing God's mercy towards the righteous.

5. God's Judgment
The event is a direct act of divine judgment, demonstrating God's holiness and intolerance of sin.
Teaching Points
God's Holiness and Justice
The destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah underscores God's holiness and His intolerance of sin. It serves as a reminder that sin has serious consequences.

The Importance of Righteousness
Lot's deliverance highlights the importance of living a righteous life. God is attentive to the righteous and provides a way of escape from judgment.

Intercession Matters
Abraham's intercession for Sodom shows the power and importance of praying for others, especially those who are lost.

Judgment as a Warning
The event serves as a warning for future generations about the reality of God's judgment. It calls believers to live in a way that honors God and to warn others of the coming judgment.

God's Mercy
Even in judgment, God's mercy is evident. He spares Lot and his family, showing that He is willing to save those who turn to Him.

What Does Genesis 19:25 Mean
Thus He destroyed

“Thus He destroyed…” (Genesis 19:25) points us straight to the Lord as the active Judge.

•He alone initiates and completes the judgment (Genesis 19:24; Deuteronomy 29:23).

•This decisive action underscores His holiness; sin is not ignored or minimized (2 Peter 2:6; Isaiah 13:19).

•At the same time, the swiftness reminds us that mercy had already been extended—Abraham’s intercession and the angels’ warning (Genesis 18:22-33; Genesis 19:12-14).

these cities

The focus is Sodom and Gomorrah, with the surrounding towns likely including Admah and Zeboiim (Deuteronomy 29:23).

•They were once thriving centers (Genesis 14:8-12), yet their prosperity could not shield them from accountability.

•God’s dealing here sets a pattern: collective sin can bring collective judgment (Jeremiah 50:40; Hosea 11:8).

•The cities stand as a lasting example, referenced all the way into the New Testament (Jude 7).

and the entire plain

The “plain” is the lush Jordan Valley Lot had chosen (Genesis 13:10-11).

•What looked like Eden became a wasteland—showing that external beauty cannot cover moral decay (Proverbs 14:12).

•The breadth of the ruin testifies that God’s judgment is comprehensive, not partial (Amos 4:11).

including all the inhabitants of the cities

Every resident, except those the angels led out, perished (Genesis 19:15-23).

•The clause stresses personal accountability: each life was evaluated (Romans 2:6).

•It also reinforces the urgency of obedience—Lot’s sons-in-law mocked and were lost (Luke 17:28-30).

and everything that grew on the ground

The devastation reached vegetation, erasing any sign of life.

•God’s judgment touched the environment, reversing the fertility that once drew people there (Genesis 2:5; Exodus 9:25).

•This detail foreshadows later prophetic imagery where sin scars the land (Isaiah 34:9-10).

summary

Genesis 19:25 presents a total, literal judgment: God personally annihilated Sodom, Gomorrah, and their fertile plain, wiping out people, cities, and vegetation. The verse showcases His holiness, the certainty of accountability, and the futility of earthly security apart from obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(25) Overthrew.--This does not mean submerged, and the agent in the destruction was fire and not water. "The plain" (Heb., the Ciccar) still existed, and when Abraham saw it, was wrapped in smoke.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - And he overthrew - literally, turned over, as a cake'; whence utterly destroyed (cf. Deuteronomy 29:23; κατέστρεψε, LXX.; subvertit, Vulgate). In Arabic "the overthrown' is a title applied, κατ ἐξοχὴν, to Sodom and Gomorrah (Gesenius). From the use of the expression καταστροφή (2 Peter 2:6), Wordsworth thinks an earthquake may have accompanied the burning - those cities, - that they were submerged as well as overthrown (Josephus) is a doubtful inference from Genesis 14:3 (vide infra, Ver. 28, on the site of cities of the plain). The archaic הָאל is again employed (cf. Genesis 19:8) - and all the plain, - kikkar, circle or district (Genesis 13:10) - and all the inhabitants of the cities, - a proof of their entire corruption (Genesis 18:32) - and that which grew upon the ground - literally, that which sprouts forth from the ground, the produce of the soil; thus converting "a fruitful land into barrenness for the wickedness of them that dwell therein" (Psalm 107:34).
