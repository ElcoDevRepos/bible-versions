And the two men turned away and went toward Sodom
This phrase refers to the two angels who had accompanied the LORD to visit Abraham. In the broader context of Genesis 18, these men are identified as divine messengers. Their turning toward Sodom signifies the impending judgment on the city due to its wickedness, as described in Genesis 19. The geographical location of Sodom, near the Dead Sea, is significant for its historical and archaeological implications, with evidence suggesting the area was once fertile but later became desolate. This movement toward Sodom sets the stage for the events of Genesis 19, where the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah is detailed.

but Abraham remained standing before the LORD
Abraham's position here is one of intercession. Standing before the LORD indicates a posture of prayer and negotiation, as Abraham pleads for the righteous within Sodom. This reflects a cultural and historical practice where individuals would stand before a king or judge to make a petition. Abraham's boldness in intercession is a type of Christ, who intercedes on behalf of humanity (Hebrews 7:25). Theologically, this moment underscores the relationship between God and His chosen people, highlighting themes of justice, mercy, and covenant. Abraham's dialogue with God also foreshadows the role of prophets and leaders in Israel who would stand in the gap for the people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Abraham
The patriarch of the Israelites, known for his faith and obedience to God. In this passage, he stands before the LORD, interceding for Sodom.

2. The LORD
Refers to God, who appears to Abraham in a theophany, engaging in a dialogue about the fate of Sodom and Gomorrah.

3. The Men
These are the two angels who accompany the LORD. They proceed toward Sodom to assess the situation and eventually execute judgment.

4. Sodom
A city known for its wickedness, which is about to face divine judgment. It represents human sinfulness and the consequences thereof.

5. The Event
This passage is part of the larger account where God reveals to Abraham His plan to judge Sodom and Gomorrah, leading to Abraham's intercession.
Teaching Points
The Power of Intercession
Abraham's willingness to stand before the LORD and plead for Sodom demonstrates the power and importance of intercessory prayer. Believers are encouraged to pray for others, especially those who are lost or in need of God's mercy.

God's Justice and Mercy
This passage highlights the balance between God's justice and His willingness to show mercy. It reminds us that while God is just, He is also compassionate and responsive to the prayers of the righteous.

Standing Firm in Faith
Abraham's position of standing before the LORD signifies a posture of faith and readiness. Believers are called to stand firm in their faith, trusting in God's character and promises.

The Role of Righteousness
Abraham's dialogue with God underscores the impact of righteousness. The presence of even a few righteous individuals can influence God's actions toward a community, emphasizing the importance of living a righteous life.

What Does Genesis 18:22 Mean
The two men turned away

• In the preceding verses (Genesis 18:1–21), three visitors appeared to Abraham; two were angels, and the third was “the LORD” Himself.

• Here, the narrative shifts focus: “the two men” (Genesis 18:16; cf. Genesis 19:1) identify the angelic messengers who will soon execute judgment.

• Hebrews 13:2 reminds us that some have “entertained angels unaware,” highlighting God’s practice of sending heavenly beings on earthly errands.

• Their departure underscores that the moment for hospitality and revelation has passed; now their mission turns to justice (Genesis 18:20-21).

and went toward Sodom

• The angels’ destination is deliberate: Sodom, a city notorious for its sin (Genesis 13:13; 18:20).

• Their movement signals that divine patience has reached its limit (Genesis 19:13; Jude 7).

• While Sodom’s inhabitants remain oblivious, heaven’s agents advance—echoing later judgments such as the flood’s approach (Matthew 24:38-39) and the sudden fall of Babylon (Revelation 18:8).

• This literal journey highlights God’s active governance of the moral order (2 Peter 2:6).

but Abraham remained standing before the LORD

• Abraham does not follow the angels; instead, he positions himself before the LORD, ready to intercede.

• The stance is purposeful: standing denotes readiness, reverence, and bold access (Exodus 33:9-11; Isaiah 6:8).

• Abraham models priestly advocacy—pleading for mercy on behalf of the righteous within Sodom (Genesis 18:23-33).

• His posture foreshadows the greater Mediator, Christ, who “always lives to intercede” (Hebrews 7:25) and invites believers to “come boldly to the throne of grace” (Hebrews 4:16).

• God welcomes such intervention; Ezekiel 22:30 laments the absence of someone to “stand in the gap,” while James 5:16 affirms that “the prayer of a righteous person is powerful.”

summary

Genesis 18:22 marks a pivotal pause: the angels move toward judgment, yet Abraham stays before the LORD, opening the door for intercession. The verse teaches that God’s justice proceeds with certainty, but He also invites His people to stand before Him, appealing for mercy. Justice and mercy meet in this moment, revealing both the righteousness of God and the privilege of believers to plead for others.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) Abraham stood yet before the Lord (before Jehovah).--The two angels went on their way in form as men, towards Sodom, but the one who was a manifestation of Jehovah (Genesis 18:13; Genesis 18:17) remained behind.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - And the men turned their faces from thence, and went toward Sodom (i.e. two of the three proceeded on their way towards the Jordan valley, while the third was detained by the patriarch, probably on the heights overlooking the plain, for a sublime act of intercession which is thus briefly but suggestively described): but Abraham stood yet before the Lord. According to the Masorites the text originally read, "And the Lord stood before Abraham, and was changed because it did not seem becoming to speak of God standing in the presence of a creature. This, however, is a mere Rabbinical conceit. As Abraham is not said to hays stood before the three men, the expression points to spiritual rather than to local contiguity.
