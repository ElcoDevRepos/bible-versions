Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
suppose the fifty righteous ones lack five.
In this phrase, Abraham is interceding for Sodom, demonstrating his role as a mediator. The number fifty is significant, often representing completeness or a full measure in biblical numerology. The idea of lacking five suggests a shortfall, testing the limits of God's mercy. This negotiation reflects the ancient Near Eastern practice of bargaining, where a petitioner would gradually lower the terms to reach an agreement. Abraham's approach shows his understanding of God's justice and mercy, as well as his deep concern for the righteous within the city.

Will You destroy the whole city for the lack of five?”
Here, Abraham questions the justice of God, highlighting the tension between divine judgment and mercy. The phrase underscores the value of the righteous and their potential to preserve a community. This reflects a broader biblical theme where the presence of the righteous can bring blessings or protection to others, as seen in Proverbs 11:10-11. The rhetorical question emphasizes the seriousness of divine judgment and the hope that God will spare the city for the sake of a few righteous individuals.

He replied, “If I find forty-five there, I will not destroy it.”
God's response reveals His willingness to show mercy and His attentiveness to Abraham's intercession. The reduction from fifty to forty-five indicates God's readiness to relent from judgment for the sake of even a small number of righteous people. This interaction foreshadows the role of Jesus Christ as the ultimate intercessor, who pleads on behalf of humanity. It also connects to the theme of God's patience and desire for repentance, as seen in 2 Peter 3:9, where God is described as not wanting anyone to perish but for all to come to repentance.

2. God (The LORD)
The sovereign Creator who is in dialogue with Abraham, demonstrating His justice and mercy.

3. Sodom
A city known for its wickedness, which God plans to judge. Abraham is negotiating for its preservation if righteous people can be found there.

4. Righteous People
The individuals whose presence could spare the city from destruction. Their righteousness is a key factor in God's judgment.

5. Intercession
The act of Abraham pleading with God on behalf of Sodom, showcasing the power of prayer and advocacy.
Teaching Points
The Power of Intercession
Abraham's dialogue with God illustrates the importance and effectiveness of intercessory prayer. Believers are encouraged to pray for others, trusting in God's mercy and justice.

God's Justice and Mercy
This passage reveals God's willingness to spare the wicked for the sake of the righteous. It underscores His just nature balanced with mercy, encouraging believers to trust in His character.

The Value of Righteousness
The presence of even a few righteous individuals can have a profound impact. This teaches the importance of living a righteous life and its potential to influence and preserve communities.

Persistent Faith
Abraham's persistence in negotiating with God demonstrates a faith that does not give up easily. Believers are encouraged to be persistent in their prayers and faith.

What Does Genesis 18:28 Mean
Suppose the fifty righteous ones lack five

“Suppose the fifty righteous ones lack five…” (Genesis 18:28a)

- Abraham has just asked the LORD to spare Sodom if fifty righteous are found (Genesis 18:24). Now he gently lowers the number by five.

- His wording shows humility: “suppose” signals respect while still pressing the point. Similar respectful boldness appears in Moses’ plea for Israel in Exodus 32:11–13.

- The request assumes that righteous people really do restrain judgment on the wicked—a truth echoed in Proverbs 11:11, where “By the blessing of the upright a city is exalted.”

- The phrase reminds us that every righteous person matters to God. Jesus highlights this same principle by leaving ninety-nine sheep to seek one (Luke 15:4–7).

Will You destroy the whole city for the lack of five?

“Will You destroy the whole city for the lack of five?” (Genesis 18:28b)

- Abraham appeals to God’s justice: it would seem disproportionate to condemn thousands if only five citizens fall short of the fifty standard.

- This argument rests on God’s revealed character as “abounding in loving devotion and truth” who “does not leave the guilty unpunished” (Exodus 34:6–7).

- The question invites us to consider the weight of righteousness within a community. Jeremiah 5:1 records God challenging Jerusalem: “Roam the streets… If you can find but one person who acts justly… I will forgive this city.”

- Abraham is also interceding for Lot and his household (Genesis 19:12–13). His love mirrors the heart behind 1 Timothy 2:1–4, where believers are urged to pray for all people that they might be saved.

He replied, “If I find forty-five there, I will not destroy it.”

“He replied, ‘If I find forty-five there, I will not destroy it.’” (Genesis 18:28c)

- The LORD immediately agrees, revealing His readiness to show mercy. Psalm 103:8–10 celebrates this: “The LORD is compassionate… He has not dealt with us according to our sins.”

- God’s answer confirms that His judgments are precise, not capricious. He weighs actual moral realities rather than acting on impulse, as underscored in Ezekiel 18:23—He takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked.

- Each step downward (from fifty to forty-five and beyond in Genesis 18:29–32) magnifies divine patience. 2 Peter 3:9 affirms, “The Lord is patient… not wanting anyone to perish.”

- Yet the absence of even ten righteous (Genesis 18:32) will eventually lead to Sodom’s destruction, illustrating Romans 1:18—God’s wrath is revealed against persistent ungodliness when no righteous remnant is found.

summary

Genesis 18:28 captures the tender tension between divine justice and mercy. Abraham, confident in God’s character, negotiates ten fewer righteous and hears God’s gracious promise: the city will stand for the sake of forty-five. The verse teaches that righteous lives carry preserving power, intercessory prayer matters, and God is eager to spare judgment when even a small nucleus of faithfulness is present.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - Peradventure there shall lack five of the fifty righteous: wilt thou destroy all the city for lack of five? Literally, on account of five, i.e. because they are wanting. A rare example of holy ingenuity in prayer. Abraham, instead of pleading for the city's safety on account of forty-five, deprecates its destruction on account of five. And its said, If I find there forty and five, I will not destroy it.
