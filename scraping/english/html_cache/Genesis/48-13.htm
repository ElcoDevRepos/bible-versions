Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And Joseph took both of them—
Joseph, the son of Jacob (Israel) and Rachel, is a key figure in the narrative of Genesis. He is now in Egypt, having risen to a position of power. The "both of them" refers to his sons, Ephraim and Manasseh, born to him in Egypt. This moment is significant as it involves the blessing of his sons by Jacob, a patriarchal tradition that carries prophetic and covenantal significance.

with Ephraim in his right hand toward Israel’s left hand,
Ephraim, the younger son, is positioned by Joseph in such a way that he would receive the blessing from Jacob's left hand, traditionally the lesser blessing. The right hand was considered the hand of greater honor and power, often associated with strength and favor. This positioning reflects Joseph's expectation of the traditional order of blessing, where the firstborn receives the primary blessing.

and Manasseh in his left hand toward Israel’s right hand—
Manasseh, the firstborn, is placed to receive the blessing from Jacob's right hand, indicating the expectation of the greater blessing. This reflects the cultural norm of primogeniture, where the firstborn son receives a double portion of inheritance and leadership within the family. However, this narrative foreshadows a reversal of expectations, a common theme in biblical stories where God often chooses the younger or the unexpected to fulfill His purposes.

and brought them close to him.
Joseph brings his sons close to Jacob, signifying the intimate and personal nature of the blessing. This act of drawing near is symbolic of the transmission of covenantal promises and blessings from one generation to the next. It also highlights the importance of family and lineage in the biblical narrative, as well as the continuation of God's promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob through their descendants. This moment is a precursor to the tribes of Israel, with Ephraim and Manasseh becoming two of the twelve tribes, despite being grandsons of Jacob.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joseph
The son of Jacob (Israel) and Rachel, who rose to prominence in Egypt. In this passage, he is presenting his sons to his father for a blessing.

2. Ephraim
The younger son of Joseph, whom Joseph places at Israel's left hand, expecting the lesser blessing.

3. Manasseh
The elder son of Joseph, whom Joseph places at Israel's right hand, expecting the greater blessing.

4. Israel (Jacob)
The patriarch, father of Joseph, who is about to bless his grandsons. His actions in this chapter are significant for the future of the tribes of Israel.

5. Egypt
The land where this event takes place, where Joseph has become a powerful leader.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Blessing
God's plans often defy human expectations. Joseph expected Manasseh to receive the greater blessing, but God had a different plan. This teaches us to trust in God's sovereignty and wisdom, even when it contradicts our own plans.

The Importance of Faith
Jacob's blessing of Ephraim and Manasseh is an act of faith, trusting in God's promises for the future. We are called to live by faith, trusting in God's promises even when we cannot see the outcome.

Generational Impact
The blessings given to Ephraim and Manasseh had long-lasting effects on the tribes of Israel. Our actions and faith can have a significant impact on future generations.

God's Grace and Choice
Ephraim, the younger, receiving the greater blessing, reminds us of God's grace and His freedom to choose whom He blesses. This challenges us to recognize that God's grace is not based on human merit.

What Does Genesis 48:13 Mean
And Joseph took both of them

• Joseph acts as the mediator between his father and his sons, demonstrating filial honor (Exodus 20:12) and parental care (Ephesians 6:4).

• He accepts Jacob’s earlier declaration that Ephraim and Manasseh will be counted as Jacob’s own sons (Genesis 48:5–6).

• The scene echoes earlier moments where God’s covenant line is deliberately confirmed—such as Isaac blessing Jacob in Genesis 27:27–29—and reminds us that family blessings are a vital thread in God’s unfolding promise (Psalm 78:5–7).

with Ephraim in his right hand toward Israel’s left hand

• The right hand in Scripture often symbolizes power and preeminence (Psalm 110:1; Matthew 25:33).

• By placing Ephraim opposite Jacob’s left hand, Joseph intends that Manasseh, the firstborn, will receive Jacob’s dominant right-hand blessing.

• Joseph’s action shows respect for the cultural norm of primogeniture (Deuteronomy 21:17), even while God often works beyond human expectations (Romans 9:12-13).

and Manasseh in his left hand toward Israel’s right hand

• Manasseh’s positioning signals Joseph’s desire for his elder son to gain the chief blessing.

• The careful arrangement underlines how blessings are not random but deliberate (Numbers 27:18-23, where Moses lays hands on Joshua).

• Yet the narrative hints that God, not parent or culture, ultimately directs destinies (Proverbs 16:9).

and brought them close to him

• “Brought them close” pictures intimacy and acceptance; Jacob is about to embrace, kiss, and bless (Genesis 48:10).

• Proximity matters in biblical blessings: touch conveys favor (Mark 10:16).

• Hebrews 11:21 recalls that “By faith Jacob, when he was dying, blessed each of Joseph’s sons,” underscoring that this moment is an act of faith looking forward to the tribes’ future inheritance (Joshua 17:14-18).

summary

Genesis 48:13 shows Joseph deliberately arranging his sons so the firstborn Manasseh will receive Jacob’s right-hand blessing, while Ephraim stands opposite the left. Joseph honors custom and seeks the best for both boys, yet the unfolding story will reveal that God sovereignly crosses Jacob’s arms (Genesis 48:14-20), granting the greater blessing to the younger. The verse highlights parental faithfulness, the significance of touch in covenant blessings, and the truth that God’s purposes surpass human conventions while consistently fulfilling His promises.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - And Joseph took them both, Ephraim in his right hand toward Israel's left hand, and Manasseh in his left hand toward Israel's right hand, and brought them near unto him. Joseph naturally expected that Jacob s right hand would fall upon the head of Manasseh, as the firstborn, although with regard to even this a doubt might have been suggested if he had remembered how Isaac had been preferred to Ishmael, and Jacob to Esau.
