Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
May I never enter their council;
This phrase reflects a strong disassociation from the actions and decisions of Simeon and Levi, the sons of Jacob. In biblical context, councils were gatherings where decisions were made, often by elders or leaders. The speaker, Jacob, distances himself from their violent and impulsive behavior, indicating a moral and spiritual separation. This can be seen as a call to avoid ungodly influences, aligning with Psalm 1:1, which advises against walking in the counsel of the wicked.

may I never join their assembly.
Assemblies in ancient Israel were significant gatherings for communal decision-making and worship. By refusing to join their assembly, Jacob emphasizes the severity of Simeon and Levi's actions. This separation underscores the importance of maintaining purity and righteousness, as seen in 2 Corinthians 6:14-17, which speaks about not being unequally yoked with unbelievers. The assembly here is not just a physical gathering but represents shared values and actions.

For they kill men in their anger,
This phrase highlights the violent nature of Simeon and Levi, referencing their actions in Genesis 34, where they avenged their sister Dinah by killing the men of Shechem. Anger leading to murder is condemned throughout Scripture, as seen in Matthew 5:21-22, where Jesus equates anger with murder in the heart. This serves as a warning against allowing emotions to lead to sin.

and hamstring oxen on a whim.
Hamstringing oxen was a cruel act that rendered the animals useless for work, reflecting wanton destruction and disregard for God's creation. This act symbolizes unnecessary violence and impulsive cruelty. In a broader sense, it represents the misuse of power and authority, contrasting with the stewardship and care expected of God's people, as seen in Proverbs 12:10, which speaks of the righteous caring for the needs of their animals.

Teaching Points
The Consequences of Anger
Simeon and Levi's actions serve as a warning about the destructive power of uncontrolled anger. Believers are called to exercise self-control and seek God's wisdom in handling conflicts.

The Importance of Godly Counsel
Jacob's desire to avoid their assembly highlights the need for surrounding oneself with wise and godly counsel. Christians should seek fellowship with those who encourage righteousness.

Redemption and Transformation
Despite their past, the tribe of Levi is later set apart for God's service. This illustrates God's ability to redeem and transform our past mistakes for His purposes.

The Impact of Our Actions on Legacy
Simeon and Levi's actions affected their descendants' inheritance. Our actions today can have long-lasting effects on future generations, emphasizing the importance of living righteously.

Zeal for God vs. Misguided Zeal
The Levites' later zeal for God contrasts with their earlier misguided actions. Believers should channel their passion into serving God and upholding His standards.

What Does Genesis 49:6 Mean
May I never enter their council

Jacob speaks with fatherly firmness as he blesses his sons, yet withdraws fellowship from Simeon and Levi. He refuses to step into their inner circle—where their plans are hatched—because partnership with unrepentant violence pollutes the one who partners. Psalm 1:1 echoes the wisdom: “Blessed is the man who does not walk in the counsel of the wicked.” Proverbs 4:14 warns, “Do not set foot on the path of the wicked,” and 1 Corinthians 15:33 reminds, “Bad company corrupts good character.” In every era, God’s people safeguard their walk by avoiding councils that mock God’s ways.

May I never join their assembly

“Assembly” carries covenant flavor; it is the place of belonging. Jacob distances himself so completely that he even rejects ceremonial solidarity with these sons’ future tribes. The same principle surfaces later in Psalm 26:4–5, where David shuns the “assembly of evildoers,” and in 2 Corinthians 6:17, “Come out from among them and be separate.” Fellowship is not merely social; it shapes destiny. Jacob’s refusal anticipates God’s call for His redeemed to remain distinct.

For they kill men in their anger

Jacob names the crime: premeditated slaughter at Shechem (Genesis 34:25-26). Their rage moved beyond justice into bloodlust. Scripture consistently exposes such anger as destructive: Proverbs 29:22 says, “An angry man stirs up dissension,” and James 1:20 teaches that “man’s anger does not bring about the righteousness of God.” Human life, created in God’s image (Genesis 9:6), is sacred; shedding it in fury invites divine judgment (Matthew 5:21-22).

and hamstring oxen on a whim

The brothers’ violence extended to animals, slashing tendons so beasts could not work again—wanton cruelty that served no moral purpose. Proverbs 12:10 affirms, “The righteous care for the needs of their animals,” while Deuteronomy 25:4 commands mercy even in labor, “Do not muzzle an ox while it is treading out the grain.” Their reckless impulse shows a heart unbridled, ignoring God’s ownership of every creature (Psalm 50:10).

summary

Genesis 49:6 reveals Jacob’s Spirit-led discernment: a godly man must refuse intimate alliance with persistent, unrepentant violence. Simeon and Levi’s unchecked anger defiled both human life and animal creation, proving they were unsafe partners in destiny. Scripture calls believers to separate from wicked schemes, cherish life, and embody mercy—walking in fellowship with God rather than with those who trample His image and handiwork.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Their secret.--The word sod used here is literally the little carpet, or cushion, upon which an Oriental sits. Consequently, for two persons to sit upon the same carpet marks a high degree of friendship and familiarity. It would therefore be more exactly translated alliance, or intimacy.

Unto their assembly, mine honour, be not thou united.--For assembly (Heb. congregation), see Genesis 28:3; Genesis 35:11. It means here their union, or confederacy. In the first clause Jacob bids his soul, his true self, not to enter their alliance; here, after the manner of the parallelism of Hebrew poetry, he intensifies the meaning. For by mine honour, he signifies all that gave him dignity and worth in the sight of God and man. And this nobleness would be degraded and lost by union with men banded together for evil.

In their self-will they digged down a wall--Self-will is worse than anger, and signifies that arrogant temper which leads on to wanton cruelty. The last words mean, they houghed an ox. The Vulgand Syriac took it as our version does, and understood it of making a breach in the walls of Shechem; but they had a different reading, shur, whereas the word in the Hebrew is shor, an ox, and it is so rendered by the LXX. The ox was in old times the symbol of majesty, and thus bulls are put for princes in Psalm 22:12; Psalm 68:30. Thus, then, the meaning is, "In their anger at the wrong done to their sister they slew Hamor, prince of Shechem, with his people; and from wanton cruelty, without any just cause for indignation, they hamstrung the noblest of their brethren, not killing Joseph outright, but disabling him by selling him into slavery, that he might there perish."
