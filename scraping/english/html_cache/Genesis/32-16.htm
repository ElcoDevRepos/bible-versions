Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He entrusted them to his servants
Jacob, preparing to meet Esau, entrusts his livestock to his servants. This act of delegation reflects his wealth and status, as he has numerous servants to manage his possessions. In biblical times, servants often played crucial roles in managing household affairs, similar to Abraham's servant Eliezer. This delegation also shows Jacob's strategic planning, as he uses his resources to appease Esau.

in separate herds
Jacob divides the animals into separate groups, a strategic move to create a buffer and potentially soften Esau's heart. This division could symbolize the careful planning and wisdom often attributed to Jacob. It also reflects the cultural practices of the time, where large herds were managed in smaller groups for better control and protection.

and told them, “Go on ahead of me
Jacob instructs his servants to lead the herds ahead, indicating his cautious approach to meeting Esau. This action demonstrates his humility and desire for reconciliation, as he sends gifts before him to pacify his brother. It echoes the biblical principle of peacemaking, as seen in Proverbs 21:14, where a gift given in secret can pacify anger.

and keep some distance between the herds.”
By instructing his servants to maintain distance between the herds, Jacob aims to create a prolonged and impactful presentation of his wealth and goodwill. This tactic could serve to impress Esau and mitigate any lingering hostility. The spacing of the herds also reflects a strategic approach to conflict resolution, emphasizing patience and foresight.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jacob
The central figure in this passage, Jacob is preparing to meet his brother Esau after many years. He is concerned about Esau's reaction and is taking strategic steps to appease him.

2. Esau
Jacob's brother, whom Jacob is about to meet. Their relationship has been strained since Jacob deceived Esau out of his birthright and blessing.

3. Servants
These are the individuals Jacob entrusts with the herds. They play a role in executing Jacob's plan to pacify Esau.

4. Herds
The livestock that Jacob sends ahead as gifts to Esau. They are divided into separate groups to create a continuous offering.

5. The Journey
The event of Jacob's return to Canaan, which is a significant moment in his life as he reconciles with his past and his brother.
Teaching Points
Strategic Peacemaking
Jacob's actions demonstrate the importance of thoughtful and strategic efforts in making peace with others. We should consider how we can proactively address conflicts in our own lives.

Trust in God's Plan
Despite his fear, Jacob moves forward with trust in God's promises. We are reminded to trust God even when facing potentially hostile situations.

Generosity as a Bridge
Jacob's use of gifts to appease Esau highlights the role of generosity in mending relationships. We can apply this by using our resources to bless and reconcile with others.

Preparation and Prayer
Jacob's preparation is coupled with prayer (Genesis 32:9-12). This teaches us the importance of combining practical steps with spiritual reliance.

Facing the Past
Jacob's journey back to Esau is a journey of facing his past mistakes. We are encouraged to confront our past with courage and seek reconciliation.

What Does Genesis 32:16 Mean
He entrusted them to his servants

Jacob literally places the animals into the hands of trusted men. The text underscores: “He entrusted them to his servants.” By doing so, Jacob models wise stewardship—he owns the flocks, yet assigns responsibility to faithful helpers (cf. Genesis 24:2 where Abraham does the same; Matthew 25:14–15 where a master entrusts talents to servants). Delegation protects the assets God has provided and frees Jacob to concentrate on the imminent meeting with Esau. It also reflects humility; rather than clutching everything himself, he relies on dependable workers whom the Lord has put under his care.

in separate herds

The livestock are deliberately grouped. This tactic is both strategic and generous:

• Spaced‐out gifts magnify the size of the present (Genesis 32:20, “I will appease him with the gift that is going before me”).

• If one herd is seized, others may survive (Proverbs 22:3, “The prudent see danger and take cover”).

• By dividing assets, Jacob follows a pattern of diversification echoed later in Ecclesiastes 11:2, “Divide your portions to seven, yes, to eight.”

Literal organization of the herds displays prudence and foresight—qualities already evident when Jacob increased his flocks under Laban (Genesis 30:43).

Go on ahead of me

Jacob directs, “Go on ahead of me.” The gifts must precede him, softening Esau’s heart before personal contact. This mirrors Proverbs 18:16, “A gift opens the way.” It anticipates God’s own practice of going before His people (Exodus 13:21) and Jesus’ assurance, “I go to prepare a place for you” (John 14:2–3). Jacob’s instruction also keeps him free to care for his family at the rear (Genesis 33:1–3). He steps forward only after making every possible peace effort—an example of living at peace with all men so far as it depends on you (Romans 12:18).

Keep some distance between the herds

Physical spacing matters: “keep some distance between the herds.” The trailing lines of animals ensure Esau encounters wave after wave of generosity, giving time for reflection between each surprise (Genesis 32:19). Orderly separation resembles Joshua 3:4, where distance around the Ark preserves reverence and clarity. It also echoes 1 Corinthians 14:40, “Let all things be done decently and in order.” The gap allows each servant to declare Jacob’s message afresh, multiplying the impact of the reconciliation gesture.

summary

Genesis 32:16 presents Jacob’s intentional, orderly plan to appease Esau: he entrusts valuable livestock to servants, divides them into successive herds, sends them ahead, and spaces them for maximum effect. Every detail shows practical wisdom, humility, and faith in God’s providence while honoring the literal narrative. The verse encourages believers to steward God’s resources responsibly, plan thoughtfully, and pursue peace actively—always trusting the Lord who goes before us.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) A space.--Heb., a breathing place. These paration of the droves would be a matter of course, as each kind would travel peaceably onward only by itself. But Jacob rightly concluded that the repeated acknowledgment of Esau as his lord, added to the great value of the gift, would fill his brother's heart with friendly feelings, and perhaps therefore he put a longer space than usual between the successive droves.
