When Eber was 34 years old
Eber is a significant figure in biblical genealogies, often considered the ancestor of the Hebrews. His name is associated with the term "Hebrew," which is used to describe the descendants of Abraham. The age of 34, while not particularly symbolic, indicates the early maturity and family responsibilities typical in ancient times. This period in Genesis is post-Flood, where lifespans were gradually decreasing, yet people still lived much longer than today. Eber's lineage is crucial as it connects to the line of Shem, one of Noah's sons, emphasizing the continuity of God's covenant through specific family lines.

he became the father of Peleg
Peleg's name is significant as it means "division" in Hebrew. This is traditionally linked to the division of the earth, which some scholars associate with the Tower of Babel event described earlier in Genesis 11. This division could refer to the scattering of peoples and languages, marking a pivotal moment in human history. Peleg's birth is a chronological marker in the genealogical record, highlighting the fulfillment of God's plan through specific individuals. The genealogies in Genesis serve to trace the lineage leading to Abraham, and ultimately to Jesus Christ, underscoring the theme of divine providence and the unfolding of salvation history.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Eber
A descendant of Shem, Eber is an important figure in the genealogical line leading to Abraham. His name is often associated with the term "Hebrew," which is derived from his name. Eber represents a link in the chain of God's unfolding plan for humanity.

2. Peleg
The son of Eber, Peleg's name means "division," which is significant because it is during his lifetime that the earth was divided, possibly referring to the division of languages at the Tower of Babel or a geographical division.

3. Genealogical Record
This verse is part of the genealogical record in Genesis 11, which traces the lineage from Shem to Abram (later Abraham). It highlights the continuity of God's promise through specific family lines.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Genealogy in Scripture
Genealogies in the Bible are not mere lists of names; they demonstrate God's faithfulness in fulfilling His promises through specific family lines. They remind us of the continuity of God's plan throughout history.

Significance of Names
In biblical times, names often held prophetic or significant meanings. Peleg's name, meaning "division," points to a pivotal event in human history. This reminds us to consider the deeper meanings and implications of names and events in our own lives.

God's Sovereignty in History
The genealogical records show that God is sovereign over history, guiding events and people according to His divine plan. This encourages us to trust in God's control over our personal and collective histories.

Faithfulness Across Generations
The lineage from Eber to Abraham highlights the importance of faithfulness across generations. It challenges us to consider how we can pass on our faith and values to future generations.

What Does Genesis 11:16 Mean
Immediate Context

- Genesis 11 continues the genealogy of Shem, moving steadily toward Abram; each link is historical, intentional, and literal (Genesis 11:10–26).

- Verse 16 records Eber’s place in that chain: “When Eber was 34 years old, he became the father of Peleg.”

- By placing this statement after the account of Babel (Genesis 11:1–9) and before Terah’s family (Genesis 11:27–32), Scripture anchors the scattering of nations and the call of Abram in real people and real years.

The Significance of Eber

- Eber is the great-grandson of Shem (Genesis 10:21–24), and his name becomes the root of the term “Hebrew” used of Abram in Genesis 14:13.

- Through Eber, God preserves a distinct family line, shielding the promise first given to Noah’s son Shem (Genesis 9:26–27).

- His appearance in Luke 3:35 situates him in the Messianic genealogy, underscoring that the Savior’s lineage is firmly grounded in Old Testament history.

The Literal Age of 34

- The precise number emphasizes the reliability of Scripture’s chronology. Moses does not use rounded figures; he records actual ages, inviting trust in the text’s accuracy (cf. Genesis 5:3, 6: “Adam lived 130 years… Seth lived 105 years…”).

- Counting backward from other ages in Genesis 11, Eber’s 34th year falls about a century after the Flood (Genesis 11:10–15), highlighting how rapidly families multiplied under God’s blessing (Genesis 9:1).

The Birth of Peleg

- Peleg means “division,” and Genesis 10:25 notes, “in his days the earth was divided.”

• This likely points to the dispersal at Babel—God’s direct intervention scattering people groups (Genesis 11:8).

• Naming a child after that event preserves its memory inside the covenant line.

- The verse reminds us that God is sovereign over both personal milestones (a son’s birth) and global events (the dividing of nations).

The Thread of Promise

- From Shem to Eber to Peleg, God keeps narrowing the line that will culminate in Abram and, eventually, Christ (Galatians 3:16).

- Each generational note echoes earlier assurances: the woman’s Seed will come (Genesis 3:15); nations will be blessed through Abram (Genesis 12:3).

- By recording even “minor” figures, Scripture shows that no link in God’s redemptive chain is accidental.

Applications for Today

- Dependability: If God tracks ages and births with such precision, He can be trusted with every detail of our lives (Psalm 139:16; Matthew 10:30).

- Heritage: Believers inherit a faith rooted in real history, not myth; this bolsters confidence when sharing the gospel (2 Peter 1:16).

- Perspective: God weaves personal joys (a child’s birth) into His vast plan for the nations (Acts 17:26), reminding us our ordinary moments carry eternal significance.

summary

Genesis 11:16 is more than a passing genealogical note. It certifies that Eber literally fathered Peleg at age thirty-four, anchoring the post-Flood world in verifiable history and advancing the unbroken line toward Abraham and Christ. The verse showcases God’s faithfulness to preserve His covenant family amid worldwide upheaval, assuring readers that every life event—big or small—fits securely within His redemptive purposes.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 16, 17. - And Eber lived four and thirty years, and begat Peleg. Division; from palag, to divide. For the reason of this cognomen vide Genesis 10:25. And Eber lived after he begat Peleg four hundred and thirty years (thus reaching the age of 464, the longest-lived of the postdiluvian fathers), and begat sons and daughters.
