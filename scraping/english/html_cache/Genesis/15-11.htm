And the birds of prey descended on the carcasses
In the ancient Near East, birds of prey were often seen as symbols of death and destruction. The presence of these birds in the narrative signifies a threat to the covenant ceremony that Abram is conducting. This imagery can be connected to the spiritual warfare that believers face, as seen in Ephesians 6:12, where spiritual forces oppose God's plans. The carcasses represent the sacrificial animals Abram laid out as part of a covenant ritual, a common practice in ancient times to seal agreements. This act foreshadows the sacrificial system later established in the Mosaic Law and ultimately points to the sacrifice of Jesus Christ, the fulfillment of God's covenant with humanity.

but Abram drove them away
Abram's action of driving away the birds demonstrates his active participation in safeguarding the covenant process. This reflects the believer's role in maintaining faith and resisting spiritual opposition, as encouraged in James 4:7, "Resist the devil, and he will flee from you." Abram's vigilance is a testament to his faith and commitment to God's promises. This act also prefigures the protective role of Christ, who intercedes for believers and ensures the fulfillment of God's promises, as seen in Hebrews 7:25. Abram's determination to protect the covenant ceremony underscores the importance of perseverance in faith and the active role believers must take in their spiritual journey.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Abram (Abraham)
The central figure in this passage, Abram is later renamed Abraham by God. He is a key patriarch in the Bible, known for his faith and obedience to God. In this context, he is in the midst of a covenant ceremony with God.

2. Birds of Prey
These represent obstacles or threats to the covenant ceremony. In the ancient Near East, birds of prey often symbolized evil or demonic forces.

3. The Carcasses
These are the animal sacrifices that Abram prepared as part of the covenant ritual with God. The animals were cut in half, a common practice in ancient covenant-making.

4. Covenant Ceremony
This event is a pivotal moment where God makes a covenant with Abram, promising him numerous descendants and land.

5. The Land of Canaan
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, the land is part of the promise God makes to Abram in this chapter.
Teaching Points
Faith in Action
Abram's act of driving away the birds of prey demonstrates his active participation in God's covenant. Faith often requires us to take action to protect and nurture what God has promised us.

Spiritual Warfare
The birds of prey symbolize spiritual attacks or distractions that can threaten our relationship with God. Like Abram, we must be vigilant and proactive in defending our spiritual commitments.

Covenant Commitment
The preparation and protection of the sacrifices highlight the seriousness of covenant relationships. Our commitments to God should be treated with reverence and dedication.

Divine Promises
God's promises to Abram remind us of His faithfulness. We can trust that God will fulfill His promises, even when obstacles arise.

Perseverance in Faith
Abram's perseverance in the face of potential disruption teaches us the importance of steadfastness in our faith journey.

What Does Genesis 15:11 Mean
And the birds of prey descended on the carcasses

• Abram has just prepared the sacrificial animals as God instructed (Genesis 15:9–10). In Scripture, exposed carcasses often attract predators and symbolize threat or desecration (Deuteronomy 28:26; Jeremiah 34:20).

• The birds represent anything that would profane, steal, or hinder the covenant God is about to ratify. Later, Jesus uses birds snatching seed to picture the evil one snatching the word from hearts (Matthew 13:4, 19), echoing this imagery.

but Abram drove them away

• Abram’s active defense shows his faith in God’s promise. Rather than passively waiting, he guards what God has set apart, illustrating James 2:17—faith acts.

• His vigilance foreshadows Israel’s call to separate from unclean influences (Exodus 19:6; 2 Corinthians 6:17) and the believer’s charge to “resist the devil” (James 4:7).

• By protecting the sacrifices until God’s fire consumes them (Genesis 15:17), Abram demonstrates perseverance in spiritual duty (Ephesians 6:13).

summary

Genesis 15:11 pictures hostile forces seeking to defile God’s covenant, yet Abram’s faithful action preserves what is holy. The verse calls believers to guard the sacred, reject corruption, and stand watch until God fulfills His word.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) And when the fowls . . . --Heb., And the birds of prey came down upon the carcases, and Abram scared them away. Had there been a sacrifice the fire would have kept the vultures from approaching; but the bodies lay exposed, and Abram therefore kept guard over them, lest the purpose of the ceremonial should be frustrated by any want of respect shown to the outward symbols.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - And when the fowls - literally, and the bird of prey, a collective singular with the article, as in Genesis 14:13, symbolizing the Egyptians and other adversaries of Israel, as in Ezekiel 17:3, 7, 12; Ezekiel 39:4, 17; Revelation 19:17, 18 (Knobel, Rosenmüller, Lunge, Keil, Kalisch), which may be regarded as probable if the divided victims represented Israel in affliction, which is doubtful (vide supra). It does not appear necessary to attach any special significance to the descent of the vultures, which are always attracted towards carrion, and the introduction of which here completes the naturalness of the scene - came down upon the caresses (the LXX. interpolates, ἐπὶ τὰ διχοτομήματα), Abram drove them away. Literally, caused them to be blown away, i.e. by blowing. "Though Abram is here represented as the instrument, yet the effect is to be ascribed primarily to the tutelar agency of omnipotence" (Bush; cf. Exodus 15:10; Ezekiel 21:31). The act of scaring the voracious birds has been taken to represent the ease with which Abram or Israel would ward off his enemies (Jonathan, Targums, Rosenmüller, Bush); the averting of destruction from the Israelites through Abram's merit (Kalisch, Keil); Abram's religious regard for and observance of God's treaty (Wordsworth); the patriarch's expectation that God was about to employ the sacrificial victims for some holy purpose (Alford); simply his anxiety to preserve the victims pure and un-mutilated for whatever end they might have to serve (Murphy).
