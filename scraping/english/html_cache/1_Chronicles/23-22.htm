Eleazar died without having any sons;
Eleazar was a Levite, a descendant of Aaron, and his death without male heirs is significant in the context of Israelite inheritance laws. According to the Torah, specifically Numbers 27:8-11, if a man died without sons, his inheritance would pass to his daughters. This reflects the importance of maintaining family lineage and property within the tribe. The absence of sons could have implications for the continuation of Eleazar's priestly duties, as the priesthood was typically passed from father to son.

he had only daughters.
The mention of daughters highlights the flexibility within the Israelite inheritance system, allowing for daughters to inherit in the absence of sons. This provision ensured that the family name and property remained within the tribe. The daughters of Eleazar would have been responsible for upholding their father's legacy and maintaining the family's role within the Levitical duties. This situation is reminiscent of the daughters of Zelophehad in Numbers 27, who successfully petitioned Moses for the right to inherit their father's land.

Their cousins, the sons of Kish, married them.
The marriage of Eleazar's daughters to their cousins, the sons of Kish, underscores the importance of endogamy, or marrying within one's tribe or family, in ancient Israelite culture. This practice helped preserve tribal identity and property within the family line. Kish was also a Levite, ensuring that the Levitical duties and inheritance remained within the appropriate tribal lineage. This arrangement reflects the broader biblical theme of God's provision and order within the community of Israel, ensuring that His laws and structures are upheld even in complex family situations.

What Does 1 Chronicles 23:22 Mean
Eleazar died

- 1 Chronicles places Eleazar, son of Mahli and grandson of Merari, inside the Levitical family tree (1 Chron 23:21).

- The Chronicler faithfully records each branch to show that every priestly duty rested on a real, historic person (cf. 1 Chron 6:1, 46).

- By noting Eleazar’s death, Scripture reminds us that priestly service passes from one generation to the next; God’s work continues even when His servants do not (Deuteronomy 33:10; Psalm 102:18).

Without having any sons

- In Israel, land and ministry assignments normally passed through sons (Numbers 18:20; Deuteronomy 21:16-17).

- The absence of male heirs could threaten both property and priestly responsibilities, yet God had already provided guidelines for such cases (Numbers 27:1-8).

- This detail underscores the precision of God’s Law and His care for every family situation.

He had only daughters

- Daughters inherited when no sons were present, but they were expected to keep the inheritance inside their tribe to prevent territory from shifting (Numbers 36:6-9).

- The Chronicler’s straightforward statement affirms that Eleazar’s line remained valid and honored, even through daughters.

- Their presence in the record highlights the value God places on women within His covenant purposes (Joshua 17:3-6).

Their cousins, the sons of Kish, married them

- Kish was Eleazar’s brother (1 Chron 23:21), so his sons were the girls’ first cousins.

- Marrying within the clan fulfilled Numbers 36:8-9, preserving both land and Levitical duties within Merari’s line.

- The arrangement echoes the kinsman-redeemer principle later exemplified by Boaz (Ruth 4:5-10), showing family loyalty that safeguards God-given inheritance.

- This marriage solution protected:

- The ancestral allotment (Leviticus 25:34).

- The continuity of service in the tabernacle and, later, the temple (1 Chron 24:26-30).

- The integrity of tribal boundaries until the promised Messiah would unite all things (Ezekiel 48:11; Luke 1:5).

summary

1 Chronicles 23:22 records a real family event to demonstrate God’s meticulous provision for His people. Eleazar’s daughters, lacking brothers, lawfully married their Merarite cousins so that land, lineage, and Levitical ministry stayed intact. The verse showcases God’s faithfulness to uphold His Law, protect inheritance, and honor both men and women in His redemptive plan.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) And Eleazar died, and had no sons.--Thus his house merged in that of the sons of Kish, who married his daughters according to the Law (Numbers 36:6-9). The sons of Mahli, then, were represented in David's day by the house of Kish. (See 1Chronicles 24:29.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Their brethren... took them; i.e. their kinsmen, as margin, "took them" to wife (Numbers 36:5-12). (For the sons of Kish, see 1 Chronicles 24:29.)
