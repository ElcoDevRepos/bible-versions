Jerahmeel had another wife
Jerahmeel is a descendant of Judah, the fourth son of Jacob, and is part of the genealogical records in 1 Chronicles. The mention of another wife indicates the practice of polygamy, which was common in ancient Israelite culture. This practice is seen in other biblical figures such as Abraham, Jacob, and David. Polygamy often led to familial strife, as seen in the stories of Leah and Rachel or Hannah and Peninnah.

named Atarah
The name Atarah means "crown" in Hebrew, which may symbolize honor or status. Names in the Bible often carry significant meaning and can reflect the character or destiny of the person. Atarah's mention by name suggests her importance in Jerahmeel's lineage, although little else is known about her. The naming of women in genealogies is relatively rare, indicating her potential significance.

who was the mother of Onam
Onam is listed as a descendant of Jerahmeel, contributing to the genealogical record of the tribe of Judah. The genealogies in Chronicles serve to establish the heritage and tribal affiliations of the Israelites, which were crucial for land inheritance and social identity. Onam's inclusion highlights the continuation of Jerahmeel's line through Atarah, emphasizing the importance of lineage and heritage in biblical narratives.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Genealogy
Genealogies in the Bible are not just historical records; they demonstrate God's faithfulness in preserving His people and fulfilling His promises. Reflect on how God has been faithful in your own family history.

Understanding Biblical Marriage Practices
The mention of multiple wives in the Old Testament reflects cultural practices of the time. Consider how cultural context influences our understanding of biblical accounts and how we apply biblical principles to modern life.

God's Sovereignty in Family Lines
Despite human imperfections and cultural practices, God's sovereign plan unfolds through family lines. Trust in God's overarching plan for your life, even when circumstances seem complex or challenging.

The Role of Women in Biblical Narratives
Atarah's mention, though brief, highlights the role of women in biblical genealogies. Recognize and appreciate the contributions of women in your own spiritual community.

Legacy and Spiritual Heritage
Onam's inclusion in the genealogy reminds us of the importance of leaving a spiritual legacy. Consider how you can invest in the next generation to ensure a lasting spiritual heritage.

What Does 1 Chronicles 2:26 Mean
Jerahmeel

• 1 Chronicles 2 places Jerahmeel among “the sons of Hezron,” identifying him as a direct descendant of Judah (1 Chron 2:4–9, 25).

• His presence in the genealogies underscores how carefully God preserved Judah’s family line, the tribe from which the Messiah would come (Genesis 49:10; Matthew 1:2–3).

• Though Jerahmeel’s branch is not the one that leads to David, its inclusion verifies every family in Judah, highlighting the historical accuracy of Scripture.

Had another wife

• The verse states, “Jerahmeel had another wife…” (1 Chron 2:26), indicating he had more than one wife.

• Scripture sometimes records polygamy without approving it; it simply reports what occurred (Genesis 4:19; 2 Samuel 5:13).

• God’s original design was one man and one woman (Genesis 2:24). Yet the Law later addressed complications of multiple wives (Deuteronomy 21:15–17), showing God’s mercy in regulating, not endorsing, the custom.

Named Atarah

• The Chronicler pauses to name this woman—“another wife named Atarah.” In Old Testament genealogies, women are rarely mentioned, so when a woman is singled out, she matters to the story (cf. Ruth 4:13–22; Matthew 1:3, 5).

• By recording Atarah, the text affirms the value of women in God’s unfolding plan, reminding readers that every individual, male or female, is known to God.

Who was the mother of Onam

• The phrase concludes: “…who was the mother of Onam.” Onam becomes important because his sons, Shammai and Jada, are listed next (1 Chron 2:28).

• Naming Onam’s mother ties future generations to their maternal line, a literary hint that God monitors each branch with precision (Psalm 139:15–16).

• The detail also sets Atarah’s children apart from those of Jerahmeel’s first wife, clarifying inheritance and tribal responsibilities (Numbers 36:1–9).

summary

1 Chronicles 2:26 records a simple fact—“Jerahmeel had another wife named Atarah, who was the mother of Onam”—yet it reinforces major biblical themes: God faithfully chronicles every family in Judah; He acknowledges women’s roles; He records, without endorsing, the realities of human marriage customs; and He tracks lineage with meticulous care so that His promises move steadily toward fulfillment in Christ.
