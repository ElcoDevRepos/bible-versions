Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Now, my son
This phrase indicates a personal and intimate address from David to Solomon, his son. It reflects the passing of responsibility and blessing from one generation to the next. In the biblical context, the father-son relationship is significant, often symbolizing the transfer of wisdom and divine promises. David's role as a father is not only biological but also spiritual, guiding Solomon in the ways of the Lord.

may the LORD be with you
This is a blessing and a prayer for divine presence and guidance. The presence of the Lord is crucial for success and prosperity, as seen throughout the Old Testament. The phrase echoes the covenantal promise God made to be with His people, as seen in Genesis 28:15 and Joshua 1:9. It underscores the belief that human efforts are futile without God's presence.

and may you succeed in building the house of the LORD your God
David expresses his desire for Solomon to fulfill the divine mandate of constructing the temple, a task David himself was not permitted to complete (1 Chronicles 17:4). The temple is not just a physical structure but a symbol of God's dwelling among His people. The success of this endeavor is tied to obedience and faithfulness to God's commands, as seen in 1 Kings 2:3-4.

as He said you would
This phrase refers to the divine promise and prophecy given to David regarding Solomon's role in building the temple (1 Chronicles 17:11-12). It highlights the fulfillment of God's word and the importance of divine sovereignty in the unfolding of biblical history. This assurance of God's promise is a recurring theme, emphasizing that God's plans are certain and will come to pass, as seen in Isaiah 55:11.

Teaching Points
Divine Presence and Success
Success in any endeavor, especially spiritual ones, requires the presence and blessing of the LORD. Just as David prayed for Solomon, we should seek God's presence in our lives and tasks.

Generational Legacy
David's charge to Solomon highlights the importance of passing on spiritual responsibilities and blessings to the next generation. We should be intentional in mentoring and preparing those who come after us.

Obedience to God's Commands
Solomon's task was not just a personal ambition but a divine command. Our pursuits should align with God's will and commands, ensuring that our efforts are not in vain.

Preparation and Planning
David made extensive preparations for the temple, showing the importance of planning and preparation in fulfilling God's purposes. We should be diligent and thoughtful in our preparations for any God-given task.

Faith in God's Promises
David's confidence in Solomon's success was rooted in God's promise. We should hold fast to God's promises, trusting that He will fulfill them in His time and way.

What Does 1 Chronicles 22:11 Mean
Now, my son

• David speaks tenderly to Solomon, passing on a father’s spiritual legacy. This echoes Moses’ call for parents to impress God’s commands on their children (Deuteronomy 6:6-7, “These words … are to be upon your hearts. Teach them diligently to your children,”).

• The personal address reminds Solomon that royal authority is still under parental—and ultimately divine—guidance, much like Jacob’s blessings over his sons in Genesis 49.

• It signals a transition of responsibility: David’s wartime reign is ending; Solomon’s peacetime building task is beginning (1 Chronicles 28:3).

may the LORD be with you

• David invokes the covenant name “YHWH,” assuring Solomon that the same God who sustained Israel will accompany him.

• This blessing mirrors Joshua 1:9, “Be strong and courageous … for the LORD your God is with you wherever you go,”, showing continuity in God’s presence from conquest to construction.

• God’s nearness is the true source of courage and wisdom (Psalm 23:4; 2 Timothy 4:17).

and may you succeed in building the house of the LORD your God

• “Succeed” ties Solomon’s future to God’s empowering favor; success is defined not by wealth or fame but by completing God’s assignment (Proverbs 16:3, “Commit your works to the LORD and your plans will be achieved,”).

• The “house of the LORD” underscores the covenant purpose: a fixed place for God’s name on earth (1 Kings 5:5).

• David’s words reinforce that Solomon’s engineering skill, political alliances, and resources are secondary to divine enablement (Psalm 127:1, “Unless the LORD builds the house, its builders labor in vain,”).

as He said you would

• David anchors his charge in God’s unbreakable promise: 2 Samuel 7:13, “He will build a house for My name, and I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever,”.

• By recalling the promise, David teaches Solomon to measure progress by God’s word rather than human opinion (Isaiah 55:11).

• The phrase affirms the literal reliability of prophecy—what God has spoken will surely come to pass (1 Kings 8:15-20 records Solomon’s later testimony that every word was fulfilled).

summary

1 Chronicles 22:11 is David’s Spirit-led blessing transferring the temple mandate to Solomon. It underscores four truths: (1) spiritual heritage flows through godly parents; (2) God’s presence is indispensable; (3) true success is fulfilling God’s specific assignment; (4) God’s promises are certain. The verse calls every believer to embrace God-given tasks with confidence that His presence and promises guarantee the outcome.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) The Lord be with thee.--See 1Chronicles 9:20. (1Samuel 3:19; 2Kings 18:7 : "The Lord was with him.") The phrase is the origin of the familiar liturgical formula, "The Lord be with you."

And prosper thou, and build the house.--Not a command, but a wish, i.e., mayest thou prosper and build. The verb "prosper" (literally, carry through, make succeed) is used transitively in 2Chronicles 7:11 and Genesis 24:40. . . .
