Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
These men listed by name
The chronicler often lists genealogies to establish the heritage and legitimacy of the tribes of Israel. Names in biblical genealogies are significant as they often reflect character, destiny, or divine intervention. The listing of names serves to preserve the memory of these individuals and their roles in the history of Israel. This practice underscores the importance of lineage and identity in the biblical narrative, reflecting God's covenantal promises to the patriarchs.

were the leaders of their clans
Leadership in ancient Israel was often based on familial lines, with clan leaders holding significant authority and responsibility. These leaders were responsible for the welfare, justice, and spiritual guidance of their people. The mention of leaders highlights the structured social order within the tribes, which was crucial for maintaining unity and identity, especially during times of exile or dispersion. Leadership in the Bible often foreshadows the ultimate leadership of Christ, who is the head of the Church.

Their families increased greatly
The growth of families is a sign of divine blessing in the Old Testament. It reflects the fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham that his descendants would be as numerous as the stars. This increase also signifies stability and prosperity, which were essential for the survival and strength of the tribes. The expansion of families can be seen as a precursor to the growth of the Church, as the spiritual descendants of Abraham, through faith in Christ, continue to multiply across the earth.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Leaders of their clans
The verse refers to individuals who held positions of authority within their family groups or tribes. These leaders were responsible for guiding and managing the affairs of their clans.

2. Families increased greatly
This phrase indicates a period of growth and prosperity for these families, suggesting that they were blessed with numerous descendants and possibly material wealth.

What Does 1 Chronicles 4:38 Mean
These men listed by name

• God intentionally preserves these names so future generations remember real people, not faceless statistics (see Exodus 32:32 and Isaiah 49:16, “I have engraved you on the palms of My hands”).

• Earlier in the chapter, similar lists trace Judah’s line (1 Chronicles 4:1–23); the inspired record affirms that each individual matters in God’s unfolding plan.

• By recording them “by name,” Scripture highlights God’s personal knowledge and care, echoing Jesus’ assurance that even “the very hairs of your head are all numbered” (Matthew 10:30).

were the leaders of their clans

• Leadership here is more than administrative; it carries spiritual weight, like the tribal chiefs Moses appointed (Numbers 1:16) and the valiant men who later joined David (1 Chronicles 12:32).

• God raises up leaders to guide families in covenant faithfulness (Deuteronomy 6:6–9). These clan heads modeled trust and obedience, much as Joshua did when he declared, “As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15).

• The phrase reminds us that influence begins at home: healthy clans build a strong nation, just as elders in the early church shepherded local congregations (Acts 14:23).

Their families increased greatly

• This growth fulfills God’s creation mandate: “Be fruitful and multiply” (Genesis 1:28). It signals His blessing, for “children are a heritage from the LORD” (Psalm 127:3–5).

• Covenant obedience brings multiplication (Deuteronomy 7:13); the Chronicler links faithfulness with flourishing.

• The verse also prepares readers for the expansion into new territory described in the next verses (1 Chronicles 4:39–43), showing how God equips His people numerically before giving broader assignments, just as He did with Israel in Egypt (Exodus 1:7).

summary

1 Chronicles 4:38 presents a snapshot of God-blessed leadership: named individuals, recognized as clan heads, experience abundant family growth. The verse reassures readers that God notices every person, values faithful leaders, and delights to multiply His people when they walk in covenant obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(38) These mentioned by their names.--Literally, these who have come (forward) with names, that is, have been adduced by name.

Were princes in their families.--Ameers or chieftains in their clans.

And the house of their fathers increased.--And their father-houses had spread greatly. Finding their territory too strait for them under these conditions, and probably also because of the encroachments of their powerful neighbours, the Judaeans and Philistines, the Simeonite chieftains went forth at the head of their clans to seek new settlements.

And (so) they went to the entrance of Gedor, even unto the east side of the valley.--Gedor can hardly be the town of that name in the hill country of Judah (Joshua 15:58). The LXX. read Gerar (??????).

Even unto the east side of the valley.--So far as to the east of the valley, that is, apparently, the valley of or near the unknown Gedor, or Gerar. The only considerable valley south-east of Judah is the Arabah, below the Dead Sea. That this locality is meant appears likely from the vicinity of Mount Seir and the Amalekites (1Chronicles 4:42).

To seek pasture for their flocks.--This statement is of interest as proving that even so late as the reign of Hezekiah, those Israelite clans which bordered on the desert were still nomades, like the Beidawis. (Comp. Genesis 13:5-12). . . .
