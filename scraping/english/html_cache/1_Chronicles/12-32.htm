From Issachar
Issachar was one of the twelve tribes of Israel, descended from Jacob's son Issachar. This tribe was known for its agricultural prowess, as their territory was fertile and conducive to farming. The tribe's location in the Jezreel Valley, a strategic and fertile area, contributed to their prosperity and influence. In the context of 1 Chronicles 12, the men of Issachar are joining David at Hebron to support his kingship, indicating their political insight and loyalty.

Men who understood the times
The men of Issachar are noted for their wisdom and discernment, particularly in understanding the times. This suggests they had a keen awareness of the political and social climate, which was crucial during the transition of power from Saul to David. Their ability to interpret the times can be likened to the wisdom literature in the Bible, such as Proverbs, which emphasizes understanding and discernment. This insight is also reminiscent of the sons of Issachar's role in advising Deborah in Judges 5:15.

And knew what Israel should do
Their understanding translated into practical action, as they knew what steps Israel should take. This reflects a combination of wisdom and leadership, as they were able to guide the nation in a time of change. This ability to discern and act is a valuable trait for leaders, as seen in other biblical figures like Joseph and Daniel, who also provided guidance during critical times.

200 chiefs
The mention of 200 chiefs indicates a significant number of leaders within the tribe of Issachar. This suggests a well-organized and influential group capable of making collective decisions. The presence of such a large number of leaders highlights the tribe's importance and the weight their support would carry in the political landscape of Israel.

With all their kinsmen at their command
The chiefs had the loyalty and support of their kinsmen, indicating a unified tribe. This unity would have been essential in presenting a strong front and ensuring that their decisions were backed by the entire tribe. The solidarity within the tribe of Issachar is a testament to their leadership and the respect they commanded, which would have been crucial in supporting David's claim to the throne.

What Does 1 Chronicles 12:32 Mean
From Issachar

The Chronicler singles out the tribe of Issachar among the warriors gathering to crown David at Hebron (1 Chronicles 12:23, 38). Earlier prophecy described Issachar as willing to “bend his shoulder to bear a burden” (Genesis 49:14–15), yet here they shoulder a different load—the burden of counsel. Their presence confirms every tribe’s share in God’s plan (Joshua 21:43–45) and reminds us that wisdom is as valuable to the nation as weaponry (Proverbs 24:5–6).

men who understood the times

• These leaders could read national currents, recognize God’s movements, and discern threats (Esther 1:13).

• Like the wise who grasp “the prudent see danger and take refuge” (Proverbs 22:3), they interpreted circumstances through God’s revealed promises (Deuteronomy 29:29).

• Jesus later commended the ability to “discern the signs of the times” (Matthew 16:2–3), showing continuity in God’s desire for spiritually alert followers.

and knew what Israel should do

• Insight moved beyond analysis to action; they translated discernment into direction (James 1:22).

• Their counsel aligned with God’s already declared will—support David, the anointed king (1 Samuel 16:12–13; Psalm 78:70–72).

• Like Moses’ father-in-law offering practical guidance (Exodus 18:19–20) and the apostles settling church disputes (Acts 15:28), they modeled godly leadership that protects unity and mission.

200 chiefs

• The text emphasizes quality over quantity; a small cadre of well-informed commanders can steer a nation (Ecclesiastes 9:13–18).

• “Chiefs” signals recognized authority (Numbers 1:16). Their agreement on David erased tribal rivalry and confirmed divine consensus (2 Samuel 5:1–3).

• God often employs a faithful remnant—whether Gideon’s 300 (Judges 7:7) or the twelve apostles (Luke 6:13)—to accomplish pivotal tasks.

with all their kinsmen at their command

• These chiefs were not isolated intellectuals; their households trusted their judgment and followed their lead (Joshua 24:15).

• Unified obedience turned discernment into decisive strength, mirroring how the early church “had one heart and soul” (Acts 4:32).

• The phrase shows that true leadership earns loyalty through proven wisdom and righteous living (1 Peter 5:3).

summary

1 Chronicles 12:32 highlights Issachar’s elders as models of spiritual perception and practical guidance. Rooted in God’s Word, they read their moment accurately, recognized David as God’s chosen king, and led their families to act in unity. The verse calls believers today to cultivate biblical insight, translate it into obedient action, and influence others toward God’s revealed purposes.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(32) And of the children of Issachar . . .--Rather, And of the sons of Issachar (came) men sage in discernment for the times (tempora, critical junctures), so as to know what Israel ought to do; viz., their chiefs two hundred (in number), and all their fellow clansmen under their orders. The old Jewish expositors concluded, from the former part of this verse that the tribe of Issachar had skill i?? astrology, so that they could read in the heavens what seasons were auspicious for action, as the ancient Babylonians professed to do. But all that the text really asserts is that those men of Issachar who went over to David thereby showed political sagacity. No similar phrase occurs elsewhere in the Old Testament.

At their commandment.--Upon their mouth. (Comp. Numbers 4:27.) The clansmen marched with their chieftains. The total number of Issachar's contingent is not assigned.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 32. - Had understanding of the times (2 Chronicles 2:12; Esther 1:13; Job 24:1). Compare Tacitus, "gnarus temporum" ('Agricola,' §6). This verse does not tell the number of the "children," but only of the "heads" of Issachar. It is possible that the number has slipped out. The description of the characteristics of Issachar here seems an advance upon that of Genesis 49:14, 15.
