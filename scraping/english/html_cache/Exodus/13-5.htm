Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And when the LORD brings you into the land of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Hivites, and Jebusites—
This phrase refers to the Promised Land, which God pledged to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. The mention of these specific groups highlights the diverse inhabitants of Canaan at the time. Historically, these tribes were known for their fortified cities and advanced cultures. Theologically, the conquest of these peoples symbolizes God's judgment on their idolatry and immorality, as well as His faithfulness to Israel. The presence of these nations also sets the stage for Israel's future challenges in maintaining purity and faithfulness to God amidst pagan influences.

the land He swore to your fathers that He would give you,
This part emphasizes the covenantal promise God made to the patriarchs: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. It underscores God's faithfulness and the certainty of His promises. The land is not just a geographical location but a tangible sign of God's enduring covenant. This promise is reiterated throughout Genesis (e.g., Genesis 12:7, 15:18-21) and serves as a foundation for Israel's identity and hope.

a land flowing with milk and honey—
This description paints a picture of abundance and prosperity. "Milk and honey" symbolize fertility and the richness of the land, indicating that it is a place where the Israelites can thrive. This phrase is used multiple times in the Pentateuch to describe the Promised Land (e.g., Exodus 3:8, Numbers 13:27). It contrasts with the Israelites' experience in Egypt and the wilderness, highlighting the blessings awaiting them.

you shall keep this service in this month.
The "service" refers to the observance of Passover and the Feast of Unleavened Bread, which commemorate Israel's deliverance from Egypt. This command underscores the importance of remembering God's salvation and maintaining a distinct identity as His people. The timing "in this month" refers to the month of Abib (later called Nisan), marking the beginning of the religious calendar for Israel. This observance is a perpetual reminder of God's mighty acts and a call to faithfulness.

What Does Exodus 13:5 Mean
And when the LORD brings you into the land

The verse opens with an assurance of divine action. Israel’s entrance is not a matter of chance or human might but of God’s personal intervention—“the LORD brings you.” This echoes earlier promises (Exodus 3:8; Deuteronomy 1:30-31) and highlights:

- God’s active leadership: He guides, protects, and shepherds His people (Psalm 23:1-4).

- Certainty, not possibility: “When,” not “if” (Numbers 23:19).

- A pattern of salvation: Just as He brought them out of Egypt (Exodus 12:51), He will bring them in (Deuteronomy 6:23).

of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Hivites, and Jebusites—

Listing the nations underscores the real, historical context of God’s promise (Exodus 3:17; Joshua 3:10). For Israel it meant:

- Tangible obstacles. These peoples were entrenched and formidable (Numbers 13:28-29).

- Total dependence on God for victory (Deuteronomy 7:1-2).

- A call to remain distinct from the surrounding culture (Leviticus 18:3; Joshua 23:12-13). Today we likewise face a surrounding culture, yet God equips us to live faithfully (1 Peter 2:9-12).

the land He swore to your fathers that He would give you,

The phrase ties the present generation to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (Genesis 17:7-8; 26:3; 28:13-15). It teaches:

- God’s covenant faithfulness spans centuries (Exodus 6:8).

- His oath guarantees fulfillment despite delays (Hebrews 6:13-18).

- Every promise finds completion on God’s timetable, inviting patient trust (Romans 4:20-21).

a land flowing with milk and honey—

This vivid picture speaks of extraordinary fertility and abundance (Numbers 13:27; Deuteronomy 8:7-10). For Israel it meant:

- Material blessing after slavery’s deprivation.

- A reminder that prosperity comes from the LORD, not from their own righteousness (Deuteronomy 9:4-6).

- A foretaste of ultimate rest and inheritance (Hebrews 4:8-9). For believers, the phrase anticipates the fullness of life found in Christ (John 10:10) and the promised heavenly country (Revelation 21:1-4).

you shall keep this service in this month.

“This service” refers to the Passover and Feast of Unleavened Bread just described (Exodus 12:14, 24-27; 13:3-10). Key ideas:

- Remembering redemption: annual observance kept the exodus fresh in communal memory (Deuteronomy 16:1-3).

- Passing faith to the next generation (Exodus 13:8).

- Obedience as grateful response. Celebration was not optional but commanded (John 14:15).

- For Christians, the Lord’s Supper fulfills the Passover’s intent, proclaiming Jesus’ deliverance “until He comes” (1 Corinthians 11:24-26).

summary

Exodus 13:5 gathers the whole story of redemption into a single sentence: the faithful LORD will surely bring His people into a real, promised land, overcoming every obstacle, lavishing them with abundance, and calling them to remember His saving acts through worshipful obedience. The verse invites confident trust in God’s promises, gratitude for His provision, and faithful remembrance that keeps the story of salvation alive from generation to generation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) The Canaanites, and the Hittites . . . --The full number of the Canaanitish nations was seven, five of which are here enumerated. The other two were the Perizzites and the Girgashites, which seem to have been the least important. The most important were the Canaanites, Hittites, and Amorites; and these are consequently almost always placed first. At the time of the Exodus, and for many centuries afterwards, the actually most powerful nation would seem to have been that of the Hittites. (See Joshua 1:4; 1Kings 10:29; 2Kings 7:6; and compare the Egyptian and Assyrian remains passim.)

A land flowing with milk and honey.--See Note on Exodus 3:8.

Thou shalt keep this service.--Kalisch concludes from this verse, and from Exodus 12:25, that there was no obligation upon the Israelites to keep the Passover until they obtained possession of Canaan. He holds that two Passovers only were celebrated before that event--one by Moses in the wilderness of Sinai (Numbers 9:1-5), and the other by Joshua at Gilgal, in the plain of Jericho (Joshua 5:10-11). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - The land of the Canaanites, etc. Compare Exodus 3:8, 17. The six nations of these passages are reduced here to five by the omission of the Perizzites, one of the less important tribes. Which he sware unto thy fathers to give thee. See Genesis 15:18; Genesis 24:7; and compare the comment on Exodus 6:8. That thou shalt keep this service. This injunction had been already given (Exodus 12:25) almost in the same words; but on the former occasion it was delivered to the elders only; now it is laid upon the whole people.
