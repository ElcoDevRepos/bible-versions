Then the LORD said to Moses
This phrase indicates direct communication from God to Moses, highlighting Moses' role as a mediator between God and the Israelites. Throughout the Pentateuch, Moses is depicted as a prophet and leader chosen by God to guide His people. This direct address underscores the seriousness of the situation and the divine authority behind the message. It reflects the covenant relationship where God speaks to His chosen leaders to guide His people.

How long will you refuse
This rhetorical question implies a pattern of disobedience and impatience among the Israelites. It echoes the recurring theme of Israel's stubbornness and lack of faith, which is a central issue throughout their wilderness journey. The question suggests God's frustration with the people's repeated failure to trust and obey Him, despite witnessing His miracles and provision.

to keep My commandments and instructions?
The commandments and instructions refer to the laws and guidelines given by God, which include the Ten Commandments and other specific directives, such as the observance of the Sabbath and the collection of manna. This phrase emphasizes the importance of obedience to God's law as a sign of faithfulness and trust in Him. The Israelites' failure to adhere to these instructions is a reflection of their spiritual immaturity and lack of commitment to the covenant. This theme of obedience is echoed throughout Scripture, with Jesus later affirming the importance of keeping God's commandments as a demonstration of love for Him (John 14:15).

What Does Exodus 16:28 Mean
Then the LORD said to Moses

• God Himself initiates the conversation, underscoring that He is personally invested in His people’s obedience (Exodus 3:14; Exodus 19:5).

• Moses, as the covenant mediator, is held accountable first. Leadership is never exempt from divine scrutiny (James 3:1; Numbers 20:12).

“How long”

• The question signals that this is not a first offense; patience has already been extended (Numbers 14:11; Psalm 95:9-10).

• It implies a grace-filled window that is rapidly closing. Persistent disobedience eventually provokes judgment (2 Peter 3:9; Hebrews 3:15).

“will you refuse”

• “Refuse” exposes a willful, stubborn heart, not mere misunderstanding (Exodus 5:2; Deuteronomy 9:7, 24).

• Disobedience is a choice set in contrast to the ready provision of manna and Sabbath rest that had just been given (Exodus 16:4-5, 26-27).

• God’s people can imitate either Pharaoh’s hardness or Moses’ earlier humility; refusal aligns them with the wrong side of the Red Sea story (Exodus 10:3).

“to keep My commandments”

• Commandments are not suggestions; they carry covenant authority (Deuteronomy 6:17; John 14:15).

• Obedience is the tangible evidence of faith and love (1 John 2:3-4; Exodus 15:26).

• The immediate command in context was to gather double on the sixth day and rest on the seventh, a test of trust in God’s provision (Exodus 16:4, 23).

“and instructions?”

• “Instructions” (or “laws”) broadens the scope from specific rules to God’s whole revealed will (Exodus 24:12; Psalm 119:4).

• God instructs for the people’s good, safety, and flourishing (Deuteronomy 10:13; Isaiah 48:17).

• Ignoring instruction means forfeiting blessing and inviting discipline (Proverbs 1:25-26; 2 Timothy 3:16-17).

summary

Exodus 16:28 is God’s loving but firm rebuke of a pattern of willful disobedience. Having lavishly provided manna and a Sabbath rest, the Lord confronts His people’s stubborn refusal to trust and obey. The verse reminds every generation that God watches, speaks, and expects heartfelt adherence to His commands and instructions, for our good and His glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) How long refuse ye to keep my commandments ?--The people had already broken one of the positive precepts with respect to the manna (see Exodus 16:20); now they broke another--in the spirit, at any rate--since they would have gathered had they found anything to gather. Thus they provoked God a second time; yet was He "so merciful, that He destroyed them not," but "turned His anger away, and did not stir up all His wrath" (Psalm 78:38). Apparently He made allowance for the ordinance being a new one, to which they were not yet accustomed.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - How long refuse ye to keep my commandments! Though Moses is addressed, it is the people who are blamed. Hence the plural verb, "refuse ye." Already there had been one act of disobedience in connection with the manna (see ver. 20) - now there was another - when would such sinful folly come to an end? When would the people learn that they could gain nothing by disobedience? It was "long" indeed before they were taught the lesson.
