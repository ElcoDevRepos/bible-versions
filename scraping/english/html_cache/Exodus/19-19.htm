And as the sound of the ram’s horn grew louder and louder
The ram's horn, or shofar, is a significant instrument in biblical tradition, often used to signal important events or divine interventions. In the context of Exodus 19, the increasing volume of the shofar signifies the intensifying presence of God on Mount Sinai. This auditory cue would have been a powerful reminder to the Israelites of God's holiness and the seriousness of the covenant being established. The shofar is also used in other biblical contexts, such as the fall of Jericho (Joshua 6:20) and the Year of Jubilee (Leviticus 25:9), symbolizing liberation and divine action.

Moses spoke
Moses, as the mediator between God and the Israelites, plays a crucial role in this narrative. His speaking indicates his unique position to communicate directly with God, a role that prefigures Christ as the ultimate mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5). Moses' leadership and prophetic role are central to the unfolding of God's plan for Israel, and his communication with God here underscores the direct relationship and responsibility he bears.

and God answered him in the thunder
The thunder represents God's powerful and awe-inspiring presence. In the ancient Near Eastern context, thunder was often associated with divine activity and authority. God's response in thunder emphasizes His majesty and the seriousness of the covenant being established. This theophany, or visible manifestation of God, is a reminder of His sovereignty and the reverence due to Him. The thunderous response also connects to other biblical instances where God's voice is described as thunderous, such as in Psalm 29:3-9, highlighting His power and glory.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites, chosen by God to deliver His people from Egypt and to mediate the covenant at Mount Sinai.

2. God
The Almighty, who reveals Himself to the Israelites at Mount Sinai, demonstrating His power and holiness.

The mountain where God gives the Law to Moses and the Israelites, a place of divine revelation and covenant.

5. The Thunder

Teaching Points
The Holiness of God
The increasing sound of the trumpet and the thunderous voice remind us of God's holiness and the reverence due to Him.

God's Communication
God speaks to His people, and His voice is powerful and authoritative. We should seek to listen and respond to His Word.

The Role of Mediators
Moses acts as a mediator between God and the Israelites, pointing to the ultimate mediator, Jesus Christ.

The Fear of the Lord
The awe and fear experienced at Sinai should inspire a healthy reverence for God in our own lives.

Preparation for God's Presence
Just as the Israelites prepared to meet God at Sinai, we should prepare our hearts to encounter Him in worship and prayer.

What Does Exodus 19:19 Mean
And as the sound of the ram’s horn grew louder and louder

• The shofar (ram’s horn) is God’s chosen alarm, summoning His people to attention (Exodus 19:16: “there were thunder and lightning flashes, and a thick cloud”).

• Its rising volume signals the Lord’s imminent arrival—holiness approaching sinful humanity.

• Similar trumpet moments underline divine intervention—Joshua 6:4-5 at Jericho and 1 Thessalonians 4:16, where “the Lord Himself will descend… with the trumpet call of God.”

• The growing intensity emphasizes urgency: draw near—yet on God’s terms of reverence and obedience.

Moses spoke

• Moses, already invited up the mountain (Exodus 19:3), steps forward as mediator: the people cannot endure the raw presence of God.

• Deuteronomy 5:5 recalls, “I was standing between the LORD and you at that time,” highlighting his role as go-between.

• His speaking models prayer—bold yet humble conversation—rooted in covenant relationship.

• Hebrews 3:5 affirms Moses’ faithfulness “as a servant in all God’s house,” validating the reliability of this moment.

and God answered him in the thunder

• The Almighty responds audibly; the mountain shakes with His voice (Psalm 29:3: “The voice of the LORD is over the waters; the God of glory thunders”).

• Thunder underscores power and purity: no one can tame or manipulate it.

• John 12:28-29 shows the pattern continuing—heaven speaks, the crowd says “it had thundered.”

• Hebrews 12:18-19 contrasts Sinai’s fearsome roar with the believer’s access to “the city of the living God,” yet reminds us that the same holy voice still speaks.

summary

The ever-louder shofar draws Israel to the foot of Sinai; Moses, their spokesman, addresses God; and the Lord thunders back. The verse captures a literal, historical encounter that showcases God’s holiness, Moses’ mediating role, and the awesome privilege of hearing the Creator’s voice—truths that still summon us to reverent obedience and confident dialogue with Him today.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - When the voice of the trumpet sounded long, and waxed louder and louder. This is a somewhat free translation; but it gives well the real meaning of the Hebrew. We may conclude that the trumpet's blast was not continuous. It sounded when the manifestation began (ver. 16). It sounded again, much louder and with a much more prolonged note, to herald the actual descent of God upon the mount. This time the sound was so piercing, so terrible, so intolerable, that Moses could no longer endure to keep silence, but burst out in speech. Were his words those recorded in Hebrews 12:21 - "I exceedingly fear and quake" - words not found now in the Old Testament - or were they others which have been wholly lost to us? It is impossible to say. His speech, however, had the effect of bringing the awful preparations to a close - "Moses spake, and God. answered him by a voice, and the Lord came down upon Mount Sinai."
