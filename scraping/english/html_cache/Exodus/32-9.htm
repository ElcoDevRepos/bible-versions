The LORD also said to Moses
This phrase indicates a direct communication from God to Moses, highlighting Moses' unique role as a mediator between God and the Israelites. This divine dialogue underscores the intimate relationship Moses had with God, as seen throughout the Pentateuch. It reflects the pattern of God speaking to His chosen leaders, as He did with Abraham and later with the prophets.

I have seen this people
God's observation of the Israelites suggests His omniscience and awareness of their actions. This phrase connects to earlier instances where God "saw" the afflictions of His people in Egypt (Exodus 3:7). It emphasizes God's active involvement and concern for His covenant people, despite their rebellion.

and they are indeed a stiff-necked people
The term "stiff-necked" is a metaphor derived from agriculture, describing an animal that resists direction. It characterizes the Israelites' stubbornness and resistance to divine guidance. This description is repeated in other parts of the Old Testament (e.g., Deuteronomy 9:6, 2 Chronicles 30:8) and serves as a warning against pride and disobedience. It foreshadows the Israelites' continued struggles with faithfulness and highlights the need for a new covenant, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who offers a way to transform the heart (Jeremiah 31:31-34).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)

5. The Golden Calf Incident
The event where the Israelites create and worship a golden calf, demonstrating their lack of faith and obedience to God.
Teaching Points
Understanding Stiff-necked Behavior
The term "stiff-necked" (Hebrew: qesheh-oref) refers to stubbornness and an unwillingness to submit to God's authority. Reflect on areas in your life where you may be resisting God's guidance.

The Danger of Idolatry
The Israelites' creation of the golden calf is a stark reminder of the human tendency to replace God with idols. Consider modern-day idols that may be taking precedence over your relationship with God.

The Role of Intercession
Moses' intercession for the Israelites (later in the chapter) demonstrates the power and importance of praying for others, especially those who are straying from God's path.

God's Patience and Justice
While God is just in His anger towards sin, He is also patient and merciful. Reflect on how God's patience has been evident in your life and how you can extend that patience to others.

Learning from Past Mistakes
The history of Israel serves as a lesson for believers today. Study the consequences of their actions and strive to learn from their mistakes to avoid similar pitfalls.

What Does Exodus 32:9 Mean
The LORD also said to Moses

- God initiates the conversation, underscoring Moses’ role as mediator (Exodus 3:4; Numbers 12:8).

- The same voice that spoke the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:1) now addresses the crisis of the golden calf (Exodus 32:1-6).

- The LORD’s direct speech reminds us that revelation is personal and authoritative (Psalm 103:7).

I have seen this people

- Nothing escapes the LORD’s notice (Psalm 33:13-15; Hebrews 4:13).

- He is not relying on Moses’ assessment; He has firsthand knowledge of their idolatry (Exodus 3:7).

- God’s “seeing” highlights accountability: His gaze pierces the camp just as Jesus later “knew all men” (John 2:24-25).

and they are indeed

- The adverb “indeed” stresses certainty—no exaggeration, no mistaken judgment.

- Israel’s rebellion is not a one-time lapse; it confirms a pattern (Deuteronomy 9:7; Psalm 78:17-19).

- This divine evaluation shows that God’s verdict on sin is measured and just (Romans 2:2).

a stiff-necked people

- The image evokes an animal that refuses to turn its head to accept guidance (Proverbs 29:1).

- It describes stubborn resistance to God’s will (2 Chronicles 30:8; Jeremiah 7:26).

- Stephen applies the same phrase to his generation (Acts 7:51), proving the warning is timeless.

- For believers, the verse calls for soft hearts and quick repentance, lest we repeat Israel’s obstinacy (Hebrews 3:12-13).

summary

Exodus 32:9 records God’s clear-eyed assessment of Israel in the wake of the golden calf. He personally addresses Moses, declares that He has witnessed their actions, affirms the certainty of their rebellion, and labels them “stiff-necked.” The verse exposes the danger of habitual stubbornness and urges every generation to yield promptly to the LORD’s righteous rule.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) It is a stiff-necked people.--This phrase, afterwards so common (Exodus 33:3; Exodus 33:5; Exodus 34:5; Deuteronomy 9:6; Deuteronomy 9:13; Deuteronomy 10:16; 2Chronicles 30:8; 2Chronicles 36:13; Psalm 75:5; Jeremiah 17:23; Acts 7:51), occurs here for the first time. It is generally explained as "obstinate," but rather means "perverse," the metaphor being taken from the horse that stiffens his neck against the pull of the rein, and will not be guided by the rider. The LXX. omit the verse, for no intelligible reason. . . .
