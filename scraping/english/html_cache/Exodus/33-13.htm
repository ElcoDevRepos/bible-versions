Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Now if indeed I have found favor in Your sight
Moses is speaking to God, expressing a desire for assurance of divine favor. The concept of "favor" in the Old Testament often implies grace or acceptance by God. This phrase reflects Moses' humility and recognition of his dependence on God's grace. It echoes the relationship between God and His chosen leaders, such as Noah and Abraham, who also found favor in God's eyes (Genesis 6:8, Genesis 18:3).

please let me know Your ways
Moses seeks a deeper understanding of God's character and plans. This request highlights the importance of knowing God's will and ways, which is a recurring theme in Scripture (Psalm 25:4, Isaiah 55:8-9). It underscores the idea that true leadership and guidance come from aligning oneself with God's purposes.

that I may know You
The desire to know God personally is central to the biblical narrative. This phrase indicates an intimate relationship with God, beyond mere knowledge of His laws. It reflects the covenant relationship God established with Israel, where knowing God involves obedience and love (Jeremiah 9:23-24, Hosea 6:6).

and find favor in Your sight
Moses reiterates his plea for divine favor, emphasizing the importance of God's approval in his leadership role. This reflects the biblical principle that success and blessing come from God's favor (Proverbs 3:3-4). It also points to the need for continual reliance on God's grace.

Remember that this nation is Your people.
Moses reminds God of His covenant with Israel, appealing to God's promises and faithfulness. This phrase underscores the collective identity of Israel as God's chosen people, a theme that runs throughout the Old Testament (Deuteronomy 7:6-8). It also highlights Moses' role as an intercessor, foreshadowing Christ's intercessory work for believers (Hebrews 7:25).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The central figure in this passage, Moses is interceding with God on behalf of the Israelites. He seeks a deeper understanding of God's ways to lead the people effectively.

2. God (Yahweh)
The one to whom Moses is speaking. God has a unique relationship with Moses, characterized by direct communication and favor.

3. The Israelites
Referred to as "this nation," they are the people Moses is leading out of Egypt towards the Promised Land. They are God's chosen people, despite their frequent disobedience.

4. Mount Sinai
The broader context of this passage is the Israelites' encampment at Mount Sinai, where they received the Ten Commandments and where Moses often met with God.

5. The Tent of Meeting
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, it is the place where Moses would go to speak with God, symbolizing the intimate relationship between God and Moses.
Teaching Points
Seeking God's Favor
Moses' request to find favor in God's sight emphasizes the importance of living a life pleasing to God. Believers should strive to align their actions and desires with God's will.

Desire for Intimacy with God
Moses' plea to know God's ways reflects a deep longing for intimacy with the Creator. Christians are encouraged to cultivate a personal relationship with God through prayer and study of His Word.

Intercession for Others
Moses intercedes on behalf of the Israelites, reminding God of His covenant with them. Believers are called to pray for others, especially those within the community of faith.

Learning God's Ways
Understanding God's ways is crucial for living a life that honors Him. This involves regular engagement with Scripture and openness to the Holy Spirit's guidance.

Remembering God's Promises
Moses reminds God of His promises to the Israelites. Christians should hold onto God's promises, trusting in His faithfulness even when circumstances are challenging.

What Does Exodus 33:13 Mean
Now if indeed I have found favor in Your sight

Moses begins by recognizing the undeserved grace God has already shown him.

• Favor (grace) is God’s goodwill toward the undeserving—see Genesis 6:8; Ephesians 2:8.

• Moses’ wording reflects humility; he knows any past favor is a gift, not a right (Psalm 84:11).

• By anchoring his request in grace, Moses models how believers approach God—confident because of God’s character, not personal merit (Hebrews 4:16).

please let me know Your ways

Having received grace, Moses longs for greater understanding.

• “Your ways” refers to God’s moral standards, plans, and methods (Psalm 25:4–5; Isaiah 55:8–9).

• Knowing God’s ways equips a leader to guide others accurately (Psalm 103:7, where God “made known His ways to Moses”).

• This request shows that revelation is relational: God discloses His ways to those already walking with Him (John 14:21).

that I may know You

The goal of learning God’s ways is personal knowledge of God Himself.

• Scripture equates knowing God with fellowship and obedience (Jeremiah 9:23–24; 1 John 2:3–4).

• Eternal life is defined as knowing the Father and the Son (John 17:3).

• Moses teaches that head knowledge of divine principles must lead to heart knowledge of the Divine Person (Philippians 3:8–10).

and find favor in Your sight

Moses seeks ongoing, deepening grace.

• Grace is not a one-time event; it is a continual state for God’s people (James 4:6).

• Walking in God’s ways results in increased favor (Proverbs 3:3–4; Luke 2:52).

• The repetition underscores that intimacy with God sustains leadership and life (Psalm 90:17).

Remember that this nation is Your people

Moses turns from personal to corporate concern, interceding for Israel.

• He reminds God of His covenant ownership (Exodus 32:11–14; Deuteronomy 9:29).

• The plea links Moses’ requests to God’s larger redemptive plan: personal favor must overflow to the community (1 Peter 2:9).

• By standing in the gap, Moses foreshadows Christ, the ultimate intercessor for God’s people (Hebrews 7:25).

summary

Exodus 33:13 records Moses’ heartfelt prayer for deeper grace: acknowledging past favor, seeking revelation of God’s ways, pursuing intimate knowledge of God, craving continued favor, and interceding for the covenant people. The verse teaches that grace leads to revelation, revelation leads to relationship, and relationship fuels intercession—patterns still essential for every believer today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Shew me now thy way--i.e., Thy course--Thy intention. Let me know if Thou really intendest to withdraw Thyself from us, and put a created being in Thy place or no.

Consider that this nation is thy people.--Moses glances back at God's words recorded in Exodus 32:7, and reminds God that the Israelites are not merely his (Moses') people, but also, in a higher sense, God's people. As such, God had acknowledged them (Exodus 3:7; Exodus 3:10; Exodus 5:1; Exodus 6:7; Exodus 7:4, &c.). . . .
