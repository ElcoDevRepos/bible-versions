Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Furthermore, the Israelites acted on Moses’ word
This phrase highlights the obedience of the Israelites to Moses, who was God's appointed leader. Moses' instructions were based on God's command (Exodus 3:21-22). This obedience is significant as it demonstrates faith in God's promises and leadership. It also sets a precedent for the importance of following divine guidance, a theme that recurs throughout the Bible.

and asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold,
The request for silver and gold is a fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham in Genesis 15:14, where God foretold that his descendants would leave their place of bondage with great possessions. This act can be seen as a form of divine reparation for the years of slavery. The wealth acquired would later be used in the construction of the Tabernacle (Exodus 25:1-8), symbolizing the use of earthly wealth for divine purposes. The Egyptians' willingness to give these items may reflect God's influence over their hearts (Exodus 12:36).

and for clothing.
Clothing was a valuable commodity in ancient times, often signifying wealth and status. The request for clothing indicates a preparation for the journey ahead, ensuring that the Israelites were equipped for their exodus from Egypt. This provision also symbolizes God's care for His people, ensuring they had what they needed for their journey to the Promised Land. The acquisition of clothing can be seen as a precursor to the spiritual "clothing" of righteousness that believers receive through faith in Christ (Isaiah 61:10).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Israelites
The descendants of Jacob, also known as the children of Israel, who were enslaved in Egypt and are now preparing to leave after the Passover.

2. Moses
The leader chosen by God to deliver the Israelites from Egyptian bondage. He instructed the Israelites to ask the Egyptians for valuables.

3. The Egyptians
The people of Egypt who had enslaved the Israelites. They were now experiencing the plagues and were inclined to give their valuables to the Israelites.

4. Articles of Silver and Gold, and Clothing
These items were requested by the Israelites from the Egyptians, fulfilling God's promise that they would leave Egypt with wealth.

5. The Exodus
The event of the Israelites leaving Egypt, marking the beginning of their journey to the Promised Land.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness to His Promises
God had promised Abraham that his descendants would leave with great wealth. This event demonstrates God's faithfulness and the fulfillment of His word.

The Israelites acted on Moses' word, showing the importance of obedience to God's appointed leaders and His instructions.

God’s Provision
Even in times of transition and uncertainty, God provides for His people, equipping them with what they need for their journey.

Favor in Adversity
God can grant favor even from unlikely sources, such as the Egyptians who had oppressed the Israelites.

What Does Exodus 12:35 Mean
Furthermore

The verse opens with “Furthermore,” signaling a direct link to the instructions given moments earlier. God had announced the final plague and the imminent departure (Exodus 11:4–7; 12:1–13). By adding this transitional word, Scripture reminds us that every detail fits into God’s unfolding plan. What follows is not a random act; it is the next step in the deliverance He promised back in Genesis 15:14 and reiterated in Exodus 3:21–22.

The Israelites acted

Israel’s response highlights simple, prompt obedience:

• Earlier they had “went and did so” concerning the Passover lamb (Exodus 12:28).

• Now they move again, showing faith expressed through action, a pattern echoed later in Joshua 1:16–17 and James 2:22.

Their readiness contrasts sharply with Pharaoh’s repeated hesitations, underlining that obedience leads to freedom while stubbornness brings judgment (Proverbs 1:24–27).

On Moses’ word

They followed “Moses’ word,” recognizing his God-given authority. This mirrors earlier scenes where Moses speaks and plagues fall (Exodus 7–11). The people’s submission foreshadows the New Testament call to heed God’s appointed messengers (Hebrews 13:7, 17). It also fulfills the Lord’s assurance to Moses in Exodus 4:12: “I will help you speak and will teach you what to say”.

And asked the Egyptians

Requesting valuables from their oppressors seems bold, yet God had prepared Egyptian hearts. Exodus 11:2 already instructed this request, and 12:36 records the Lord giving Israel “favor.” Similar divine favor appears when Joseph gains respect in Egypt (Genesis 39:21) and when Nehemiah receives provisions from King Artaxerxes (Nehemiah 2:7-9). The lesson: God can turn the hearts of even unbelieving authorities to bless His people (Proverbs 21:1).

For articles of silver and gold

These riches were not mere souvenirs. God later directs that same silver and gold for the Tabernacle (Exodus 25:1-8; 35:5, 22). What the enemy had hoarded would fund Israel’s worship. The principle resurfaces in Haggai 2:8—“The silver is Mine and the gold is Mine”—and in 2 Corinthians 9:8 where God supplies every need for every good work.

And for clothing

Garments were practical for a desert journey and symbolic of new identity. Deuteronomy 8:4 and Nehemiah 9:21 note that Israel’s clothes did not wear out for forty years, underscoring supernatural provision. Later, clothing imagery describes righteousness bestowed by God (Isaiah 61:10; Revelation 19:8). Even here, the Lord begins dressing His redeemed people for a future as His holy nation.

summary

Exodus 12:35 captures one decisive moment: obedient Israelites, trusting Moses, receive treasures and clothing from a once-hostile nation. The verse showcases God’s faithfulness to earlier promises, His sovereignty over human hearts, and His purpose in providing resources that will fuel future worship. Obedience unlocks provision, and every gift given prepares God’s people for the journey and service ahead.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - The children of Israel did according to the word of Moses. See above, Exodus 11:2. They borrowed. On this mistranslation, see the comment upon Exodus 3:22. It is plain that the gold and silver articles and the raiment, were free-will gifts, which the Egyptians never expected to see again, and which the Hebrews asked and took, but in no sense "borrowed." Hengstenberg and Kurtz have shown clearly that the primary meaning of the words translated "borrowed" and "lent," is "asked" and "granted," and that the sense of "borrowing" and "lending" is only to be assigned them when it is required by the context.
