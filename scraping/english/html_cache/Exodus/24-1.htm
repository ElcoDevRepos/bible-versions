Then the LORD said to Moses
This phrase indicates direct communication from God to Moses, highlighting Moses' unique role as a mediator between God and the Israelites. This divine instruction underscores the covenant relationship established at Sinai. Moses is often seen as a type of Christ, serving as a mediator and leader who guides God's people.

Come up to the LORD
The invitation to ascend signifies a call to a higher spiritual experience and encounter with God. This ascent is both physical and spiritual, symbolizing drawing nearer to God. The mountain, often associated with divine encounters, serves as a sacred space where God reveals His will.

you and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu
Aaron, Moses' brother, is the high priest, and Nadab and Abihu are his sons, representing the priestly line. Their inclusion signifies the importance of priestly mediation in worship and covenantal ceremonies. Nadab and Abihu's later disobedience (Leviticus 10) serves as a cautionary tale about the holiness required in approaching God.

and seventy of Israel’s elders
The seventy elders represent the leadership and governance structure of Israel, symbolizing the collective responsibility and participation of the community in the covenant. This number may also reflect completeness and order, as seen in other biblical contexts (e.g., the Sanhedrin).

and you are to worship at a distance
Worshiping at a distance emphasizes the holiness of God and the separation between the divine and human realms. It reflects the reverence and awe required in approaching God, acknowledging His transcendence. This distance is later bridged through Christ, who provides direct access to God for believers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who initiates the call to worship and establishes His covenant with His people.

2. Moses
The leader and prophet of Israel, chosen by God to mediate the covenant and lead the people.

3. Aaron
Moses' brother and the first high priest of Israel, representing the priestly line.

4. Nadab and Abihu
Sons of Aaron, who later play significant roles in the priesthood but also face consequences for disobedience.

5. Seventy Elders of Israel
Representatives of the tribes of Israel, signifying the communal aspect of the covenant.

6. Worship at a Distance
The act of worshiping God with reverence and acknowledging His holiness, which requires separation due to human sinfulness.
Teaching Points
The Call to Worship
God initiates the call to worship, inviting His people into His presence. This reminds us that worship is a response to God's invitation and grace.

Reverence and Holiness
Worshiping "at a distance" signifies the need for reverence and recognition of God's holiness. We must approach God with humility and respect.

Leadership and Representation
Moses, Aaron, and the elders represent the people before God. This highlights the importance of godly leadership and the role of intercession in the community of faith.

Covenant Relationship
The gathering at Sinai is a pivotal moment in establishing the covenant. It reminds us of the importance of covenant faithfulness and obedience to God's commands.

Community Worship
The inclusion of the elders signifies the communal nature of worship. Our worship should involve and reflect the broader community of believers.

What Does Exodus 24:1 Mean
Then the LORD said to Moses

• Scripture underscores that the initiative always begins with God; Moses does not volunteer—he is summoned (Exodus 3:4; Acts 7:33).

• The same divine voice that spoke creation now speaks covenant, confirming continuity from Genesis to Sinai (Genesis 1:3; Exodus 20:1).

• God’s direct address reaffirms Moses’ unique mediating role, later echoed when prophets hear, “Thus says the LORD” (Jeremiah 1:4; Amos 3:7).

Come up to the LORD

• “Come up” signals both physical ascent of Sinai and spiritual drawing near, foreshadowing New Testament access through Christ (Exodus 19:20; Hebrews 4:16).

• The mountain scene pictures heaven’s throne room on earth, anticipating later visions where believers are invited upward (Isaiah 6:1; Revelation 4:1).

• Movement toward God always requires His prior invitation; apart from it, approach would mean judgment (Exodus 19:12-13).

You and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu, and seventy of Israel’s elders

• God names the participants, showing worship is not a casual drop-in but a commanded assembly (Leviticus 10:3; Hebrews 12:23).

• Aaron will serve as high priest, his sons as priests, and the seventy as representatives of the nation—mirroring later structures of priesthood and council (Numbers 11:16-17; Luke 10:1).

• Including leaders models corporate responsibility: revelation received at the top is meant to cascade to the people (Deuteronomy 31:9-13).

And you are to worship at a distance

• Holiness demands separation; even invited guests cannot rush in unprepared (Exodus 19:21-24; 1 Samuel 6:19-20).

• The distance protects the worshipers while highlighting God’s transcendence—a tension resolved in Christ, who brings us “near” (Ephesians 2:13; Hebrews 10:19-22).

• Worship involves reverent awe, not familiarity; the elders will soon behold God yet still remain mindful of His otherness (Exodus 24:9-11).

summary

Exodus 24:1 records God’s sovereign call for Moses and chosen representatives to ascend Sinai, underscoring divine initiative, ordered access, representative leadership, and reverent distance. Together these elements reveal God’s holiness and mercy, setting the pattern for covenant worship that culminates in Christ, who fulfills the mediator role and invites all believers to draw near with confidence.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XXIV.

THE RATIFICATION OF THE COVENANT.

(1) And he said.--We should have expected "And God said," or "And Jehovah said." The omission of the nominative is probably to be accounted for by the insertion into Exodus at this point of "the Book of the Covenant," which was originally a distinct document. Exodus 24:1 of Exodus 24 probably followed originally on Exodus 20:21 of Exodus 20. The sequence of the words was then as follows: "And Moses drew near unto the thick darkness where God was. And he said unto Moses," &c.

Come up.--The ascent of Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and the seventy elders seems to have been commanded in order to give greater solemnity to the ratification of the covenant between God and Israel, which is the main subject of this section. Moses received instructions on the subject before descending, and no doubt was divinely guided in the steps which he took previously to ascending with them.

Nadab, and Abihu.--Aaron's two elder sons. (See Exodus 6:23.)

Seventy of the elders.--These are not the "judges" of Exodus 18:21-26, who were not yet appointed (see Note on Exodus 18:24-25), but rather the heads of tribes and families who had exercised authority over the Israelites in Egypt, and through whom Moses had always communicated with the people. (See Exodus 3:16; Exodus 4:29; Exodus 12:21; Exodus 17:5-6.) . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-8. - THE RATIFICATION OF THE COVENANT. The giving of the Book of the Covenant being now completed, Moses, having received directions with respect to another ascent into the mount (vers. 1, 2), descended to the people, and in the first instance declared to them the main heads of the Covenant, which they received with favour, and expressed their willingness to obey (ver. 3). Not, however, regarding this as a sufficiently formal ratification, the Prophet proceeded to write out in a "Book" the whole of the commands which he had received, He then built an altar, erected twelve pillars, offered sacrifice, and having collected half the blood of the victims in basins, summoned the people to an assembly. At this, he read over solemnly all the words of the Book to them, and received their solemn adherence to it (ver. 7); whereupon, to complete the ceremony, and mark their entrance into covenant, he sprinkled the blood from the basins on the twelve tribes, represented by their leaders, and declared the acceptance complete (ver. 8). The ceremony was probably modelled on some customary proceedings, whereby important contracts between man and man were ratified among the Hebrews and Syrians. Verses 1, 2. - It has been supposed that these verses are out of place, and suggested to remove them to the end of verse 8. But no change is necessary. It is quite natural that God should have given the directions before Moses descended from the mount, and that he should have deferred executing them until the people had accepted the covenant. Nadab and Abihu were the two eldest of Aaron's sons, and so his natural successors in the priesthood, had they not sinned by offering "strange fire" (Leviticus 10:1, 2). They had been mentioned previously, in Exodus 6:23. Seventy of the elders. On the elders of Israel, see Exodus 3:16, and Exodus 18:21. The "seventy" eiders may, together with Nadab and Abihu, have represented the twelve tribes, six from each. Worship ye afar off. Though all were to ascend the mount to a certain height, only Moses was to go to the top. The others, being less holy than Moses, had to worship at a distance.
