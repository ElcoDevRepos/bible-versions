Tell the whole congregation of Israel
This directive is given to Moses and Aaron to communicate to the entire assembly of the Israelites. The term "congregation" indicates a collective identity, emphasizing unity and shared purpose. This is the first time the term is used in the Bible, marking the beginning of Israel's national identity. The instruction is communal, highlighting the importance of collective obedience and participation in God's plan.

that on the tenth day of this month
The specific timing is significant, as it marks the beginning of the Jewish religious calendar with the month of Nisan (or Abib). The tenth day is chosen to allow time for the lamb to be selected and observed, ensuring it is without blemish. This timing also foreshadows the triumphal entry of Jesus into Jerusalem, which occurred on the tenth day of Nisan, aligning with His role as the Lamb of God.

each man must select a lamb for his family
The selection of a lamb is a personal responsibility, emphasizing the role of the head of the household in spiritual leadership. The lamb is to be chosen with care, symbolizing purity and innocence. This act prefigures Christ, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world. The personal selection underscores the necessity of individual faith and commitment in the redemption process.

one per household
The provision of one lamb per household signifies the sufficiency of the sacrifice for each family unit. It reflects the communal aspect of salvation, where each household is covered by the blood of the lamb. This mirrors the New Testament teaching that Christ's sacrifice is sufficient for all who believe. The household focus also points to the importance of family in God's covenantal promises and the transmission of faith across generations.

Teaching Points
Obedience to God's Instructions
The Israelites were required to follow God's specific instructions for the Passover. This teaches us the importance of obedience in our walk with God.

Family and Community
The instruction to select a lamb per household emphasizes the role of family and community in spiritual practices. It encourages us to involve our families in our faith journey.

Preparation and Anticipation
The selection of the lamb on the tenth day signifies preparation. In our lives, we should prepare our hearts and minds for God's work and anticipate His deliverance.

Symbolism of the Lamb
The lamb represents innocence and sacrifice. This points us to Jesus, the ultimate Lamb, whose sacrifice brings redemption.

God's Deliverance
The Passover marks the beginning of Israel's deliverance from Egypt. It reminds us of God's power to deliver us from bondage, whether physical or spiritual.

What Does Exodus 12:3 Mean
Tell the whole congregation of Israel

- God addresses every Israelite, not merely the elders. His redemption plan is communal (cf. “Moses and Aaron … spoke to all the congregation of Israel,” Exodus 12:21).

- The whole nation hearing together reminds us that salvation in Christ is proclaimed openly to all people (cf. Acts 2:14 ff.; Romans 10:17).

- A unified response is required; ignoring the instruction would bring judgment, just as refusing Christ brings condemnation (John 3:18).

on the tenth day of this month

- The month is Abib/Nisan, the first month of Israel’s new calendar (Exodus 12:2). God re-sets time around redemption.

- The specific date shows divine precision. Joshua 4:19 notes Israel crossing the Jordan on the tenth of the first month—another salvation milestone.

- Jesus entered Jerusalem on what corresponds to the tenth of Nisan (John 12:1, 12-13), publicly presented before His crucifixion. God’s timing is exact.

each man must select a lamb

- Salvation is personal; “each man” acts in obedience. Faith cannot be delegated (Ezekiel 18:20; Romans 14:12).

- The lamb points directly to Christ: “Behold, the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world” (John 1:29).

- The lamb had to be “without blemish” (Exodus 12:5), foreshadowing Christ’s sinlessness (1 Peter 1:19).

for his family, one per household

- God’s plan embraces family units. The Passover meal gathered generations around a single sacrifice (Exodus 12:26-27).

- Household faith echoes through Scripture: Noah’s ark (Genesis 7:1), Rahab’s house (Joshua 2:13), the Philippian jailer (Acts 16:31).

- Where a household was too small for a whole lamb, neighbors shared (Exodus 12:4), prefiguring the church’s fellowship around Christ (1 Corinthians 10:17).

summary

Exodus 12:3 shows God calling every Israelite, on a precise date, to select a flawless lamb that would shelter each family from judgment. The verse anticipates Jesus Christ, God’s perfect Lamb, chosen before the foundation of the world and presented publicly at the exact hour God ordained. Salvation is offered openly, received personally, and meant to bless entire households, drawing a redeemed people together under one Sacrifice.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) In the tenth day.--It is evident that this direction must have been given before the tenth day had arrived, probably some days before. The object of the direction was to allow ample time for the careful inspection of the animal, so that its entire freedom from all blemish might be ascertained. The animal was not to be killed till four days later (Exodus 12:6).

A lamb.--The word used (seh) is a vague one, applied equally to sheep and goats, of any age and of either sex. Sex and age were fixed subsequently (Exodus 12:5), but the other ambiguity remained; and it is curious that practically only lambs seem to have been ever offered. The requirement indicates a social condition in which there was no extreme poverty. All Israelites are supposed either to possess a lamb or to be able to purchase one.

According to the house of their fathers.--Rather, for the house of their fathers: i.e., for their family.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Speak ye unto all the congregation. Under the existing circumstances Moses could only venture to summon the elders of Israel to a meeting. He necessarily left it to them to signify his wishes to the people. (See ver. 21.) A lamb. The Hebrew word is one of much wider meaning than our "lamb." It is applicable to both sheep and goats, and to either animal without limit of age, In the present case the age was fixed at a year by subsequent enactment (ver. 5); but the offerer was left free with respect to the species. It is curious that, such being the case, the lamb alone should, so far as appears, ever have been offered. According to the house of their fathers. Literally, "for a father's house," i.e. for a family.
