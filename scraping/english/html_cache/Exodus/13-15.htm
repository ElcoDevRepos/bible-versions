Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And when Pharaoh stubbornly refused to let us go
This phrase highlights Pharaoh's hardened heart, a recurring theme in the Exodus narrative. Pharaoh's refusal to release the Israelites despite numerous plagues demonstrates his resistance to God's will. This stubbornness is a fulfillment of God's earlier declaration to Moses that He would harden Pharaoh's heart (Exodus 4:21). It serves as a backdrop for God's power and sovereignty over earthly rulers, emphasizing that human obstinacy cannot thwart divine plans.

the LORD killed every firstborn in the land of Egypt, both of man and beast
The death of the firstborn was the tenth and final plague, a decisive act of judgment against Egypt's gods and Pharaoh, who was considered a deity. This event is pivotal in the Exodus story, leading to the liberation of the Israelites. It underscores the seriousness of sin and the cost of disobedience to God. The inclusion of both man and beast signifies a comprehensive judgment affecting all levels of Egyptian society and economy, highlighting the totality of God's power.

This is why I sacrifice to the LORD the firstborn male of every womb
The practice of sacrificing the firstborn male is rooted in the Passover event, serving as a perpetual reminder of God's deliverance. It reflects the principle of giving God the first and best, acknowledging His provision and sovereignty. This act of sacrifice is a form of worship and gratitude, reinforcing the covenant relationship between God and His people. It also prefigures the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, the firstborn over all creation (Colossians 1:15), who redeems humanity.

but I redeem all the firstborn of my sons.
Redemption of the firstborn sons involves a substitutionary act, where a sacrifice is made in place of the child. This practice points to the concept of redemption, a key theme in Scripture, where a price is paid for liberation. It foreshadows the redemptive work of Christ, who is the ultimate Redeemer. The act of redeeming the firstborn sons serves as a continual reminder of God's mercy and the cost of salvation, emphasizing the value God places on human life and His desire for relationship with His people.

What Does Exodus 13:15 Mean
When Pharaoh stubbornly refused to let us go

“And when Pharaoh stubbornly refused to let us go…”

• The statement recalls Exodus 5–10, where Pharaoh’s hardened heart rejected every divine warning (Exodus 7:13; 9:12).

• God’s patience gave Egypt repeated chances, yet Pharaoh’s continued defiance magnified the justice of the final judgment (Romans 2:4–5).

• Israel is reminded that their liberation was entirely God’s work, not political skill or chance (Deuteronomy 7:7–8).

The LORD killed every firstborn in the land of Egypt

“…the LORD killed every firstborn in the land of Egypt…”

• The tenth plague (Exodus 12:29) was a direct, sovereign act; the text stresses that “the LORD” Himself acted, underlining divine authority (Psalm 135:8–9).

• This decisive blow shattered Egypt’s religious system, showing Yahweh’s supremacy over all pretended deities (Numbers 33:4).

• It also created a stark contrast: mercy toward homes marked by the Passover blood (Exodus 12:13; Hebrews 11:28).

Both of man and beast

“…both of man and beast.”

• The scope of judgment was total, extending beyond human heirs to livestock, the economic backbone of Egypt (Exodus 12:29–30).

• This mirrored earlier plagues striking cattle (Exodus 9:3–6) and underscored the cost of rebellion: nothing was exempt (Proverbs 11:21).

This is why I sacrifice to the LORD the firstborn male of every womb

“This is why I sacrifice to the LORD the firstborn male of every womb…”

• The memory of redemption shaped Israel’s worship. Each firstborn animal was offered to acknowledge that life belongs to God (Exodus 13:11–12).

• Sacrifice served as a living memorial, teaching each generation what God had done (Joshua 4:6–7).

• It affirmed God’s ongoing ownership over His redeemed people (1 Corinthians 6:20).

But I redeem all the firstborn of my sons’

“…but I redeem all the firstborn of my sons.’”

• Human life, precious to God, was not sacrificed but “redeemed” through a substitute—later codified as five shekels per child (Numbers 18:15–16).

• Redemption highlighted grace: God accepts a ransom pointing forward to the ultimate Substitute, Christ the “firstborn over all creation” who redeems us by His blood (Colossians 1:15; 1 Peter 1:18–19).

• Families enacted this truth personally, passing faith from parent to child (Deuteronomy 6:20–25).

summary

Exodus 13:15 anchors Israel’s rituals in a real, historical rescue. Pharaoh’s stubbornness brought judgment; God’s decisive act freed His people. Therefore every firstborn animal is sacrificed and every firstborn son redeemed—continual reminders that the Lord owns, protects, and saves. The pattern points ahead to Christ, the greater Firstborn, whose death and resurrection secure eternal redemption for all who trust Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - When Pharaoh would hardly let us go. Bather, "when Pharaoh hardened himself against letting us go." At his last interview with Moses, Pharaoh had absolutely refused to let them go with their cattle (Exodus 10:24-27), and Moses had absolutely refused to go without them. I sacrifice all that openeth the womb, being males. And being clean animals. The common sense of the reader or hearer, is expected to supply the restriction. Of my children. Rather, as in verse 13, "of my sons."
