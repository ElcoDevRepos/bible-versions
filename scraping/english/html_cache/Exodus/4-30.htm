Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and Aaron relayed everything the LORD had said to Moses.
Aaron, Moses' brother, acts as a spokesperson due to Moses' reluctance and claimed speech impediment (Exodus 4:10-16). This partnership highlights the importance of community and support in fulfilling God's mission. Aaron's role as a communicator is significant, as he becomes the first high priest, foreshadowing Christ as our ultimate High Priest (Hebrews 4:14-16). The phrase underscores the importance of obedience and faithfulness in delivering God's message accurately. This moment is pivotal as it marks the beginning of the liberation of the Israelites from Egyptian bondage, fulfilling God's promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:13-14).

And Moses performed the signs before the people,
Moses performs miraculous signs to authenticate his divine commission, including turning a staff into a serpent, making his hand leprous, and turning water from the Nile into blood (Exodus 4:2-9). These signs serve as a testament to God's power and presence, reassuring the Israelites of His commitment to their deliverance. The signs prefigure the miracles of Jesus, who also performed signs to validate His divine authority (John 20:30-31). This act of performing signs is crucial in building the faith of the Israelites, preparing them to trust in God's plan for their exodus from Egypt.

What Does Exodus 4:30 Mean
Aaron relayed everything the LORD had said to Moses

• Aaron steps into the God-appointed role of spokesman (Exodus 4:14–16), confirming the Lord’s earlier instruction that the brothers would labor together.

• By faithfully repeating “everything,” Aaron models the prophet’s duty to transmit God’s word without addition or subtraction (Deuteronomy 4:2; Jeremiah 1:7).

• His words carry divine authority, preparing the people’s hearts to recognize that the coming deliverance is the Lord’s doing, not human ingenuity (Exodus 3:18; 7:2).

• Aaron’s obedience underscores the pattern that faith comes through hearing the word of God (Romans 10:17), and Israel’s future leaders will be measured by the same standard (Joshua 1:7-8).

• The immediate acceptance of Aaron’s message (Exodus 4:31) anticipates the later covenant scene at Sinai, where the nation pledges, “All that the LORD has spoken we will do” (Exodus 19:8).

And Moses performed the signs before the people

• The staff-to-serpent, leprous hand, and water-to-blood signs (Exodus 4:2-9) visibly confirm the spoken word, providing the “two or three witnesses” principle later affirmed in Deuteronomy 19:15 and 2 Corinthians 13:1.

• Miracles validate the messenger but never replace the message; they serve as God’s signature, not Moses’ self-promotion (John 2:11; Acts 14:3).

• These signs foreshadow the greater demonstrations of power in the ten plagues (Exodus 7–12) and ultimately point to Christ’s mighty works that authenticate His gospel (Hebrews 2:3-4).

• The sequence—word first, then confirming signs—mirrors Jesus sending out the apostles who “went forth and preached everywhere, while the Lord worked with them and confirmed the word by the signs that accompanied it” (Mark 16:20).

• Israel’s positive response (“the people believed,” Exodus 4:31) shows that God’s covenant community is built on both revelation and redemption: hearing truth and witnessing deliverance (Psalm 98:1-3).

summary

Exodus 4:30 highlights a two-fold dynamic: Aaron faithfully delivers God’s exact words, and Moses substantiates those words with God-given signs. Together they demonstrate that the Lord both speaks and acts, inviting His people to trust Him fully.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 30. - Aaron spake. Aaron at once entered on his office of "spokesman" (ver. 16), declaring to the elders all God's dealings with his brother. Aaron also, and not Moses, us we should have expected (ver. 17), did the signs, God, by allowing him to do them, sanctioning this delegation of power. On later occasions, we find Aaron more than once required by God to work the miracles. (See below, Exodus 7:19; Exodus 8:5, 16.) In the sight of the people. It is not probable that the people were present at the first meeting of the elders; but the sacred historian, anxious to compress his narrative, and bent simply on conveying to us the fact of Aaron's success with both elders and people, omits stages in the history which he supposes that any reader can supply, e.g. the doing of the signs in the sight of the elders, their belief in them, and their subsequent assembling of the people.
