Therefore you shall keep this statute
This phrase emphasizes the importance of obedience to God's commands. The statute refers to the observance of the Feast of Unleavened Bread, which commemorates the Israelites' exodus from Egypt. This command is a reminder of God's deliverance and faithfulness. The use of "shall" indicates a non-negotiable directive, underscoring the seriousness of maintaining this tradition as a perpetual reminder of God's covenant with His people.

at the appointed time
The phrase "appointed time" signifies a specific period set by God for the observance of this statute. It highlights the importance of divine timing and order in worship. This concept of appointed times is seen throughout the Old Testament, such as in Leviticus 23, where God establishes various feasts and holy days. It reflects the structured nature of Israelite worship and the rhythm of their religious calendar, which was designed to keep the community focused on God's acts of salvation and provision.

year after year
This phrase indicates the perpetual nature of the observance. It was not a one-time event but a continual practice meant to be passed down through generations. This annual observance served as a teaching tool for future generations, ensuring that the story of God's deliverance was not forgotten. It also points to the cyclical nature of God's covenant relationship with His people, which is renewed and remembered through regular observance. This concept of remembrance is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus institutes the Lord's Supper as a continual remembrance of His sacrifice (Luke 22:19).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites, chosen by God to lead His people out of Egypt. He is the one conveying God's commandments to the Israelites.

2. Israelites
The descendants of Jacob, who are being led out of slavery in Egypt and are receiving God's laws and ordinances.

3. Egypt
The land from which the Israelites are being delivered. It represents bondage and oppression in the biblical account.

4. Passover
The event commemorated by the ordinance mentioned in this verse, marking the deliverance of the Israelites from Egypt.

5. Promised Land
The destination of the Israelites, symbolizing freedom and God's faithfulness to His promises.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Remembrance
The ordinance serves as a reminder of God's deliverance and faithfulness. Regular observance helps keep the memory of God's mighty acts alive in the community.

Obedience to God's Commands
The Israelites are instructed to keep the ordinance "year after year," highlighting the importance of consistent obedience to God's laws.

Spiritual Significance of Traditions
Traditions like the Passover are not just rituals but carry deep spiritual significance, pointing to God's salvation and covenant with His people.

Continuity of Faith
Observing the ordinance annually ensures that each generation understands and values their spiritual heritage and God's role in their history.

Christ as Our Passover
For Christians, the Passover foreshadows Christ's sacrifice. Just as the Israelites were delivered from physical bondage, believers are delivered from sin through Jesus.

What Does Exodus 13:10 Mean
Therefore

The word “therefore” ties Exodus 13:10 back to verses 3–9, where the LORD explains the Feast of Unleavened Bread and its purpose.

• God has just redeemed Israel from Egypt; “therefore” calls for a response (Exodus 12:26–27).

• Obedience flows from salvation, never the other way around (Exodus 20:2-3; Titus 2:14).

• Similar language appears in Exodus 12:24: “And you are to keep this command as a permanent statute for you and your descendants.”

you shall keep

“Shall” makes the command non-negotiable; “keep” carries the sense of guarding something precious.

• The people are not merely to remember but to practice (Deuteronomy 6:17; John 14:15).

• Keeping God’s word preserves identity and witness (Psalm 119:11; 1 Peter 2:9).

this statute

The “statute” points specifically to the Passover and the seven-day Feast of Unleavened Bread (Exodus 13:3-7).

• It commemorates deliverance by the blood of the lamb (Exodus 12:13; 1 Corinthians 5:7).

• It teaches each generation the cost of redemption (Exodus 13:8-9).

• Jesus fulfilled the statute while instituting the Lord’s Supper (Luke 22:15-20).

at the appointed time

God sets the calendar; His appointments are not arbitrary.

• Leviticus 23:4 calls the feasts “the LORD’s appointed times.”

• Regular rhythms train hearts to anticipate greater fulfillment (Galatians 4:4; Colossians 2:16-17).

• Meeting God on His timetable nurtures trust and dependence (Ecclesiastes 3:1).

year after year

The practice is perpetual, shaping collective memory.

• Exodus 12:14 names it “a permanent ordinance.”

• Elkanah’s family went “year after year” to Shiloh (1 Samuel 1:3), and Joseph took Jesus to Jerusalem annually (Luke 2:41).

• Ongoing observance guards against drifting and rekindles gratitude (Hebrews 2:1).

summary

Exodus 13:10 commands a redeemed people to guard the Passover-Unleavened Bread statute faithfully, on God’s schedule, every year. The verse calls believers to consistent, wholehearted obedience that keeps the story of salvation alive from one generation to the next, all pointing to and fulfilled in Christ, our Passover Lamb.
