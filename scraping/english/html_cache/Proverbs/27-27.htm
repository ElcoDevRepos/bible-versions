You will have plenty of goats’ milk to feed you—
This phrase emphasizes the provision and abundance that comes from diligent stewardship and care of one's resources. In ancient Israel, goats were a common livestock, valued for their milk, which was a staple in the diet. The mention of "plenty" suggests a blessing of abundance, often seen as a reward for wise management and hard work, as reflected in Proverbs 27:23-26, which speaks of knowing the condition of your flocks. This abundance can be seen as a metaphor for spiritual blessings that come from living a life aligned with God's wisdom.

food for your household—
The household in ancient times extended beyond the immediate family to include servants and sometimes even extended family members. Providing for one's household was a significant responsibility, and having enough food was a sign of God's favor and the head of the household's diligence. This phrase underscores the biblical principle of stewardship and responsibility, as seen in 1 Timothy 5:8, which emphasizes the importance of providing for one's family. It also reflects the cultural context where the family unit was central to societal structure.

and nourishment for your maidservants.
Maidservants were part of the extended household and their well-being was a reflection of the master's integrity and care. In biblical times, servants were often considered part of the family unit, and their treatment was a reflection of one's faithfulness to God's commands, as seen in Deuteronomy 24:14-15, which instructs fair treatment of workers. This phrase highlights the importance of justice and kindness in leadership, echoing the biblical theme of caring for those who serve us, as Jesus exemplified in His teachings on servant leadership (Mark 10:42-45).

What Does Proverbs 27:27 Mean
You will have plenty of goats’ milk

- The phrase looks back to verses 23–26, which urge careful oversight of flocks; diligent stewardship brings tangible provision (Proverbs 12:11; 2 Corinthians 9:6–8).

- Goats’ milk in agrarian Israel was a dependable, renewable resource—symbolizing steady, daily supply, not luxury (Deuteronomy 28:4; Psalm 37:25).

- The Lord ties plenty to faith-filled labor: when we responsibly tend what He places under our care, He satisfies needs (Philippians 4:19).

to feed you—food for your household

- “You” points first to the owner-shepherd; God respects personal needs even while teaching self-denial (1 Timothy 5:8).

- “Household” widens the circle: wise management blesses spouse, children, and any depending relatives (Joshua 24:15; Proverbs 31:27).

- Daily staples rather than extravagant feasts are in view, echoing the prayer for “our daily bread” (Matthew 6:11; Proverbs 30:8–9).

and nourishment for your maidservants

- Servants were integral to ancient households; God’s provision extends to every level of the community we influence (Colossians 4:1).

- The verse models covenant compassion: employees share in the fruit of their employer’s stewardship (Leviticus 19:13; James 5:4).

- Generosity toward workers testifies to faith in a God who sees and rewards (Proverbs 11:25; Luke 6:38).

summary

Proverbs 27:27 teaches that attentive, faithful management of God-given resources yields reliable provision, sufficient for ourselves, our families, and those who serve alongside us. The verse reassures that the Lord blesses diligence with practical supply, calling us to steward His gifts generously and responsibly.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - Goats' milk. Dr. Geikie ('Holy Land and Bible,' 1:311) notes that in most parts of Palestine goats' milk in every form - sour, sweet, thick, thin, warm, or cold - makes, with eggs and bread, the main food of the people. And maintenance for thy maidens; who milk the goats, etc., and tend the cattle, and do the household work. There is no mention of the use of animal flesh as food. It was only on great occasions, as high festivals, or the presence of an honoured guest, that kids, lambs, and calves were killed and eaten. This picture of rural peace and plenty points to a time of security and prosperity, free alike from internal commotion and external danger. The famous passage in Cicero, 'De Senect.,' 15, on the pleasures and advantages of the agricultural life. will occur to all classical readers. So also Horace ('Epod.,' 2), "Beatus ille qui procul negotiis," etc. The LXX. makes short work of this verse, "My son, thou hast from me sayings mighty for thy life and for the life of thy servants."
