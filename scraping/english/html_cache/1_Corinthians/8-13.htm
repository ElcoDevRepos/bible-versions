Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Therefore, if what I eat causes my brother to stumble
This phrase highlights the principle of Christian liberty balanced with love and consideration for others. In the context of 1 Corinthians 8, Paul addresses the issue of eating food offered to idols. In Corinth, a city known for its diverse religious practices, meat sold in markets was often part of pagan sacrifices. While some Christians understood that idols were nothing and felt free to eat such meat, others, particularly those with a background in idol worship, might see this as a return to pagan practices. Paul emphasizes that knowledge must be tempered with love, as causing a fellow believer to stumble spiritually is a serious matter. This principle is echoed in Romans 14:13-21, where Paul advises believers to avoid actions that might cause others to fall into sin.

I will never eat meat again
Paul's declaration here is a personal commitment to abstain from meat if it risks harming another believer's faith. This reflects the self-sacrificial love that is central to Christian ethics, mirroring Christ's own sacrifice for humanity. The willingness to give up personal rights for the sake of others is a recurring theme in Paul's letters, as seen in Philippians 2:3-8, where believers are encouraged to have the same mindset as Christ, who humbled Himself for the sake of others. This phrase also underscores the importance of community and the responsibility believers have towards one another.

so that I will not cause him to stumble
The repetition of the concern for causing a brother to stumble underscores the gravity of the issue. In the cultural context of Corinth, where social and religious practices were deeply intertwined, the potential for misunderstanding and spiritual harm was significant. Paul’s pastoral concern is to protect the conscience of weaker believers, ensuring that their faith is not jeopardized by the actions of others. This reflects the broader biblical principle of being a stumbling block, as warned against in Matthew 18:6, where Jesus speaks of the severe consequences for those who cause others to sin. The emphasis is on building up the body of Christ, fostering unity and spiritual growth, as seen in Ephesians 4:1-3.

Teaching Points
Christian Liberty and Responsibility
While Christians have freedom in Christ, this liberty should be exercised with consideration for the spiritual well-being of others.

Love Over Knowledge
Knowledge can lead to pride, but love builds up. Our actions should be motivated by love for our fellow believers.

Sensitivity to Conscience
We must be sensitive to the consciences of others, especially those who may be weaker in faith, and avoid actions that could lead them into sin.

Self-Sacrifice for Unity
True Christian maturity involves sacrificing personal rights for the sake of unity and the spiritual health of the community.

Imitating Christ's Example
Jesus exemplified self-sacrifice and love for others, and we are called to follow His example in our interactions with fellow believers.

What Does 1 Corinthians 8:13 Mean
Therefore

The word signals Paul’s conclusion to the whole discussion on food offered to idols.

• “Food does not bring us near to God; we are no worse if we do not eat and no better if we do” (1 Corinthians 8:8).

• Knowledge is real—idols are nothing—but love must govern the use of that knowledge (1 Corinthians 8:1; 10:23).

If what I eat causes my brother to stumble

Paul shifts from theory to the possible spiritual damage his liberty could inflict.

• “Brother” is any fellow believer, part of the same body (Ephesians 4:25).

• “Stumble” means being led into sin against one’s conscience (Romans 14:13–15).

• The immediate issue: a weaker Christian might follow Paul’s example and violate his own conscience (1 Corinthians 8:10).

I will never eat meat again

Love willingly surrenders lawful rights.

• Paul is not banning meat universally; he is setting aside a freedom whenever it harms another (Galatians 5:13).

• His pattern: “I have become all things to all people” to win some (1 Corinthians 9:19-23).

• Christ set the ultimate example by not pleasing Himself (Romans 15:3).

So that I will not cause him to stumble

The aim is the spiritual safety of the weaker believer.

• Avoiding offense preserves unity (Ephesians 4:3).

• Jesus warned against causing a believer to stumble (Matthew 18:6).

• The principle reaches beyond diet: “Give no offense to Jews or Greeks or the church of God” (1 Corinthians 10:32).

summary

1 Corinthians 8:13 teaches that Christian liberty is always regulated by love. If exercising a right endangers another’s conscience, the loving response is to lay that right aside. Paul’s resolve never to eat meat again—should it cause a brother to fall—models placing another believer’s spiritual good above personal preference.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Wherefore.--He states his own solemn determination, arising from the considerations which have just been urged. If a matter of food cause a brother to fall in his Christian course, I will certainly never again eat any kind of flesh, lest I should be the cause of so making him to fall.

It is noticeable that St. Paul in discussing this question makes no reference whatever to the decision of the Council at Jerusalem (see Acts 15:29), that the Christians should abstain from "meats offered to idols, and from things strangled, and from blood." Probably, the Apostle felt the importance of maintaining his own apostolic authority in a Church where it was questioned by some, and he felt that to base his instruction upon the decision of the Church at Jerusalem might have seemed to imply that he had obtained authority from them, and not directly from the Lord. It was also more in accordance with St. Paul's usual style of instruction to base the smallest details of conduct upon that highest of all principles--our union as Christians with Christ. An appeal to the letter sent from Jerusalem would have been no step in the ascending argument, which reaches its great climax in the 11th and 12th verses, and which, in 1Corinthians 8:13, the Apostle enunciates as the guide of his own life.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Make my brother to offend. "Make to offend" is, in the original, the verb "scandalize." The word for "meat" means any kind of food. Flesh. The particular subject of discussion here. "I will," says St, Paul, "abstain from flesh altogether rather than by eating it lead a weaker brother into sin." While the world standeth. The same expression is elsewhere rendered "forever." Literally it means to the aeon. St. Paul is often led into these impetuous expressions of the depth of his feelings. The reader will find the whole question argued in s similar spirit in Romans 14:19-22. Lest; namely, in the case supposed. In reality there was no need for taking so severe a pledge of abstinence.
