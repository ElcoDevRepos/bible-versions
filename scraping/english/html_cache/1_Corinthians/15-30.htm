Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
Paul is addressing the Corinthian church, emphasizing the reality and importance of the resurrection. The rhetorical question highlights the risks and sacrifices he and other apostles face for the sake of the Gospel.

And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
Paul's use of "we" suggests a collective experience among the apostles and early Christians. The phrase "endanger ourselves" indicates the constant threats they faced, including persecution, imprisonment, and even martyrdom. This reflects the historical context of the early church, which often faced hostility from both Jewish and Roman authorities.

And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
The phrase "every hour" underscores the continuous nature of the danger. This is not a sporadic or occasional risk but a constant reality. It echoes Paul's own experiences as recorded in Acts and his other epistles, where he recounts numerous hardships, such as beatings, shipwrecks, and stonings (2 Corinthians 11:23-27).

And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
Paul's question is rhetorical, pointing to the absurdity of enduring such dangers if there were no resurrection. It connects to the broader argument in 1 Corinthians 15, where Paul defends the resurrection of the dead as a cornerstone of Christian faith. Without the hope of resurrection, the sacrifices and sufferings of believers would be in vain.

And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
This phrase also serves as a type of Christ, who willingly faced danger and death for the sake of humanity. Just as Jesus endured suffering with the hope of resurrection, Paul and the apostles follow in His footsteps, motivated by the promise of eternal life.

And why do we endanger ourselves every hour?
The question challenges believers to consider their own commitment to the faith. It invites reflection on the cost of discipleship and the willingness to endure hardship for the sake of the Gospel, drawing parallels to Jesus' call to take up one's cross and follow Him (Matthew 16:24).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of 1 Corinthians, addressing the church in Corinth. He is discussing the implications of the resurrection of the dead.

2. Corinth
A major city in ancient Greece, known for its diverse population and moral challenges. The church in Corinth faced various doctrinal and ethical issues.

3. The Resurrection
The central theme of 1 Corinthians 15, where Paul defends the truth of the resurrection of Jesus and its implications for believers.
Teaching Points
The Cost of Discipleship
Following Christ involves risks and sacrifices. Paul’s willingness to face danger underscores the seriousness of his commitment to the Gospel.

The Reality of Persecution
Believers should expect opposition and challenges as they live out their faith. This is a normal part of the Christian experience.

The Hope of the Resurrection
The willingness to face danger is rooted in the hope of the resurrection. Our present sufferings are temporary compared to the eternal glory that awaits.

Living with Purpose
Paul’s example challenges us to evaluate our own lives. Are we living with the same sense of urgency and purpose for the Gospel?

Encouragement in Trials
Knowing that others have faced similar trials can encourage us to persevere. We are part of a larger account of faithfulness throughout history.

What Does 1 Corinthians 15:30 Mean
And why

“Why” signals Paul’s logical challenge. He has just proven Christ’s resurrection (1 Corinthians 15:12-28), so he presses: if the dead are not raised, what possible motive remains for a life of costly ministry? His tone sounds like Romans 8:31—if God is for us, why fear? Without a real resurrection, every sacrifice would be pointless (15:19, 32).

Do we endanger

Paul isn’t exaggerating; his ministry bio in 2 Corinthians 11:23-28 lists beatings, shipwrecks, and prisons.

• Spiritual courage grows from certainty that the gospel is true (Acts 20:22-24).

• Risk is expected for faithful witnesses (Philippians 1:29).

• The cross-shaped life makes danger a normal part of obedience (Luke 9:24).

Ourselves

This isn’t theoretical. Paul and his companions put their own bodies on the line (1 Corinthians 4:9-13).

• Love for Christ outweighs self-preservation (Galatians 2:20).

• Offering ourselves is a “living sacrifice” (Romans 12:1).

• The church benefits when servants embrace personal loss (Colossians 1:24).

Every hour

The peril is constant, not occasional. Paul echoes 2 Corinthians 4:11, “For we who are alive are always delivered to death for Jesus’ sake.”

• Daily cross-bearing keeps eternity in view (Luke 9:23).

• Ongoing trials refine faith (Acts 14:22).

• Continuous danger exposes the bankruptcy of a faith that ends at the grave; only resurrection hope sustains unbroken courage (2 Corinthians 1:8-10).

summary

1 Corinthians 15:30 confronts any thought of a cost-free Christianity. Paul’s unwavering exposure to danger makes sense only because Christ’s resurrection is historical and our resurrection is guaranteed. Confident in that promise, believers joyfully risk comfort, reputation, and even life, knowing “our labor in the Lord is not in vain” (1 Corinthians 15:58).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(30) And why stand we in jeopardy every hour?--This is the same kind of argument now applied to the Apostles themselves. Their conduct also would be illogical if they did not believe in a resurrection. Notice the strong contrast between "them," in the previous verse, and "we" in this verse.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 30. - Why stand we in jeopardy every hour? The verb means "Why do we incur peril?" The best comment on it will be found in 2 Corinthians 11:26. Cicero says ('Tusc. Disp.,' 1:15) that "no one would be so mad as to live in labour and perils if our instinctive anticipation of future life were taken away."
