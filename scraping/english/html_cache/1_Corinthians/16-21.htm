This greeting
In the ancient world, letters were often dictated to a scribe, but personal greetings were sometimes added by the author to authenticate the letter. This practice is seen in other Pauline epistles, such as Galatians 6:11, where Paul mentions writing with large letters in his own hand. This personal touch served to confirm the letter's authenticity and to convey a sense of personal connection and affection to the recipients.

is in my own hand
Paul's use of his own handwriting was significant for several reasons. It was a mark of authenticity, ensuring the recipients that the letter was genuinely from him. This was particularly important in a time when false teachings and letters purportedly from apostles could circulate. Additionally, it demonstrated Paul's personal care and involvement with the Corinthian church, emphasizing his pastoral concern and authority. The act of writing in his own hand also reflects the personal nature of his relationship with the Corinthians, as seen in 2 Corinthians 3:2-3, where he describes them as a letter from Christ.

—Paul.
The mention of his name at the end of the letter is a customary practice in ancient letter writing, serving as a signature. Paul's name carries significant weight, as he was an apostle chosen by Christ (Acts 9:15). His authority and teachings were foundational for the early church, and his letters form a substantial part of the New Testament. The personal mention of his name here reinforces his role as a spiritual father to the Corinthians, as seen in 1 Corinthians 4:15, where he describes himself as having begotten them through the gospel.

Teaching Points
The Personal Touch of Ministry
Paul’s personal greeting underscores the importance of personal connection in ministry. Leaders should strive to engage personally with those they serve, fostering genuine relationships.

Authenticity in Communication
By writing in his own hand, Paul assures the Corinthians of the letter's authenticity. In our communication, especially in matters of faith, we should strive for honesty and transparency.

The Importance of Apostolic Authority
Paul's personal signature serves as a reminder of his apostolic authority. Recognizing and respecting spiritual authority is crucial for maintaining order and unity within the church.

The Role of Written Communication in the Church
Paul's letters were vital for teaching and correcting early churches. Today, written communication, whether through letters, emails, or social media, remains a powerful tool for ministry and encouragement.

What Does 1 Corinthians 16:21 Mean
This greeting

- “This greeting” points back to the final words of the letter (1 Colossians 16:19-20) and highlights that what follows carries a personal warmth that cannot be faked.

- Scripture repeatedly shows that personal greetings convey genuine love in the body of Christ (Romans 16:3-16; Philippians 4:21-22).

- By singling out the greeting, Paul underscores that every word is purposeful and reliable, reminding us that “The words of the LORD are pure words” (Psalm 12:6).

is in my own hand

- Paul normally dictated his letters (Romans 16:22), but here he grabs the pen to authenticate the message.

- This handwritten line acts as a spiritual signature, protecting the church from forged teachings (2 Thessalonians 3:17: “This greeting is in my own hand—Paul. This is my mark in every letter; this is how I write.”).

- It also models personal involvement in ministry: leaders do not remain distant but invest themselves tangibly (Galatians 6:11; Colossians 4:18).

- The phrase reinforces the trustworthiness of the entire epistle, aligning with the promise that “All Scripture is God-breathed” (2 Titus 3:16).

Paul

- Naming himself last gives weight to the authority Christ granted him as an apostle (1 Colossians 1:1).

- It reminds the Corinthians of his relationship to them—both fatherly (1 Colossians 4:15) and firm when needed (2 Colossians 13:10).

- Placing his name right after the handwritten note signals accountability: the same man who taught them now signs off personally, echoing Jesus’ words “Let your ‘Yes’ be ‘Yes,’ and your ‘No,’ ‘No’” (Matthew 5:37).

summary

Paul pauses at the end of 1 Corinthians to write a short, handwritten line: “This greeting is in my own hand—Paul.” Each phrase matters. The greeting conveys affectionate authenticity, the handwriting guarantees the epistle’s integrity, and the name “Paul” affirms apostolic authority and accountability. Taken together, the verse assures us that every inspired word we hold is personal, trustworthy, and lovingly delivered.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) The salutation of me Paul with mine own hand.--It was the Apostle's habit to dictate his Epistles, but to add a few words at the end in his own handwriting. (See 2Thessalonians 3:17.) The concluding verses here are accordingly St. Paul's autograph. The earlier portions had been written by Sosthenes. (See 1Corinthians 1:1.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - With mine own hand. Every one of St. Paul's Epistles, except that to the Galatians (Galatians 6:11), seems to have been written by an amanuensis. The blaze of light in the vision on the road to Damascus seems to have left him with acute and permanent ophthalmia as his "thorn in the flesh;" and this would naturally disincline him to the physical labour of writing. When he did write, his letters seem to have been large and straggling (Galatians 6:11), But this was an age in which documents were frequently falsified by designing persons, and this seems to have happened to St. Paul after he had written his very first extant letter. After warning the Thessalonians not to be frightened "by epistle as from us" (2 Thessalonians 2:2), he adds, at the close of the letter, that henceforth he intends to authenticate every letter by an autograph salutation (2 Thessalonians 3:17; Colossians 4:18; Romans 16:22). To this bad and dangerous practice of forgery is due the energetic appeal of Revelation 22:18, 19. A similar appeal to copyists, couched in the most solemn language, is found in Irenaeus ('Opp.,' 1:821, edit. Stieren), and at the end of Rufinus's prologue to his translation of Origen's 'De Principiis.'
