Now to each one
This phrase emphasizes the individual nature of the gifts of the Spirit. Each believer is uniquely endowed with spiritual gifts, highlighting the personal relationship between the Holy Spirit and the believer. This individual distribution reflects the diverse nature of the body of Christ, as seen in 1 Corinthians 12:12-14, where Paul explains that just as a body has many parts, so does the church. The concept of individual gifting is also seen in Romans 12:6-8, where Paul discusses the variety of gifts given according to the grace given to each person.

the manifestation of the Spirit
The term "manifestation" refers to the visible and tangible expression of the Holy Spirit's presence and power in the life of a believer. This is not merely an internal experience but one that is evident to others, serving as a testament to the Spirit's work. In the early church, such manifestations included speaking in tongues, prophecy, and healing, as recorded in Acts 2:4 and Acts 19:6. These manifestations serve as a continuation of the work of Jesus, who promised the coming of the Spirit in John 14:16-17.

is given
The passive construction "is given" indicates that these gifts are not earned or achieved by human effort but are bestowed by the Holy Spirit according to His will. This aligns with Ephesians 4:7, where Paul states that grace is given to each believer according to the measure of Christ's gift. The giving of these gifts is an act of divine grace, underscoring the sovereignty of God in the distribution of spiritual gifts.

for the common good
The purpose of the spiritual gifts is for the benefit of the entire church community, not for personal gain or status. This communal focus is a recurring theme in Paul's letters, as seen in Ephesians 4:12, where the gifts are given to equip the saints for the work of ministry and to build up the body of Christ. The idea of working for the common good reflects the early Christian community's emphasis on unity and mutual support, as described in Acts 2:44-47. This principle challenges believers to use their gifts selflessly, following the example of Christ, who came not to be served but to serve (Mark 10:45).

Teaching Points
The Purpose of Spiritual Gifts
Spiritual gifts are given by the Holy Spirit to each believer for the common good, not for personal glory or gain.

Unity in Diversity
The variety of gifts reflects the diversity within the body of Christ, yet they all work together to build up the church.

Stewardship of Gifts
Believers are called to be faithful stewards of the gifts they have received, using them to serve others and glorify God.

Dependence on the Holy Spirit
The manifestation of the Spirit in each believer's life is a reminder of our dependence on the Holy Spirit for empowerment and guidance.

Encouragement and Edification
The exercise of spiritual gifts should lead to encouragement and edification within the church community, fostering love and unity.

What Does 1 Corinthians 12:7 Mean
Now to each one

– Paul begins with “Now to each one” (1 Corinthians 12:7), making it clear that no believer is overlooked.

– Every follower of Christ is included, echoing “All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, who apportions them to each one as He determines” (1 Corinthians 12:11).

– Romans 12:3 reminds us that “God has allotted to each a measure of faith,” and 1 Peter 4:10 urges, “Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others.”

– The emphasis on each believer guards against envy or pride; gifts are diverse but universally distributed within the body of Christ.

the manifestation of the Spirit

– Spiritual gifts are a “manifestation,” an outward display that the Holy Spirit truly dwells among God’s people (cf. 1 Corinthians 2:4–5).

– They reveal His power and character, just as Galatians 5:22–23 shows the Spirit’s fruit in the believer’s life and Acts 1:8 promises power for witness.

– Because the gifts are His work, the glory returns to God, not to the gifted person (see 2 Corinthians 4:7).

is given

– “Is given” stresses pure grace—gifts are not earned credentials but generous bestowals from God (Ephesians 4:7; James 1:17).

– John 3:27 affirms, “A man can receive only what is given him from heaven,” fostering humility and dependence on the Giver.

– Knowing the source keeps us from boasting (1 Corinthians 4:7) and encourages gratitude.

for the common good

– The Spirit’s gifts are aimed at building up others, not spotlighting self. Paul reiterates this in 1 Corinthians 14:12: “Since you are zealous for spiritual gifts, strive to excel in gifts that build up the church.”

– Ephesians 4:12 explains that gifts exist “to equip the saints for works of ministry, to build up the body of Christ,” while Philippians 2:4 calls believers to look “to the interests of others.”

– This purpose stitches the church together, preventing division and promoting mutual care (1 Corinthians 12:25–26).

summary

1 Corinthians 12:7 teaches that every believer receives a gracious, Spirit-empowered gift. These gifts visibly display the Holy Spirit’s presence and are granted so the whole church flourishes. Recognizing both the Giver and the communal purpose cultivates humility, unity, and active service among God’s people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) But the manifestation of the Spirit.--These gifts which flow from one source are intended to flow towards one object, viz., the benefit of the whole Church. If it were only for a man's own benefit it would cease to be a "manifestation"--it would be sufficient for the person to possess the spirit consciously to himself. But the object of light is to give light to others. The object of the spiritual light is to make manifest to others.
