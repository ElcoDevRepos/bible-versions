And when the locusts had eaten every green plant in the land
This phrase describes a vision of devastation, where locusts consume all vegetation. Locust plagues were a common and feared disaster in the ancient Near East, often symbolizing divine judgment (Exodus 10:12-15). The imagery of locusts is used elsewhere in Scripture to depict destruction and judgment, such as in Joel 1:4. The complete consumption of green plants signifies total desolation, leaving the land barren and the people without sustenance.

I said, “Lord GOD, please forgive!
Amos, as a prophet, intercedes on behalf of Israel, pleading for God's mercy. This reflects the role of prophets as mediators between God and His people. The plea for forgiveness indicates recognition of sin and the need for divine grace. The use of "Lord GOD" emphasizes the sovereignty and authority of God, acknowledging His power to forgive and relent from sending calamity.

How will Jacob survive, since he is so small?”
"Jacob" refers to the nation of Israel, using the patriarch's name to evoke the covenant relationship between God and His people. The phrase "since he is so small" highlights Israel's vulnerability and weakness, emphasizing their inability to withstand such judgment without divine intervention. This plea underscores the theme of God's mercy and the hope for restoration despite Israel's shortcomings. The reference to Israel's smallness can also be seen as a call for compassion, as God often shows favor to the humble and lowly (Psalm 138:6).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Amos
A prophet from Tekoa, called by God to deliver messages of judgment and hope to Israel. He is known for his boldness and directness in addressing the sins of the people.

2. Lord GOD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who is both just and merciful. In this passage, He is the one to whom Amos directs his intercession.

3. Jacob
A reference to the nation of Israel, descended from Jacob, the patriarch. Here, it symbolizes the people of Israel in their vulnerable state.

4. The Locusts
Represent a divine judgment sent by God to consume the land, symbolizing the impending devastation due to Israel's disobedience.

5. The Land
Refers to the land of Israel, which is under threat of destruction due to the people's sins and the subsequent divine judgment.
Teaching Points
The Power of Intercession
Amos's prayer demonstrates the importance of interceding for others, especially when they are facing judgment. Believers are called to stand in the gap for those who are spiritually vulnerable.

Recognizing Our Smallness
Amos acknowledges Israel's smallness and vulnerability, reminding us of our dependence on God's mercy. We must recognize our limitations and seek God's strength and forgiveness.

God's Willingness to Forgive
Despite the severity of the judgment, Amos appeals to God's forgiving nature. This teaches us that no matter how dire the situation, God's mercy is always available to those who repent.

The Consequences of Disobedience
The impending destruction by locusts serves as a warning about the consequences of turning away from God. It is a call to live in obedience and faithfulness to His commands.

What Does Amos 7:2 Mean
And when the locusts had eaten every green plant in the land

• Amos is shown a devastating vision in which “the locusts had eaten every green plant in the land” (Amos 7:1-2).

• Such total destruction echoes past judgments: the plague on Egypt where “nothing green remained” after the locusts (Exodus 10:14-15), and the covenant warnings that “locusts shall consume” the harvest (Deuteronomy 28:38-42).

• The picture is literal—real loss of crops, animals, and livelihoods—but it also signals spiritual ruin. Joel 1:4 uses similar imagery to awaken repentance, and Revelation 9:3 shows God still able to employ locusts for judgment.

• By letting Amos see the land stripped bare before the judgment actually falls, the Lord is inviting intercession before acting (cf. Ezekiel 22:30).

I said

• Immediately, the prophet steps in: “I said.” He does not stay silent in the face of disaster.

• Amos follows a line of intercessors God has raised up—Abraham pleading for Sodom (Genesis 18:23-33), Moses for Israel after the golden calf (Exodus 32:11-14), Samuel for the nation (1 Samuel 12:19-23).

• The pattern is clear: the Lord discloses judgment, a godly servant speaks up, and divine mercy is sought. God delights to involve His people in His purposes (Isaiah 62:6-7).

Lord GOD, please forgive!

• Amos’s first request is not “stop the locusts” but “please forgive!” Forgiveness addresses the root cause—sin—not just the symptom—loss of crops.

• This plea mirrors David’s cry, “Be gracious to me, O God… blot out my transgressions” (Psalm 51:1-2), and anticipates the promise, “If My people… turn from their wicked ways, then I will hear… and forgive” (2 Chronicles 7:14).

• God’s character encourages such bold prayer: He “pardons iniquity” and “delights in mercy” (Micah 7:18-19), and “If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive” (1 John 1:9).

• True intercession begins with a recognition of guilt and a confident appeal to the Lord’s compassion.

How will Jacob survive, since he is so small?

• “Jacob” is a covenant name for the northern kingdom, reminding God of His promises to the patriarchs (Genesis 32:28).

• Amos points to Israel’s frailty: “so small.” They are no powerhouse; without divine help they cannot stand. Moses once noted, “The LORD set His affection on you… because you were the fewest of all peoples” (Deuteronomy 7:7).

• Isaiah later calls them “O worm Jacob” (Isaiah 41:14), underlining dependence on God. Psalm 103:13-14 adds that He “knows our frame.”

• By stressing weakness, Amos appeals to God’s protective nature toward the humble (Psalm 34:18; James 4:6).

summary

Amos 7:2 shows a prophet witnessing impending judgment, then urgently pleading for forgiveness and mercy. The locust vision underscores total ruin without divine intervention. Amos takes his place among faithful intercessors, appealing to God’s forgiving heart and Israel’s vulnerability. In response to such prayer, verse 3 records, “So the LORD relented concerning this,” proving that earnest intercession rooted in God’s character can move His hand and spare His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) The grass of the land.--The same word is used in the original in Genesis 1:11, signifying herbs and vegetables. Amos saw the first wave of disaster in the destruction of the food of the people, and he interceded for respite and forgiveness. The cry takes the form, Who is Jacob that he should stand? (E.V., "by whom," is incorrect) for he is small.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - The grass of the land. The term includes vegetables of all sorts, the feed of man and beast (Genesis 1:11; see note on Zechariah 10:1). O Lord,...forgive. The prophet is not concerned to obtain the fulfilment of his prophecy; his heartfelt sympathy for his people yearns for their pardon, as he knows that punishment and restoration depend upon moral conditions. By whom shall Jacob arise? better, How shall Jacob stand? literally, as who? If he is thus weakened, as the vision portends, how shall he endure the stroke? Small; weakened by internal commotions and foreign attack (2 Kings 15:10-16, 19).
