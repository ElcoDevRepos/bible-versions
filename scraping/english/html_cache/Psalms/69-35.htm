For God will save Zion
This phrase reflects the assurance of divine intervention and protection over Zion, often synonymous with Jerusalem. Zion holds a central place in biblical history as the city of David and the location of the Temple, symbolizing God's dwelling place among His people. The promise of salvation for Zion is a recurring theme in the Psalms and prophetic literature, emphasizing God's covenant faithfulness. This assurance is echoed in passages like Isaiah 52:7-10, where the salvation of Zion is proclaimed as a demonstration of God's power and glory. Theologically, Zion's salvation can be seen as a type of the ultimate redemption through Jesus Christ, who is often associated with Zion as the cornerstone (1 Peter 2:6).

and rebuild the cities of Judah
The rebuilding of Judah's cities signifies restoration and renewal after periods of destruction or exile. Historically, this can be linked to events such as the Babylonian exile and the subsequent return under leaders like Zerubbabel and Nehemiah, who physically rebuilt Jerusalem's walls and the Temple. This rebuilding is not just physical but also spiritual, as it represents the re-establishment of God's people in their land and their covenant relationship with Him. Prophets like Jeremiah (Jeremiah 31:38-40) and Ezekiel (Ezekiel 36:33-36) speak of this restoration, which also prefigures the eschatological hope of a new heaven and new earth in Revelation 21.

that they may dwell there
The phrase emphasizes the purpose of God's saving and rebuilding actions: to provide a secure and lasting home for His people. Dwelling in the land is a fulfillment of God's promises to the patriarchs, such as Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, regarding the Promised Land (Genesis 17:8). It signifies peace, stability, and the presence of God among His people. In a broader theological sense, this dwelling foreshadows the eternal dwelling of believers with God, as seen in Revelation 21:3, where God will dwell with His people in the new Jerusalem.

and possess it
Possession of the land is a key aspect of the covenant relationship between God and Israel. It involves not only living in the land but also exercising stewardship and enjoying the blessings associated with it. This concept is rooted in the promises made to the patriarchs and reiterated throughout the Old Testament (Deuteronomy 1:8). Theologically, possession of the land can be seen as a type of the believer's inheritance in Christ, as described in Ephesians 1:11, where believers are said to have obtained an inheritance through Him. This inheritance is both a present reality and a future hope, culminating in the eternal kingdom of God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. God
The central figure in this verse, God is depicted as the savior and restorer of Zion and Judah. His actions are central to the hope and future of His people.

2. Zion
Often used to refer to Jerusalem or the people of Israel, Zion represents the spiritual and physical center of God's covenant with His people.

3. Judah
One of the twelve tribes of Israel, Judah is significant as the tribe from which King David and ultimately Jesus Christ descended. It represents the southern kingdom of Israel.

4. Cities of Judah
These cities symbolize the broader community of God's people, which will be restored and inhabited once again.

5. The People of God
Implicit in the verse, they are the beneficiaries of God's saving and rebuilding actions, representing those who are faithful to Him.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness in Restoration
God is faithful to His promises, and His plans for restoration are certain. Believers can trust in His ability to rebuild and restore what is broken in their lives.

The Importance of Zion and Judah
Zion and Judah are not just historical places but represent the spiritual heritage and future hope for believers. Understanding their significance helps us appreciate God's redemptive plan.

Living in God's Promises
As God promises to save and rebuild, believers are called to live in the assurance of His promises, actively participating in His work of restoration in their communities.

Hope in Times of Desolation
Even in times of personal or communal desolation, believers can find hope in God's promise to save and rebuild, knowing that He is always at work for their good.

What Does Psalm 69:35 Mean
For God will save Zion

“God will save Zion” (Psalm 69:35) is a ringing declaration that the Lord Himself steps in to rescue His people.

• This is not a vague hope but a covenant promise, echoing Psalm 48:8 and Isaiah 46:13, where God pledges deliverance for His holy hill.

• Salvation here is both spiritual and physical. Just as He delivered Israel from Egypt (Exodus 14:13) and later from Babylonian exile (Isaiah 52:9), He remains faithful to intervene whenever His people cry out.

• Zion, often synonymous with Jerusalem, stands as the visible center of God’s kingdom on earth (Psalm 132:13–14). The verse reassures every generation that God guards His dwelling place and those who worship there.

and rebuild the cities of Judah

Rebuilding signals complete restoration, more than a patch-up job.

• Jeremiah 30:18 foretells, “The city will be rebuilt on her ruins,” a picture fulfilled when exiles returned under Zerubbabel, Ezra, and Nehemiah (Nehemiah 2:17–18).

• Amos 9:14 confirms God’s intent to “restore the fortunes of My people Israel,” underscoring His covenant faithfulness.

• “Cities of Judah” widens the promise beyond Jerusalem to every town and village—God’s redemption touches the whole land, mirroring His character as Redeemer of both individuals and communities (Isaiah 61:4).

that they may dwell there and possess it

The rescue and rebuilding have a purpose: secure, lasting inheritance.

• “Dwell” evokes settled peace, the opposite of exile or wandering (Leviticus 26:5; Micah 4:4). God wants His people rooted, not restless.

• “Possess” recalls Deuteronomy 1:8, where Israel is told to go in and take the land promised to the fathers. God never withdraws that pledge (Ezekiel 37:25).

• Future-looking passages like Isaiah 65:21-22 picture families building houses and planting vineyards—life in covenant blessing. Thus Psalm 69:35 links immediate historical hope with the ultimate, Messianic restoration confirmed in Revelation 21:3.

summary

Psalm 69:35 assures that

• God Himself intervenes to save His people,

• He fully restores what sin and judgment had ruined, and

• He plants His redeemed in secure, everlasting possession of the land He promised.

The verse captures God’s unchanging heart: rescue, rebuild, and root His people for His glory and their joy.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - For God will save Zion. It is not necessary to suppose that Jerusalem was in any immediate danger. The psalmist merely means that the same God from whom he now confidently expects deliverance will always watch over his city, over his people, over his inheritance, and whenever danger threatens, will exert his protecting power and save. Prophecies of this kind are always conditional, and thus Zion, when she rejected God for idols (2 Chronicles 36:14), and again when she rejected him for Barabbas (Matthew 27:21), forfeited the promised blessing of continuance, and brought about her own destruction. And will build the cities of Judah; i.e. maintain them, keep them from decay and ruin. That they may dwell there; i.e. continue to inhabit the cities. And have it (i.e. Zion, or Jerusalem) in possession.
