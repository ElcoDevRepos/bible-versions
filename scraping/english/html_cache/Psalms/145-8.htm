Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The LORD is gracious and compassionate
This phrase highlights God's character as one who shows unmerited favor and deep empathy towards His creation. The Hebrew words used here, "chanun" (gracious) and "rachum" (compassionate), are often associated with God's covenantal love for Israel. This description of God is consistent throughout the Old Testament, such as in Exodus 34:6, where God reveals His nature to Moses. The grace and compassion of God are foundational to His interactions with humanity, seen in His patience and mercy despite Israel's repeated disobedience. This reflects the New Testament portrayal of Jesus, who embodies grace and compassion in His ministry, healing the sick and forgiving sins.

slow to anger
This phrase emphasizes God's patience and long-suffering nature. The Hebrew term "erek apayim" literally means "long of nostrils," a metaphor for being slow to wrath. This characteristic of God is crucial in understanding His dealings with humanity, as seen in the history of Israel, where God repeatedly withholds judgment to allow for repentance. This patience is echoed in the New Testament, where 2 Peter 3:9 states that God is patient, not wanting anyone to perish but everyone to come to repentance. It underscores the opportunity for salvation and transformation through Christ, who bore the wrath of God on behalf of sinners.

and abounding in loving devotion
The phrase "abounding in loving devotion" uses the Hebrew word "chesed," which is often translated as steadfast love, mercy, or lovingkindness. This term is central to the covenant relationship between God and His people, signifying loyalty and faithfulness. God's "chesed" is not just an emotional response but a committed action towards His people, ensuring their well-being and redemption. This steadfast love is a theme throughout the Psalms and is fulfilled in the New Testament through Jesus Christ, whose sacrificial love on the cross is the ultimate expression of God's "chesed." The abundance of this loving devotion assures believers of God's unwavering commitment to them, providing hope and security in His promises.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The central figure in this verse, Yahweh is depicted as gracious, compassionate, slow to anger, and abounding in loving devotion, highlighting His divine attributes.

The nation to whom these attributes of God were particularly significant, as they experienced God's grace and compassion throughout their history.
Teaching Points
Understanding God's Nature
Recognize that God's grace and compassion are foundational to His character. This understanding should shape our view of God as loving and patient.

Reflecting God's Attributes
As believers, we are called to emulate God's graciousness and compassion in our interactions with others, being slow to anger and rich in love.

Patience in Trials
Trust in God's timing and His patient nature, especially during difficult times. His slowness to anger assures us of His enduring patience with our shortcomings.

Abounding in Love
God's loving devotion is abundant and never-ending. We should strive to love others with the same depth and commitment.

Repentance and Forgiveness
God's grace invites us to repentance, knowing that He is always ready to forgive and restore us to Himself.

What Does Psalm 145:8 Mean
The LORD is gracious

- David begins by celebrating that God’s very nature is to give freely and generously. Exodus 34:6 first unveiled this self-description: “The LORD, the LORD God, compassionate and gracious…”—words David is clearly echoing.

- Grace means God does not wait for us to earn His favor. Romans 5:8 confirms this pattern: “While we were still sinners, Christ died for us.”

- Because grace starts with God, we can freely approach Him (Hebrews 4:16), confident that His throne is one of grace, not reluctance.

and compassionate

- Compassion adds emotional depth to grace. God not only helps; He feels with us. Psalm 103:13 draws the same picture: “As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear Him.”

- Jesus embodies this trait in the Gospels. When He saw the crowds, “He was moved with compassion for them, because they were harassed and helpless” (Matthew 9:36).

- For believers, experiencing God’s compassion motivates us to extend the same mercy to others (Colossians 3:12).

slow to anger

- Patience is another facet of God’s character. Though He is holy and just, He withholds immediate judgment, offering time for repentance. 2 Peter 3:9 explains, “The Lord is not slow in keeping His promise… but is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish.”

- Israel’s history showcases this patience repeatedly; even after cycles of rebellion in Judges, God waited and delivered them when they cried out.

- His slowness to anger should never be mistaken for indifference (Romans 2:4-5), but rather seen as a merciful window to turn to Him.

and abounding in loving devotion

- The phrase pictures love overflowing its banks, never running dry. Lamentations 3:22-23 celebrates it: “Because of the LORD’s loving devotion we are not consumed… His mercies never fail; they are new every morning.”

- God’s covenant love is steadfast. Even when His people falter, His commitment remains (Psalm 136, with its refrain “His loving devotion endures forever”).

- In Christ, this overflowing love reaches its climax: “See what great love the Father has lavished on us, that we should be called children of God!” (1 John 3:1).

summary

Psalm 145:8 offers a fourfold portrait of God: He freely gives, deeply cares, patiently waits, and relentlessly loves. Recognizing these attributes draws us to worship, instills confidence in prayer, fuels repentance, and inspires us to treat others with the same grace, compassion, patience, and steadfast love we daily receive from Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - The Lord is gracious, and full of compassion; slow to anger, and of great mercy. Professor Cheyne compares the epithets in a Babylonian hymn to the sun-god; but a closer parallel is to be found in Exodus 34:6, 7, "The Lord God is merciful and gracious, long-suffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity, and transgression, and sin" (see also Psalm 86:15).
