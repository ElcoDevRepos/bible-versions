Turn away from evil
This phrase emphasizes the biblical principle of repentance, which involves a conscious decision to turn away from sin and wrongdoing. In the context of the Psalms, this reflects the wisdom literature's call to live a life that is pleasing to God. The concept of turning away from evil is echoed in other scriptures, such as Isaiah 1:16-17, where the prophet calls the people to wash themselves and cease to do evil. This turning away is not merely a physical act but a transformation of the heart and mind, aligning with the teachings of Jesus in the New Testament, who calls for inner purity and righteousness.

and do good;
The call to "do good" is a fundamental aspect of biblical ethics, urging believers to actively engage in righteous behavior. This aligns with the teachings of Jesus, who summarized the law and the prophets with the command to love God and love one's neighbor (Matthew 22:37-40). The historical context of the Psalms often reflects a community-oriented society where doing good would involve acts of justice, mercy, and kindness, as seen in Micah 6:8. This phrase encourages proactive engagement in positive actions, reflecting God's character and His desire for His people to be a light to the nations.

seek peace
Seeking peace involves more than the absence of conflict; it is about pursuing wholeness and harmony in relationships. In the Hebrew context, peace (shalom) encompasses well-being, prosperity, and completeness. This pursuit of peace is a recurring theme in the Bible, seen in the Beatitudes where Jesus blesses the peacemakers (Matthew 5:9). The historical backdrop of the Psalms often includes times of war and strife, making the call to seek peace particularly poignant. It reflects God's ultimate plan for reconciliation and peace through Christ, who is referred to as the Prince of Peace (Isaiah 9:6).

and pursue it.
The word "pursue" implies an active, ongoing effort, suggesting that peace is not easily attained and requires diligence and perseverance. This aligns with the New Testament exhortations to strive for peace with everyone (Hebrews 12:14) and to live at peace as much as it depends on you (Romans 12:18). The cultural context of the ancient Near East, with its frequent conflicts and tribal disputes, underscores the radical nature of this command. Pursuing peace is a reflection of God's kingdom values, where believers are called to be ambassadors of reconciliation (2 Corinthians 5:18-20), embodying the peace that Christ has made possible through His sacrifice.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 34, David wrote this psalm during a time of personal distress, specifically when he pretended to be insane before Abimelech, who drove him away (1 Samuel 21:10-15). This context highlights David's reliance on God for deliverance and guidance.

2. Abimelech
A Philistine king, also known as Achish, who encountered David when he fled from Saul. This event underscores the theme of seeking God's protection and peace amidst adversity.

3. The Israelites
The original audience of the psalm, who were often reminded to live righteously and seek peace as part of their covenant relationship with God.
Teaching Points
Rejecting Evil
As believers, we are called to actively turn away from evil. This involves a conscious decision to reject sin and its influences in our lives.

Pursuing Goodness
Doing good is not passive; it requires intentional actions that reflect God's righteousness and love in our daily interactions.

Seeking Peace
Peace is not merely the absence of conflict but the presence of God's harmony. We are to actively seek and pursue peace in our relationships and communities.

Continuous Pursuit
The Hebrew word for "pursue" (radaph) implies a relentless chase. Our pursuit of peace and goodness should be persistent and unwavering.

Holistic Living
Turning from evil and doing good encompasses all aspects of life, including thoughts, words, and deeds, aligning our entire being with God's will.

What Does Psalm 34:14 Mean
Turn away from evil

Psalm 34:14 begins, “Turn away from evil…”. David is calling for an intentional break with everything God calls wicked, not a casual distancing. The command is as binding today as when first penned, because every word of Scripture is true and enduring.

• God repeatedly urges His people to shun sin: Job 28:28; Proverbs 3:7; 1 Thessalonians 5:22.

• Turning away involves decisive choices—changing what we watch, where we go, whom we imitate (James 4:7; 2 Timothy 2:22).

• It is never a suggestion; it is the evidence that we fear the Lord (Proverbs 8:13).

and do good

Stopping evil isn’t enough; we must “do good.” God’s children are saved for active righteousness (Ephesians 2:10).

• Doing good is continual (Galatians 6:9-10), visible (Matthew 5:16), and practical (Titus 3:14).

• It silences critics (1 Peter 2:15) and proves love sincere (Romans 12:9).

• The Spirit enables these works, so the focus stays on God’s grace, not self-effort.

seek peace

“Seek peace” shifts the spotlight from private piety to relational harmony.

• Peace with others pictures our peace with God (Romans 5:1), so Christ calls us peacemakers (Matthew 5:9).

• We are to make peace a priority—initiating reconciliation whenever possible (Romans 12:18).

• Inner peace flows from trusting God (Isaiah 26:3; Philippians 4:7) and equips us to extend peace outward.

and pursue it

Peace isn’t passive; we “pursue it.” The verb implies a vigorous chase.

• Hebrews 12:14 commands believers to “Pursue peace with everyone,” underscoring urgency.

• We pursue peace by guarding our words, forgiving quickly, and promoting unity (Ephesians 4:3; Colossians 3:15).

• Peter quotes this very verse (1 Peter 3:11), showing that New-Testament believers are to chase the same goal.

summary

Psalm 34:14 calls for a fourfold response: renounce evil, practice good, prioritize peace, and chase it relentlessly. In doing so we reflect the character of our Lord, reveal the power of the gospel, and enjoy the blessing God promises to all who take His Word at face value.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) And do good.--Negative goodness is not sufficient. Practical good must be added.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Depart from evil, and do good. From words the psalmist proceeds to acts, and, in the briefest possible way, says all that can be said. First, he enjoins negative goodness - "depart from evil," i.e. do nothing that is wrong; break no laws of God, no command of conscience; have a conscience void of offence, both towards God and towards man. Secondly, he requires positive goodness - "Do good;" i.e. actively perform the will of God from the heart; discharge every duty; practise every virtue; carry out the precepts of the moral law in every particular. Seek peace, and pursue it. It is not clear why this virtue - one of many - is specially enjoined; but probably some circumstances of the time made the recommendation advisable.
