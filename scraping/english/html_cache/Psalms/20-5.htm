May we shout for joy at your victory
This phrase reflects the communal celebration of a king's triumph, likely in a military context. In ancient Israel, victories were seen as direct blessings from God, affirming His favor and presence with His people. The act of shouting for joy is a public expression of gratitude and acknowledgment of God's intervention. This mirrors the celebration seen in Exodus 15:1-21, where Moses and the Israelites sang a song of victory after crossing the Red Sea. The victory here can also be seen as a type of Christ's ultimate victory over sin and death, which believers celebrate with joy.

and raise a banner in the name of our God
Raising a banner was a common practice in ancient warfare, serving as a rallying point for troops and a symbol of identity and allegiance. In this context, the banner represents the acknowledgment of God's sovereignty and the public declaration of His power and protection. The name of God signifies His character and authority, as seen in Exodus 17:15, where Moses built an altar and called it "The LORD is my Banner." This act of raising a banner can also be seen as a prophetic symbol of Christ, who is lifted up as a banner for all nations (Isaiah 11:10).

May the LORD grant all your petitions
This phrase is a prayer for God to fulfill the desires and requests of the king or leader, reflecting a deep trust in God's ability to provide and intervene. In the historical context, kings often sought divine guidance and support before battles or significant decisions. The petitions likely include requests for wisdom, protection, and success. This aligns with the broader biblical theme of God answering the prayers of His people, as seen in 1 John 5:14-15, where believers are encouraged to have confidence that God hears and answers their prayers according to His will. This also points to Jesus Christ, who intercedes on behalf of believers, ensuring that their petitions align with God's purposes.

What Does Psalm 20:5 Mean
May we shout for joy at your victory

Psalm 20 opens as a congregational prayer for the king who is about to engage in battle. When verse 5 says, “May we shout for joy at your victory,” the people are looking ahead in faith, confident that God will bring triumph.

• The “we” underscores corporate solidarity; victory for the king is victory for the nation (2 Samuel 10:12).

• Joy is rooted in God’s proven track record—He “has done marvelous things; His right hand and His holy arm have worked salvation” (Psalm 98:1).

• Celebrating before the battle mirrors Israel’s past: at the Red Sea they sang even while Pharaoh’s army still lay behind them (Exodus 15:1).

• For believers today, the ultimate fulfillment is Christ’s resurrection victory, prompting us to “give thanks to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ” (1 Corinthians 15:57).

and raise a banner in the name of our God

Ancient banners were rallying points on the battlefield, visible symbols of allegiance.

• Moses built an altar and called it “The LORD is My Banner” after God’s triumph over Amalek (Exodus 17:15), revealing that the Lord Himself is the rallying point.

• A banner proclaimed identity; here it is raised “in the name of our God,” openly declaring trust in His character and promises (Proverbs 18:10).

• Isaiah prophesied that Messiah would stand “as a banner for the peoples” (Isaiah 11:10), showing that Christ now gathers and leads His people.

• Practical take-away: public, unabashed confession of Christ is part of our witness (Matthew 10:32).

May the LORD grant all your petitions

The congregation finishes by asking God to answer every prayer the king has offered (Psalm 20:1–4).

• The request is comprehensive—“all your petitions.” Because the king’s desires align with God’s purposes, they can be fully granted (1 John 5:14).

• David testified to this grace: “You have given him the desire of his heart and have not withheld the request of his lips” (Psalm 21:2).

• For any believer, Jesus pledges, “Whatever you ask in My name, that will I do” (John 14:13-14), linking answered prayer to His glory and will.

• Approaching God with confidence rests on His unchanging faithfulness (Hebrews 4:16).

summary

Psalm 20:5 pictures a people so certain of God’s intervention that they celebrate beforehand. They anticipate victory, lift high God’s banner as their identity and rallying point, and trust Him to answer every prayer that advances His righteous cause. The verse invites us to the same posture—joyful confidence, public allegiance to Christ, and bold, expectant prayer.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) We will set up our banners.--Rather, we will wave our banners. (Comp. Song of Solomon 6:10.) The whole army, or their representatives, assembled in the Temple courts, raise the encouraging shout.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - We will rejoice in thy salvation. David's" salvation" is here his triumph over his enemies, which the people confidently anticipate, and promise themselves the satisfaction of speedily celebrating with joy and rejoicing. And in the Name of our God we will set up our banners. Plant them, i.e., on the enemy's forts and strongholds. The Lord fulfil all thy petitions. A comprehensive prayer, re-echoing the first clause of ver. 1 and the whole of ver. 4, but reaching out further to all that the monarch may at any future time request of God, The first part of the psalm here ends, and the people pause for a while.
