He will redeem them from oppression and violence
This phrase highlights the role of a righteous king or leader who acts as a redeemer, a common theme in the Old Testament. The concept of redemption is deeply rooted in the Hebrew tradition, where a kinsman-redeemer would buy back or rescue a relative from slavery or debt (Leviticus 25:25). This anticipates the ultimate redemption through Jesus Christ, who redeems humanity from the bondage of sin (Galatians 3:13). The mention of "oppression and violence" reflects the social injustices prevalent in ancient Israel and surrounding nations, where the poor and marginalized often suffered under corrupt rulers. The psalmist envisions a just ruler who will protect the vulnerable, echoing the messianic hope for a future king who embodies God's justice and mercy (Isaiah 11:4).

for their blood is precious in his sight
This phrase underscores the value and sanctity of human life in the eyes of God. In biblical times, blood symbolized life itself (Leviticus 17:11), and the shedding of innocent blood was a grave offense (Genesis 4:10). The psalmist emphasizes that the lives of the oppressed are not overlooked by God; they are precious and worthy of protection. This reflects God's character as a defender of the weak and a judge of the wicked (Psalm 9:12). The phrase also foreshadows the New Testament revelation of Christ's sacrificial death, where His blood is described as precious, redeeming believers from sin (1 Peter 1:18-19). The value placed on human life here aligns with the broader biblical narrative of God's love and care for all people, particularly those who are downtrodden and afflicted.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Solomon
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 72, Solomon was the son of King David and known for his wisdom and wealth. This psalm is often seen as a prayer for his reign and a foreshadowing of the Messiah's kingdom.

2. The Oppressed
The psalm speaks of those who suffer under oppression and violence, highlighting God's concern for justice and care for the marginalized.

3. The Redeemer
This refers to God, who is seen as the one who will deliver and save the oppressed, a role ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

4. The Kingdom of Israel
The context of the psalm is the kingdom of Israel, under Solomon's rule, which is a type of the coming Messianic kingdom.

5. The Messianic Kingdom
The psalm is often interpreted as a prophecy of the reign of Christ, where justice and peace will prevail.
Teaching Points
God's Heart for Justice
God values justice and is deeply concerned for those who are oppressed. As believers, we are called to reflect His heart by advocating for justice and supporting those in need.

The Preciousness of Life
Every life is precious in God's sight. This should influence how we view and treat others, recognizing their inherent value and dignity.

Christ as the Ultimate Redeemer
Jesus fulfills the role of the Redeemer, offering salvation and deliverance from spiritual oppression. We are invited to trust in His redemptive work.

Hope in God's Kingdom
The psalm points to the hope of a future kingdom where righteousness and peace reign. This hope should inspire us to live in a way that reflects God's kingdom values now.

Prayer for Leaders
Just as the psalm is a prayer for Solomon's reign, we should pray for our leaders, that they would govern with justice and compassion.

What Does Psalm 72:14 Mean
He will redeem them

Psalm 72 presents an ideal, righteous King whose reign points beyond Solomon to the promised Messiah. “He will redeem” announces certain rescue, not wishful thinking. Scripture repeatedly portrays the Lord as the Kinsman-Redeemer who steps into real history to buy back His people:

• Psalm 103:4 – “who redeems your life from the pit.”

• Isaiah 35:10 – “The redeemed of the LORD will return and enter Zion with singing.”

• Luke 1:68 – “Blessed be the Lord, the God of Israel, because He has visited and redeemed His people.”

The promise covers body and soul. Redemption embraces forgiveness of sin (Ephesians 1:7) and tangible deliverance from every enemy (Colossians 1:13). The King’s authority guarantees both.

from oppression and violence

God’s people often face harsh rulers, economic injustice, and physical danger. The verse singles out “oppression and violence” to stress that no form of tyranny intimidates the Redeemer. Consider:

• Psalm 9:9 – “The LORD is a refuge for the oppressed.”

• Psalm 72:4 (earlier in this psalm) – “May he vindicate the afflicted among the people and save the children of the needy; may he crush the oppressor.”

• Acts 10:38 – Jesus “went around doing good and healing all who were oppressed by the devil.”

Whether the threat arises from corrupt governments, criminal aggression, or satanic forces, the King intervenes. His rule is protective, not merely symbolic.

for their blood is precious

The motivation behind the rescue is the immense value God places on each life:

• Psalm 116:15 – “Precious in the sight of the LORD is the death of His saints.”

• Genesis 9:6 – Life is so sacred that shedding blood demands accountability.

• Matthew 10:31 – “You are worth more than many sparrows.”

Because life is precious, the Redeemer will not tolerate lasting harm to His people. He treats their blood as priceless, insisting that injustice be answered and wrongs be righted (Revelation 6:10).

in his sight

Everything unfolds before the all-seeing eyes of the King. Nothing escapes His notice:

• Psalm 33:13-15 – The LORD observes all who dwell on the earth.

• 2 Chronicles 16:9 – “The eyes of the LORD range throughout the earth to strengthen those whose hearts are fully devoted to Him.”

• Hebrews 4:13 – “Nothing in all creation is hidden from God’s sight.”

His vision is personal and compassionate, ensuring that the oppressed are neither forgotten nor overlooked.

summary

• The righteous King personally guarantees redemption.

• He targets oppression and violence, breaking their grip on His people.

• He acts because every believer’s life is priceless to Him.

• His vigilant eyes oversee the whole process, assuring that no injustice slips by.

Psalm 72:14 therefore offers unshakable hope: the Messiah actively rescues, fiercely values, and constantly watches over those who belong to Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) And precious . . .--The parallelism shows the meaning. The life of his people is dear to the king, and he therefore protects them from violence.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - He shall redeem their soul from deceit and violence. Messiah's especial tenderness towards the poor and lowly is a main feature in all descriptions of his kingdom (see Isaiah 11:4; Isaiah 29:19; Isaiah 41:17, etc.), and was emphasized by our Lord when he came upon earth (Matthew 11:5; Luke 4:18). And precious shall their blood be in his sight. Worth, therefore, a mighty ransom (comp. Psalm 116:15).
