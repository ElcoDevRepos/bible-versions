Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Great salvation He brings to His king.
This phrase highlights God's deliverance and protection for the king, specifically King David. The term "great salvation" refers to the comprehensive deliverance from enemies and adversities that David experienced throughout his life. This salvation is not just physical but also spiritual, as it signifies God's favor and covenantal faithfulness. The concept of God as a deliverer is a recurring theme in the Psalms and is seen in other scriptures such as 2 Samuel 22, which parallels Psalm 18. The king here is a type of Christ, the ultimate King, who brings salvation to His people. The deliverance of David foreshadows the greater salvation brought by Jesus Christ, who is often referred to as the King of Kings.

He shows loving devotion to His anointed,
The term "anointed" refers to someone chosen and consecrated by God for a specific purpose. In the Old Testament, kings, priests, and prophets were anointed with oil as a sign of God's selection and empowerment. David was anointed by the prophet Samuel (1 Samuel 16:13), marking him as God's chosen king. The phrase "loving devotion" translates the Hebrew word "chesed," which encompasses God's steadfast love, mercy, and faithfulness. This covenantal love is a key attribute of God, ensuring His promises are fulfilled. The anointing of David is a precursor to the anointing of Jesus, the Messiah (meaning "Anointed One"), who fulfills the ultimate purpose of God's redemptive plan.

to David and his descendants forever.
This phrase underscores the Davidic Covenant, where God promises David that his throne will be established forever (2 Samuel 7:12-16). This covenant is unconditional and eternal, pointing to the lineage of David through which Jesus Christ would come. The promise of an everlasting dynasty is fulfilled in Christ, who is a descendant of David (Matthew 1:1) and whose kingdom is eternal. The phrase "forever" emphasizes the perpetual nature of God's promises, which are not limited by time. This assurance of a lasting dynasty is a testament to God's faithfulness and His sovereign plan for salvation history, culminating in the reign of Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 18, David was the second king of Israel, known for his deep relationship with God and his role as a warrior and leader. This psalm is a song of thanksgiving for God's deliverance and faithfulness.

2. God
The central figure in this verse, God is depicted as the source of salvation and loving devotion, emphasizing His covenant faithfulness.

3. David's Descendants
Refers to the lineage of David, which is significant in biblical history as it leads to the birth of Jesus Christ, the Messiah.

4. Anointed
This term refers to those chosen by God for a special purpose, often kings or prophets. In this context, it specifically refers to David and, by extension, the Messiah.

5. Salvation
The deliverance and victory granted by God, both in a physical and spiritual sense, highlighting His power and grace.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness
God's promises are enduring and reliable. Just as He was faithful to David, He remains faithful to us today.

The Role of the Anointed
Being anointed by God comes with a responsibility to lead and serve according to His will. We are called to recognize and support those whom God has placed in leadership.

Salvation Through Christ
The ultimate fulfillment of God's promise to David is found in Jesus Christ, who offers salvation to all. We are invited to accept this gift and live in the light of His grace.

Legacy of Faith
David's legacy is not just in his kingship but in his faith and relationship with God. We are encouraged to build a legacy of faith that impacts future generations.

Covenant Relationship
Our relationship with God is based on His covenant love, which calls us to respond with obedience and devotion.

What Does Psalm 18:50 Mean
Great salvation He brings to His king

“Great salvation He brings to His king” (Psalm 18:50a) is David’s joyful testimony that the LORD personally intervenes for the one He has placed on the throne.

•For David, that “great salvation” was visible every time the LORD delivered him from Saul, Philistines, and countless rivals (2 Samuel 22:1–4, a parallel to this psalm).

•The phrase carries the idea of sweeping, complete rescue—body, soul, and kingdom—echoing earlier declarations such as “The LORD saves His anointed” (Psalm 20:6).

•Because God Himself installed David (1 Samuel 16:13), He takes it upon Himself to guard that installation (Psalm 144:10).

•The ultimate expression of this salvation is found in David’s greater Son, Jesus Christ. At the cross and resurrection God gave “great salvation” to His King once for all (Acts 2:32–36; Revelation 17:14).

•Those who trust the King share in that same deliverance (Hebrews 2:3).

He shows loving devotion to His anointed

“He shows loving devotion to His anointed” (Psalm 18:50b) highlights the covenant love (Psalm 89:24, 28).

•“Loving devotion” (steadfast love) is covenant language—unchanging, loyal, purposeful. God’s relationship with His anointed isn’t fickle; it is pledged (2 Samuel 7:15).

•The term “anointed” points first to David, literally anointed with oil by Samuel (1 Samuel 16:13). But it also points forward to the Messiah, the Anointed One par excellence (Isaiah 61:1; Luke 4:18).

•God’s loving devotion is seen in the way He bore with David’s sins yet never withdrew the throne (Psalm 89:33–34), and in the way He confirmed Jesus’ kingship by raising Him from the dead (Romans 1:3–4).

•Because the Father’s love for His Anointed is secure, every promise bound up in that love is secure for us who are “in Christ” (2 Corinthians 1:20–22).

to David and his descendants forever

“To David and his descendants forever” (Psalm 18:50c) ties the whole verse to the Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7:12–16).

•God swore that David’s line would endure; Psalm 89:35–37 calls the promise as certain as the rising sun.

•Historically, that line continued through Solomon, fell into exile, yet never disappeared (2 Kings 25:27–30; Ezra 3:2).

•The New Testament traces the genealogy straight to Jesus (Luke 1:32–33; Acts 13:22–23). In Him the word “forever” finds literal fulfillment, for He “lives forever” (Hebrews 7:24) and will “reign forever and ever” (Revelation 11:15).

•Believers are counted among those descendants by faith (Galatians 3:29), meaning the promise spills over to a worldwide family—Jew and Gentile alike—who will share in Christ’s eternal kingdom (Romans 15:12).

summary

Psalm 18:50 celebrates three linked realities: God powerfully rescues His chosen King; He does so out of unwavering covenant love; and He guarantees that love to David’s line forever—finding its fullest expression in Jesus the Messiah. Because these words are literally true, every believer can rest in the same saving power, steadfast love, and everlasting kingdom secured in Christ.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(50) This verse is by many treated as a late liturgical addition to the hymn. The change to the third person is certainly somewhat suggestive of this, but by no means conclusive.

The question of the relation of the two copies of this hymn to each other is far too complicated and difficult for discussion here. Each has been again and again claimed as the original. The best explanation of the variations is that the compositions were independent copies of some original, and that the psalm, like many others, was altered in preparation for the choir use.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 50. - Great deliverance giveth he to his king; literally, he magnifies salutations to his king. The primary reference seems to be to the gracious message which God sent to David by Nathan when he had brought the tabernacle into Jerusalem, and purposed to build a "house" worthy of it (see 2 Samuel 7:8-16). God had then "saluted" David as "his servant" (ver. 5), and sent him a message of the most gracious character, even promising the kingdom to him and to his seed "for ever" (vers. 13, 16). And showeth mercy to his anointed, to David. No doubt David is primarily intended, both by the "king" of the first clause, and by the "anointed" of the second; but the combination of the two, and the immediate mention of the "seed" which is to reign "for ever," carry the passage beyond the psalmist individually, and give to the conclusion of the psalm, at any rate, a semi-Messianic character. As Hengstenberg says, "Psalms of this kind are distinguished from those which may more strictly be called Messianic, only by this - that in the latter the Messiah exclusively is brought into view, while here he is presented to our notice only as a member of the seed of David" ('Commentary on the Psalms,' vol. 1. p. 324, Engl. trans.).
