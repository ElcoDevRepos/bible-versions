Praise Him with clashing cymbals;
The use of cymbals in worship is deeply rooted in the history of Israelite worship practices. Cymbals were part of the musical instruments used in the temple, as seen in 1 Chronicles 15:16, where David appointed the Levites to play loud instruments, including cymbals, to accompany the Ark of the Covenant. The clashing cymbals signify a loud, joyful noise, emphasizing the exuberance and intensity of worship. This reflects the broader biblical theme of worshiping God with all one's might and resources, as seen in 2 Samuel 6:14, where David danced before the Lord with all his might. The clashing cymbals symbolize the call for all creation to join in the praise of God, echoing the universal call to worship found throughout the Psalms.

praise Him with resounding cymbals.
The repetition of cymbals, described here as "resounding," underscores the importance of using music to express the glory and majesty of God. Resounding cymbals produce a sustained, echoing sound, which can be seen as a metaphor for the enduring and far-reaching impact of true worship. This phrase highlights the idea that worship should not only be loud and joyful but also profound and lasting. The use of cymbals in this context can be connected to the worship practices in the temple, where music played a crucial role in the liturgical life of Israel. The resounding cymbals can also be seen as a type of the proclamation of the Gospel, which reverberates throughout the world, calling all nations to worship the one true God, as prophesied in Isaiah 42:10-12.

Teaching Points
The Role of Music in Worship
Music is a powerful tool for expressing praise and adoration to God. It engages the heart and mind, allowing worshippers to connect deeply with the divine.

Diversity in Worship
The use of different instruments, including cymbals, signifies the diversity and richness of worship. It encourages believers to embrace various forms of expression in their praise.

Joyful and Exuberant Praise
The call to use "clashing cymbals" and "resounding cymbals" suggests that worship should be vibrant and full of life. Believers are encouraged to worship God with enthusiasm and joy.

Community in Worship
The collective use of instruments in worship highlights the communal aspect of praising God. Worship is not just a personal act but a shared experience that unites believers.

Biblical Foundation for Worship Practices
Understanding the biblical basis for using instruments in worship can enrich the worship experience and provide a deeper appreciation for traditional and contemporary practices.

What Does Psalm 150:5 Mean
Praise Him with clashing cymbals

“Praise Him with clashing cymbals;” (Psalm 150:5a)

• The command is straightforward: worshipers are to lift up literal cymbals—percussion instruments struck together to create an abrupt, sharp sound. Scripture affirms that such tangible, audible praise pleases God (2 Samuel 6:5; 1 Chronicles 15:16).

• The clashing or “loud” quality underscores uninhibited celebration. We are invited to let our praise be heard, not muffled. Similar scenes appear whenever God’s people rejoice over His mighty acts (Psalm 33:3; Ezra 3:11–13).

• This verse affirms that worship is not confined to quiet contemplation; the whole range of human expression, including rousing percussion, is fitting for the Lord who “reigns over all the earth” (Psalm 47:7).

Praise Him with resounding cymbals

“praise Him with resounding cymbals.” (Psalm 150:5b)

• The repetition intensifies the call. “Resounding” speaks of sustained, echoing sound—a rolling, ongoing crash that fills the space. Worship is to be both immediate and enduring (Psalm 96:1–3).

• By pairing clashing and resounding cymbals, the psalmist depicts a full spectrum of percussive praise: sudden peaks and lingering reverberations. Our worship, likewise, embraces both spontaneous bursts and prolonged devotion (Revelation 5:13).

• Instruments here are not ends in themselves; they magnify the glory of the One who “is enthroned upon the praises of Israel” (Psalm 22:3). The volume and vigor spotlight the greatness of God, not the performers (Psalm 115:1).

summary

Psalm 150:5 charges God’s people to honor Him with loud, vibrant percussion—both sharp clashes and rolling echoes—signaling that wholehearted, audible, enthusiastic worship is fitting for the King of all creation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Cymbals.--Heb., tseltselim (2Samuel 6:5), a word evidently formed to express the sound of the instrument. Two kinds are evidently indicated in this verse, the "loud" cymbals (literally, cymbals of hearing), and "high-sounding" (literally, of tumult). As the Arabs use at present a larger and smaller instrument (see Bible Educator, 2, 211, 311), it is possible that the same distinction is made here, but which would be the larger instrument it is impossible from the Hebrew to determine.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Praise him upon the loud cymbals; praise him upon the high-sounding cymbals. "We can hardly," says Professor Cheyne, "venture to distinguish two kinds of cymbals on the ground of these two epithet" The mention of "cymbals" is reserved to the last, as being the instrument of music most expressive of joy and jubilation. It completes the musical climax, as ver. 6 completes the ideal one.
