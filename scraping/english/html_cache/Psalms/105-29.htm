He turned their waters to blood
This phrase refers to the first plague that God inflicted upon Egypt through Moses and Aaron, as recorded in Exodus 7:14-24. This miraculous transformation of the Nile River's waters into blood was a direct challenge to the Egyptian gods, particularly Hapi, the god of the Nile, demonstrating the supremacy of the God of Israel. The Nile was central to Egyptian life, providing water, transportation, and fertile soil. Turning it to blood symbolized a disruption of life and order, emphasizing God's power over creation. This act also foreshadows the blood of Christ, which brings both judgment and redemption.

and caused their fish to die
The death of the fish in the Nile further underscores the severity of the plague. Fish were a staple in the Egyptian diet and an economic resource, so their death would have had significant social and economic repercussions. This event highlights the comprehensive nature of God's judgment, affecting not just the water but the ecosystem and livelihood of the Egyptians. The death of the fish also serves as a reminder of the consequences of sin and rebellion against God, as seen throughout Scripture, such as in Romans 6:23, which speaks of the wages of sin being death. This plague, like others, points to the ultimate deliverance and judgment found in Jesus Christ, who has authority over all creation.

Persons / Places / Events

3. Moses and Aaron
- Though not mentioned directly in this verse, they were God's chosen leaders to confront Pharaoh and lead the Israelites out of Egypt.

4. Pharaoh
- The ruler of Egypt who hardened his heart against God’s command to let the Israelites go.

5. The Plague of Blood
- The first of the ten plagues, where God turned the Nile River and all water sources in Egypt into blood.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nature
This verse highlights God's absolute control over the natural world. He can alter creation to fulfill His purposes and demonstrate His power.

Judgment and Mercy
The plagues, including the turning of water to blood, were acts of judgment against Egypt's idolatry and oppression. Yet, they also served as a call to repentance and acknowledgment of the one true God.

The Consequences of Hardening One's Heart
Pharaoh's refusal to heed God's command led to severe consequences for Egypt. This serves as a warning against hardening our hearts to God's voice.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The plagues were part of God's plan to deliver Israel, fulfilling His promise to Abraham. This reminds us of God's faithfulness to His word and His people.

The Importance of Obedience
Moses and Aaron's obedience in carrying out God's commands was crucial in the unfolding of God's plan. Our obedience to God can have significant impacts on His work in the world.

What Does Psalm 105:29 Mean
He turned

- The initiative belongs entirely to the LORD; the verse opens with His decisive action, echoing Exodus 7:17: “By this you will know that I am the LORD: … I will strike the water of the Nile with the staff in my hand, and it will be turned to blood.”

- Psalm 105 is a historical psalm, rehearsing God’s mighty works. By recalling the first plague, the psalmist underscores God’s faithfulness to keep His covenant with Abraham (Psalm 105:8-10) and His power to humble proud nations (Psalm 107:40).

- The verb highlights sovereignty—God is not reacting; He is directing events for His redemptive purpose, just as He controlled the Red Sea (Exodus 14:21) and later the Jordan (Joshua 3:13-16).

their waters to blood

- “Waters” in Exodus 7:19 include rivers, canals, ponds, and reservoirs, showing a comprehensive judgment. Nothing the Egyptians relied on escaped His reach.

- Blood is a vivid, unmistakable sign of death and impurity (Leviticus 17:11). Turning life-giving water into blood signaled that Egypt’s life source was now a sentence of judgment.

- The miracle was literal, not symbolic only; Moses records, “all the water was turned to blood” (Exodus 7:20). Psalm 78:44 repeats the same fact, reinforcing the historicity.

- Revelation 16:3-4 looks back to this plague and projects it forward into end-times judgment, linking God’s past acts with His future justice.

and caused their fish to die

- Exodus 7:21 states, “The fish in the Nile died, and the river smelled so bad that the Egyptians could not drink its water.” The death of the fish proves the transformation was real, not an optical illusion.

- Practical fallout:

• Food scarcity—fishing was a staple industry (Isaiah 19:8).

• Economic blow—fish were central to trade and diet.

• Environmental crisis—the stench made daily life unbearable, dramatizing the cost of resisting God.

- Spiritually, the death of the fish struck at Egypt’s gods. The Nile was deified, and fish were linked to their mythology. By destroying them, the LORD demonstrated, “I am the LORD, and there is no other” (Isaiah 45:5).

summary

Psalm 105:29 reminds us that the first plague on Egypt was a literal, historical act of God. He personally turned water into blood, stripping Egypt of life’s essentials and exposing the futility of its idols. The verse teaches that the LORD keeps covenant promises, judges rebellion, and wields creation itself to accomplish salvation for His people.
