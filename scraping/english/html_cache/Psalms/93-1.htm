Psalm 93
1. The majesty, stability, power, and holiness of Christ's kingdom.














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The LORD reigns!
This declaration emphasizes God's sovereign rule over all creation. It reflects the biblical theme of God's kingship, seen throughout the Psalms and the Old Testament. The phrase echoes the enthronement psalms (e.g., Psalm 47, 95-99) and affirms God's ultimate authority, contrasting with earthly rulers. It also anticipates the New Testament revelation of Christ as King of Kings (Revelation 19:16).

He is robed in majesty;
The imagery of God being "robed in majesty" conveys His splendor and glory. In ancient Near Eastern cultures, a king's robe symbolized his power and status. This phrase highlights God's divine nature and the honor due to Him. It parallels descriptions of Christ's transfiguration (Matthew 17:2) and His glorious return (Revelation 1:13-16).

the LORD has clothed and armed Himself with strength.
This emphasizes God's omnipotence and readiness to act. The clothing and arming with strength suggest preparation for battle, reflecting God's role as a divine warrior who protects and delivers His people. This is seen in the Exodus narrative (Exodus 15:3) and in Christ's victory over sin and death (1 Corinthians 15:57).

The world indeed is firmly established;
This phrase underscores the stability and order of creation, attributed to God's sovereign will. It reflects the Genesis creation account, where God brings order out of chaos (Genesis 1). The stability of the world is a testament to God's faithful governance and is echoed in the New Testament assurance of Christ sustaining all things (Colossians 1:17).

it cannot be moved.
This affirms the permanence and reliability of God's creation, symbolizing His unchanging nature. It reassures believers of God's steadfastness and the security found in Him. This immovability is echoed in the promise of an unshakeable kingdom through Christ (Hebrews 12:28) and the eternal nature of God's word (Isaiah 40:8).

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty
The LORD's reign is absolute and unchallenged. Recognize His authority in every aspect of life and trust in His control over all circumstances.

Majesty and Strength
God's majesty is not just a matter of appearance but is accompanied by strength. This should inspire awe and reverence in our worship and daily lives.

Stability of Creation
The world is firmly established by God, reminding us of the stability and order He brings. In a world of chaos, we can find peace in His unchanging nature.

Worship and Reverence
Our response to God's majesty should be one of worship and reverence. Let this understanding deepen our worship practices and personal devotion.

Faith in God's Plan
Since the world is established by God, we can have faith in His plan and purpose for our lives, knowing that He is in control.

What Does Psalm 93:1 Mean
The LORD reigns!

- This opening declaration is not wishful thinking; it is a statement of unchangeable reality.

- Psalm 47:8 affirms, “God reigns over the nations; God is seated on His holy throne”.

- Because He reigns, His authority is absolute—over creation, history, governments, and personal lives.

- Revelation 19:6 echoes the same note of triumph: “Hallelujah! For our Lord God Almighty reigns”.

- Confidence flows from knowing that the throne of heaven is never vacant and never threatened.

He is robed in majesty

- The language pictures God wrapped in regal splendor, conveying both beauty and dignity.

- Psalm 104:1-2 paints a similar scene: “O LORD my God, You are very great; You are clothed with splendor and majesty, covering Yourself with light as with a garment”.

- Majesty points us to His holiness—He is utterly set apart, deserving of awe and worship.

- Isaiah 6:1 records Isaiah’s vision: “I saw the Lord seated on a high and lofty throne, and the train of His robe filled the temple”. That overwhelming glory is what the psalmist has in view.

the LORD has clothed and armed Himself with strength

- Unlike human rulers who borrow power, the Lord’s strength is His own; He “arms” Himself because omnipotence inherently belongs to Him.

- Psalm 24:8 asks, “Who is this King of glory? The LORD strong and mighty, the LORD mighty in battle”.

- Isaiah 59:17 pictures God as a warrior who “put on righteousness like a breastplate.” His strength defends truth, judges evil, and secures His people.

- For believers, the command in Ephesians 6:10—“Be strong in the Lord and in His mighty power”—flows from this assurance that His strength is inexhaustible.

The world indeed is firmly established; it cannot be moved

- The stability of creation rests on the Creator. Genesis 1:1 roots the universe in God’s intentional act: “In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth”.

- Psalm 119:90 testifies, “You established the earth, and it stands”. Because He sustains it, natural laws hold and seasons keep their rhythm.

- Colossians 1:17 adds the Christ-centered perspective: “He is before all things, and in Him all things hold together”.

- The psalm is not denying natural disasters but declaring that nothing can derail God’s purposes for His world or for His people.

summary

Psalm 93:1 lifts our eyes to the throne. The Lord’s reign is certain, His majesty dazzling, His strength undefeatable, and His creation secure. Every phrase calls us to trust, worship, and rest in the unshakable reality that our God rules now and forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) The Lord reigneth.--Comp. Psalm 97:1; Psalm 99:1. Better, Jehovah has become king: the usual term for ascending the throne (2Samuel 15:10; 1Kings 1:11; 1Kings 1:13; 2Kings 9:13); used in Isaiah of the re-establishment of the State after the Captivity (Isaiah 24:23; Isaiah 52:7); and by the latest of Israel's poets, in that prophetic strain which looks beyond time and this world (Revelation 19:6). The robing and girding with the sword were part of the ceremony of inauguration of a monarch's reign. (See Note, Psalm 45:3.) . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - The Lord reigneth; rather, is become King (ἐβασίλευσεν, LXX.); comp. Psalm 10:16; Psalm 47:6; Psalm 96:10; Psalm 97:1, etc. God is regarded as having for a time laid aside, or hidden, his sovereignty, but as now at length coming forward and inaugurating the theocracy. The writer may have in his mind some recent manifestation of Divine power, or he may be anticipating the final establishment of the reign of Messiah. He is clothed with majesty; or, "he hath robed himself in majesty" (Cheyne). The Lord is clothed with strength, wherewith he hath girded himself; literally, the Lord is clothed, he hath girded himself, with strength (comp. "Awake, awake, put on strength, O arm of the Lord," Isaiah 51:9). The world also is stablished, that it cannot be moved. When God "takes his kingdom," he firmly establishes his sway over the earth, with its inhabitants, in such sort that "it cannot be moved" - it can suffer no violent agitation or disturbance.
