Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
A Psalm of David.
This introduction identifies King David as the author, a central figure in the history of Israel. David's life, marked by both triumph and tribulation, provides a rich backdrop for understanding his psalms. As a shepherd, warrior, and king, David's experiences reflect a deep reliance on God. His psalms often express personal struggles and a profound trust in God's sovereignty. This attribution also connects the psalm to the Davidic covenant, where God promises David an everlasting dynasty (2 Samuel 7:12-16), foreshadowing the coming of Jesus Christ, the Son of David.

O LORD, hear my prayer.
David begins with a direct appeal to Yahweh, the covenant name of God, emphasizing a personal relationship. This plea for God to hear is a common motif in the Psalms, reflecting the belief that God is attentive to the cries of His people (Psalm 34:15). The use of "LORD" underscores God's eternal nature and His role as the covenant-keeping God of Israel. This phrase also echoes the prayers of other biblical figures who sought God's intervention, such as Moses (Exodus 32:11) and Solomon (1 Kings 8:28).

In Your faithfulness, give ear to my plea;
David appeals to God's faithfulness, a key attribute of His character. This faithfulness is rooted in God's unchanging nature and His commitment to His promises (Deuteronomy 7:9). The plea for God to "give ear" suggests an urgent need for divine attention and intervention. This reflects the cultural context of ancient Near Eastern supplication, where petitioners sought the favor of a sovereign. The emphasis on God's faithfulness connects to the broader biblical narrative, where God's steadfast love and faithfulness are central themes (Lamentations 3:22-23).

in Your righteousness, answer me.
David's request for an answer based on God's righteousness highlights the moral and ethical dimensions of God's character. Righteousness in the biblical sense involves justice, integrity, and the fulfillment of God's covenantal obligations. This appeal suggests that David's confidence in receiving an answer is not based on his own merit but on God's just nature. The concept of divine righteousness is further developed in the New Testament, where it is fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who embodies God's righteousness and offers it to believers (Romans 3:21-22).

Teaching Points
The Importance of Prayer
David's example shows us the importance of turning to God in times of need. Prayer is a vital part of our relationship with God.

God's Faithfulness and Righteousness
We can trust in God's character. His faithfulness and righteousness assure us that He hears and answers our prayers.

Honesty in Prayer
David's plea is honest and direct. We should feel free to express our true feelings and needs to God.

Dependence on God
This verse reminds us of our need to rely on God rather than our own strength or understanding.

Confidence in God's Response
Just as David expected God to answer, we too can have confidence that God will respond to our prayers in His perfect timing and way.

What Does Psalm 143:1 Mean
A Psalm of David

- David labels this song as his own, signaling personal experience behind every word (see Psalm 34 title; 2 Samuel 22:1).

- The superscription reminds us that inspired Scripture often springs from real-life trials, making its lessons immediately relatable (Romans 15:4).

O LORD, hear my prayer

- David addresses “LORD” (YHWH), anchoring his hope in the covenant name of God (Exodus 3:15).

- He does not merely recite words; he asks God to listen, confident that the Almighty welcomes sincere cries (Psalm 18:6; 1 John 5:14).

- The petition highlights relationship: prayer is conversation with a Father who delights to engage His children (Matthew 7:7-11).

In Your faithfulness, give ear to my plea

- David appeals to God’s proven faithfulness, not his own merit (Lamentations 3:22-23; 2 Timothy 2:13).

- “Plea” shows urgency; life’s pressures drive him to cling to the unchanging character of God (Psalm 40:11).

- By rooting his request in divine constancy, he models how believers today can pray with assurance that past acts of faithfulness guarantee future help (Hebrews 10:23).

In Your righteousness, answer me

- David ties the expected answer to God’s righteousness—His perfect justice and moral reliability (Psalm 71:2; Isaiah 45:21).

- God’s righteous nature ensures that His response will be both right and timely (Psalm 97:2).

- Because the LORD judges rightly, David trusts Him to act against evil and for the good of His servant (Psalm 9:8-10; Romans 8:31-33).

summary

Psalm 143:1 shows David praying with bold humility: he knows his need, yet he anchors every request in God’s faithful and righteous character. The verse invites us to approach the LORD the same way—confident that He hears, bound by His covenant love, and committed to act in perfect justice for His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) Faithfulness . . . righteousness.--The first word recalls the covenant promise, the second the faith, expressed so frequently, on which the covenant rested, that the Judge of all the world must do right. St. John founds the appeal for forgiveness on the same pair of Divine qualities (1John 1:9; comp. Psalm 65:5.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - Hear my prayer, O Lord, give ear to my supplications (comp. Psalm 28:2; Psalm 39:12; Psalm 54:2; Psalm 55:1, etc.). In thy faithfulness answer me, and in thy righteousness. In thy faithfulness to thy promises, since thou hast promised to hear prayer, and in thy mere righteousness, since it is right and just that thou shouldest do so, hearken unto me.
