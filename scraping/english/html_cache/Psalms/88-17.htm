Psalm 88
1. A prayer containing a grievous complaint.














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All day long they engulf me like water;
This phrase captures the relentless nature of the psalmist's suffering, likening it to a flood. In biblical literature, water often symbolizes chaos and overwhelming circumstances (Genesis 7:11, Psalm 69:1-2). The imagery of water engulfing someone is used to convey a sense of being overwhelmed by troubles, similar to the floodwaters that covered the earth in Noah's time. This can also be seen as a metaphor for spiritual or emotional turmoil, reflecting the psalmist's deep distress. The continuous nature of the suffering ("all day long") suggests a persistent and unending trial, reminiscent of the trials faced by Job (Job 30:14-15).

they enclose me on every side.
The sense of being surrounded on all sides indicates a feeling of entrapment and isolation. This imagery is consistent with other biblical passages where enemies or troubles are described as encircling the faithful (Psalm 22:12-13, Psalm 118:10-12). The psalmist's experience can be seen as a type of Christ, who was surrounded by adversaries and felt forsaken (Matthew 27:46). The phrase also reflects the cultural understanding of divine protection being absent, as God's presence was often associated with deliverance from encircling threats (Psalm 34:7). The psalmist's lament highlights the absence of this protection, deepening the sense of despair.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Heman the Ezrahite
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 88, Heman was a wise man and a musician in the time of King David. He is known for his role in the temple worship and his contributions to the Psalms.

2. The Sons of Korah
This group is associated with the composition and performance of several psalms. They were Levites who played a significant role in temple worship.

3. The Temple in Jerusalem
The central place of worship for the Israelites, where psalms like this one would have been sung or recited.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Suffering
Psalm 88 is a reminder that suffering is a real and persistent part of the human experience. It acknowledges the depth of despair that believers can feel, even when they are faithful.

The Language of Lament
This psalm teaches us the importance of bringing our deepest pains and struggles before God. Lament is a valid and necessary form of prayer.

God's Presence in Darkness
Even when we feel engulfed by troubles, we can trust that God is present. The psalm encourages us to seek Him persistently, even when we do not feel His presence.

Community Support
The psalmist's cry for help highlights the need for community support. As believers, we should be attentive to those around us who are suffering and offer them support and prayer.

Hope Beyond Despair
While Psalm 88 ends in darkness, it is part of a larger biblical account that points to hope and redemption through Christ. We are reminded that our ultimate hope is in Him.

What Does Psalm 88:17 Mean
All day long

• The psalmist speaks of an unrelenting trial—suffering that never pauses. Psalm 38:6 echoes this round-the-clock burden: “I am bowed down and brought very low; all day long I go about mourning”.

• Unlike fleeting troubles, this is continual pressure. Romans 8:36 reminds believers, “For Your sake we face death all day long; we are considered as sheep to be slaughtered”, yet God remains present even when His help seems delayed.

• The phrase highlights endurance; the writer is not doubting God’s sovereignty but honestly describing a day after day reality that drives him to seek divine intervention.

They engulf me like water

• Water imagery conveys overwhelming force. Psalm 69:1–2 cries, “Save me, O God! For the waters are up to my neck. I sink in deep mire, where there is no foothold”.

• Waves roll in one after another. Jonah 2:3 records a similar experience in the prophet’s prayer from the fish’s belly: “All Your breakers and waves swept over me”.

• The comparison reminds us that trials can feel as if they sweep over us without pause, yet Isaiah 43:2 promises, “When you pass through the waters, I will be with you”. God’s presence does not negate the reality of the waves but assures the sufferer he will not drown spiritually.

They enclose me on every side

• The sense of being surrounded leaves no escape route. Lamentations 3:7 states, “He has walled me in so I cannot escape; He has weighed me down with chains”.

• This claustrophobic language reflects spiritual and emotional confinement. Job 19:8 says, “He has blocked my way so I cannot pass; He has veiled my paths with darkness”.

• In the New Testament, Paul testifies, “We are hard pressed on every side, but not crushed” (2 Corinthians 4:8), showing that even when boxed in, believers are upheld by God’s sustaining power.

• The enclosure reminds us that enemies, circumstances, or even illness may hem us in, yet Psalm 34:7 encourages: “The angel of the LORD encamps around those who fear Him”. The believer is ultimately surrounded by divine protection, not merely human threats.

summary

Psalm 88:17 paints a vivid picture of relentless anguish—trouble that lasts “all day long,” overwhelms “like water,” and traps “on every side.” While the imagery is stark, it honestly voices the believer’s darkest moments, allowing us to bring raw pain to the Lord. Scripture consistently affirms that God remains present through continuous trials, drowning waves, and enclosing pressures. He hears, sustains, and, in His perfect timing, delivers those who cling to Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) They--i.e., the terrors or horrors, now likened to a flood, a figure of frequent occurrence. (See Psalm 18:16, &c.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - They came round about me daily like water. God's terrors encompass the psalmist "daily," or "all day long," like water; i.e. like an overwhelming flood (compare the first clause of ver. 16). They compassed me about together; or, "they compass me about in a mass."
