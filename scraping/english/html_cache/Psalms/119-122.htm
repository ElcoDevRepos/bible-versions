Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Ensure Your servant’s well-being;
This phrase is a plea for divine protection and care. The term "servant" reflects a relationship of submission and loyalty to God, similar to the relationship between a master and servant in ancient Near Eastern cultures. The psalmist acknowledges their dependence on God for sustenance and safety. This request for well-being can be seen as a call for both physical and spiritual prosperity, aligning with the biblical theme of God as a shepherd who cares for His flock (Psalm 23). The concept of well-being here is comprehensive, encompassing peace, security, and fulfillment, which are often promised to those who follow God's commandments (Deuteronomy 28:1-14).

do not let the arrogant oppress me.
The "arrogant" refers to those who are prideful and often disregard God's laws, a recurring theme in the Psalms where the wicked are contrasted with the righteous. Oppression by the arrogant can be understood as both physical persecution and spiritual or emotional distress. Historically, the Israelites faced oppression from surrounding nations and internal leaders who strayed from God's ways. This plea for deliverance from oppression echoes the cries of the Israelites in Egypt (Exodus 3:9) and the consistent biblical narrative of God as a deliverer. The request for protection from the arrogant also foreshadows the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ, who offers freedom from sin and its oppressive power (John 8:36).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who is expressing a deep desire for God's protection and justice.

2. The Servant
Refers to the psalmist himself, who identifies as a servant of God, seeking divine intervention and care.

3. The Arrogant
Represents those who are prideful and oppressive, often seen as adversaries to the faithful.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God for Justice
The psalmist's plea for well-being underscores the importance of relying on God for justice rather than taking matters into our own hands.

The Danger of Arrogance
Arrogance is a recurring theme in Scripture as a trait that leads to downfall. Believers are called to humility and trust in God's timing and justice.

Prayer as a Tool for Protection
The psalmist models how prayer can be a powerful tool for seeking God's protection and intervention in times of oppression.

God's Faithfulness to His Servants
The request for well-being is rooted in the understanding of God's faithfulness to those who serve Him. Believers can find comfort in God's promises to protect and sustain them.

Living as a Servant of God
Identifying as a servant of God involves a commitment to His ways and a trust in His provision and protection, even amidst adversity.

What Does Psalm 119:122 Mean
Ensure

• The psalmist asks God to “Ensure,” to guarantee, to stand as surety. This is a confident appeal to God’s covenant faithfulness (cf. Psalm 121:3, “He will not allow your foot to slip”).

• Scripture shows the Lord willingly taking responsibility for His people’s welfare—Exodus 14:14, “The LORD will fight for you, and you must be still.”

• The plea demonstrates active faith: the writer does not rely on personal strength but on God’s pledge of protection (Psalm 18:2).

Your servant’s

• By calling himself “Your servant,” the psalmist claims the privileged position of belonging to God, echoing Psalm 116:16, “Truly, O LORD, I am Your servant.”

• Servanthood in Scripture carries both humility and assurance; because the servant serves the King, the King safeguards the servant (Isaiah 54:17).

• This identity invites God’s special attention, just as Christ taught in Matthew 20:27–28 that greatness is tied to serving under God’s authority.

well-being

• The term points to comprehensive safety—body, soul, and situation. Psalm 4:8 reflects the same trust: “You alone, O LORD, make me dwell in safety.”

• God’s promise in Jeremiah 29:11 underscores His desire for welfare, not harm.

• Well-being is covenant blessing realized (Deuteronomy 28:1-6), reminding us that obedience and dependence lead to tangible peace.

do not let

• This phrase shows submission: the psalmist recognizes God’s control over circumstances (Proverbs 21:1, “The king’s heart is in the hand of the LORD”).

• It also underscores urgency—a cry for immediate intervention like Psalm 70:1, “O God, come quickly to deliver me.”

• “Do not let” acknowledges that without divine restraint, evil would prevail (2 Thessalonians 3:3).

the arrogant oppress me

• “The arrogant” are the proud who defy God’s law (Psalm 119:21). Their hallmark is oppression—using power to crush the righteous (Psalm 94:4-5).

• The writer has likely suffered under such people; yet he trusts God to reverse their schemes (Psalm 9:13).

• God consistently opposes the proud and gives grace to the humble (James 4:6); thus the servant’s appeal aligns with divine character.

summary

Psalm 119:122 captures a humble yet confident plea: the servant of God looks to his faithful Master to guarantee his complete safety and to restrain proud oppressors. It is a model of trusting prayer, grounded in covenant relationship and anchored in the certainty that the Lord defends those who belong to Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
AIN.

(122) Be surety.--Just as Judah became surety for the safety of Benjamin (Genesis 43:9), so the psalmist asks God to be answerable for the servant who had been faithful to the covenant, and stand between him and the attacks of the proud. So Hezekiah (Isaiah 38:14) asks God to "undertake" for him against the threat of death. There is also, no doubt, the further thought that the Divine protection would vindicate the profession which the loyal servant makes of his obedience, as in Job 17:3, where God is summoned as the only possible guarantee of the sufferer's innocence. This and Psalm 119:132 are the only verses not actually mentioning, under one of its terms, the Law.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 122. - Be surety for thy servant for good (comp. Job 17:3; Isaiah 38:14). "For good" means "so that it may be well with him." Let not the proud oppress me (comp. vers. 51, 69, 78, 85, etc.).
