All You have made will give You thanks
This phrase emphasizes the universal acknowledgment of God's creation. It reflects the biblical theme that all creation, animate and inanimate, is designed to glorify God. This is echoed in passages like Psalm 19:1, where the heavens declare the glory of God. The idea that all creation will give thanks can be seen as a prophetic vision of the ultimate restoration and harmony of creation, as described in Romans 8:19-22, where creation eagerly awaits the revealing of the children of God. The phrase also suggests a future time when all creation will recognize and respond to God's sovereignty, aligning with the eschatological hope found in Revelation 5:13, where every creature in heaven and on earth praises God.

O LORD
The use of "LORD" in all capital letters signifies the Hebrew name Yahweh, the covenant name of God revealed to Moses in Exodus 3:14. This name underscores God's eternal, self-existent nature and His faithfulness to His promises. It is a reminder of God's personal relationship with His people, Israel, and by extension, all who are grafted into this covenant through faith in Jesus Christ, as seen in Romans 11:17-24. The invocation of Yahweh here highlights the personal and relational aspect of thanksgiving and blessing, as it is directed towards the God who is both transcendent and immanent.

and Your saints will bless You
"Saints" refers to those set apart for God, often used in the Old Testament to describe the faithful in Israel and in the New Testament to describe believers in Christ. This phrase indicates that those who are in a covenant relationship with God will actively bless Him, which involves both praise and a life lived in accordance with His will. The concept of blessing God is seen throughout the Psalms, such as in Psalm 103:1-2, where David calls on his soul to bless the LORD. This reflects a reciprocal relationship where God's people respond to His grace and goodness with worship and obedience. The saints' blessing of God is a foretaste of the eternal worship described in Revelation 7:9-12, where a great multitude from every nation, tribe, people, and language stands before the throne and the Lamb, praising God eternally.

Teaching Points
Universal Praise
All of creation is designed to reflect God's glory and give Him thanks. This includes not only humans but all of nature, which inherently praises God through its existence and beauty.

Role of the Saints
As God's people, we have a unique role in blessing and praising God. Our lives should be a testament to His goodness and faithfulness, leading others to recognize and honor Him.

Gratitude as Worship
Giving thanks is a form of worship that acknowledges God's provision and sovereignty. Cultivating a heart of gratitude aligns us with God's purpose and brings us closer to Him.

Living in Awe of Creation
Observing the natural world should lead us to worship. Recognizing God's handiwork in creation can deepen our appreciation for His power and creativity.

Community of Praise
The saints are called to bless God together, emphasizing the importance of communal worship and fellowship in strengthening our faith and witness.

What Does Psalm 145:10 Mean
All You have made

“All” includes everything created—people, angels, animals, oceans, stars (Psalm 19:1; Psalm 148:1-13; Revelation 5:13). Because God personally brought every atom into being (Genesis 1:1; Colossians 1:16-17), the whole universe owes Him acknowledgment.

• The scope is universal, leaving no corner of creation exempt (Romans 1:20).

• The statement anticipates the day when every knee bows and every tongue confesses Christ’s lordship (Philippians 2:10-11).

will give You thanks

Creation’s right and inevitable response is gratitude. Future tense stresses certainty; this will happen, not merely should happen (Psalm 107:1; 1 Thessalonians 5:18).

• Thanksgiving recognizes God’s goodness and generosity (Psalm 136:25-26).

• It redirects attention from self to the Giver, cultivating humility.

• Thanksgiving fuels obedience; grateful hearts gladly do His will (Colossians 3:17).

O LORD

The thanks are addressed to the covenant God who reveals His name, character, and faithfulness (Exodus 3:14; Malachi 3:6).

• He is personal, not an impersonal force.

• His unchanging nature guarantees that the gratitude offered is never misplaced (James 1:17).

and Your saints

“Saints” are the people set apart by God—those redeemed and belonging to Him (Daniel 7:27; Romans 1:7; 1 Peter 2:9).

• While all creation thanks God, saints bear a special calling to lead that chorus.

• Their lives, words, and worship become visible testimonies of God’s grace (Revelation 19:8).

will bless You

To bless God is to praise Him, speak well of Him, and declare His worth (Psalm 103:1-2; Hebrews 13:15).

• Thanksgiving focuses on gifts received; blessing emphasizes the Giver Himself.

• Saints bless the Lord by proclaiming His works, obeying His commands, and reflecting His character (Ephesians 1:3).

summary

Psalm 145:10 paints a two-part symphony of worship. First, every creature acknowledges the Creator’s goodness with grateful thanks. Second, the redeemed community rises to the forefront, intentionally blessing His name. Together they reveal a universe ordered toward God’s glory, moving inexorably toward the day when that praise will resound without interruption or rival.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - All thy works shall praise thee, O Lord (comp. Psalm 148:2-13, where all creation is called on to praise the Lord). And thy saints shall bless thee; or, "thy loving ones" - those who are devoted to thy service.
