I crushed them so they could not rise;
This phrase reflects the complete and decisive victory over enemies, a common theme in the Psalms where God empowers His people to overcome adversaries. The imagery of crushing suggests total defeat, leaving the enemies powerless to recover. This can be seen as a fulfillment of God's promises to protect and deliver His people, as seen in Deuteronomy 28:7, where God promises that enemies will be defeated before Israel. The language of crushing also echoes the prophecy in Genesis 3:15, where the seed of the woman will crush the serpent's head, symbolizing ultimate victory over evil. This can be seen as a type of Christ, who through His death and resurrection, defeated sin and death, ensuring that His enemies cannot rise against Him.

they have fallen under my feet.
The imagery of enemies falling under one's feet signifies complete subjugation and authority over them. In ancient Near Eastern culture, placing one's foot on the neck of a defeated enemy was a symbolic act of victory and dominance. This phrase can be connected to Joshua 10:24, where Joshua commands his commanders to put their feet on the necks of the captured kings, symbolizing God's deliverance and the Israelites' triumph. In a messianic context, this foreshadows Christ's ultimate victory over all His enemies, as seen in 1 Corinthians 15:25, where it is stated that He must reign until He has put all His enemies under His feet. This victory is not just physical but spiritual, indicating the comprehensive nature of Christ's reign and the assurance of His ultimate triumph.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 18, David is expressing his gratitude and praise to God for delivering him from his enemies. This psalm is a song of victory and thanksgiving.

2. Enemies
The adversaries of David, who sought to harm him. In a broader sense, they represent any forces of evil or opposition against God's people.

3. God's Deliverance
The overarching event in this psalm is God's intervention and deliverance of David from his enemies, showcasing God's power and faithfulness.
Teaching Points
God's Empowerment for Victory
Just as God empowered David to overcome his enemies, He empowers believers today to overcome spiritual battles. Trust in God's strength rather than your own.

The Certainty of God's Deliverance
David's confidence in God's deliverance is a reminder that God is faithful to His promises. Reflect on past victories as a testament to God's faithfulness.

The Role of Faith in Overcoming Challenges
David's faith in God was crucial to his victory. Strengthen your faith through prayer, scripture, and fellowship with other believers.

Spiritual Warfare Awareness
Recognize that believers are in a spiritual battle. Equip yourself with the Word of God and prayer to stand firm against the enemy.

Humility in Victory
Acknowledge that victories come from God, not personal strength or wisdom. Give glory to God for every triumph.

What Does Psalm 18:38 Mean
I crushed them

• David speaks of decisive victory: “I pursued my enemies and overtook them; I did not turn back until they were consumed” (Psalm 18:37).

• The verb pictures total defeat, as when Joshua routed the Amorite kings and “the LORD threw them into confusion” (Joshua 10:10).

• The credit lies with God’s empowerment. Earlier David testified, “It is God who arms me with strength and makes my way clear” (Psalm 18:32).

• New-Testament echo: “The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet” (Romans 16:20). The pattern of God enabling His people to crush evil remains constant.

So they could not rise

• The fall of the enemy is irreversible. Like chaff blown away, they lack power to regroup.

• Psalm 21:11-12 mirrors this finality: “Though they intend You harm…You will put them to flight when You aim Your bow at their faces.”

• Malachi 4:3 extends the picture to the ultimate day of the Lord: “You will trample the wicked, for they will be ashes under the soles of your feet.”

• The point is assurance: when God gives victory, the threat is removed, not merely postponed.

They have fallen under my feet

• Ancient conquerors placed a foot on a vanquished king’s neck. Joshua did this with Israel’s leaders: “Come forward and place your feet on the necks of these kings” (Joshua 10:24).

• David’s words anticipate the Messiah: “For He must reign until He has put all His enemies under His feet” (1 Corinthians 15:25; cf. Hebrews 10:13).

• The image highlights both authority and safety—enemies are not only beaten but placed in a position of permanent subjection.

Summary

Psalm 18:38 presents a three-stage snapshot of God-given triumph: the crushing blow, the inability of foes to rise again, and their placement under the victor’s feet. David’s testimony assures believers that the Lord who empowered him still grants decisive, lasting victory over every adversary opposed to His purposes.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 38. - I have wounded them that they were not able to rise: they are fallen under my feet. It is remarkable that the nations which David subdued scarcely ever, while he lived, rose up again in revolt.
