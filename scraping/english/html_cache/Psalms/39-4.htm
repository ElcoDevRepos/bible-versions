Show me, O LORD, my end
This phrase is a plea for divine revelation and understanding of one's mortality. In biblical context, acknowledging one's end is a call for wisdom and humility. The psalmist seeks God's guidance to comprehend the finite nature of human life. This request reflects the wisdom literature theme found in Ecclesiastes 7:2, which emphasizes the importance of contemplating one's mortality to gain a heart of wisdom. The use of "LORD" signifies a personal relationship with God, invoking His covenant name, Yahweh, which denotes His eternal nature in contrast to human transience.

and the measure of my days
Here, the psalmist desires to understand the brevity and limitations of his lifespan. This reflects a common biblical theme where life is often compared to a shadow or a breath, as seen in Job 14:5 and Psalm 90:12. The "measure" implies a fixed, divinely appointed span, suggesting that life is under God's sovereign control. This understanding encourages believers to live purposefully and in accordance with God's will, recognizing that their time on earth is limited and should be used wisely.

Let me know how fleeting my life is
The request to "know" underscores a deep yearning for awareness and acceptance of life's transitory nature. The term "fleeting" conveys the idea of life being ephemeral and passing quickly, akin to a vapor or mist, as described in James 4:14. This acknowledgment serves as a reminder of human frailty and the need for dependence on God. It also points to the eternal perspective that believers are encouraged to adopt, focusing on heavenly treasures rather than earthly ones, as taught in Matthew 6:19-21. The fleeting nature of life is a call to prioritize spiritual growth and readiness for eternity.

What Does Psalm 39:4 Mean
Show me, O LORD, my end

• David invites God to uncover the finish line of his earthly journey. He is not guessing about fate; he wants God’s clear revelation.

• Scripture consistently affirms that every life has a divinely appointed conclusion: “Man’s days are determined; You have decreed the number of his months” (Job 14:5).

• The plea recognizes accountability. Hebrews 9:27 reminds, “people are appointed to die once, and after that to face judgment.”

• Knowing the certainty of an end keeps the heart anchored: “Our God is in the heavens; He does all that He pleases” (Psalm 115:3).

and the measure of my days

• David goes a step further—he longs to grasp the span, the “measure,” of those days.

• Psalm 31:15 declares, “My times are in Your hands,” linking the length of life to God’s governance.

• Practical outcomes of grasping this measure:

– Focused stewardship of time, talents, and relationships.

– Readiness to obey promptly, echoing Psalm 119:60, “I hurried without hesitating to keep Your commandments.”

– Freedom from anxiety, because “all the days ordained for me were written in Your book before one of them came to be” (Psalm 139:16).

• James 4:14 warns, “You do not even know what will happen tomorrow. What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes.” The measure is short enough to demand urgency, long enough to fulfill God’s purpose.

Let me know how fleeting my life is.

• The psalmist asks for a vivid sense of life’s brevity so that he will live wisely, not morbidly.

• Isaiah 40:6–8 paints the same picture: “All flesh is grass… The grass withers, the flowers fall, but the word of our God stands forever.”

• Recognizing fleetingness produces:

– Humility: “Man is like a breath; his days are like a passing shadow” (Psalm 144:4).

– Detachment from worldly pride, aligning with 1 John 2:17: “The world and its desires pass away, but whoever does the will of God lives forever.”

– Eager hope in the eternal: “To live is Christ and to die is gain” (Philippians 1:21).

summary

Psalm 39:4 captures a threefold prayer: revelation of life’s endpoint, awareness of its allotted span, and conviction of its brevity. Grasping these truths cultivates humility, urgent obedience, wise stewardship, and steadfast hope in the God whose word endures forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) Rhythmically and from every other reason the psalm onward from this verse must be treated as the utterance to which the poet's feelings have at length driven him.

How frail I am.--This is to be preferred to the margin, which follows the LXX. and Vulg. The Hebrew word, from a root meaning to "leave off," though in Isaiah 53:3 it means "forsaken," here, as in Ezekiel 3:27, is active, and implies "ceasing to live."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - Lord, make me to know mine end, and the number of my days. This is not exactly the request of Job, who desired to be at once cut off (Job 6:9; Job 7:15; Job 14:13), but it is a request conceived in the same spirit. The psalmist is weary of life, expects nothing from it, feels that it is "altogether vanity." He asks, therefore, not exactly for death, but that it may be told him how long he will have to endure the wretched life that he is leading. He anticipates no relief except in death, and feels, at any rate for the time, that he would welcome death as a deliverer. That I may know how frail I am. So most moderns; but Hengstenberg denies that חדל can ever mean "frail," and falls back upon the old rendering, "that I may know when I shall cease [to be]," which certainly gives a very good sense.
