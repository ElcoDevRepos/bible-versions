Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He sent darkness, and it became dark—
This phrase refers to the ninth plague that God sent upon Egypt as described in Exodus 10:21-23. The darkness was a direct act of God, demonstrating His power over creation and the Egyptian gods, particularly Ra, the sun god. This plague was a supernatural darkness, possibly a thick, tangible darkness that could be felt, emphasizing the severity of God's judgment. The darkness lasted for three days, symbolizing a complete period of divine judgment. This event foreshadows the darkness that covered the land during the crucifixion of Jesus Christ (Matthew 27:45), symbolizing judgment and the power of God over life and death.

yet they defied His words.
Despite the miraculous and terrifying nature of the plagues, Pharaoh and the Egyptians continued to resist God's command to let the Israelites go. This defiance highlights the hardness of Pharaoh's heart, a recurring theme in the Exodus narrative (Exodus 7:13, 9:12). It serves as a warning against the dangers of pride and disobedience to God's will. The phrase underscores the futility of resisting God's sovereign plans, as His purposes will ultimately prevail. This defiance is contrasted with the obedience of Jesus Christ, who submitted to the Father's will even unto death (Philippians 2:8), serving as the ultimate example of faithfulness and submission to God's word.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Power
God has control over nature and uses it to fulfill His purposes. The darkness in Egypt was a direct act of God, demonstrating His authority over creation.

The Consequences of Defiance
Pharaoh's defiance against God's words led to severe consequences. This serves as a warning against hardening our hearts to God's commands.

Spiritual Darkness vs. Light
The physical darkness in Egypt symbolizes spiritual darkness. Believers are called to walk in the light of Christ, who dispels darkness.

Obedience to God's Word
The Israelites' deliverance was contingent upon obedience to God's instructions. Similarly, our spiritual freedom is linked to our obedience to God's Word.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
Despite the darkness and defiance, God remained faithful to His promise to deliver Israel. This reassures us of God's faithfulness in our lives.

What Does Psalm 105:28 Mean
He sent darkness

“He sent darkness…” (Psalm 105:28)

• The psalmist is recalling the ninth plague on Egypt (Exodus 10:21-23). God Himself initiates the judgment; darkness does not simply happen—He sends it.

• This act showcases God’s sovereign authority over creation (Psalm 135:6; Job 38:12-13).

• By highlighting that the darkness originates with God, the verse underscores that He actively intervenes to fulfill His redemptive plan for Israel (Exodus 6:6-7).

And it became dark

“…and it became dark—” (Psalm 105:28)

• The effect is immediate and total, mirroring Moses’ description that “a palpable darkness” covered Egypt (Exodus 10:22).

• Creation responds instantly to God’s word, just as light appeared at His command in Genesis 1:3.

• Darkness in Scripture often symbolizes judgment (Isaiah 8:22; Matthew 8:12). Here, it is a tangible sign that the Lord is confronting Egypt’s idolatry and oppression (Exodus 12:12).

Yet they defied His words

“…yet they defied His words.” (Psalm 105:28)

• Despite the overwhelming sign, Pharaoh and his officials “hardened their hearts” (Exodus 10:24-27; 11:10).

• Defiance is not ignorance but deliberate rebellion against clear revelation (Exodus 5:2; Romans 1:21-23).

• The pattern warns that miraculous signs alone do not soften a resistant heart; true change comes through humble submission to God’s word (Hebrews 3:7-15).

summary

Psalm 105:28 recounts the ninth Egyptian plague to remind God’s people that He is sovereign, His commands are instantly effective, and human pride can still resist unmistakable evidence of His power. The verse calls believers to trust His active rule over creation and to respond to His word with obedient faith rather than stubborn defiance.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) Darkness.--The enumeration of the plagues omits the fifth and sixth, and begins with the ninth, and appends a clause which, from the first, has troubled translators. Of whom is it said, "They rebelled not against his words"? Of the Egyptians it is not true; and to refer the words to Moses and Aaron, in contrast with their resistance to the Divine command at Massah and Meribah, is feeble. The LXX. and the Syriac solved the difficulty by rejecting the negative. (Comp. the Prayer Book Version.)

The simplest explanation is to take the verb as imperfect subjunctive: "He sent darkness, and made it dark, that they might not rebel against his word."

But this fails to supply a reason for the position in the list of the ninth plague, and the suggested emendation of Mr. Burgess is so satisfactory in this respect, that it almost by itself carries conviction with it. By a very slight change, he obtains: "He sent darkness, and darkened them, that they might not discern his tokens;" taking deber in the same sense that it bears in Psalm 105:27.

Thus the plague of darkness is, by a slight device of the poet, made to symbolise the moral blindness displayed by the Egyptians throughout.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - He sent darkness, and made it dark (see Exodus 10:21-23). And they rebelled not against his word. If the "not" is to stand in this passage, it must be referred to Moses and Aaron. Professor Cheyne, however, following the Septuagint and Peshito versions, boldly cancels the "not."
