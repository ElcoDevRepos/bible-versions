Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The troubles of my heart increase;
This phrase reflects the deep emotional and spiritual turmoil experienced by the psalmist, often identified as David. In the biblical context, David faced numerous adversities, including threats from enemies, personal sin, and familial strife. The heart, in Hebrew thought, is the center of emotions and will, indicating that the psalmist's inner being is overwhelmed. This mirrors the human condition of facing increasing challenges and the need for divine intervention. The increasing troubles can be seen as a test of faith, similar to the trials faced by Job, who also experienced profound suffering and sought understanding from God.

free me from my distress.
The plea for freedom from distress highlights the psalmist's reliance on God for deliverance. In the historical context, David often sought refuge in God during times of persecution, such as when fleeing from King Saul or during Absalom's rebellion. This cry for liberation is a common theme in the Psalms, where the faithful seek God's intervention in times of trouble. Theologically, this can be seen as a foreshadowing of the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ, who offers freedom from the bondage of sin and spiritual distress. The concept of divine rescue is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are encouraged to cast their anxieties on God, as seen in 1 Peter 5:7.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 25, David is expressing his personal struggles and seeking God's deliverance.

2. God
The central figure to whom David directs his plea for relief from his increasing troubles.

3. Israel
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of the Psalms often reflects the collective struggles and spiritual journey of the nation of Israel.
Teaching Points
Acknowledging Our Troubles
Recognize that it is natural to experience increasing troubles and that acknowledging them is the first step toward seeking God's help.

Turning to God in Distress
Like David, we should turn to God in our times of distress, trusting in His ability to deliver us from our troubles.

The Power of Prayer
This verse highlights the importance of prayer as a means of communicating our struggles to God and seeking His intervention.

God's Faithfulness
Trust in God's faithfulness to respond to our cries for help, as He has done for His people throughout history.

Spiritual Growth Through Trials
Understand that trials can lead to spiritual growth and a deeper reliance on God, as seen in David's life and writings.

What Does Psalm 25:17 Mean
The troubles of my heart increase

– David voices that his inner burdens are multiplying, not just lingering.

• Psalm 38:4 echoes this sense: “For my iniquities have overwhelmed me; they are a burden too heavy to bear.”

• Troubles “of the heart” points to anxiety, guilt, fear—more than outer hardship. Compare Proverbs 12:25, where “Anxiety in a man’s heart weighs him down.”

• “Increase” reminds us that trials can feel wave-upon-wave; Peter later warns believers not to be surprised when a “fiery trial” comes upon them (1 Peter 4:12).

• Yet God allows these seasons to draw us nearer: Psalm 34:18 affirms, “The LORD is near to the brokenhearted.”

free me from my distress

– Having named the rising tide of trouble, David pleads for release.

• The phrase tells us where rescue must come from. Self-help won’t cut it; Psalm 121:2 states, “My help comes from the LORD, the Maker of heaven and earth.”

• “Free me” implies bondage; distress can imprison the soul much like Paul’s literal chains in Acts 16:25, where worship unlocked a jail cell.

• Distress here is both emotional and spiritual. Isaiah 43:2 promises, “When you pass through the waters, I will be with you… the rivers will not sweep over you.”

• God’s deliverance is often immediate inside, even if circumstances remain. Philippians 4:7 describes the “peace of God” guarding hearts and minds.

summary

Psalm 25:17 captures the honest cry of a believer overwhelmed yet hopeful. David admits escalating inner turmoil, then turns directly to the only One able to liberate him. Scripture reinforces that increasing troubles are normal, but so is God’s nearness and power to set the heart free. Turning our distress into prayer places us on the path from bondage to peace.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 17, 18. - The troubles of my heart are enlarged: O bring thou me out of my distresses. Look upon mine affliction and my pain; and forgive all my sins. The affliction, of whatever kind it may have been, was regarded by David as a punishment sent on him for his sins. Of his sins he was at this time deeply conscious (vers. 7, 11) and deeply repentant. Probably they included his great sin (see the comment on ver. 11).
