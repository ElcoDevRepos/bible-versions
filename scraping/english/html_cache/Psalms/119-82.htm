Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
My eyes fail
This phrase captures the intense longing and weariness of the psalmist. The imagery of failing eyes suggests a deep, almost physical exhaustion from waiting and watching for God's intervention. In biblical times, the eyes were often seen as a window to the soul, and their failing could symbolize a spiritual or emotional depletion. This expression of weariness is echoed in other scriptures, such as Lamentations 2:11, where the prophet Jeremiah speaks of his eyes failing with tears. The psalmist's experience is a common theme in the Bible, reflecting the human condition of waiting on God amidst trials.

looking for Your promise;
The psalmist is focused on God's promise, which in the context of the Old Testament often refers to the covenantal promises made to Israel, including deliverance, protection, and the coming of the Messiah. This longing for God's promise is a central theme throughout the scriptures, as seen in the lives of the patriarchs like Abraham, who waited for the fulfillment of God's promises (Hebrews 11:13). The psalmist's anticipation of God's promise also points forward to the New Testament, where believers await the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises in Christ (2 Peter 3:13).

I ask, “When will You comfort me?”
This question reveals the psalmist's deep desire for God's comfort and intervention. In the historical and cultural context of ancient Israel, comfort from God was often associated with deliverance from enemies or relief from suffering. The plea for comfort is a recurring theme in the Psalms, as seen in Psalm 23:4, where God's presence is a source of comfort. This longing for divine comfort also foreshadows the New Testament promise of the Holy Spirit, referred to as the Comforter (John 14:16). The psalmist's question reflects a universal human experience of seeking God's presence and reassurance in times of distress.

Teaching Points
Longing for God's Promises
The psalmist's intense desire for God's promises should inspire believers to cultivate a similar longing and anticipation for God's word and His assurances.

Endurance in Waiting
The phrase "My eyes fail" suggests perseverance despite weariness. Believers are encouraged to remain steadfast in faith, even when God's promises seem delayed.

Seeking Divine Comfort
The psalmist's question, "When will You comfort me?" highlights the importance of seeking God's comfort in times of distress, trusting in His perfect timing.

Faith in God's Faithfulness
The psalmist's reliance on God's promises underscores the need for believers to trust in God's faithfulness, knowing that He will fulfill His word.

Active Expectation
The act of looking for God's promise involves active expectation. Believers should engage in prayer, study, and worship as they await God's intervention.

What Does Psalm 119:82 Mean
My eyes fail

• The psalmist confesses, “My eyes fail,” acknowledging physical and emotional exhaustion after long seasons of waiting. Like Psalm 69:3—“I am weary with my crying; my throat is parched; my eyes fail, looking for my God”—he admits his strength is spent.

• This is not doubt; it is honest transparency before the Lord. In Lamentations 2:11 Jeremiah speaks similarly: “My eyes fail from weeping… because the daughter of my people has been destroyed.”

• Such candor models how believers may pour out their hearts without fear, knowing that “He knows how we are formed” (Psalm 103:14).

Looking for Your promise

• The focus of his longing is God’s own “promise”—the sure word previously given. Earlier he said, “I put my hope in Your word” (Psalm 119:81).

• Waiting is purposeful: he fixes his gaze on what God has pledged, not on circumstances. As Hebrews 10:23 urges, “Let us hold resolutely to the hope we profess, for He who promised is faithful.”

• Psalm 130:5 echoes this posture: “I wait for the LORD; my soul waits, and in His word I put my hope.”

I ask

• He does more than wait—he keeps asking. Relationship with God invites ongoing dialogue. Psalm 55:17, “Evening, morning, and noon, I cry out and He hears my voice.”

• Jesus commends persistent prayer (Luke 18:1), and Paul urges, “Pray without ceasing” (1 Thessalonians 5:17).

• Rather than silent resignation, the psalmist voices his need, proving faith is active, not passive.

When will You comfort me?

• The core question—“When?”—reveals a heart certain that comfort is coming, yet struggling with delay. Psalm 6:3 records David’s similar cry, “My soul is deeply distressed. How long, O LORD, how long?”

• He seeks the specific comfort God alone provides: “May Your unfailing love be my comfort” (Psalm 119:76).

• Isaiah 40:1 opens with God’s answer: “Comfort, comfort My people.” This finds ultimate fulfillment in Christ, the “God of all comfort” (2 Corinthians 1:3–4), who personally enters our suffering.

summary

Psalm 119:82 captures the honest tension between confident faith and weary waiting. Eyes grow dim, yet the believer keeps searching Scripture, praying persistently, and expecting God’s sure comfort. Such transparent longing is neither unbelief nor complaint but an act of trust that God’s promised help will arrive right on time.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(82) Mine eyes fail.--The failing of the eyes is here evidently to be understood of the effort of straining to catch or keep sight of a distant object, not, as so frequently in the Psalms (see Psalm 6:7, &c), from sickness or even grief. Comp.

"I would have broke my eye-strings, cracked them, but

To look upon him."--SHAKESPEARE: Cymbeline.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 82. - Mine eyes fail for thy Word. Yet even here his "eyes fail" - he has looked so long for the aid promised, and it has not come. Saying, When wilt thou comfort me? "Lord, how long?" is the constant cry of God's servants under affliction or persecution. When will relief come and the tyranny be overpast?
