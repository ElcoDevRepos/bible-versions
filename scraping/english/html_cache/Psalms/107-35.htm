He turns a desert into pools of water
This phrase highlights God's transformative power, often seen throughout Scripture. In biblical times, deserts symbolized desolation and hardship. The transformation of a desert into pools of water signifies renewal and divine intervention. This imagery is reminiscent of God's provision for the Israelites in the wilderness, where He provided water from a rock (Exodus 17:6). It also foreshadows the spiritual renewal found in Christ, who offers living water (John 4:10-14). The transformation of barren land into fertile ground is a recurring theme, symbolizing hope and restoration.

and a dry land into flowing springs
The transformation of dry land into flowing springs further emphasizes God's ability to bring life and abundance where there was once barrenness. This imagery is consistent with the prophetic visions of restoration found in Isaiah, where the desert is made to bloom (Isaiah 35:1-7). It also reflects the blessings of the Messianic age, where spiritual dryness is replaced with the outpouring of the Holy Spirit (Joel 2:28-29). The concept of flowing springs suggests continuous and abundant provision, aligning with the promise of eternal life through Jesus Christ (John 7:38).

Persons / Places / Events
1. God
The central figure in this verse, God is depicted as the one who transforms barren landscapes into fertile and life-giving environments. This highlights His sovereignty and power over creation.

2. Desert
Represents a place of barrenness and desolation. In the biblical context, deserts often symbolize spiritual dryness or times of trial and testing.

3. Pools of Water
Symbolize abundance, life, and refreshment. Water is often used in the Bible as a metaphor for spiritual renewal and the presence of God.

4. Dry Land
Similar to the desert, it signifies a lack of resources and vitality. It can also represent the human condition without God's intervention.

5. Flowing Springs
Indicate continuous provision and sustenance. Springs are sources of life and are often associated with God's blessings and the Holy Spirit.
Teaching Points
God's Transformative Power
God has the power to change any situation, no matter how desolate or hopeless it may seem. Just as He transforms deserts into pools of water, He can bring renewal and life to our spiritual deserts.

Spiritual Renewal
Just as physical water is essential for life, spiritual renewal through God's Word and Spirit is essential for our spiritual vitality. We should seek His presence to refresh and sustain us.

Trust in God's Provision
In times of spiritual dryness or difficulty, trust that God can and will provide. He is faithful to turn our dry lands into flowing springs, meeting our needs in His perfect timing.

Symbolism of Water
Water is a powerful symbol of cleansing, renewal, and life. Reflect on how God uses the imagery of water throughout Scripture to convey His promises and blessings.

Hope in God's Promises
This verse reminds us of the hope we have in God's promises. No matter the circumstances, we can have confidence that God is able to bring about transformation and fulfillment.

What Does Psalm 107:35 Mean
He turns a desert

• The subject is unmistakably the LORD, the covenant-keeping God who actively intervenes in His creation.

• “Turns” signals decisive, sovereign action—He is not advising the desert; He is transforming it.

• A “desert” pictures lifeless barrenness, but also real wilderness that ancient Israel knew well.

• Cross references highlight God’s delight in reversing hopeless landscapes:

– Isaiah 35:1-2, “The wilderness and dry land will be glad… it will blossom abundantly.”

– Isaiah 41:18, “I will open rivers on barren heights.”

– Exodus 17:6, water from the rock at Horeb, showing literal provision in a literal desert.

into pools of water

• The reversal is complete—parched ground now holds “pools,” sustained bodies of life-giving water.

• These pools speak to:

– Physical supply: God meets tangible needs (Psalm 78:15-16).

– Spiritual refreshment: Jesus later echoes this in John 7:37-38, promising rivers of living water to believers.

• Cross references:

– Numbers 21:16-18, the well at Beer.

– Revelation 7:17, “fountains of living water,” showing the theme carries to eternity.

and a dry land

• “Dry land” emphasizes what was once cracked, unproductive, and without hope of growth.

• God’s people often found themselves in such places geographically (Nehemiah 9:15) and spiritually (Psalm 63:1).

• He allows seasons of dryness to reveal His power when He ends them.

• Cross references:

– Deuteronomy 8:15-16, God leads through “thirsty ground” to humble and test.

– Jeremiah 2:6, Israel remembers the land of “drought and darkness.”

into flowing springs

• “Flowing” pictures perpetual motion—water that doesn’t stagnate or run out.

• Springs suggest an internal source; the supply is self-renewing because it comes from God Himself.

• Practical takeaways:

– Expect God’s provision to keep moving, refreshing, and spreading.

– What He starts, He sustains (Philippians 1:6).

• Cross references:

– Psalm 84:6, “passing through the Valley of Baca, they make it a spring.”

– Isaiah 58:11, “You will be like a well-watered garden, like a spring whose waters never fail.”

summary

Psalm 107:35 celebrates the LORD’s power to transform utter barrenness into abundant life. He literally turned Israel’s wilderness wanderings into occasions of miraculous water, and He still turns spiritual deserts into places of overflowing grace. The verse calls believers to trust the God who not only supplies water but creates springs that never stop flowing.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) Standing water.--Or, a pool of water. (See Isaiah 35:7; Isaiah 41:18-19; Isaiah 42:15.)

The dependence of this psalm on these passages in Isaiah is indubitable. But the images are employed in a different manner. The prophet only thinks of the joy of returning Israel (Psalm 107:39-41). But here the thought is that in the reverses of fortune, which even the chosen nation must be prepared for, God will intervene to protect and save. But the construction is very awkward, owing to the mode in which, in Psalm 107:40, two clauses from Job 12:21; Job 12:24 are introduced. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - He turneth the wilderness into a standing water; rather, a wilderness (comp. Isaiah 35:7; Isaiah 41:18). And dry ground into water springs. The entire verse is antithetical to ver. 33.
