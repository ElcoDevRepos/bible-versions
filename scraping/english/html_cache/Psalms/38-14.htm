I am like a man who cannot hear
This phrase reflects a state of helplessness and vulnerability. In the biblical context, being unable to hear can symbolize a lack of understanding or awareness. In ancient Israel, hearing was often associated with obedience and wisdom, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:4, "Hear, O Israel." The psalmist's comparison to a man who cannot hear may indicate a sense of isolation or being cut off from divine guidance. This imagery can also be connected to the suffering servant in Isaiah 53, who silently endures affliction, prefiguring Christ's own silent suffering during His trial and crucifixion (Matthew 27:12-14).

whose mouth offers no reply
This phrase suggests a deliberate choice to remain silent in the face of accusation or adversity. In the cultural context of the ancient Near East, silence could be a sign of wisdom or resignation. Proverbs 17:28 states, "Even a fool is considered wise if he keeps silent." The psalmist's silence may reflect a trust in God to be his defender, as seen in Exodus 14:14, "The LORD will fight for you; you need only to be still." This also parallels Jesus' response during His trial, where He chose not to defend Himself against false charges (Mark 15:3-5), fulfilling the prophecy of the suffering servant who "did not open his mouth" (Isaiah 53:7).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed to King David, this psalm is a penitential psalm where David expresses deep sorrow for his sins and the resulting physical and emotional suffering.

2. Enemies
The psalm references adversaries who take advantage of David's weakened state, highlighting the theme of persecution and betrayal.

3. God
The ultimate recipient of David's plea for mercy and healing, emphasizing the relationship between the psalmist and the divine.
Teaching Points
The Power of Silence
Silence can be a powerful response in the face of accusation or conflict, allowing God to be our defender.

Trust in God's Justice
By not responding to his enemies, David demonstrates trust in God's ultimate justice and timing.

Humility in Suffering
David's silence reflects a humble acceptance of his situation, recognizing his need for God's mercy and intervention.

Self-Control and Restraint
Practicing restraint in speech can prevent further conflict and demonstrate spiritual maturity.

Prayerful Dependence
In times of distress, turning to God in prayer rather than reacting impulsively can lead to peace and resolution.

What Does Psalm 38:14 Mean
I am like a man who cannot hear

• David pictures himself choosing not to take in the taunts of his enemies (see the immediate context in Psalm 38:12–13).

• This is not physical deafness but deliberate restraint: he turns a “deaf ear” so slander cannot lodge in his heart (compare Proverbs 26:4).

• Scripture often commends this guarded listening; James 1:19 urges believers to be “quick to listen, slow to speak,” which requires filtering ungodly noise.

• By refusing to absorb accusations, David trusts the Lord to judge (Psalm 62:1,5).

whose mouth offers no reply

• Silence becomes an act of faith; David will not defend himself in the flesh. The pattern foreshadows Christ, who “did not open His mouth” under false charges (Isaiah 53:7; 1 Peter 2:23).

• Remaining quiet guards against sinning with the tongue (Psalm 39:1) and keeps the sufferer from escalating conflict (Proverbs 15:1).

• Such silence signals submission to God’s timing—He alone vindicates the righteous (Psalm 37:5–6).

summary

Psalm 38:14 portrays the believer’s deliberate deafness to malicious voices and disciplined silence before attackers. By refusing both to hear slander and to retaliate, David demonstrates confident reliance on God’s justice, modeling the same trust perfected in Jesus.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Thus I was as a man that heareth not, and in whose mouth are no reproofs; i.e. I was like a man who is unable to answer, to reprove, or rebuke an adversary. So great was my self-restraint.
