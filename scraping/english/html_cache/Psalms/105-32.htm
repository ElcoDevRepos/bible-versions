He gave them hail for rain
This phrase refers to one of the plagues that God sent upon Egypt as described in Exodus 9:22-26. The transformation of rain into hail was a direct act of divine intervention, showcasing God's power over nature. Hail, a destructive force, was used as a tool of judgment against the Egyptians, who worshipped a pantheon of gods, including those believed to control the weather. This act demonstrated the supremacy of the God of Israel over the Egyptian deities. The plague of hail was particularly significant because it was not a natural occurrence in Egypt, a land known for its dry climate, thus emphasizing the miraculous nature of the event.

with lightning throughout their land
The inclusion of lightning alongside hail further underscores the severity and supernatural aspect of this plague. Lightning, often associated with divine presence and power in the Bible, served to intensify the fear and chaos among the Egyptians. This imagery is reminiscent of other biblical instances where God’s presence is accompanied by thunder and lightning, such as at Mount Sinai (Exodus 19:16-19). The phrase "throughout their land" indicates the widespread impact of the plague, affecting all of Egypt and leaving no part untouched. This comprehensive judgment highlights God's control over the entire land and serves as a precursor to the ultimate deliverance of the Israelites, foreshadowing the power of Christ to bring salvation and judgment.

Persons / Places / Events
1. God
The central figure in this verse, God is the one who exercises His power over nature to bring about hail instead of rain.

2. Egypt
The land where this event took place, as part of the plagues that God sent to persuade Pharaoh to release the Israelites from slavery.

3. Moses and Aaron
Though not mentioned directly in this verse, they were God's chosen leaders to confront Pharaoh and lead the Israelites out of Egypt.

4. Pharaoh
The ruler of Egypt who hardened his heart against God’s commands, leading to the plagues.

5. The Israelites
God's chosen people, who were enslaved in Egypt and were the beneficiaries of God's deliverance through these miraculous signs.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nature
This verse reminds us that God has complete control over the natural world. He can use it to fulfill His purposes and demonstrate His power.

Judgment and Mercy
The plagues, including the hail, were acts of judgment against Egypt but also acts of mercy towards the Israelites, as they were steps towards their liberation. This duality is a reminder of God's justice and His compassion.

The Hardness of Heart
Pharaoh's refusal to heed God's warnings led to the plagues. This serves as a warning against hardening our hearts to God's voice and commands.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The plagues were part of God's plan to fulfill His promise to Abraham to deliver his descendants. This encourages us to trust in God's faithfulness to His promises.

What Does Psalm 105:32 Mean
He gave them

– “He” points unmistakably to the LORD Himself. Scripture presents Him not as a distant observer but as the direct Initiator of these events (Exodus 9:23–24; Isaiah 45:7).

– In Psalm 105 the psalmist is recounting the historical plagues on Egypt, emphasizing God’s faithfulness to His covenant people (Psalm 105:8–15).

– By stating that God “gave” the plague, the text underscores His sovereign authority over nature; creation obeys His commands just as surely as Israel must trust His promises (Job 37:6; Jeremiah 10:13).

– This act of giving also reveals divine justice—Pharaoh’s hardness met with God’s righteous response (Exodus 7:13; Romans 9:17).

hail for rain

– “He gave them hail for rain” swaps what should have been a life-giving blessing for a destructive judgment. Instead of nourishing showers, Egypt received pounding hailstones (Exodus 9:18–26).

• Hail signals both power and precision: God can withhold or redirect the normal cycles of weather to achieve moral purposes (Joshua 10:11; Haggai 1:10–11).

• The plague devastated crops and livestock, exposing the futility of Egypt’s gods—especially those linked to fertility and weather (Numbers 33:4; Isaiah 19:1).

• For the believing reader, the literal event becomes a reminder that obedience invites blessing, while rebellion courts loss (Deuteronomy 28:12, 24).

with lightning throughout their land

– The phrase adds, “with lightning throughout their land,” heightening the terror. Lightning (or fire flashing amid the hail) emphasized that the storm was no ordinary meteorological occurrence (Exodus 9:24).

• The entire land, from royal palace to peasant field, felt the strike; no corner of Egypt could claim immunity (Psalm 78:48).

• Lightning often depicts God’s holiness and might (Psalm 29:7; Revelation 4:5). Here it exposes Egypt’s idols and magnifies the LORD’s unmatched glory.

• For Israel, watching from Goshen, the plague reinforced that the covenant-keeping God distinguishes His people (Exodus 9:26), emboldening their faith for the coming exodus.

summary

Psalm 105:32 recounts the seventh plague in two vivid strokes—hail replacing rain and lightning ripping through the land—to showcase the LORD’s sovereign power, righteous judgment, and faithful guardianship of His covenant people. The verse invites us to trust the same God who can turn the forces of nature to fulfill His promises, comfort His own, and confront hardened hearts.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 32. - He gave them hail for rain (see Exodus 9:23). And flaming fire in their land; i.e. lightning, described in Exodus 9:23 as "fire that ran along upon the ground."
