Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He who dwells in the shelter of the Most High
This phrase emphasizes the importance of residing or living in a place of divine protection. The term "dwells" suggests a continuous, habitual action, indicating a lifestyle of remaining in God's presence. The "shelter" implies a place of refuge and safety, reminiscent of the cities of refuge in ancient Israel where individuals could find protection. The "Most High" is a title for God, El Elyon, emphasizing His supreme authority and sovereignty over all creation. This title is used in Genesis 14:18-20, where Melchizedek, king of Salem, blesses Abram, highlighting God's supreme power and blessing.

will abide in the shadow of the Almighty
"Abide" suggests a settled, enduring presence, indicating a promise of ongoing protection and care. The "shadow" symbolizes protection and comfort, much like the shade provided by a large tree or rock in a desert landscape, offering relief from the harsh sun. The "Almighty" is translated from the Hebrew "Shaddai," a name for God that conveys His omnipotence and sufficiency. This name is used in Genesis 17:1 when God appears to Abram, reinforcing His power and ability to fulfill His promises. The imagery of a shadow also connects to the protective wings of God mentioned in Psalm 91:4, where He is described as a refuge and fortress. This phrase can be seen as a type of Christ, who offers ultimate protection and rest for believers, as seen in Matthew 11:28-30, where Jesus invites the weary to find rest in Him.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Most High (Elyon)
This title for God emphasizes His supreme authority and sovereignty over all creation. It is a reminder of His exalted position above all other powers and deities.

2. The Almighty (Shaddai)
Another name for God, highlighting His omnipotence and ability to provide and protect. This name is often associated with God's power to sustain and nurture.

3. The Dweller
Refers to the individual who chooses to live in close relationship with God, seeking His presence and protection.

4. The Shelter
Symbolizes God's protection and refuge, a place of safety and security for those who trust in Him.

5. The Shadow
Represents God's presence and the comfort and protection it provides, much like the shade offers relief from the harshness of the sun.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Dwelling
To "dwell" implies a continuous, intentional choice to remain in God's presence. It is not a temporary visit but a permanent residence in His shelter.

Understanding God's Names
Recognizing God as the Most High and Almighty helps us trust in His supreme power and ability to protect and provide for us.

Seeking God's Presence
Actively seek to live in the shadow of the Almighty through prayer, worship, and meditation on His Word. This is where true peace and security are found.

Trust in Divine Protection
In times of fear or uncertainty, remember that God's shadow is a place of refuge. Trust in His promises and His ability to shield you from harm.

Living in Faith
Dwelling in God's shelter requires faith. It is a daily decision to trust in His protection and provision, even when circumstances seem daunting.

What Does Psalm 91:1 Mean
He who dwells

• The verb pictures a settled, ongoing choice to live close to God, not a temporary visit.

• Jesus echoes this call in John 15:4: “Remain in Me, and I will remain in you.”

• Psalm 15 portrays the kind of life that consistently “dwells” with the Lord—blameless, truthful, honoring God.

in the shelter

• God’s presence is portrayed as a safe lodge, a sturdy refuge.

• Psalm 27:5: “For in the day of trouble He will hide me in His shelter; He will conceal me under the cover of His tent.”

• Proverbs 18:10 reminds us, “The name of the LORD is a strong tower; the righteous run to it and are safe.”

• The believer is invited to trade self-reliance for divine protection.

of the Most High

• “Most High” exalts God above every earthly or spiritual power (Genesis 14:18-20; Daniel 4:34).

• By choosing to live under His roof, we acknowledge no threat is greater than our Host.

• Isaiah 57:15 affirms His supremacy and yet His nearness to the contrite.

will abide

• “Abide” extends the idea of dwelling into permanence—settling down for good.

• Psalm 23:6 closes with, “I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever.” The promise is lifelong and eternal.

• John 14:23 connects abiding with loving obedience: Father and Son “will come to him and make Our home with him.”

in the shadow

• In the heat of an ancient Near-Eastern day, shade means life. God offers cooling relief from pressure and judgment.

• Psalm 121:5-6 assures, “The LORD is your keeper… the sun will not strike you by day, nor the moon by night.”

• Isaiah 25:4 describes Him as “a refuge from the storm, a shade from the heat.” His nearness shields us from withering trials.

of the Almighty

• “Almighty” (Shaddai) stresses God’s unrivaled power to make every promise stand (Job 42:2; Revelation 1:8).

• The believer resting in His shadow enjoys protection backed by omnipotence—security nothing can pierce (Romans 8:31-39).

• Our confidence is not in faith itself but in the Might of the One who covers us.

summary

Psalm 91:1 paints a vivid portrait of unshakable security: the person who chooses to settle down under the roof of the supreme, all-powerful God discovers continual, refreshing protection. Because Scripture is true and literal, this promise stands for every believer who turns from self-reliance to daily, loving communion with the Most High.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1, 2) He . . . I.--The especial difficulty of this psalm, its abrupt changes of person, meets us at the outset. The text literally rendered, runs: "He sitting in the hiding place of the Most High; In the shadow of the Almighty he lodgeth, I say to Jehovah, My refuge and my fortress, My God, I trust in Him. The change in the last clause presents no particular difficulty, as many similar instances occur; but that from the third person, in the first verse, to the first, in the second, is very awkward, and many shifts have been adopted to get out of it. The best is to supply the word blessed: "Blessed is he that," &c[16] The different names for God employed here should be noticed. By their accumulation the poet makes the sum of assurance doubly sure.

[16] The omission of this word by a copyist would be very natural, from its confusion with the numerical heading of the psalm and the initial letter of the word that now begins it.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - He that dwelleth in the secret place of the Most High (comp. Psalm 90:1). He who has his thoughts always on God is said to "dwell in him" - to "make his abode with him" - to "sit down in his secret place." He has the Almighty, as it were, for his constant companion. Shall abide under the shadow of the Almighty. This is not "tautology." What is meant is that "loving faith on man's part shall be met by faithful love on God's part" (Kay). God will extend his "shadow" over the man who places himself under his protection.
