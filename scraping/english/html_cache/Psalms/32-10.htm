Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Many are the sorrows of the wicked
The phrase highlights the consequences of living in opposition to God's will. Throughout the Bible, the wicked are often depicted as those who reject God's commandments and pursue their own desires. This leads to a life filled with troubles and distress, as seen in Proverbs 13:21, which states, "Trouble pursues the sinner." The sorrows of the wicked can be understood as both the natural consequences of sinful actions and the divine judgment that comes upon those who live in rebellion against God. Historically, the Israelites experienced this during periods of disobedience, such as the Babylonian exile, which was a direct result of their turning away from God.

but loving devotion surrounds him who trusts in the LORD
This phrase contrasts the fate of the wicked with the blessings of those who place their trust in God. "Loving devotion" refers to God's steadfast love and mercy, often translated from the Hebrew word "chesed," which encompasses kindness, loyalty, and covenant faithfulness. Those who trust in the LORD experience His protection and favor, as seen in Psalm 91:4, where God is described as a refuge and fortress. This trust is not merely intellectual assent but involves a deep, personal reliance on God, akin to the faith exemplified by Abraham, who "believed the LORD, and He credited it to him as righteousness" (Genesis 15:6). The surrounding of loving devotion suggests a comprehensive protection and blessing, reminiscent of the way God surrounded the Israelites with His presence during their journey through the wilderness. This phrase also points to the ultimate fulfillment in Jesus Christ, who embodies God's loving devotion and offers salvation to all who trust in Him.

Teaching Points
The Consequences of Wickedness
The verse highlights the inevitable sorrows that accompany a life lived in opposition to God. This serves as a warning and a call to repentance.

The Blessing of Trust
Trusting in the LORD is not just a passive state but an active reliance on His character and promises, resulting in experiencing His loving devotion.

Understanding Chesed
God's loving devotion, or "chesed," is a profound expression of His covenantal love, which is both steadfast and enduring, offering comfort and assurance to believers.

The Protective Nature of God's Love
Just as a shield surrounds a warrior, God's loving devotion encircles those who trust in Him, providing protection and peace amidst life's challenges.

Encouragement to Trust
Believers are encouraged to cultivate a deeper trust in God, knowing that His loving devotion is a constant presence in their lives.

What Does Psalm 32:10 Mean
Many

• The verse opens with a spotlight on quantity: “many.” Trouble for those opposed to God is not occasional but plentiful, echoing Genesis 6:5 where evil was described as “great” on the earth.

• Psalm 73:18–19 illustrates how plentiful woes accumulate until sudden ruin comes.

• The word alerts us that what follows is not minor or temporary—it is a repeating pattern.

are the sorrows

• “Sorrows” portrays life marked by grief, restlessness, and inner torment (Psalm 38:3–4).

• Isaiah 57:20–21 compares the wicked to a tossed sea, “whose waters cast up mire and mud… ‘There is no peace,’ says my God, ‘for the wicked.’”

• These sorrows are both self-inflicted (Galatians 6:7–8) and God-allowed as righteous judgment (Romans 1:24–32).

• Without repentance, sorrow deepens into eternal separation (Matthew 25:46).

of the wicked

• “Wicked” identifies those who live in defiance of God’s commands (Proverbs 11:3).

• Wickedness is not merely notorious crime; it is any willful independence from the Lord (James 4:17).

• Proverbs 13:15 notes that “the way of the treacherous is hard,” explaining why sorrow naturally clings to sin.

• The contrast of “wicked” versus “trusts in the LORD” places everyone into one of two camps (Matthew 7:13–14).

but loving devotion surrounds

• “But” signals a dramatic reversal. Though sorrows pursue the wicked, God’s loyal love actively encircles the believer (Psalm 5:12: “You surround the righteous with favor as a shield”).

• “Loving devotion” (steadfast love) is covenant language—God’s unbreakable commitment (Lamentations 3:22–23).

• To “surround” suggests protection on every side, reminiscent of 2 Kings 6:17 where angelic hosts protect Elisha.

• Instead of fleeting happiness, the trusting soul enjoys a climate of God’s persistent kindness (Psalm 23:6).

him who trusts in the LORD

• Trust is wholehearted reliance, not mere acknowledgment (Proverbs 3:5–6).

• Saving faith results in ongoing dependence—picture Psalm 37:3, “Trust in the LORD and do good.”

• Jeremiah 17:7 promises, “Blessed is the man who trusts in the LORD, whose confidence is in Him.”

• Such trust replaces self-reliance with surrender, inviting the Lord to bear the weight of life’s burdens (1 Peter 5:7).

• The outcome: sorrows may touch believers, but they never dominate; they are hemmed in by God’s loyal love (Romans 8:35–39).

summary

Psalm 32:10 draws a stark line: constant grief trails those who persist in rebellion, while God’s faithful love wraps round every person who places full confidence in Him. The difference is not luck or circumstance but posture toward the LORD. One path multiplies pain; the other experiences protective, surrounding devotion now and forever.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - Many sorrows shall be to the wicked. A further warning to those addressed in the preceding verse. The LXX. emphasize this by substituting for the generic "sorrows" the specific μάστιγες, "lashes," the usual punishment of the horse and mule. But he that trusteth in the Lord, mercy shall compass him about (comp. Deuteronomy 32:10).
