Remember Your word to Your servant
This phrase emphasizes the psalmist's plea for God to recall His promises. In biblical context, "word" often refers to God's promises or commandments. The psalmist identifies as God's "servant," highlighting a relationship of obedience and submission. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, as seen in Exodus 19:5-6, where Israel is called to be a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. The concept of God remembering is anthropomorphic, as God is omniscient, but it signifies a call for divine action based on His faithfulness.

upon which You have given me hope
The psalmist expresses reliance on God's word as the foundation of hope. In the Hebrew Bible, hope is often linked to trust in God's faithfulness and future deliverance, as seen in Lamentations 3:21-24, where hope is renewed by recalling God's steadfast love. This hope is not wishful thinking but a confident expectation rooted in God's character and promises. Theologically, this reflects the assurance believers have in God's unchanging nature, as echoed in Hebrews 6:18-19, where hope is described as an anchor for the soul.

Persons / Places / Events

Teaching Points
The Power of God's Word
God's word is not just a historical document but a living source of hope and guidance for believers today.

The Importance of Remembrance
Just as the psalmist asks God to remember His word, believers are called to remember and meditate on God's promises in their daily lives.

Hope Anchored in Divine Promises
True hope is found not in worldly assurances but in the steadfast promises of God, which are unchanging and reliable.

Prayer as a Means of Connection
The psalmist's prayer is a model for believers to bring their concerns and hopes before God, trusting in His faithfulness.

Living as Servants of God
The identity of the believer as a servant of God calls for a life of obedience and trust in His word.

What Does Psalm 119:49 Mean
Remember

• The psalmist appeals to God’s faithfulness, asking Him to “remember,” just as He did in Psalm 106:4: “Remember me, O LORD, in Your favor to Your people.”

• This is not reminding a forgetful God; it is a covenant plea, echoing Exodus 2:24 where “God heard their groaning, and God remembered His covenant.”

• The request is confident, trusting the unchanging nature of the Lord (Malachi 3:6).

Your word

• The focus is God’s revealed promise—“The words of the LORD are flawless” (Psalm 12:6).

• His word is settled forever (Psalm 119:89), giving a solid foundation for every petition.

• Scripture never fails (Joshua 21:45), so the psalmist rests his case entirely on what God has spoken.

to Your servant

• By calling himself “Your servant,” the writer acknowledges God’s authority and his own submission (Psalm 119:17).

• Servant language reminds us of Samuel’s openness: “Speak, LORD, for Your servant is listening” (1 Samuel 3:9).

• The intimacy of the relationship allows boldness (Hebrews 4:16) while maintaining humility.

upon which You have given me hope

• God’s promise is the ground of hope—“I wait for the LORD; in His word I put my hope” (Psalm 130:5).

• Hope birthed by Scripture produces endurance (Romans 15:4) and anchors the soul (Hebrews 6:19).

• The psalmist’s confidence mirrors Lamentations 3:21-24: “This I recall to mind, and therefore I have hope…Great is Your faithfulness.”

summary

Psalm 119:49 shows a servant confidently asking God to act in line with His dependable word. The plea rests on God’s remembrance, the perfection of His promise, a humble servant-heart, and the living hope that flows from Scripture.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 49. - Remember the word unto thy servant; i.e. "the word spoken to thy servant." Some special Divine communication made to the writer is perhaps meant. Upon which (rather, because) thou hast caused me to hope (comp. vers. 74, 81, 147).
