O LORD my God
This phrase emphasizes a personal relationship with God, highlighting the covenantal nature of the relationship between the psalmist and the divine. The use of "LORD" (YHWH) reflects the sacred name of God, which is central to the Hebrew faith. This invocation is a plea for divine attention and justice, setting the tone for the psalm as a prayer for vindication. It echoes the intimate language found in other psalms, such as Psalm 23:1, where God is depicted as a personal shepherd.

if I have done this
The psalmist is making a conditional statement, suggesting a self-examination or a call for divine judgment based on his actions. This reflects the biblical principle of accountability before God, as seen in passages like Job 31, where Job lists potential sins and calls for God to judge him if he is guilty. The phrase implies a confidence in innocence, yet an openness to divine scrutiny.

if injustice is on my hands
Here, the psalmist refers to the concept of "injustice," which in the Hebrew context often relates to social and moral wrongs, such as deceit or oppression. The imagery of "hands" symbolizes actions and deeds, a common biblical metaphor for one's work or behavior (e.g., Psalm 24:4). This phrase connects to the broader biblical theme of justice, a key attribute of God's character, as seen in Deuteronomy 32:4. The psalmist's appeal suggests a desire for God to act as a righteous judge, a role that is ultimately fulfilled in the person of Jesus Christ, who embodies perfect justice and righteousness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of this psalm, David is seeking God's judgment and vindication. He is expressing his innocence and asking God to examine his heart and actions.

2. Cush the Benjamite
Although not much is known about Cush, he is mentioned in the title of the psalm. He may have been an adversary of David, possibly spreading false accusations against him.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, whom David addresses directly, seeking divine justice and intervention.

4. Injustice
The central issue David is addressing, as he pleads his innocence before God.

5. The Context of Persecution
David often faced false accusations and persecution, which is the backdrop for this psalm.
Teaching Points
Self-Examination Before God
Like David, believers should regularly invite God to search their hearts and reveal any hidden sin or injustice.

Trust in Divine Justice
Trust that God is the ultimate judge who sees all and will vindicate the righteous in His perfect timing.

Responding to False Accusations
When faced with false accusations, respond with integrity and leave vindication to God, as David did.

The Importance of a Clear Conscience
Maintaining a clear conscience before God and others is crucial for a life of integrity and peace.

Prayer as a Means of Seeking Justice
Use prayer as a way to seek God's justice and express trust in His righteous judgment.

What Does Psalm 7:3 Mean
O LORD my God

“​O LORD my God…” (Psalm 7:3)

- David begins with the covenant name “LORD” (YHWH) joined to “my God,” showing both reverence and intimacy. He is not appealing to a distant deity but to the God who has personally bound Himself to His people (Exodus 3:15; Psalm 18:2).

- By using “my,” David publicly acknowledges God’s ownership of him and his dependence on God’s character for vindication (Psalm 23:1; 2 Samuel 22:3).

- The address reminds us that when falsely accused, believers should run first to the Lord who knows the heart (Jeremiah 17:10; Hebrews 4:13) rather than to human courts or public opinion.

if I have done this

“…if I have done this…” (Psalm 7:3)

- David willingly invites God to investigate the specific charge brought against him, likely the slander of Cush the Benjamite (title of the psalm).

- He models the humble confidence of a clear conscience (Acts 24:16; 1 Corinthians 4:4).

- This openness echoes, “Search me, O God, and know my heart” (Psalm 139:23-24). A believer who walks in integrity can welcome divine scrutiny without fear (Proverbs 10:9).

- By saying “this,” he identifies a concrete accusation, not a vague sense of guilt, showing that confession and repentance deal with actual sin, not imagined faults (1 John 1:9).

if injustice is on my hands

“…if injustice is on my hands” (Psalm 7:3)

- “Hands” symbolize actions. David denies having committed a wrong that would stain his hands with guilt (Psalm 24:3-4; 26:6).

- In the law, innocent blood on one’s hands demanded judgment (Deuteronomy 19:10). David asserts he has no such stain.

- He sets a standard for leaders: integrity must be visible in deeds, not merely words (2 Samuel 23:3; Proverbs 20:11).

- The phrase invites believers to examine whether any unjust act—cheating, slander, oppression—clings to their own hands (Micah 6:8; James 4:8).

summary

- David approaches God personally and reverently.

- He invites divine examination of the specific charge against him.

- He affirms clean hands, confident that God will vindicate righteousness and expose falsehood.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) This--i.e., this with which I am charged--the Benjamite's slander.

If there be iniquity.--A comparison with 1Samuel 24:12-13, and still more 1Samuel 26:18, shows how closely this psalm is connected with the two notorious instances of David's magnanimous and generous conduct towards Saul.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - O Lord my God, if I have done this; i.e. "this which is laid to my charge." The general charge against David in Saul's lifetime was that he "sought the king's hurt" (1 Samuel 24:9). Afterwards he was accused of being "a bloody man" (2 Samuel 16:8) - the death of Ishbosheth, and perhaps of others, being regarded as his work. If there be iniquity in my hands. If, i.e., I have committed any criminal act, if any definite offence can be charged against me. Human weakness and imperfection David does not mean to deny, but, like Job, he maintains in a certain qualified sense his righteousness.
