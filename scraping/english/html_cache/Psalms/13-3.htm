Consider me and respond, O LORD my God.
This plea reflects a deep personal relationship with God, emphasizing the psalmist's reliance on divine intervention. The use of "consider" suggests a desire for God's attention and care, echoing the intimate language found in other psalms (e.g., Psalm 25:16). The phrase "O LORD my God" underscores a covenant relationship, reminiscent of God's promises to the patriarchs (Genesis 17:7). The psalmist's request for a response indicates a belief in God's active involvement in human affairs, aligning with the biblical narrative of God hearing and answering prayers (1 Kings 8:28-30).

Give light to my eyes,
This metaphorical expression signifies a request for renewed vitality and understanding. In ancient Near Eastern culture, "light" often symbolized life and divine favor (Job 33:30). The eyes are considered the windows to the soul, and their brightness is associated with health and well-being (Proverbs 15:30). This plea can be seen as a request for spiritual enlightenment and guidance, paralleling the illumination provided by God's Word (Psalm 119:105). The imagery also foreshadows the New Testament revelation of Jesus as the "light of the world" (John 8:12).

lest I sleep in death,
The fear of death is a common theme in the Psalms, reflecting the human condition and the reality of mortality. "Sleep" as a euphemism for death is found throughout Scripture (Daniel 12:2, John 11:11-14), indicating a temporary state before resurrection. This phrase highlights the psalmist's desperation and the urgency of divine intervention. It also points to the hope of deliverance and eternal life, themes fulfilled in the resurrection of Jesus Christ (1 Corinthians 15:20-22). The plea underscores the belief in God's power over life and death, a central tenet of biblical faith.

The phrase "lest I sleep in death" underscores the severity of David's despair, reminding us that spiritual desolation can feel like death, but God's presence brings life.

What Does Psalm 13:3 Mean
Consider me

David begins with a simple, aching plea for God’s attention: “Consider me” (Psalm 25:18; 31:7).

• The psalmist is convinced that the Lord already sees all things (2 Chronicles 16:9), yet he still invites that gaze to rest on him personally.

• This invitation reveals humble transparency—David is willing for God to look directly at his pain, confusion, and weakness (Psalm 139:23–24).

• For believers today, the call to be “considered” by God is an act of faith that He is neither distant nor indifferent (Hebrews 4:15–16).

And respond

“and respond” echoes earlier cries: “Answer me when I call, O God of my righteousness” (Psalm 4:1).

• David is not satisfied with mere observation; he longs for divine action (Psalm 55:16–17).

• Scripture repeatedly assures us that the Lord answers prayer in His perfect timing and way (Jeremiah 33:3; 1 John 5:14–15).

• This line models persistent, expectant petition rather than passive resignation.

O LORD my God

Calling God “LORD” (the covenant name) and “my God” personalizes the request (Exodus 3:15; Psalm 23:1).

• The covenant title affirms God’s unchanging faithfulness (Deuteronomy 7:9).

• The possessive “my” expresses intimate relationship—David stands on promises that belong to God’s people (Psalm 118:28).

• This combination turns a general cry into a child’s appeal to his Father (Romans 8:15).

Give light to my eyes

Light is often a picture of vitality, insight, and hope.

• Physically, Jonathan’s “eyes brightened” after tasting honey (1 Samuel 14:27), showing renewed strength.

• Spiritually, God “lights my lamp” (Psalm 18:28) and opens “the eyes of your heart” (Ephesians 1:18).

• David seeks renewed clarity—strength to keep going, wisdom to interpret his circumstances, and faith to see beyond the darkness (Psalm 119:105).

Lest I sleep in death

Without God’s intervention, David fears utter collapse.

• “Sleep” can picture death (John 11:11–14; 1 Thessalonians 4:14), a fate he sees looming if God withholds help.

• Similar pleas appear in Psalm 6:4–5 and 30:9, where the psalmist reasons that the living can praise God, the dead cannot.

• The phrase highlights human frailty while underscoring confidence that only the Lord can deliver from the final enemy (Psalm 56:13; 2 Corinthians 1:9–10).

summary

Psalm 13:3 captures a believer’s urgent, personal cry for God’s attention, action, and renewal. David appeals to the covenant-keeping Lord, asking Him to notice, answer, and revive his failing spirit so that death will not overtake him. The verse invites us to approach God the same way—honestly, expectantly, and with full confidence that the One who sees also saves and restores.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Lighten.--Literally, give light to my eyes that I may not go to sleep in death, i.e., go to sleep and never wake; "sleep unto death," as the LXX. (Comp. for the nature of the fear, Psalm 6:5; and for the form of expression, 1Samuel 14:27; 1Samuel 14:29.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Consider and hear me, O Lord my God (comp. Psalm 5:1; Psalm 9:13; Psalm 141:1, etc.). David will not allow himself to be "forgotten;" he will recall himself to God's remembrance. "Consider - hear me," he says, "O Lord my God;" still "my God," although thou hast forgotten me, and therefore bound to "hear me." Lighten mine eyes. Not so much "enlighten me spiritually," as "cheer me up; put brightness into my eyes; revive me" (comp. Ezra 9:8, "Grace hath been showed from the Lord our God, to leave us a remnant to escape... that our God may lighten out eyes, and give us a little reviving"). Lest I sleep the sleep of death; literally, lest I sleep death. Death is compared to a sleep by Job (Job 11:12), Jeremiah (Jeremiah 51:39, 57), Daniel (Daniel 12:2), and here by David, in the Old Testament; and by our Lord (John 11:11-13) and St. Paul in the New (1 Corinthians 11:30; 1 Corinthians 15:51; 1 Thessalonians 4:14, 15). The external resemblance of a corpse to a sleeping person was the root of the metaphor, and we shall do wrong to conclude from its employment anything with respect to the psalmist's views concerning the real nature of death.
