Answer me, O LORD
This phrase is a direct appeal to God, reflecting a personal and urgent plea for divine intervention. The psalmist, traditionally understood to be David, often found himself in situations of distress and sought God's help. This reflects a common theme in the Psalms where the writer calls upon God in times of trouble (e.g., Psalm 4:1, Psalm 17:6). The use of "LORD" (YHWH) emphasizes the covenant relationship between God and His people, highlighting the psalmist's reliance on God's faithfulness.

for Your loving devotion is good
The term "loving devotion" is often translated from the Hebrew word "chesed," which encompasses God's steadfast love, mercy, and kindness. This phrase underscores the character of God as one who is inherently good and faithful to His promises. The psalmist appeals to God's nature, knowing that His loving devotion is a reliable foundation for hope and deliverance. This concept is echoed throughout the Old Testament, such as in Exodus 34:6-7, where God describes Himself as abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness.

turn to me
This request for God to "turn" suggests a desire for God's attention and favor. In the cultural context of the ancient Near East, turning one's face toward someone was a sign of favor and blessing, while turning away indicated displeasure or rejection. The psalmist seeks God's favorable presence, which is a recurring theme in the Psalms (e.g., Psalm 27:9, Psalm 80:3). This plea reflects a deep yearning for God's personal involvement in the psalmist's plight.

in keeping with Your great compassion
The appeal to God's "great compassion" highlights His merciful and gracious nature. Compassion, often linked with mercy, is a key attribute of God, as seen in passages like Lamentations 3:22-23, where God's mercies are described as new every morning. The psalmist's request is grounded in the understanding that God's compassion is vast and unfailing. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative that portrays God as a compassionate Father who cares for His children, a theme that finds its ultimate expression in the New Testament through the life and ministry of Jesus Christ, who embodied God's compassion (e.g., Matthew 9:36).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 69, David is expressing a deep cry for help and deliverance from his distress.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people.

3. Israel
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of the Psalm often reflects the struggles and deliverance of the nation of Israel.

4. Enemies/Oppressors
Implicit in the plea for deliverance are those who are causing distress to the psalmist.

5. Zion
Later in the Psalm, Zion is mentioned, symbolizing the place of God's dwelling and the hope of restoration.
Teaching Points
God's Loving Devotion
God's loving devotion (Hebrew: "chesed") is central to His character. It is a steadfast, covenantal love that assures us of His goodness and faithfulness.

The Power of Prayer
Like David, we are encouraged to cry out to God in our times of need, trusting in His compassion and willingness to answer.

God's Compassion
God's compassion (Hebrew: "rachamim") is great and abundant. It is a reminder that He is deeply moved by our suffering and is eager to bring relief.

Trust in God's Timing
While we may desire immediate answers, trusting in God's timing and His perfect will is crucial. His ways are higher than ours.

Hope in God's Character
Our hope is anchored in who God is—His goodness, compassion, and faithfulness. This gives us confidence to approach Him boldly.

What Does Psalm 69:16 Mean
Answer me, O LORD

• David calls on the covenant name of God, “LORD,” trusting that the One who revealed Himself to Moses (Exodus 3:14-15) still hears His people.

• The request “Answer me” shows confidence that God speaks and intervenes, as promised in Psalm 34:17, “The righteous cry out, and the LORD hears.”

• This appeal comes from a place of distress (see the whole psalm), yet it rests on the sure fact that the Lord’s ear is open to the cries of the faithful (Psalm 18:6; 1 Peter 3:12).

for Your loving devotion is good

• David grounds his plea in God’s “loving devotion” (often rendered “steadfast love” or “mercy”). Because that devotion is “good,” he can expect a good response (Psalm 63:3, “Your loving devotion is better than life”).

• Scripture repeatedly links God’s goodness with His faithful love: “Give thanks to the LORD, for He is good; His loving devotion endures forever” (Psalm 136:1).

• By appealing to this attribute, David shows that prayer is anchored not in our performance but in God’s proven character (Romans 2:4; Titus 3:4-5).

turn to me in keeping with Your great compassion

• “Turn to me” asks for God’s personal attention, echoing Psalm 25:16, “Turn to me and be gracious to me, for I am lonely and afflicted.”

• “Great compassion” reassures the sufferer that God’s mercy is vast and active (Psalm 103:13, “As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear Him”).

• The verse invites believers today to expect God’s nearness in hardship, just as Jeremiah trusted: “Because of the LORD’s loving devotion we are not consumed, for His compassions never fail” (Lamentations 3:22-23).

summary

Psalm 69:16 presents a threefold pattern for prayer: confidently call on the Lord to answer, appeal to His proven goodness and steadfast love, and welcome His personal, compassionate engagement. The verse assures us that the God who heard David remains faithful, good, and compassionate, ready to respond to all who cry out to Him in faith.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Hear me, O Lord, for thy loving kindness is good (comp. ver. 13). Turn unto me according to the multitude of thy tender mercies. The psalmist implies that God's face had been for some time turned away from him, and begs to be restored to favour.
