Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
They fully intend to cast him down from his lofty perch;
This phrase suggests a deliberate and malicious intent to overthrow someone in a position of power or honor. In the biblical context, this can be seen as a reflection of the constant threats faced by King David from his enemies, both external and internal. The "lofty perch" symbolizes a place of authority or divine favor, which David often occupied as God's anointed king. This imagery can also be connected to the broader biblical theme of the righteous being targeted by the wicked, as seen in other Psalms (e.g., Psalm 37:12-15). The phrase may also foreshadow the ultimate rejection and crucifixion of Jesus Christ, who was cast down by those who opposed Him despite His divine authority.

they delight in lies;
This part of the verse highlights the deceitful nature of David's adversaries. In the cultural and historical context of ancient Israel, truthfulness was a valued trait, and lying was seen as a serious moral failing. The delight in lies indicates a deep-seated corruption and a willingness to use falsehoods to achieve one's ends. This behavior is condemned throughout Scripture, as seen in Proverbs 6:16-19, where lying is listed among the things the Lord hates. The delight in lies can also be seen as a type of the false accusations and deceit faced by Jesus during His ministry and trial.

with their mouths they bless, but inwardly they curse.
This phrase describes the hypocrisy of David's enemies, who outwardly appear to offer blessings while secretly harboring ill intentions. This duplicity is a common theme in the Psalms, where the righteous often lament the deceitful nature of their foes (e.g., Psalm 55:21). In the broader biblical narrative, this behavior is reminiscent of the Pharisees and religious leaders during Jesus' time, who outwardly appeared righteous but were inwardly corrupt (Matthew 23:27-28). The contrast between outward speech and inward intent underscores the biblical principle that God judges the heart, not just external actions (1 Samuel 16:7).

Selah
The term "Selah" is often used in the Psalms as a pause for reflection or emphasis. While its exact meaning is uncertain, it serves as an invitation for the reader or listener to contemplate the preceding words deeply. In this context, "Selah" encourages reflection on the nature of deceit and the challenges faced by the righteous, prompting a meditation on God's justice and faithfulness in the face of human treachery.

What Does Psalm 62:4 Mean
They fully intend to cast him down from his lofty perch

David pictures enemies bent on removing a man—likely himself—from a place of honor God has granted.

• This is a deliberate, planned overthrow, not a momentary flare-up (Psalm 31:13; 1 Samuel 18:11).

• The “lofty perch” points to God-given position or calling; attacking it is ultimately an assault on God’s ordering (Psalm 3:3; Romans 13:1).

• Believers should expect similar hostility when standing where God has placed them (2 Timothy 3:12).

Still, the psalm’s earlier lines insist that salvation comes from God alone (Psalm 62:1–2), anchoring us amid plots to topple.

they delight in lies

The adversaries are not merely dishonest; they take pleasure in falsehood.

• Slander and distortion become sport (Psalm 52:3–4; Jeremiah 9:3–5).

• Lies about God’s servant aim to erode public confidence and personal peace (Nehemiah 6:6–9).

• Jesus identified Satan as “a liar and the father of lies” (John 8:44), so whenever people relish deceit they reflect his character, not God’s.

For us, loving truth and refusing to spread rumors is essential armor (Ephesians 6:14).

with their mouths they bless, but inwardly they curse

Hypocrisy crowns the assault: smooth words mask hostile hearts.

• “They speak peace to their neighbors, but evil is in their hearts” (Psalm 28:3).

• David heard flattering speech while swords were secretly drawn (Psalm 55:21).

• Jesus quoted Isaiah: “This people honors Me with their lips, but their hearts are far from Me” (Matthew 15:8).

• James warns that blessing God and cursing people from the same mouth should not be so (James 3:9–10).

The verse calls us to integrity: let private attitudes match public speech.

Selah

A purposeful pause.

• The word invites reflection—linger over the contrast between God’s steadfastness and human deceit (Psalm 46:11).

• It also signals worship: respond to the warning by renewing trust in the Lord (Psalm 3:2, 4, 8).

• For David’s hearers—and for us—Selah becomes a breath of faith before the psalm moves on to affirm unshakable confidence in God (Psalm 62:5–8).

summary

Psalm 62:4 exposes enemies who plot to dethrone, revel in lies, and wear a mask of blessing while cursing inside. The verse warns of the reality of such opposition, unmasks its satanic roots, and urges the faithful to rest in God’s protection, speak truth, and maintain integrity, pausing (Selah) to let these lessons sink deep.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) Their mouth.--Literally, his mouth. They bless each with his mouth, &c

Excellency.--Rather, height, carrying on the metaphor of preceding verse.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - They only consult to cast him down from his excellency; i.e. they have no other thought but this - to cast me down from my high station, while I have no other thought but to trust in God, and to look to him for support and protection (vers. 1, 2, 6). They delight in lies. Some indication of the "lies" circulated against David at this Time is given in 2 Samuel 15:3-5; 2 Samuel 16:7, 8. They bless with their mouth, but they curse inwardly; literally, they bless with his mouth, which may be explained as meaning either, "They bless, each of them, with his mouth" (Kay, Cheyne), or "they Bless through the mouth of their leader " - i.e. Ahithophel (Canon Cook).
