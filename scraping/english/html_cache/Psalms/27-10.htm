Though my father and mother forsake me
This phrase acknowledges the possibility of being abandoned by one's closest family members, which would have been a profound fear in ancient Israelite society where family ties were crucial for survival and identity. The cultural context of the time placed great importance on family loyalty and support, making the idea of parental abandonment particularly distressing. This phrase can also be seen as a hyperbolic expression to emphasize the depth of human rejection one might face. Biblically, it echoes the experiences of figures like Joseph, who was betrayed by his brothers, and David, who faced familial strife. It also foreshadows the ultimate rejection Jesus faced, as He was deserted by His followers and felt forsaken on the cross (Matthew 27:46).

the LORD will receive me
In contrast to human abandonment, this phrase highlights the unwavering faithfulness of God. The term "receive" suggests acceptance, care, and protection, indicating that God’s love and support are constant and reliable. This assurance is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, as seen in Deuteronomy 31:6, where God promises never to leave nor forsake His people. Theologically, this reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where God is portrayed as a divine parent who never abandons His children. This also points to the New Testament understanding of believers being adopted into God's family through Christ (Ephesians 1:5), emphasizing the security and belonging found in Him.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 27, David was the second king of Israel, known for his deep faith and reliance on God amidst trials.

2. Father and Mother
Symbolic of the closest human relationships, representing ultimate earthly support and care.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His faithfulness and eternal presence.

4. Forsaking
The act of abandonment or neglect, which in this context highlights the potential failure of human relationships.

5. Receiving
The act of taking in or accepting, illustrating God's unwavering acceptance and support.
Teaching Points
God's Unfailing Support
Even when human relationships fail, God's support is constant and reliable. Believers can find solace in His unwavering presence.

Divine Adoption
The concept of God receiving us highlights our adoption into His family, offering a sense of belonging and identity beyond earthly ties.

Trust in God's Faithfulness
Trusting in God's faithfulness is crucial, especially when facing abandonment or loneliness. His promises are sure and steadfast.

Prioritizing God's Relationship
While earthly relationships are important, our relationship with God should take precedence, as it is eternal and unchanging.

Healing from Abandonment
For those who have experienced abandonment, this verse offers hope and healing, reminding us that God never forsakes His children.

What Does Psalm 27:10 Mean
Though my father and mother

• David begins with the most secure earthly relationship imaginable—parents (cf. Exodus 20:12).

• By naming both father and mother, he communicates total familial support.

• Yet Scripture acknowledges even godly families can fracture (Genesis 37:28; Micah 7:6).

• In a fallen world, blood ties may fail, reminding us not to anchor ultimate hope in human bonds (Jeremiah 17:5).

forsake me

• “Forsake” paints a picture of deliberate abandonment (Job 19:13–14).

• David contemplates a worst-case scenario: utter rejection by those who should nurture him (Isaiah 49:15).

• The verse echoes earlier cries for help when companionship collapsed (Psalm 22:11; 41:9).

• Even if every earthly safety net tears, the believer is not left defenseless (2 Timothy 4:16–17).

the LORD

• David pivots to the covenant name Yahweh, the “I AM” who never changes (Exodus 3:14; Malachi 3:6).

• God’s faithfulness contrasts starkly with human fragility (Numbers 23:19).

• The Lord is repeatedly portrayed as Father to the fatherless and defender of the vulnerable (Psalm 68:5; Deuteronomy 10:18).

• Because His character is perfect love and steadfastness, He alone deserves our fullest trust (Psalm 118:8–9).

will receive me

• “Receive” conveys personal welcome and secure embrace (John 14:18).

• God doesn’t merely tolerate abandoned children; He gathers them to Himself (Isaiah 40:11; Hosea 11:4).

• This reception includes protection (Psalm 91:1), provision (Matthew 6:31–33), and permanence (John 10:27–29).

• David’s assurance foreshadows the gospel promise that believers are adopted into God’s family through Christ (Romans 8:15; Ephesians 1:5).

summary

Even if the strongest human relationship fails, God’s covenant love never does. Parents may abandon, but the Lord stands ready to welcome, protect, and keep His children forever. Our deepest security rests not in people’s faithfulness but in the unchanging, receiving heart of God.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - When my father and my mother forsake me, then the Lord will take me up. We are not to gather from this that David's father and mother had forsaken him. They were probably dead at the time of his flight from Absalom. What David means is that, even if forsaken by his nearest and dearest, he would not be forsaken by God. The expression is proverbial.
