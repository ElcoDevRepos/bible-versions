O LORD my God
This phrase emphasizes a personal relationship with God, highlighting the covenantal nature of the relationship between God and His people. The use of "LORD" (Yahweh) signifies the God of Israel, the one true God who revealed Himself to Moses in the burning bush (Exodus 3:14). The possessive "my God" indicates a personal faith and trust in God, reflecting the intimate connection that believers are encouraged to have with Him. This phrase is a reminder of the personal nature of prayer and worship, as seen throughout the Psalms.

I cried to You for help
The act of crying out to God is a common theme in the Psalms, reflecting a deep dependence on God in times of distress. This cry is not just a casual request but an earnest plea, indicating the psalmist's desperation and faith that God is the only source of deliverance. This mirrors the cries of the Israelites in Egypt (Exodus 2:23-25) and the consistent call for divine intervention throughout the Old Testament. It underscores the belief that God hears and responds to the prayers of His people.

and You healed me
Healing in the biblical context often refers to both physical and spiritual restoration. The psalmist acknowledges God's power to heal, which is a recurring theme in Scripture, pointing to God's compassion and mercy. This healing can be seen as a type of the ultimate healing brought by Jesus Christ, who healed many during His earthly ministry (Matthew 8:16-17) and offers spiritual healing through salvation. The healing here can also be understood as a metaphor for deliverance from distress or danger, reflecting God's ability to restore and renew His people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 30, King David, is expressing gratitude for God's deliverance and healing. This psalm is traditionally associated with the dedication of the temple, though it reflects personal deliverance.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people. David calls upon Yahweh for help and healing.

3. Healing
The event of healing is both physical and spiritual, indicating God's intervention in David's life. It reflects a broader theme of restoration and deliverance.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God
David's cry for help underscores the importance of turning to God in times of need. We are encouraged to rely on God as our first source of help and healing.

God's Faithfulness
The healing David experiences is a testament to God's faithfulness. Believers can trust in God's promises and His ability to restore and heal.

Prayer as a Means of Healing
The act of crying out to God highlights the power of prayer. It is a reminder that prayer is a vital tool for seeking God's intervention in our lives.

Spiritual and Physical Healing
While physical healing is significant, this verse also points to spiritual restoration. Believers should seek holistic healing that encompasses both body and soul.

Gratitude for Deliverance
David's response to healing is one of gratitude. We are encouraged to cultivate a heart of thankfulness for God's work in our lives.

What Does Psalm 30:2 Mean
O Lord

- David begins by calling on the covenant-keeping Lord, “O Lord” (Psalm 30:2). This matches the pattern of exalting God before presenting a request (Psalm 8:1; Psalm 27:1).

- He appeals to the same self-revealed God who declared, “I AM WHO I AM” (Exodus 3:14), grounding his plea in God’s eternal, sovereign nature (Psalm 103:19).

my God

- The shift from “Lord” to “my God” reveals intimacy. David owns the relationship secured by covenant (2 Samuel 7:28; Psalm 31:14).

- Personal trust echoes throughout Scripture: “My Lord and my God” (John 20:28); “The Lord is my helper” (Hebrews 13:6).

- Because of Christ’s work, we too “have been brought near” (Ephesians 2:13) and can claim Him as our own.

I cried to You for help

- David’s “cry” is urgent and honest, like “The righteous cry out, and the Lord hears” (Psalm 34:17).

- His focus is singular—“to You”—mirroring Psalm 121:1-2 and Jeremiah 33:3.

- New-covenant believers follow the same pattern: Peter’s “Lord, save me!” met an immediate answer (Matthew 14:30-31).

- Practical cues:

• Bring every need first to God.

• Pray plainly; He honors sincerity (Philippians 4:6-7).

• Expect an answer; He delights to respond.

and You healed me

- God’s reply is decisive: “You healed me.” David was rescued from the brink of death (Psalm 30:3).

- Healing reflects God’s character: “He heals all your diseases” (Psalm 103:3). Jesus fulfilled Isaiah 53:4-5 by healing the sick (Matthew 8:16-17).

- While ultimate wholeness awaits resurrection (Revelation 21:4), God still heals today (James 5:14-16).

- The completed tense encourages praise even before feelings align, just as Hannah’s countenance changed after prayer (1 Samuel 1:18).

summary

Psalm 30:2 traces a life-giving path: acknowledge who God is, embrace Him as yours, cry to Him in need, and celebrate His healing work. The same Lord who answered David stands ready to hear and to heal all who call on Him today.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - O Lord my God, I cried unto thee, and thou hast healed me. "Heal" may be used metaphorically for the removal of mental sufferings (see Psalm 41:4; Psalm 147:3); but David's grief when he saw the sufferings of his people from the plague seems to have wholly prostrated him, both in mind and body. For the nature of the "cry" spoken of, comp. vers. 8-10, which are an expansion of the present verse.
