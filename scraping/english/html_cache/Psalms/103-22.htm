Bless the LORD, all His works
This phrase calls for all of creation to praise God. The term "works" encompasses everything God has made, reflecting the biblical theme of creation's testimony to God's glory (Psalm 19:1). The call to bless the Lord is an invitation for all created things to acknowledge God's sovereignty and goodness. This echoes the creation narrative in Genesis, where God saw that everything He made was "very good" (Genesis 1:31).

in all places of His dominion.
God's dominion is universal, extending over all the earth and the heavens. This phrase emphasizes God's omnipresence and omnipotence, reminding believers that there is no place beyond His rule. The concept of God's dominion is a recurring theme in the Psalms, highlighting His authority over nations and nature (Psalm 47:2). It also points to the future establishment of God's kingdom on earth, as prophesied in Daniel 2:44.

Bless the LORD, O my soul!
The psalmist concludes with a personal exhortation, urging his own soul to bless the Lord. This reflects a deep, personal commitment to worship and gratitude. The repetition of this phrase from the beginning of the psalm (Psalm 103:1) underscores the importance of individual devotion. It serves as a reminder that worship is not only a communal activity but also a personal responsibility. This personal call to worship is seen throughout the Psalms, where the psalmist often speaks to his own soul, encouraging faith and trust in God (Psalm 42:11).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal, self-existent nature and His relationship with His people.

2. All His Works
Refers to all of creation, both animate and inanimate, that God has made and sustains.

3. Places of His Dominion
Encompasses the entire universe, as God’s dominion is limitless and extends over all creation.

4. The Psalmist (David)
Traditionally attributed to King David, who is expressing a personal and communal call to worship and bless the LORD.

5. The Soul
Represents the innermost being of a person, emphasizing a deep, heartfelt worship and blessing of God.
Teaching Points
Universal Call to Worship
All of creation is called to bless the LORD, reminding us that worship is not limited to humans but is a cosmic activity.

Personal Responsibility in Worship
The psalmist’s call to his own soul emphasizes personal responsibility in worship. We must actively engage our hearts and minds in blessing God.

Recognition of God’s Sovereignty
Acknowledging God’s dominion over all places reminds us of His ultimate authority and control over the universe, encouraging trust and reverence.

Integration of Worship in Daily Life
Blessing the LORD should be a continuous act, integrated into every aspect of our lives, not just reserved for specific times or places.

Encouragement in Community Worship
The communal aspect of the psalm encourages believers to join together in worship, strengthening the body of Christ through shared praise.

What Does Psalm 103:22 Mean
Bless the LORD

• The psalmist opens with a clear command: “Bless the LORD.” This is an intentional act of praise directed to the covenant God, Yahweh, recognizing His character and works (Psalm 103:1).

• Scripture repeatedly calls all people to exalt Him. “Praise the LORD! Give thanks to the LORD, for He is good; His loving devotion endures forever” (Psalm 106:1).

• Such blessing is never empty ritual; it springs from grateful hearts that know the Lord personally (Psalm 34:1; Revelation 7:12).

All His works

• The scope widens from individual worshipers to everything God has made. His “works” include creation, providence, redemption, and judgment.

• “All You have made will praise You, O LORD, and Your saints will bless You” (Psalm 145:10).

• Creation itself joins the chorus: “The heavens declare the glory of God” (Psalm 19:1). Even the fields, trees, and seas are pictured rejoicing before Him (Psalm 96:11-12; Isaiah 55:12).

In all places of His dominion

• God’s reign is absolute and universal, reaching “from everlasting to everlasting” (Psalm 103:17). No corner of the universe lies outside His authority.

• “The earth is the LORD’s, and the fullness thereof” (Psalm 24:1) affirms the same truth.

• Jesus echoes this cosmic rule: “All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me” (Matthew 28:18).

• Because His dominion is everywhere, the call to bless Him extends to heaven, earth, and every realm in between (Daniel 4:34-35; Revelation 5:13).

Bless the LORD, O my soul!

• The closing line brings the focus back to the individual heart. After summoning creation, the psalmist again speaks to his own soul, modeling personal responsiveness.

• This mirrors the psalm’s beginning (Psalm 103:1), bookending the whole song with private devotion inside public praise.

• Mary’s Magnificat shows the same inward overflow: “My soul magnifies the Lord” (Luke 1:46).

• Genuine praise starts deep within—mind, will, emotions—then flows outward to join the universal chorus.

summary

Psalm 103:22 is a sweeping call for universal praise that moves from personal worship to cosmic adoration and back again. Every creature, in every corner of God’s boundless kingdom, is summoned to bless the LORD for who He is and all He has done. The psalmist’s final self-address reminds us that lofty theology must land in humble, heartfelt thanksgiving: “Bless the LORD, O my soul!”

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Bless the Lord, all his works in all places of his dominion (comp. Psalm 19:1-4; Psalm 145:10; Psalm 148:7-13). The "works of God" - i.e. his material universe - cannot, of course, he said to "bless" God in the same sense that men and angels do; but, in a language of their own, they set forth his glory, and to the poetic mind seem truly to sing his praise. The "Song of the Three Children" is a natural outburst from devout hearts. Bless the Lord, O my soul (comp. ver. 1, and the comment ad loc.).
