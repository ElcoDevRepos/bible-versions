He brought Israel out with silver and gold
This phrase refers to the Exodus, when God delivered the Israelites from slavery in Egypt. The silver and gold were given to the Israelites by the Egyptians, as described in Exodus 12:35-36, where the Israelites asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold, and the Lord made the Egyptians favorably disposed toward them. This act fulfilled God's promise to Abraham in Genesis 15:14 that his descendants would come out of bondage with great possessions. The wealth given to the Israelites can be seen as a form of recompense for their years of slavery. It also prefigures the spiritual riches believers receive in Christ, as mentioned in Ephesians 1:3.

and none among His tribes stumbled
This phrase highlights the divine protection and strength given to the Israelites during their journey. Despite the harsh conditions of the wilderness, God ensured that none of the tribes faltered or were weakened. This can be connected to Deuteronomy 8:4, where it is noted that their clothes did not wear out, nor did their feet swell during the forty years in the desert. It signifies God's sustaining power and faithfulness. Spiritually, it can be seen as a type of Christ's sustaining grace for believers, as He promises to uphold and strengthen His followers, ensuring they do not stumble in their spiritual journey, as seen in Jude 1:24.

Teaching Points
God's Provision and Abundance
God not only delivers but also provides abundantly for His people, as seen in the wealth given to the Israelites.

Divine Protection and Health
The fact that "none among His tribes stumbled" signifies God's protection and the health He granted them, a reminder of His care for our physical and spiritual well-being.

Faithfulness to Promises
God's actions in the Exodus demonstrate His faithfulness to His promises, encouraging us to trust in His word and timing.

Spiritual Deliverance
The physical deliverance of the Israelites is a foreshadowing of the spiritual deliverance we receive through Christ, who frees us from the bondage of sin.

Gratitude and Remembrance
Reflecting on God's past faithfulness should lead us to gratitude and a deeper trust in His future provision and guidance.

What Does Psalm 105:37 Mean
He brought Israel out

“Then the LORD brought Israel out from Egypt with a mighty hand” (Psalm 105:37a).

• This recalls the historical Exodus (Exodus 12:41, 51; Acts 7:36), emphasizing that God Himself, not human ingenuity, was the deliverer.

• The verb “brought out” testifies to a definite moment in time—Israel’s bondage ended because God acted exactly as He had promised Abraham centuries earlier (Genesis 15:13-14).

• Psalm 77:20 and Hosea 11:1 echo the same picture: God shepherded, carried, and literally relocated His covenant people.

• For believers today, the Exodus stands as a concrete illustration of redemption—God still intervenes decisively to set His people free (Colossians 1:13-14).

with silver and gold

“...with silver and gold” (Psalm 105:37b).

• Exodus 12:35-36 records that the Israelites “asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold...and the LORD gave the people favor,” so they left with tangible wealth.

• This fulfills God’s promise to Abram that his descendants would depart “with great possessions” (Genesis 15:14).

• The riches were not plunder stolen by force but gifts granted by divine favor, underscoring that God can transfer resources at His discretion (Proverbs 13:22).

• These resources later financed the tabernacle (Exodus 25:1-8), demonstrating that God provides for worship and service as He delivers.

and none among His tribes stumbled

“...and none among His tribes stumbled” (Psalm 105:37c).

• The phrase points to physical wholeness; even the elderly and children marched without faltering (Deuteronomy 29:5; Nehemiah 9:21).

• Isaiah 63:9 affirms, “In all their distress, He too was afflicted, and the Angel of His Presence saved them”—God’s sustaining power kept every Israelite on his feet.

• Their sandals and clothes did not wear out (Deuteronomy 8:4), reinforcing that preservation is as much a miracle as liberation.

• Spiritually, it illustrates that those God redeems He also keeps (Jude 24; John 10:28-29).

summary

Psalm 105:37 celebrates a historical fact: the LORD personally led Israel out of Egypt, enriched them with silver and gold, and upheld every individual so none collapsed on the journey. The verse assures believers that the same God who powerfully rescues also abundantly provides and faithfully sustains, turning bondage into freedom, scarcity into provision, and weakness into steady strength.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) Feeble person.--Literally, stumbling. (Comp. Isaiah 5:27 : "None shall be weary or stumble among them," i.e., none unfit for the march and military duty.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 37. - He brought them forth also with silver and gold (Exodus 12:35, 36; comp. 3:21, 22). And there was not one feeble person among their tribes; literally, there was not one that stumbled among their tribes, or among his tribes. Probably there were many feeble persons, who were carried on beasts of burden, or in carts, or by their friends. But all those who walked had strength given to them, and did not stumble by the way (comp. Isaiah 5:27).
