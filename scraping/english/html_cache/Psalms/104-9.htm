You set a boundary they cannot cross
This phrase reflects God's sovereign control over creation, specifically the waters. In the Genesis account of creation, God separates the waters and establishes boundaries for them (Genesis 1:9-10). This act demonstrates His authority and order in the universe. The boundary set by God is a reminder of the flood narrative in Genesis 6-9, where God once allowed the waters to cover the earth as a judgment but then promised never to do so again (Genesis 9:11). The boundary signifies God's covenant faithfulness and His power to restrain chaos. In the ancient Near Eastern context, water often symbolized chaos and disorder, and God's ability to set limits on the waters underscores His supremacy over all creation.

that they may never again cover the earth
This part of the verse alludes to the promise God made to Noah after the flood, ensuring that such a cataclysmic event would not recur (Genesis 9:11). It highlights God's mercy and the stability He provides to the world. Theologically, it points to God's ongoing commitment to His creation and His desire for order and life to flourish. This assurance is a precursor to the ultimate peace and restoration found in Christ, who calms the storms (Mark 4:39) and brings reconciliation to all creation (Colossians 1:20). The phrase also serves as a reminder of God's control over natural elements, which is a recurring theme throughout the Psalms, emphasizing His role as the Creator and Sustainer of the universe.

What Does Psalm 104:9 Mean
You set a boundary they cannot cross

“ You set a boundary they cannot cross” (Psalm 104:9) pictures God drawing an unbreakable line for the waters after the Flood.

• God’s authority: As in Job 38:8-11, He speaks to the sea, saying, “This far you may come, but no farther,” underscoring His ongoing governance of creation.

• A gracious safeguard: Genesis 9:11-15 records His covenant with Noah—no future global flood—so the boundary is a promise, not merely a physical barrier.

• Daily reminder: Every shoreline testifies that divine order is still holding (Proverbs 8:29), inviting us to trust His reliability in all things.

that they may never again cover the earth

The purpose clause “that they may never again cover the earth” highlights God’s intention behind the boundary.

• Permanence of His word: Isaiah 54:9-10 links the no-more-flood promise to the unshakable nature of God’s covenant love.

• Mercy restrained: By holding back the waters, He also restrains judgment, giving humanity space for repentance (2 Peter 3:5-9).

• Stability for stewardship: Earth’s inhabitants can cultivate, build, and worship without fear of cosmic chaos (Jeremiah 5:22).

summary

Psalm 104:9 celebrates the Creator who fixed the oceans in place, ensuring they will never again overwhelm the entire earth. He sets inviolable limits, keeps His covenant promises, and provides a stable world where we can live out faithful obedience and confident hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) A bound.--It is striking to observe what a deep impression their little line of coast, the barrier which beat off the waves of the Mediterranean, made on the Hebrew mind. The sea was an object of dread. Or if dread passes into reverent wonder, as in Psalm 104:25-26, it ends there; the Jew never took delight in the sea. Hence, the coast has for him only one purpose and suggestion. It is not for enjoyment or recreation, or even for uses of commerce. It is simply the defence set by God against the hostile waters.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - Thou hast set (or, didst set) a bound that they may not (rather, might not) pass over (comp. Job 38:10, 11; Jeremiah 5:22). The Deluge is for the time beyond the ken of the poet, who is singing God's greatness in nature, and in the general laws under which he has placed it. Neither turn again to cover the earth. This law, once Broken by the miracle of the Deluge, was thenceforth made absolute and inviolable (Genesis 9:15).
