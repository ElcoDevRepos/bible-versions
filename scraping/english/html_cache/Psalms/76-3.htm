There He shattered the flaming arrows
This phrase highlights God's power and intervention in battle. In ancient warfare, flaming arrows were used to cause destruction and panic. The imagery of God shattering these arrows signifies His ability to protect His people and nullify the threats against them. This can be connected to Ephesians 6:16, where believers are encouraged to take up the shield of faith to extinguish the flaming arrows of the evil one, illustrating God's protective power over spiritual attacks.

the shield and sword and weapons of war
The mention of shields, swords, and weapons of war emphasizes the comprehensive nature of God's victory over human military might. In biblical times, these were the primary tools of warfare, representing human strength and strategy. God's ability to render them ineffective demonstrates His supremacy over human efforts. This echoes the theme found in 2 Chronicles 20:15, where the battle is declared to belong to the Lord, not to human warriors. It also foreshadows the ultimate peace brought by Christ, the Prince of Peace, who will end all wars (Isaiah 9:6-7).

Selah
The term "Selah" is often used in the Psalms to indicate a pause for reflection. It invites the reader to contemplate the preceding message of God's overwhelming power and protection. This pause allows for meditation on the assurance that God provides, encouraging trust in His sovereignty and strength. The use of "Selah" here underscores the importance of internalizing the truth of God's victory and the peace that follows His intervention.

While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the context of Psalm 76 suggests that the setting is Jerusalem, a place of God's dwelling and protection for His people.

3. Enemies of Israel
The implied adversaries whose weapons are shattered by God, representing any force that opposes God's people.

4. Weapons of War

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Conflict
God is in control of all situations, including conflicts and wars. Believers can trust in His ability to protect and deliver them from adversaries.

Divine Protection
Just as God shattered the weapons of war, He can dismantle the spiritual and physical threats against His people today. Trust in His protection.

Reflective Pause (Selah)
Take time to pause and reflect on God's power and past deliverances. This reflection strengthens faith and encourages reliance on God.

Spiritual Warfare
Recognize that our battles are not just physical but spiritual. Equip yourself with the armor of God and rely on His strength.

Peace Through God's Intervention
True peace comes not from human efforts but from God's intervention. Seek His peace in times of turmoil.

What Does Psalm 76:3 Mean
There He shattered

• “There” points back to Zion, the place God chose for His name (Psalm 76:2). The psalmist wants us to picture the scene right where God dwells, reminding us of moments when the Lord personally intervened on Israel’s behalf—such as at the Red Sea (Exodus 15:4-6) or against Sennacherib’s army outside Jerusalem (2 Kings 19:35).

• “He shattered” underscores God’s direct action. He doesn’t merely weaken enemy arms; He smashes them beyond repair, echoing His pledge in Psalm 46:9: “He breaks the bow and shatters the spear; He burns the shields in the fire”.

the flaming arrows

• Flaming arrows (or “fiery darts”) evoke long-range threats meant to terrorize before troops even clash. God extinguishes them on the spot, showing that no tactic reaches beyond His defense (cf. Isaiah 54:17; Ephesians 6:16 portrays the spiritual parallel).

• By breaking the arrows, the Lord removes both the immediate danger and the fear that often precedes physical attack (Psalm 91:5-6).

the shield and sword

• These close-combat weapons symbolize both defense (shield) and offense (sword). God neutralizes everything the enemy trusts, repeating what He did in 2 Chronicles 20:17 when He told Judah, “You need not fight this battle… stand firm and see the salvation of the LORD”.

• The psalm offers comfort: whatever comes at us—whether outward assault or inward anxiety—God can disarm it completely (Psalm 3:3; Romans 8:31).

and weapons of war

• The phrase widens the scope: every instrument of hostility lies broken. Like the prophecy of Micah 4:3—“They will beat their swords into plowshares”—this verse previews ultimate peace under God’s reign.

• Historically, Israel saw partial fulfillments (Joshua 10:11; 2 Chronicles 32:21). Prophetically, Christ will finalize it, reigning until “He has put all His enemies under His feet” (1 Corinthians 15:25).

Selah

• Selah invites a deliberate pause. The call is to breathe in the truth that God alone is the decisive warrior.

• Reflection points:

– Remember past deliverances (Psalm 77:11-12).

– Rest in present security (Psalm 46:1).

– Rejoice in future victory (Revelation 19:11-16).

summary

Psalm 76:3 paints a vivid picture of God’s unmatched power: right where He dwells, He smashes every projectile, every defensive piece, and every offensive blade the enemy can muster. The verse assures us that the Lord who defended Israel still stands guard over His people today, rendering hostile schemes powerless and inviting us to pause, reflect, and trust Him for total, lasting peace.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) There.--This word in Psalm 14:5 does not appear to have a strictly definite local sense; and here may refer to time, possibly to some event, which we are not able with certainty to recover.

Arrows.--Literally, flashes. (See Note, Song of Solomon 8:6.) The image may be derived from the lightning speed of the flight of arrows, or from the custom of shooting bolts tipped with flame (see Note, Psalm 7:13), or the connection may be from the metaphor in Psalm 91:5-6, since the Hebrew word here used denotes pestilence in Habakkuk 3:5. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - There brake he the arrows of the bow. The expression "there" seems certainly to show that the deliverance celebrated took place at, or very near to, Jerusalem. This would sufficiently suit the destruction of Sennacherib's army, which certainly occurred in the neighbourhood, though not very close to the city (see 2 Kings 19:32, 38). The word translated "arrows" (רשׁפי) means properly "lightnings" (comp. Psalm 78:48), and expresses the swift flight of the arrow, not actual "fiery darts." The shield, and the sword, and the battle; rather, the war equipment (Kay, Cheyne).
