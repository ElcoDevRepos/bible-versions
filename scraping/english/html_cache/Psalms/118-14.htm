The LORD is my strength
This phrase emphasizes reliance on God as the source of strength. In the context of ancient Israel, strength was often associated with military might and protection. The psalmist acknowledges that true strength comes from the LORD, not from human power or resources. This is a recurring theme in the Psalms and throughout the Old Testament, where God is depicted as a warrior and protector of His people (Exodus 15:2, Isaiah 12:2). The phrase also reflects a personal relationship with God, indicating trust and dependence on Him in times of trouble.

and my song
The reference to "song" suggests a response of praise and worship. In biblical times, songs were a common way to express joy, gratitude, and remembrance of God's deeds. This phrase indicates that the LORD is not only the source of strength but also the reason for joy and celebration. The use of song in worship is seen throughout the Psalms and is a key element of Israelite worship practices. It also points to the joy of salvation and deliverance, themes that are central to the psalm.

and He has become my salvation
This phrase highlights the transformative work of God in the life of the believer. Salvation here can be understood both in a physical sense, as deliverance from enemies or danger, and in a spiritual sense, as deliverance from sin and death. The concept of God as Savior is foundational in the Old Testament and is fulfilled in the New Testament through Jesus Christ. The phrase "has become" suggests a personal experience of God's saving power, which is a testimony to His faithfulness and mercy. This connects to the broader biblical narrative of redemption and points to the ultimate salvation through Christ (Luke 2:30, Acts 4:12).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people.

2. Psalmist (likely David)
Traditionally attributed to King David, this psalm is a song of thanksgiving and praise for deliverance.

3. Israel
The nation of Israel, often the collective voice in the Psalms, expressing communal gratitude and reliance on God.

4. Temple Worship
This psalm was likely used in temple worship, celebrating God's deliverance and faithfulness.

5. Exodus Event
The language of "strength" and "song" echoes the song of Moses after the Exodus, celebrating God's deliverance from Egypt.
Teaching Points
God as Our Strength
Recognize that true strength comes from the LORD, not from our own abilities or resources.

In times of weakness, rely on God's power to sustain and uplift you.

God as Our Song
Let your life be a song of praise to God, reflecting His goodness and faithfulness.

Worship should be a natural response to God's work in your life.

God as Our Salvation
Salvation is not just a past event but a present reality and future hope in Christ.

Trust in God's deliverance in every aspect of life, both spiritual and physical.

The Importance of Remembrance
Regularly recall and celebrate God's past deliverances to strengthen your faith.

Use personal and communal worship as a means to remember and proclaim God's salvation.

What Does Psalm 118:14 Mean
The LORD is my strength

• The verse opens with a confession that real power comes from the LORD, not from our own resources (Psalm 28:7; Isaiah 40:29–31).

• Moses sang the identical line after the Red Sea victory: “The LORD is my strength and my song, and He has become my salvation” (Exodus 15:2), proving God’s enabling power is consistent across generations.

• This strength is:

– Sustaining in hardship (2 Corinthians 12:9; Philippians 4:13).

– Protective in battle (Ephesians 6:10–11).

– Empowering for obedience (Psalm 119:28; 1 Peter 4:11).

• Because Scripture is accurate and literal, we receive this promise at face value: the living God personally supplies the might we lack.

and my song

• Strength naturally overflows into praise; the rescued heart sings (Psalm 40:3; 95:1–2).

• “Song” implies:

– Joy that is God–centered, not circumstance–centered (Habakkuk 3:17–19).

– A testimony others can hear, as Paul and Silas demonstrated in prison (Acts 16:25).

– Ongoing worship that fills home, church, and daily life (Colossians 3:16; Ephesians 5:19).

• The Lord Himself is both the theme and the composer of our praise—He gives the melody and the reason to sing.

and He has become my salvation

• “Has become” points to a completed, personal rescue. Israel experienced it when God crushed their enemies (Psalm 118:10–13) and believers experience it fully in Christ (John 3:16; Acts 4:12).

• Salvation includes:

– Deliverance from danger (Psalm 18:2).

– Forgiveness of sin through Jesus’ substitutionary death and resurrection (Romans 5:8–9; 1 Peter 2:24).

– Assurance of future glory (1 Thessalonians 5:9–10; Revelation 7:10).

• Simeon echoed this verse as he held the infant Messiah: “For my eyes have seen Your salvation” (Luke 2:30), confirming that the promised deliverance is perfectly realized in Jesus.

summary

Psalm 118:14 declares that the LORD Himself is our power, our praise, and our deliverance. We lean on Him for strength, celebrate Him as our song, and rest in the finished salvation He has personally worked for us through Christ.
