Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For You, O Lord, are kind and forgiving
This phrase highlights the character of God as both kind and forgiving. The Hebrew word for "kind" can also be translated as "good," emphasizing God's benevolent nature. The concept of forgiveness is central to the biblical narrative, seen in God's covenant with Israel and His willingness to forgive their transgressions. This reflects God's nature as described in Exodus 34:6-7, where He is "compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness." The forgiveness of God is a recurring theme throughout the Psalms and is foundational to the New Testament understanding of salvation through Jesus Christ, who embodies God's forgiveness through His sacrificial death and resurrection.

rich in loving devotion
The term "loving devotion" is often translated from the Hebrew word "chesed," which encompasses love, mercy, and covenant faithfulness. This phrase underscores the abundance of God's steadfast love, which is not limited or conditional. In the context of the Old Testament, this loving devotion is part of God's covenant relationship with Israel, where He remains faithful despite their unfaithfulness. This concept is echoed in the New Testament, where God's love is fully revealed in Christ (Romans 5:8). The richness of God's loving devotion is a source of comfort and assurance for believers, affirming that His love is inexhaustible and ever-present.

to all who call on You
This phrase emphasizes the inclusivity of God's mercy and love. It is not limited to a specific group but is available to all who seek Him. The act of calling on God implies a relationship and dependence on Him, as seen in the practice of prayer throughout the Bible. This is consistent with the biblical teaching that God is near to all who call on Him in truth (Psalm 145:18). In the New Testament, this is expanded to include all people, Jew and Gentile alike, who call on the name of the Lord for salvation (Romans 10:12-13). This universal offer of grace is a testament to God's desire for all to come to repentance and knowledge of the truth.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 86, David is a central figure in the Old Testament, known for his deep relationship with God, his psalms, and his role as the second king of Israel.

2. God (Yahweh)
The central figure in this verse, God is described with attributes of kindness, forgiveness, and loving devotion, emphasizing His character and relationship with His people.

3. Israel
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the nation of Israel is the primary audience of the Psalms, and the context of David's prayers often reflects the collective experiences and spiritual journey of Israel.
Teaching Points
God's Character
God is inherently kind and forgiving. His nature is unchanging, and His attributes are consistently revealed throughout Scripture.

Call on the Lord
The invitation to call on God is open to all. This verse encourages believers to approach God with confidence, knowing His readiness to forgive and show loving devotion.

Rich in Loving Devotion
God's love is abundant and steadfast. Believers can rely on His unwavering commitment to those who seek Him.

Forgiveness and Repentance
Understanding God's forgiving nature should lead us to repentance and a desire to live in accordance with His will.

Prayer as a Response
Prayer is a vital response to God's character. It is through prayer that we call on God, experience His kindness, and receive His forgiveness.

What Does Psalm 86:5 Mean
For You, O Lord

- David begins by addressing the covenant-keeping LORD, the only true God (Psalm 100:3; Exodus 20:2).

- By using “You,” he speaks personally, showing that prayer is relational rather than ritual (Psalm 62:8).

- Recognizing God’s unique lordship sets the tone for the entire verse—everything that follows is true precisely because of who He is (Psalm 95:3).

are kind

- God’s kindness is active goodness—He continually does what is beneficial for His people (Psalm 145:17; Romans 2:4).

- This attribute draws sinners and saints alike to trust Him, knowing He never withholds what is truly good (Psalm 84:11).

- His kindness seen in daily mercy calls believers to imitate that same spirit toward others (Ephesians 4:32).

and forgiving

- The verse links kindness to forgiveness, showing that God’s goodness flows into pardon (Exodus 34:6–7; 1 John 1:9).

- “Forgiving” underscores complete removal of guilt, not mere overlooking of sin (Isaiah 55:7; Micah 7:18–19).

- Because He forgives, believers can approach Him confidently and honestly, knowing condemnation is lifted in Christ (Romans 8:1).

rich in loving devotion

- “Rich” points to abundance—God’s steadfast love never runs thin (Lamentations 3:22–23; Psalm 103:8).

- Loving devotion (often called steadfast love) is covenant commitment: He binds Himself to love His people and never breaks that promise (Deuteronomy 7:9).

- This love is experienced, not only declared; every act of redemption, provision, and discipline flows from it (Ephesians 2:4–5).

to all who call on You

- The invitation is universal in scope yet specific in response: anyone who turns to the LORD finds these blessings (Joel 2:32; Romans 10:13).

- Calling implies faith, humility, and dependence—acknowledging need and trusting His character (Psalm 145:18; Jeremiah 29:12).

- No sincere caller is excluded; God’s kindness, forgiveness, and love are ready for all who seek Him (Matthew 7:7–8).

summary

Psalm 86:5 affirms that the one true LORD is personally kind, fully forgiving, and overflowing with steadfast love. These qualities are not limited to a select few but are freely granted to every person who turns to Him in faith. The verse invites continual prayer, confident that God’s character guarantees a gracious response to all who call on His name.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) For thou.--Up to this time the psalmist has only put forward his needs in various aspects as a plea for God's compassion. Now, not without art, he clenches his petition by an appeal to the nature itself of the Divine Being. The originals of the expressions in this verse will be found in Exodus 20:6; Exodus 34:6-9; Numbers 14:18-19.

Ready to forgive.--The Hebrew word occurs nowhere else in the form found here. Etymologically it means remitting. The LXX. have ????????, a word for which perhaps our considerate is the nearest equivalent, implying that legal right is overlooked and suspended in consideration of human weakness. Wisdom Of Solomon 12:18 gives a good description of this Divine attribute.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - For thou, Lord, art good, and ready to forgive. The word translated "ready to forgive," סַלָח, occurs here only; but the context sufficiently fixes its meaning, which is well expressed by the ἐπιεικὴς of the LXX. As God was "good" and "forgiving," he would be likely to grant the petitions just addressed to him. And plenteous in mercy unto all them that call upon thee (comp. Exodus 34:6; Joel 2:13).
