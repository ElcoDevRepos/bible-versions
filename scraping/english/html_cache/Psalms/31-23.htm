Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Love the LORD, all His saints.
This phrase is a call to devotion and worship directed towards those who are set apart for God, often referred to as "saints." In the Old Testament context, "saints" refers to the faithful followers of God, the Israelites who are in covenant with Him. The command to "love the LORD" is central to the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-5), which is foundational in Jewish tradition and emphasizes total devotion to God. This love is not merely emotional but involves obedience and commitment to God's commandments. The call to love God is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus identifies it as the greatest commandment (Matthew 22:37-38). The phrase underscores the relational aspect of faith, where love for God is both a response to His love and a duty of His people.

The LORD preserves the faithful,
This part of the verse highlights God's protective nature towards those who are loyal and steadfast in their faith. The term "faithful" refers to those who trust in God and live according to His will. Preservation by the LORD implies both physical protection and spiritual safeguarding. Throughout the Psalms, there is a recurring theme of God as a refuge and protector (Psalm 91:1-2). This assurance of preservation is seen in the lives of biblical figures like Daniel, who remained faithful amidst trials and was protected by God (Daniel 6). The concept of divine preservation is also reflected in the New Testament, where believers are assured of God's faithfulness to keep them (1 Thessalonians 5:23-24).

but fully repays the arrogant.
This phrase introduces a contrast between the fate of the faithful and the arrogant. The "arrogant" are those who are prideful, self-reliant, and dismissive of God's authority. In biblical literature, arrogance is often associated with rebellion against God and is condemned (Proverbs 16:18). The idea of God repaying the arrogant is consistent with the principle of divine justice, where God rewards righteousness and punishes wickedness. This concept is evident in the story of Pharaoh, whose arrogance led to his downfall (Exodus 5-14). The New Testament also warns against pride, emphasizing humility as a virtue (James 4:6). The phrase serves as a reminder of the moral order established by God, where actions have consequences, and divine justice prevails.

Teaching Points
Love for God as a Command
Loving God is not merely an emotion but a command that requires intentional action and devotion.

Faithfulness is Rewarded
God promises to preserve those who are faithful, encouraging believers to remain steadfast in their walk with Him.

The Danger of Pride
Arrogance leads to downfall and divine repayment, serving as a warning to cultivate humility.

Community of Saints
The call to love God is directed to the community of believers, emphasizing the importance of collective worship and support.

God's Justice
God's justice is evident in His treatment of the faithful and the arrogant, reminding us of His righteous character.

What Does Psalm 31:23 Mean
Love the LORD

• The verse opens with a direct call: “Love the LORD.” Love is not optional— it is the believer’s defining response to God’s covenant grace.

• Deuteronomy 6:5 echoes the same command: “You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart…” Jesus restates it as the greatest commandment in Matthew 22:37.

• Love for God grows out of His prior love for us (1 John 4:19) and expresses itself in obedience (John 14:15).

all His saints

• “Saints” refers to all who have been set apart by God through faith, not a special class. Paul greets entire churches as saints (Ephesians 1:1; Colossians 1:2).

• This inclusive address means every believer is summoned to active, affectionate devotion. There’s no spectator section in God’s family (1 Peter 2:9).

The LORD preserves the faithful

• “Preserves” speaks of God’s continuous, hands–on protection. Psalm 97:10 says, “He preserves His saints; He delivers them…”

• Faithful believers are kept, not merely spared trouble, but guarded through it (2 Thessalonians 3:3; 1 Samuel 2:9).

• David, the psalmist, had experienced this preservation repeatedly (Psalm 31:20), so his assurance is personal and testimonial.

but fully repays the arrogant

• God’s justice is as certain as His mercy. Proverbs 16:18 warns that pride precedes destruction, and James 4:6 reminds us that God “opposes the proud.”

• “Fully repays” stresses completeness; no proud act escapes the Lord’s notice (Romans 2:5-6; Psalm 94:2).

• The contrast highlights a moral universe in which humility and faith are rewarded, while arrogance meets divine recompense.

summary

Psalm 31:23 calls every believer to love God wholeheartedly, reminds us that we’re all saints set apart for that purpose, assures us that the Lord actively secures those who remain faithful, and cautions that pride invites certain judgment. Choosing humble, persevering love aligns us with the preserving care of God and shields us from the ruin that pride inevitably brings.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - O love ye the Lord, all ye his saints. The psalmist winds up with a short burst of song, in which his heart goes out to others. He calls upon all God's saints to "love" him, on the ground of his own experience, which is that the Lord preserveth the faithful (literally, those who stand firm, Kay), and plentifully rewardeth the proud doer; i.e. visits with ample vengeance such as in their pride set themselves against him and against his people.
