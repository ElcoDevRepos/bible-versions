But the king will rejoice in God;
This phrase highlights the king's reliance on God for joy and strength. In the context of David's life, this likely refers to his time in the wilderness, fleeing from Saul or Absalom. The king's rejoicing in God signifies trust and confidence in divine protection and provision. Historically, kings were often seen as representatives of their people before God, and their relationship with God was crucial for the nation's well-being. This phrase also foreshadows the ultimate King, Jesus Christ, who perfectly rejoices in and glorifies God.

all who swear by Him will exult,
Swearing by God indicates a deep commitment and allegiance to Him. In ancient Israel, oaths were taken seriously and were a way to affirm one's faithfulness and trust in God. Those who align themselves with God and His purposes will find joy and victory. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where loyalty to God results in blessings and joy. It also connects to the New Testament teaching that those who confess Christ and live by His teachings will share in His joy and triumph.

for the mouths of liars will be shut.
This phrase speaks to the ultimate triumph of truth over falsehood. In the biblical context, liars often represent those who oppose God's anointed and spread deceit. The shutting of their mouths indicates divine justice and vindication for the righteous. This can be seen as a prophetic assurance that God will silence those who speak against His people and His truth. It echoes the theme found throughout Scripture that God will ultimately judge and silence all falsehood, as seen in the final judgment described in Revelation.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 63, David is expressing his trust and joy in God despite being in the wilderness, likely during his flight from Absalom.

2. The King
Refers to David himself, who is confident in God's deliverance and justice.

3. Liars
Represents those who oppose David and speak falsely against him, possibly his enemies during his time of distress.

4. Wilderness of Judah
The setting of the Psalm, symbolizing a place of trial and testing where David seeks God earnestly.

5. God
The central figure in whom David places his trust and finds his joy, despite his circumstances.
Teaching Points
Rejoicing in God Amidst Trials
David's example teaches us to find joy in God even when circumstances are challenging. Our relationship with God should be our source of strength and joy.

The Power of Truth
The assurance that God will silence liars reminds us of the power and ultimate triumph of truth. We should strive to live truthfully and trust in God's justice.

The Importance of Loyalty to God
Those who "swear by Him" are those who are loyal and committed to God. Our allegiance to God should be unwavering, and we should find joy in our relationship with Him.

God's Justice and Vindication
The promise that liars' mouths will be shut assures us of God's justice. We can trust that God will vindicate us in His time.

What Does Psalm 63:11 Mean
But the king will rejoice in God

“​But the king will rejoice in God” (Psalm 63:11a)

• David, the earthly king, never forgets his higher allegiance; his joy rises from God’s character and faithfulness, not from circumstances (Psalm 16:11; 2 Samuel 6:21).

• This delight marks the difference between a self-reliant ruler and one who knows “the LORD is my strength and my shield” (Psalm 28:7).

• The line also foreshadows the greater King, Jesus, whose deepest joy was “to do the will of Him who sent Me” (John 4:34; Acts 2:26-28).

• Rejoicing in God becomes a testimony that the throne rests on divine, not human, stability (Psalm 21:1; Proverbs 29:14).

all who swear by Him will exult

“​all who swear by Him will exult” (Psalm 63:11b)

• Swearing by God means publicly identifying with Him in loyalty and truth (Deuteronomy 10:20; Isaiah 45:23).

• Such wholehearted allegiance moves worshipers from mere words to exuberant praise because they know “the LORD redeems the life of His servants” (Psalm 34:22).

• This exultation unites the people with their rejoicing king, forming a community whose celebration centers on God alone (Psalm 132:9; Philippians 3:3).

• It carries a promise: God honors those who honor Him (1 Samuel 2:30; Hebrews 6:13-18).

for the mouths of liars will be shut

“​for the mouths of liars will be shut” (Psalm 63:11c)

• God’s justice guarantees that deception and slander will not have the final word (Psalm 31:18; Job 5:16).

• Shutting mouths pictures complete silencing—no rebuttal, no escape—much like “every mouth may be silenced and the whole world held accountable to God” (Romans 3:19).

• This promise comforts the faithful when falsehood seems loudest; the Lord “cuts off all flattering lips” (Psalm 12:3) and vindicates truth in His timing (Psalm 37:5-7).

• The contrast is stark: rejoicing for the loyal, silence for the deceitful. God Himself draws that line (Proverbs 10:31; Revelation 21:27).

summary

Psalm 63:11 concludes David’s wilderness psalm with confident contrasts: the king and all who trust God erupt in joy, while liars are permanently muted. The verse assures believers that loyalty to God brings lasting celebration, and falsehood, however shrill today, will be hushed by His righteous verdict.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) Sweareth by him.--This is explained as meaning, "swear allegiance to him as the king," on the analogy of Zephaniah 1:5. And this suits the context. On the other hand, the natural way to understand the phrase, "swear by" or "in him," is to refer it to the only oath allowed to the Israelite," by the name of Jehovah" (Deuteronomy 6:13; Isaiah 65:16; comp. Amos 8:14), in which case we must explain by Deuteronomy 10:20-21, "Swear by his (Jehovah's) name; He is thy praise." Those who are loyal to Jehovah, who appeal to Him in all troubles, will find this promise true, "They shall glory," while the unfaithful and false, not daring to make the solemn appeal, will have their mouth stopped. (Comp. Romans 3:19.) . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - But the king shall rejoice in God. The "king," thus suddenly introduced, cannot be an entirely new personage, unknown to the rest of the psalm, and, therefore, must be the composer, speaking of himself in the third person (comp. Psalm 18:50; Psalm 72:1). Every one that sweareth by him (i.e. by God) shall glory; or, shall triumph (Kay). Those who swear by the Name of God show themselves to be believers in God, and will be upheld by him in time of danger (see Deuteronomy 6:13; Isaiah 65:16). But the mouth of them that speak lies shall be stopped. (On the falsehoods told by David's enemies, see 2 Samuel 15:3; 2 Samuel 16:7, 8; and comp. Psalm 38:12; Psalm 41:5-8.)
