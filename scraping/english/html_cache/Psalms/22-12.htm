Many bulls surround me;
This phrase evokes a vivid image of being encircled by powerful and threatening forces. In biblical times, bulls were symbols of strength and ferocity. The psalmist, traditionally understood to be David, uses this imagery to convey a sense of being overwhelmed by enemies. This can be seen as a metaphor for the adversaries that David faced throughout his life, including King Saul and various foreign nations. The imagery of being surrounded by bulls also foreshadows the intense opposition Jesus faced from religious leaders and Roman authorities during His crucifixion, as Psalm 22 is often viewed as a messianic prophecy.

strong bulls of Bashan encircle me.
Bashan was a region known for its fertile land and strong cattle, located east of the Jordan River. The reference to "strong bulls of Bashan" emphasizes the formidable nature of the adversaries. Historically, Bashan was a place of great prosperity and strength, often associated with powerful kings and armies. This phrase suggests not only the physical strength of the enemies but also their intimidating presence. In a prophetic sense, this can be seen as a type of the spiritual and physical forces that opposed Christ, highlighting the intensity of His suffering and the fulfillment of prophecy in His passion. The encircling of these "bulls" underscores the isolation and vulnerability experienced by both David and Jesus in their respective trials.

Teaching Points
Understanding Spiritual Opposition
Just as David felt surrounded by powerful enemies, Christians today may feel overwhelmed by spiritual or worldly opposition. Recognizing these forces helps us rely on God's strength.

Symbolism of Strength and Oppression
The "bulls of Bashan" symbolize not just physical strength but also spiritual and emotional oppression. Identifying these in our lives can help us seek God's deliverance.

Prophetic Fulfillment in Christ
This verse, within the context of Psalm 22, foreshadows the suffering of Christ. Understanding this connection deepens our appreciation for the fulfillment of prophecy in the New Testament.

Trust in God's Deliverance
Despite being surrounded by enemies, the psalmist ultimately trusts in God's deliverance. This teaches us to maintain faith in God's power to save us from our adversaries.

Vigilance Against Spiritual Enemies
Just as the psalmist was aware of his enemies, Christians are called to be vigilant against spiritual threats, using prayer and scripture as defenses.

What Does Psalm 22:12 Mean
Many bulls surround me

“Many bulls surround me” (Psalm 22:12a) pictures an overwhelming number of opponents closing in on David—and, prophetically, on Christ at the cross.

• David often spoke of enemies crowding him (Psalm 3:1; 17:9).

• The crowd that jeered at Jesus likewise “stood watching” (Luke 23:35), a living fulfillment of this verse.

• The plural “many” reminds us that hostility can feel relentless, yet God remains greater than the numbers (2 Kings 6:16).

strong bulls of Bashan

“Strong bulls of Bashan” (22:12b) identifies these foes as unusually powerful. Bashan’s rich pastures (Deuteronomy 32:14) produced the largest, healthiest cattle, so the phrase became shorthand for proud strength (Amos 4:1).

• In David’s day, adversaries like Saul’s loyalists or Philistine commanders fit the image.

• At Calvary the “strong” included religious leaders (Matthew 26:3–4) and Roman authorities (John 19:10–11).

• The verse underscores that opposition may possess real might, yet God’s plan is never threatened (Psalm 33:10–11).

encircle me

These bulls “encircle me” (22:12c), tightening the ring so escape seems impossible.

• David felt hemmed in before, crying, “They confronted me in the day of my calamity, but the LORD was my support” (Psalm 18:18).

• Jesus experienced literal encirclement—soldiers, rulers, and mobs surrounding the cross (Mark 15:16; John 19:25).

• When pressure closes in, believers can echo Psalm 34:7: “The angel of the LORD encamps around those who fear Him, and he delivers them.”

summary

Psalm 22:12 paints a three-fold picture: a multitude of foes, unusually strong adversaries, and a suffocating closeness. David’s words accurately described his own trials and, even more vividly, the crucifixion of Christ. The verse reassures us that no matter how numerous, powerful, or near the enemy seems, the Lord’s saving purpose stands firm and will ultimately prevail.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) Bulls of Bashan.--For "Bashan" see Numbers 21:33; for its pastures and cattle, comp. Deuteronomy 32:14, and for the figures, Amos 4:1. Instead of "fat bulls," the LXX. and Vulgate paraphrase "strong ones of Bashan." The point of the comparison lies in the wantonness and insolence of pampered pride, displayed by the minions of fortune.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - Many bulls have compassed me. The Sufferer represents the adversaries who crowd around him under the figure of "bulls" - fierce animals in all parts of the world, and in Palestine particularly' wild and ferocious. "Bulls,, and buffaloes are very numerous, says Canon Tristram, "in Southern Judaea; they are in the habit of gathering in a circle around any novel or unaccustomed object, and may be easily instigated into charging with their horns" ('Natural History of the Bible,' p. 71). Strong bulls of Bashan have beset me round. Bashan, the richest pasture-g"round of Palestine, produces the largest and strongest animals (Ezekiel 39:18). Hence "the kine of Bashan" became an expression for powerful oppressors (Amos 4:1).
