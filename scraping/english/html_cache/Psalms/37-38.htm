Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But the transgressors will all be destroyed;
This phrase emphasizes the ultimate fate of those who live in rebellion against God's laws. In biblical context, "transgressors" refers to those who deliberately violate God's commandments. The destruction mentioned is not merely physical but also spiritual, indicating eternal separation from God. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative where sin leads to death (Romans 6:23). Historically, Israel witnessed the downfall of nations and individuals who opposed God's will, such as the Canaanites and the Babylonian exile. This serves as a warning and a call to repentance, echoing the prophetic messages found throughout the Old Testament.

the future of the wicked will be cut off.
The "future" here refers to the hope or legacy that the wicked might expect. In a cultural context, lineage and posterity were highly valued in ancient times, and to have one's future "cut off" was a severe judgment. This phrase connects with Proverbs 24:20, which similarly states that the lamp of the wicked will be snuffed out, symbolizing the end of their influence and prosperity. Theologically, this reflects the justice of God, who ensures that evil does not ultimately prevail. It also points to the eschatological hope found in the New Testament, where the final judgment will see the complete eradication of wickedness (Revelation 20:11-15).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Transgressors
Individuals who violate God's laws and commandments. In the context of Psalm 37, they are contrasted with the righteous who trust in the Lord.

2. The Wicked
Those who live in opposition to God's will, often characterized by their pursuit of evil and injustice.

3. Destruction
The ultimate fate of the wicked as decreed by God. This is a recurring theme in the Psalms, emphasizing divine justice.

4. The Righteous
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, they are the implied contrast to the wicked, often highlighted throughout Psalm 37 as those who will inherit the land and enjoy peace.

5. The Future
Refers to the destiny or end result of one's life path. For the wicked, this future is bleak and marked by destruction.
Teaching Points
Divine Justice
God's justice is certain and will ultimately prevail. The wicked may seem to prosper temporarily, but their end is destruction.

The Importance of Righteous Living
Believers are called to live righteously, trusting in God's promises and avoiding the path of the wicked.

Eternal Perspective
Christians should maintain an eternal perspective, understanding that earthly success is fleeting, and true reward comes from God.

Trust in God's Timing
While the wicked may seem to thrive, believers must trust in God's timing for justice and remain steadfast in faith.

Encouragement for the Righteous
The fate of the wicked serves as a reminder and encouragement for the righteous to continue in their faithfulness to God.

What Does Psalm 37:38 Mean
But the transgressors

• The conjunction “But” shifts the focus from the peace and security promised to the righteous (Psalm 37:37) to those who refuse God’s law.

• “Transgressors” are people who knowingly cross God’s boundaries (1 John 3:4). Their lifestyle is characterized by rebellion rather than isolated mistakes (Psalm 1:1; Isaiah 59:2).

• The verse reminds us there are only two paths—obedience that leads to life, or transgression that leads to loss (Deuteronomy 30:19; Romans 6:23).

will all be destroyed;

• “All” leaves no loophole—every unrepentant sinner faces certain judgment (Proverbs 11:21; 2 Peter 3:7).

• Destruction includes:

– Temporal ruin—sin eventually corrodes health, relationships, and reputation (Psalm 73:18-19).

– Spiritual death—alienation from God now (Ephesians 2:1-3).

– Eternal punishment—final separation in the lake of fire (Matthew 25:41; Revelation 20:15).

• God’s justice may seem delayed, yet His timetable is perfect (Habakkuk 2:3; 2 Peter 3:9).

the future of the wicked

• “Future” (literally “afterpart” or “outcome”) points to one’s destiny and legacy. Though the wicked may flourish for a season, their prospects are hollow (Job 8:13; Psalm 73:3-12).

• Scripture contrasts this with the righteous, whose future is secure and everlasting (Psalm 37:37; Proverbs 23:18; Jeremiah 29:11).

• The rich fool illustrates this truth—planning for earthly ease, yet losing everything overnight (Luke 12:16-21).

will be cut off.

• To be “cut off” is covenant language for complete removal from God’s blessings (Genesis 17:14; Exodus 12:15).

• It signifies:

– Exclusion from the inheritance of the land (Psalm 37:9, 22).

– Final severance from God’s presence (Matthew 7:23).

– No enduring legacy—posterity and plans perish (Psalm 109:13; Proverbs 24:20).

• This ultimate cutoff underscores the urgency of repentance and faith in Christ, the only refuge from divine wrath (John 3:36; Acts 4:12).

summary

Psalm 37:38 delivers a sober promise: every unrepentant transgressor faces total, irreversible judgment. While the righteous enjoy a secured inheritance, the wicked—no matter how prosperous today—will be destroyed, their future erased. God’s justice is certain, comprehensive, and final, urging each reader to forsake sin and embrace the salvation He freely offers.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 38. - But the transgressors shall be destroyed together (comp. vers. 2, 9, 10, 15, 20, and 34). The end of the wicked shall be cut off. If achargth be taken to mean "posterity" in the preceding verse, it must be given the same signification here.
