Then I would not be ashamed
The concept of shame in biblical times often related to a sense of dishonor or disgrace, both personally and communally. In the Hebrew culture, shame was not just an individual feeling but a social reality. The psalmist expresses a desire to live in a way that avoids this shame, which is often associated with sin or disobedience to God's law. In the broader biblical narrative, shame entered the world through the fall of Adam and Eve (Genesis 3:7-10), and the psalmist's longing reflects a desire to return to a state of innocence and righteousness. The New Testament echoes this theme, where believers are encouraged to live in a manner that is above reproach (Philippians 1:10).

when I consider
The act of considering or meditating on God's commandments is a recurring theme in Psalm 119. This meditation is not a passive activity but an active engagement with God's word, involving study, reflection, and application. In the ancient Near Eastern context, meditation was often done aloud, as seen in Joshua 1:8, where the law is to be spoken and meditated upon day and night. This practice aligns with the Jewish tradition of reciting the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-9), emphasizing the importance of internalizing God's commandments.

all Your commandments
The psalmist's reference to "all Your commandments" underscores the comprehensive nature of God's law. It is not selective obedience that is sought, but a wholehearted commitment to all that God has instructed. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where obedience to the law was a sign of faithfulness and devotion. The commandments are seen as a reflection of God's character and will, and thus, adherence to them is a way to align oneself with God's purposes. In the New Testament, Jesus summarizes the law in the commandments to love God and love one's neighbor (Matthew 22:37-40), showing the continuity and fulfillment of the law in Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep love and reverence for God's law.

2. God
The divine lawgiver whose commandments are the focus of the psalmist's meditation and obedience.

3. Israel
The nation to whom the commandments were originally given, serving as a historical and spiritual context for the psalm.

4. Commandments
The laws and decrees given by God, which the psalmist seeks to follow diligently.

5. Shame
An emotional state the psalmist seeks to avoid by adhering to God's commandments.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Meditation on God's Word
Regularly considering God's commandments helps believers align their lives with His will, reducing the likelihood of shame.

Obedience as a Path to Honor
By living in accordance with God's commandments, believers can live honorably and confidently, free from the shame of disobedience.

The Role of God's Commandments in Spiritual Growth
Engaging deeply with God's laws fosters spiritual maturity and a closer relationship with Him.

Avoiding Shame through Righteous Living
Shame is often a result of sin and disobedience; by adhering to God's commandments, believers can avoid the pitfalls of shame.

The Transformative Power of God's Word
God's commandments have the power to transform lives, leading to a life of integrity and purpose.

What Does Psalm 119:6 Mean
Then I would not be ashamed

• The psalmist longs for a life free of the sting of spiritual embarrassment.

• Shame vanishes when heart and conduct match God’s revealed will (Psalm 119:5).

• Romans 10:11 echoes the promise: “Everyone who believes in Him will not be put to shame.”

• 1 John 2:28 points to the final judgment: abiding in Christ now means “we may be confident and unashamed before Him at His coming.”

• A clear conscience before God gives boldness with people (Proverbs 28:1).

when I consider

• “Consider” is purposeful, regular reflection, not a passing glance.

• Joshua 1:8 reminds us, “You shall meditate on it day and night… so that you may be careful to do all that is written in it.”

• Psalm 1:2 celebrates the one whose “delight is in the law of the LORD, and on His law he meditates day and night.”

• James 1:25 links careful study with obedient action: “He who looks intently into the perfect law… and continues to do so… will be blessed in what he does.”

• Ongoing meditation transforms shame into steadfast confidence.

all Your commandments

• The psalmist refuses selective obedience; God’s Word is an undivided whole (Psalm 119:160).

• Jesus sets the same standard: “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments” (John 14:15).

• Matthew 28:20 broadens it further: disciples are to observe “all that I have commanded you.”

• Obedience encompasses:

– Personal morality (Exodus 20:1-17)

– Worship and devotion (Psalm 95:6-7)

– Love for neighbor (Leviticus 19:18; John 13:34)

• Whole-hearted submission invites God’s approval and crowds out shame.

summary

Psalm 119:6 teaches that a life spent thoughtfully embracing every one of God’s commands produces freedom from shame. Careful, continual reflection on the entirety of Scripture, followed by obedient action, secures a clean conscience today and fearless confidence when we stand before the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Have respect unto.--Literally, look upon, or into, as in a mirror. (Comp. James 1:23.) The Divine Law is as a mirror, which shows man his defects; the faithful, in looking in it, have no cause to blush.

Judgments.--Not here in common sense of visitations for sin, but only one of the change of synonyms for law. (See this use in Exodus 21:1; Exodus 24:3, &c.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Then shall I not be ashamed, when I have respect unto all thy commandments. In that case I shall not be ashamed, either before God or man. Shame follows transgression: I shall escape shame, if my obedience is perfect.
