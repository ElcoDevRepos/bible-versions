Though you lie down among the sheepfolds
This phrase evokes imagery of pastoral life, common in ancient Israel. The sheepfolds were enclosures where sheep were kept, symbolizing peace and rest. Lying down among them suggests a state of tranquility and security. Biblically, sheepfolds can represent the people of God, as seen in John 10:1-16, where Jesus describes Himself as the Good Shepherd. This imagery may also allude to the tribes of Israel, particularly those who were more settled and less involved in warfare, such as Issachar (Genesis 49:14-15).

the wings of the dove are covered with silver
Doves are often symbols of peace and the Holy Spirit (Matthew 3:16). The mention of silver suggests purity and redemption, as silver was used in the tabernacle and temple (Exodus 26:19; 1 Kings 6:20). This could symbolize the divine protection and blessing over Israel, or the church, as they rest in God's provision. The dove's wings covered with silver may also imply the beauty and value of God's presence and peace among His people.

and her feathers with shimmering gold.
Gold in the Bible often represents divinity, kingship, and glory (Exodus 25:11; Revelation 21:18). The shimmering gold feathers of the dove could symbolize the glory and majesty of God resting upon His people. This imagery might also point to the transformation and sanctification of believers, reflecting God's glory (2 Corinthians 3:18). The combination of silver and gold suggests a completeness of divine favor and blessing, reminiscent of the wealth and splendor of Solomon's reign (1 Kings 10:21).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Sheepfolds
A sheepfold is an enclosure for sheep, symbolizing a place of rest and security. In biblical times, it was a common sight in pastoral settings.

2. Dove
The dove is often a symbol of peace, purity, and the Holy Spirit in the Bible. Here, it represents beauty and divine provision.

3. Silver and Gold
These precious metals symbolize wealth, purity, and divine blessing. They are often used in the Bible to describe the splendor and glory of God's creation or His blessings.
Teaching Points
Rest in God's Provision
Just as the sheep rest in the sheepfolds, believers are called to find rest in God's provision and protection. Trusting in His care allows us to experience peace and security.

Divine Beauty and Blessing
The imagery of the dove with silver and gold feathers reminds us of the beauty and richness of God's blessings. We are encouraged to recognize and appreciate the divine beauty in our lives.

Symbolism of the Dove
The dove, often representing the Holy Spirit, calls us to live lives marked by peace, purity, and the presence of God. We should strive to embody these qualities in our daily walk.

Spiritual Wealth
The silver and gold symbolize spiritual wealth that comes from a relationship with God. We are reminded to seek treasures in heaven rather than earthly wealth.

Contentment in Simplicity
The setting of the sheepfolds suggests contentment in simplicity. We are encouraged to find joy and satisfaction in the simple, everyday blessings God provides.

What Does Psalm 68:13 Mean
Though you lie down among the sheepfolds

“Though you lie down among the sheepfolds” (Psalm 68:13) pictures God’s people in a place of seeming insignificance and ordinary work.

• Genesis 49:14 speaks of Issachar “lying between the sheepfolds,” portraying a tribe content with humble labor.

• Judges 5:16 asks, “Why did you sit among the sheepfolds, listening to the whistling for the flocks?”—a gentle rebuke to those slow to join God’s victory.

• Psalm 23:1-3 reminds us that even in quiet pastures the Lord is shepherding us toward His purposes.

Taken together, the phrase shows that the Lord meets His people in everyday settings and can raise them from routine or even reluctance to share in His triumph.

The wings of the dove are covered with silver

Next the psalm shifts from lowly sheepfolds to a dazzling dove: “the wings of the dove are covered with silver.”

• Doves often symbolize peace and the Spirit (Genesis 8:11; Matthew 3:16). Here the bird is not plain but radiant, illustrating how God clothes His people with honor (Isaiah 61:10).

• Silver in Scripture suggests redemption and value (Exodus 30:11-16; 1 Peter 1:18-19). God takes believers from obscurity and covers them with redemptive splendor.

• Malachi 3:17 echoes this upgrade: “They will be Mine… on the day I prepare My treasured possession.”

What begins as a scene of commonplace rest turns into a vision of heaven-sent beauty because the Lord intervenes.

And her feathers with shimmering gold

The crescendo continues: “and her feathers with shimmering gold.”

• Gold speaks of the divine nature, royalty, and enduring glory (Exodus 25:11; Revelation 21:18).

• Psalm 45:13 describes the king’s bride “in her chamber, her gown embroidered with gold,” paralleling this image of a people made royally splendid.

• 2 Corinthians 3:18 declares that we “are being transformed into the same image from glory to glory,” capturing the move from simple to magnificent.

God does not merely rescue; He beautifies. The once-ordinary dove now flashes with heaven’s precious metals, signaling complete transformation.

summary

Psalm 68:13 traces a journey from the commonplace sheepfold to dazzling, gold-tipped wings. The verse assures believers that God can lift His people from routine or reluctance, cover them with redemption’s silver, and adorn them with the gold of His glory. The Shepherd who finds us in humble places is the King who equips us for radiant victory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13, 14) The agreement of the ancient versions in rendering these difficult verses shows that their obscurity does not arise, as in the case of so many passages of the Psalms, from any corruptions in the text, but from the fact that they are an adaptation of some ancient war-song to circumstances to which we have no clue. If we could recover the allusions, the language would probably appear clear enough.

"Why rest ye among the sheepfolds?"

"A dove's wings are (now) covered with silver, and her

feathers with the sheen of gold."

"When the Almighty scattered kings there,

It was snowing on Tsalmon."

Even in our ignorance of these allusions we at once recognise in the first member of this antique verse the scornful inquiry of Judges 5:16, addressed to the inglorious tribe that preferred ease at home to the dangers and discomforts of battle.

The word here rendered "sheepfolds" (in the Authorised Version pots, a meaning which cannot represent the Hebrew word or its cognates in any other place) is cognate to that used in Judges 5:16, and occurs in its present form in Ezekiel 40:43, where the margin renders, "andirons, or two hearthstones." The derivation from to set would allow of its application to any kind of barrier. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Though ye have lien among the pots; rather, Will ye lie down among the sheepfolds? Will ye, O ye laggarts of Israel, like the Reubenites in the war against Sisera, instead of going out to war with your brethren, "abide among the sheepfolds, to hear the bleatings of the flocks" (see Judges 5:16)? Yet shall ye be as the wings of a dove covered with silver, and her feathers with yellow gold. It is certainly wrong to supply, yet shall ye be before as the wings of a dove." There can be no promise of good made to these laggarts. Probably the meaning is, "Will ye be," or "Will ye seek to be as the wings of a dove, covered with silver, and her feathers of yellow gold?" i.e. Will ye abide in your prosperity and your riches, decked in gorgeous apparel, resplendent with silver and gold, while your brethren are bearing the brunt of battle, with all its ghastly sights and sounds, in your and the land's defence?
