In God, whose word I praise
This phrase emphasizes the psalmist's deep trust and reverence for God's word. The term "God" here is translated from the Hebrew "Elohim," which signifies the Creator and Judge of the universe. The psalmist's praise for God's word reflects a recognition of its authority and reliability. In the context of ancient Israel, the word of God was central to worship and daily life, often recited and memorized. This phrase also connects to Psalm 119, which extensively praises God's word, highlighting its role as a guide and source of wisdom. The act of praising God's word suggests a personal relationship and reliance on divine promises, which is a recurring theme throughout the Psalms.

in the LORD, whose word I praise
The use of "LORD" here is derived from the Hebrew "Yahweh," the covenant name of God, emphasizing a personal and relational aspect of God. This distinction between "God" and "LORD" in the same verse underscores the multifaceted nature of God as both the universal Creator and the personal covenant-keeper. The repetition of "whose word I praise" reinforces the importance of God's word in the life of the believer. It suggests a dual acknowledgment of God's transcendence and immanence. This phrase can be linked to the New Testament, where Jesus is described as the Word made flesh (John 1:14), fulfilling the promises and prophecies of the Old Testament. The psalmist's praise for the word of Yahweh anticipates the ultimate revelation of God through Christ, who embodies the word and fulfills the covenant promises.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 56, David wrote this psalm during a time of great distress when he was seized by the Philistines in Gath. His reliance on God during this period is a central theme.

2. Philistines
A group of people who were often in conflict with Israel. David's capture by them sets the backdrop for this psalm, highlighting his trust in God despite being in enemy territory.

3. Gath
A Philistine city where David was taken. This location is significant as it represents a place of danger and fear for David, yet also a place where he chose to trust in God.
Teaching Points
The Power of God's Word
God's word is not just a collection of texts but a powerful, living force that can guide, comfort, and protect us in times of trouble.

Trust in God's Promises
Like David, we are called to trust in the promises of God, even when circumstances seem dire. His word is a foundation we can rely on.

Praise as a Response to Fear
David's response to fear was to praise God. In our own lives, turning to praise can shift our focus from our problems to God's sovereignty.

Consistency in Faith
David's repeated praise of God's word ("whose word I praise") shows a consistent faith. We should strive for a faith that remains steadfast regardless of our situation.

What Does Psalm 56:10 Mean
In God

David opens with a deliberate focus on the Person behind every promise.

• Choosing “God” first reminds us of His unrivaled sovereignty (Psalm 46:10; Isaiah 45:5-6).

• Trust is settled before the circumstance is addressed—“When I am afraid, I put my trust in You” (Psalm 56:3).

• The emphasis falls on relationship: real fear answered by a real God (Psalm 27:1; Jeremiah 17:7).

• Because God cannot lie (Numbers 23:19; Titus 1:2), every word that follows is rock-solid.

whose word I praise

Having fixed his eyes on God, David immediately celebrates God’s spoken revelation.

• Praise here is not abstract admiration; it is joyful confidence that what God has said will stand (Psalm 119:160; Isaiah 55:11).

• Scripture fuels worship: “I rejoice in Your promise like one who finds great spoil” (Psalm 119:162).

• By praising God’s word, David is publicly aligning himself with it—echoing Joshua 1:8 and 1 Thessalonians 2:13 where the word is both treasured and obeyed.

• Gratitude defies fear; faith sings before the battle is won (2 Chronicles 20:21).

in the LORD

David repeats the affirmation but shifts to the covenant name—Yahweh—highlighting intimacy and faithful love.

• This is the God who bound Himself to His people (Exodus 3:15; Deuteronomy 7:9).

• Covenant faithfulness guarantees personal protection: “The LORD is my rock, my fortress, and my deliverer” (Psalm 18:2).

• By naming the LORD, David moves from general sovereignty to personal commitment; the Shepherd knows His sheep (Psalm 23:1; John 10:14).

whose word I praise

The refrain returns, doubling the spotlight on Scripture.

• Repetition drives the truth deeper: God’s word deserves continual, not occasional, praise (Philippians 3:1).

• Twofold mention echoes Deuteronomy 19:15—establishing a matter by multiple witnesses—underscoring reliability.

• In times of fear, repetition steadies the heart; proclaiming the word twice arms faith for the fight (Matthew 4:4, 7).

• The verse as a whole models a rhythm every believer can adopt: look to God, honor His word, repeat.

summary

Psalm 56:10 teaches a simple, powerful pattern: anchor confidence “in God,” celebrate “whose word I praise,” and then reinforce it “in the LORD, whose word I praise.” Sovereign power and covenant love meet in the unbreakable promises of Scripture, giving courage to praise even while the battle rages.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - In God will I praise his word; rather, through God (see the comment on ver. 4). In the Lord (rather, through the Lord) will I praise his word. Professor Cheyne looks upon this as "a feeble Jehovistic interpolation, which interrupts the refrain." But other commentators see in it a certain force.
