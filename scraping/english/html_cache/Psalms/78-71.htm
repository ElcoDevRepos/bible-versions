Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
from tending the ewes
This phrase highlights David's humble beginnings as a shepherd, a common occupation in ancient Israel. Shepherding was a vital part of the agrarian society, requiring diligence and care. David's experience with the ewes prepared him for leadership, teaching him responsibility and protection. This mirrors the biblical theme of God choosing the humble and lowly for great purposes, as seen in the lives of Moses and Gideon. The shepherd motif is significant throughout Scripture, symbolizing guidance and care, and is later used to describe Jesus as the Good Shepherd (John 10:11).

He brought him
This indicates divine intervention and selection. God actively chose David, demonstrating His sovereign will in raising leaders. This act of bringing David from the fields to the throne underscores the biblical principle that God exalts the humble (1 Samuel 16:11-13). It also reflects the theme of God's providence and His ability to orchestrate events for His purposes, as seen in Joseph's rise to power in Egypt (Genesis 41:41).

to be shepherd of His people Jacob
The term "shepherd" here is metaphorical, representing leadership and governance. David's role as king was to guide, protect, and nurture the nation of Israel, much like a shepherd with his flock. The reference to "His people Jacob" connects to the covenantal relationship God established with the patriarchs, emphasizing continuity and faithfulness. This shepherd imagery is prophetic of Christ, who is the ultimate Shepherd of God's people, fulfilling the promises made to Jacob (Micah 5:4).

of Israel His inheritance
Israel is described as God's inheritance, highlighting the special relationship between God and His chosen people. This concept of inheritance is rooted in the Abrahamic covenant, where God promised to make Abraham's descendants a great nation (Genesis 12:2). It signifies possession, care, and a lasting legacy. The idea of inheritance is also eschatological, pointing to the future fulfillment of God's promises through Christ, who secures an eternal inheritance for believers (Ephesians 1:11).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The verse refers to David, who was taken from shepherding sheep to shepherding the people of Israel. David is a central figure in the Old Testament, known for his heart for God and his role as the second king of Israel.

2. Jacob
Represents the people of Israel, as Jacob's name was changed to Israel, and his descendants became the twelve tribes of Israel.

3. Israel
The nation chosen by God as His inheritance, whom David was appointed to lead as king.

4. Shepherding
The act of tending sheep, which symbolizes leadership and care. David's experience as a shepherd prepared him for his role as king.

5. God's Calling
The divine act of selecting and appointing David from a humble position to a significant leadership role over His people.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereign Choice
God often chooses unlikely individuals for significant roles, emphasizing His sovereignty and wisdom. David's selection as king from a shepherd's life illustrates that God values the heart over outward appearances.

Preparation through Humble Beginnings
David's time as a shepherd was not wasted; it was a period of preparation for his future leadership. God uses our current circumstances to equip us for future responsibilities.

Leadership as Service
True leadership, as exemplified by David, involves serving and caring for those under one's charge. This reflects the heart of God, who is the ultimate Shepherd.

Faithfulness in Small Things
David's faithfulness in tending sheep prepared him for greater responsibilities. We are called to be faithful in our current tasks, trusting that God will use them for His purposes.

God's Inheritance
The people of Israel are described as God's inheritance, highlighting their value and the responsibility of leaders to care for them. Believers today are also God's inheritance, called to live in a way that honors Him.

What Does Psalm 78:71 Mean
From tending the ewes

“from tending the ewes” (Psalm 78:71a)

• David’s story starts in literal sheepfolds, a picture of unglamorous faithfulness (1 Samuel 16:11; Psalm 78:70).

• Shepherd work forged qualities the Lord values—watchfulness, courage, gentle care (1 Samuel 17:34-36).

• God delights in raising the humble (1 Samuel 2:8; James 4:10). David’s obscurity was Heaven’s training ground.

He brought him

“He brought him” (Psalm 78:71b)

• The initiative is entirely God’s: “I took you from the pasture” (2 Samuel 7:8).

• Divine calling is purposeful, not accidental (Psalm 75:6-7).

• Moving David from hills to throne shows the Lord’s directing hand over history (Proverbs 19:21).

• When God moves, He also equips (1 Samuel 16:13; Philippians 1:6).

To be shepherd of His people Jacob

“to be shepherd of His people Jacob” (Psalm 78:71c)

• The same skills that guarded lambs now guard a nation (2 Samuel 5:2).

• Shepherd-leadership emphasizes:

– Guidance into truth (Psalm 23:3).

– Protection from enemies (2 Samuel 22:3-4).

– Tender care for the weak (Isaiah 40:11).

• David foreshadows the Messiah: “I will raise up one shepherd over them” (Ezekiel 34:23) fulfilled in Christ the “Good Shepherd” (John 10:11).

Of Israel His inheritance

“of Israel His inheritance” (Psalm 78:71d)

• Israel is God’s treasured possession (Deuteronomy 32:9; Psalm 33:12).

• An inheritance is guarded and kept; leadership therefore carries holy accountability (Deuteronomy 7:6).

• David’s charge illustrates how the Lord entrusts His most valued treasure—His people—to leaders shaped by humility and obedience (Acts 13:22).

summary

Psalm 78:71 celebrates God’s sovereign grace: He lifts a humble shepherd, equips him, and sets him over the flock that is dearest to His heart. The verse reminds us that God shapes servants in hidden places, moves them by His own hand, and entrusts them with His priceless inheritance for His glory and their good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(71) Ewes great with young--So also in Isaiah 40:11; but properly, ewes with lambs. Literally, giving suck.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 71. - From following the ewes great with young he brought him (comp. Isaiah 40:11). The Hebrew word translated "ewes great with young" really means "ewes that are giving suck." This is the portion of the flock which needs the tenderest care. To feed Jacob his people, and Israel his inheritance (see 1 Chronicles 11:2). As Peter, James, and John were called from their occupation of fishers to be "fishers of men" (Matthew 4:19), so David was called from feeding sheep to feed God's people.
