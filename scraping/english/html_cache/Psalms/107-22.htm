Let them offer sacrifices of thanksgiving
In the Old Testament, sacrifices were a central part of worship and covenant relationship with God. Thanksgiving sacrifices, specifically, were offerings made to express gratitude to God for His blessings and deliverance. This phrase emphasizes the importance of acknowledging God's provision and mercy. The concept of thanksgiving sacrifices can be seen in Leviticus 7:12-15, where peace offerings included expressions of gratitude. In a broader sense, this foreshadows the New Testament teaching of offering spiritual sacrifices, as seen in Hebrews 13:15, where believers are encouraged to continually offer a sacrifice of praise.

and declare His works with rejoicing
Declaring God's works involves testifying to His mighty deeds and faithfulness. This was a common practice in Israel's worship, as seen in the Psalms, where recounting God's acts was a way to build faith and community. The call to declare His works with rejoicing highlights the joy that comes from recognizing God's intervention and blessings. This aligns with the New Testament encouragement found in Philippians 4:4, where believers are urged to rejoice in the Lord always. The act of rejoicing in God's works not only strengthens personal faith but also serves as a witness to others, drawing them to the knowledge of God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 107, traditionally attributed to King David, who calls the people to worship and thank God for His deliverance and steadfast love.

2. The Redeemed
The people of Israel who have experienced God's deliverance from various troubles and are called to respond with gratitude and praise.

3. Sacrifices of Thanksgiving
A practice in ancient Israel where offerings were made to express gratitude to God, symbolizing a heart of thankfulness.

4. God's Works
The mighty acts and deeds of God, including creation, deliverance, and provision, which are to be declared with joy.

5. Rejoicing
The act of expressing joy and gladness, particularly in response to God's goodness and faithfulness.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Thanksgiving
Thanksgiving is not just a ritual but a heartfelt response to God's goodness. It acknowledges His sovereignty and grace in our lives.

Declaring God's Works
Sharing testimonies of God's faithfulness encourages others and strengthens our own faith. It is a vital part of worship and community life.

Joyful Worship
Rejoicing in God's works is an expression of trust and hope. It shifts our focus from our circumstances to God's unchanging nature.

Sacrificial Living
In the New Covenant, our lives are to be living sacrifices, characterized by gratitude and praise, reflecting the transformation God has worked in us.

Community of Praise
Engaging in corporate worship and thanksgiving fosters unity and builds up the body of Christ, as we collectively acknowledge God's goodness.

What Does Psalm 107:22 Mean
Let them offer

- “Let” invites a willing, heartfelt response. God never coerces worship; He calls His redeemed to participate freely, just as He called Israel after each deliverance in Psalm 107’s earlier verses.

- “Them” refers to those whom the LORD has rescued (v. 2, v. 6, v. 13, v. 19, v. 28). Redemption brings responsibility. Romans 12:1 echoes the call: “present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God.”

- “Offer” signals intentional action, not mere feeling. Like David in 2 Samuel 24:24, we come determined to give something that costs us.

Sacrifices of thanksgiving

- Under the Law, a thank offering (Leviticus 7:12) was voluntary, celebrating God’s goodness. Today Christ’s finished work ends animal sacrifice, yet Hebrews 13:15 urges, “Through Jesus, therefore, let us continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise.”

- Gratitude marks genuine faith:

• Psalm 50:14 “Sacrifice a thank offering to God.”

• Jonah 2:9 “With a voice of thanksgiving I will sacrifice to You.”

- Practical ways to “offer” thanks:

• Verbal praise in private and public worship.

• Obedient living that honors the Redeemer (Colossians 3:17).

• Generous giving that mirrors God’s generosity (2 Corinthians 9:11-12).

Declare His works

- Thanks quickly turns outward. Gratitude naturally tells a story. Psalm 105:1 urges, “Give thanks to the LORD, call upon His name; make His deeds known among the nations.”

- “Works” highlights God’s mighty acts—creation, providence, salvation. Psalm 40:5 shares the same theme: “Many, O LORD my God, are the wonders You have done.”

- Testimony is commanded, not optional. Mark 5:19 records Jesus telling the delivered demoniac, “Tell them how much the Lord has done for you.”

- Practical expressions:

• Sharing personal salvation stories.

• Recounting answered prayers in small groups.

• Celebrating God’s faithfulness across generations (Psalm 78:4).

With rejoicing

- Joy crowns and authenticates thanksgiving. Psalm 32:11: “Be glad in the LORD and rejoice, you righteous.”

- Joy keeps testimony from sounding mechanical. Philippians 4:4 repeats, “Rejoice in the Lord always.”

- Reasons for rejoicing:

• Deliverance from distress (Psalm 107’s theme).

• Ongoing presence of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22).

• Certain future hope (1 Peter 1:8-9).

- Joy is expressed:

• In song (Psalm 92:4).

• In serving others cheerfully (2 Corinthians 9:7).

• In resilient hope amid trials (James 1:2).

Summary

Psalm 107:22 calls the redeemed to act: freely approach God, present sincere thanks, openly publicize His mighty deeds, and do it all with contagious joy. Thanksgiving is more than a moment; it is a lifestyle that honors the Deliverer and invites the watching world to trust Him too.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - And let them sacrifice the sacrifices of thanksgiving; compare the expression, "the calves of our lips" (Hosea 14:2), and see also Hebrews 13:15. And declare his works with rejoicing; i.e. joyfully pro claim the great things that God has done for them.
