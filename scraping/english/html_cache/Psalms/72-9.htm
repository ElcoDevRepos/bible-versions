May the nomads bow before him
This phrase suggests a universal reign where even those who are typically outside the established societal structures, such as nomads, acknowledge the authority of the king. In biblical times, nomads were often seen as outsiders or wanderers, living on the fringes of settled society. This imagery can be connected to the broader biblical theme of all nations and peoples recognizing the sovereignty of God's anointed king. The bowing of nomads signifies submission and respect, indicating a reign of peace and justice that extends beyond traditional boundaries. This can be seen as a type of Christ, who is prophesied to have a kingdom that includes all peoples and nations, as seen in Philippians 2:10-11, where every knee shall bow to Jesus.

and his enemies lick the dust
This phrase is a vivid depiction of total defeat and humiliation for the king's adversaries. In ancient Near Eastern culture, to "lick the dust" was a metaphor for complete subjugation and disgrace. It implies that the enemies are brought so low that they are prostrate on the ground, a position of utter defeat. This imagery is consistent with other biblical passages that describe the ultimate triumph of God's chosen ruler over his foes, such as in Isaiah 49:23, where kings and queens bow down with their faces to the earth. This can also be seen as a prophetic foreshadowing of Christ's ultimate victory over sin and evil, as described in Revelation 19:11-16, where Christ is depicted as the conquering King of Kings.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Nomads
Refers to wandering tribes or people who do not have a permanent home. In the context of ancient Israel, this could include groups like the Bedouins or other desert-dwelling peoples.

2. Enemies
Those who oppose or are hostile to the king or the kingdom. In a broader sense, this can represent any force or person that stands against God's anointed.

3. The King
While the psalm is attributed to Solomon, it prophetically points to the Messiah, the ultimate King who will reign with justice and righteousness.

4. The Desert
Often symbolizes desolation and hardship but also a place where God's provision and protection are evident.

5. The Reign of the Messiah
This psalm is often seen as a messianic prophecy, pointing to the future reign of Christ where peace and justice will prevail.
Teaching Points
Submission to Christ's Authority
Just as the nomads and enemies are depicted as bowing, we are called to submit to Christ's authority in our lives. This involves recognizing His lordship and aligning our actions with His will.

Victory Over Spiritual Enemies
The imagery of enemies licking the dust reminds us of the ultimate victory we have in Christ over sin and spiritual adversaries. We can live confidently, knowing that Christ has overcome the world.

The Universal Reign of Christ
This psalm points to the future reign of Christ where all nations and peoples will acknowledge His sovereignty. We are encouraged to participate in His mission by sharing the gospel and living as ambassadors of His kingdom.

Humility Before God
The act of bowing is a posture of humility. We are reminded to approach God with humility, acknowledging our dependence on Him for guidance and strength.

What Does Psalm 72:9 Mean
Setting the Scene

Psalm 72 is a royal psalm that ultimately points to the Messiah’s reign. Solomon authored it, yet the language reaches far beyond any earthly king, anticipating Jesus Christ, “the King of kings” (Revelation 19:16). The inspired text assures us that every promise here will come to pass exactly as written.

May the nomads bow before Him

“May the nomads bow before him”.

• Nomads (or “desert tribes”) represent peoples outside Israel’s settled boundaries—reminding us that Messiah’s rule is not provincial but worldwide (Isaiah 42:4; Matthew 28:18–20).

• Bowing expresses voluntary submission and worship, echoing prophecies like Isaiah 60:6 where “camels will cover you… they will proclaim the praises of the LORD.”

• Even distant seekers—like the Magi coming from the East (Matthew 2:1–11)—model this picture.

• The New Testament confirms that “at the name of Jesus every knee should bow” (Philippians 2:10), including those who once wandered far off (Ephesians 2:12–13).

Cross references woven through: Isaiah 49:6–7; Acts 13:47; Revelation 7:9.

And His enemies lick the dust

“and his enemies lick the dust”.

• “Lick the dust” is vivid imagery for absolute defeat, first hinted in the serpent’s curse: “dust you will eat” (Genesis 3:14).

• The picture suggests foes humbled so completely they lie prostrate on the ground, as also seen in Micah 7:17 and Isaiah 49:23.

• This victory is both spiritual and literal. Christ triumphed at the cross, disarming rulers and authorities (Colossians 2:15), and will finalize that triumph at His return when “He treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God” (Revelation 19:15).

• For believers, the promise resonates with Romans 16:20: “The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet.” Our enemies—sin, death, Satan—are destined for the dust.

summary

Psalm 72:9 declares the universal reach and unstoppable authority of the Messiah. Wanderers will willingly worship, while rebels will be utterly subdued. Every knee—whether seeking or defiant—will ultimately acknowledge King Jesus, fulfilling God’s faithful Word to the last detail.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) They that dwell in the wilderness. . . .--The Hebrew word in other places is used of "wild animals" (Psalm 74:14; Isaiah 23:13). Here apparently it refers to the nomad tribes wandering over the desert. The LXX. and ancient versions generally have "'thiopians."

Lick the dust.--The allusion is to the Eastern etiquette of prostration before a sovereign.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - They that dwell in the wilderness shall how before him (for the meaning here assigned to tsiyyim (ציּים), see Psalm 74:14; Isaiah 23:13). The wild tribes of the Syrian and Arabian deserts are probably intended (comp. Isaiah 60:6, 7). And his enemies shall lick the dust; i.e., prostrate themselves at his feet with their faces in the dust. See the Assyrian representations of Oriental prostrations ('Ancient Monarchies,' vol. 1, pp. 266, 502).
