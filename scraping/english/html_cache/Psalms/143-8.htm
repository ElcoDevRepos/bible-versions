Let me hear Your loving devotion in the morning
This phrase emphasizes the importance of starting the day with an awareness of God's steadfast love. In the ancient Near Eastern context, morning was often seen as a time of new beginnings and hope. The psalmist's desire to hear God's loving devotion reflects a reliance on divine guidance and mercy, akin to the Israelites' dependence on God's provision of manna each morning (Exodus 16:21). The morning is also a time for prayer and reflection, as seen in the practices of Jesus, who often withdrew to pray early in the day (Mark 1:35).

for I have put my trust in You
Trust in God is a central theme throughout the Psalms and the entire Bible. This trust is not passive but an active reliance on God's character and promises. The psalmist's declaration of trust echoes the faith of figures like Abraham, who believed God's promises despite not seeing their immediate fulfillment (Genesis 15:6). Trusting in God involves a commitment to His ways and a rejection of self-reliance, as seen in Proverbs 3:5-6, which encourages believers to trust in the Lord with all their heart.

Teach me the way I should walk
The request for divine teaching highlights the psalmist's desire for guidance and wisdom. In the biblical context, "the way" often refers to a path of righteousness and obedience to God's commandments (Psalm 119:105). This plea for instruction is reminiscent of the wisdom literature, where the fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge (Proverbs 1:7). The psalmist seeks not just knowledge but the ability to live according to God's will, reflecting a heart aligned with God's purposes.

for to You I lift up my soul
Lifting up one's soul to God signifies a deep, personal devotion and surrender. This expression of worship and dependence is found throughout the Psalms, where the soul represents the innermost being of a person (Psalm 25:1). The act of lifting up the soul is a form of prayer and supplication, acknowledging God's sovereignty and the psalmist's need for His presence and intervention. It is an act of humility and faith, similar to the posture of Jesus in the Garden of Gethsemane, where He submitted His will to the Father (Matthew 26:39).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of this psalm, King David, is expressing his deep reliance on God amidst his trials. Known for his heart after God, David often sought divine guidance and deliverance.

2. God
The central figure to whom David directs his prayer. God is portrayed as a source of loving devotion and guidance.

3. Morning
Symbolic of new beginnings and a time for seeking God’s direction and mercy at the start of the day.
Teaching Points
Trust in God’s Loving Devotion
David’s plea to hear God’s loving devotion highlights the importance of trusting in God’s steadfast love. In times of uncertainty, believers are encouraged to rely on God’s unchanging nature.

The Importance of Morning Devotion
Starting the day with God sets the tone for the rest of the day. Morning devotion is a time to seek God’s presence, guidance, and strength.

Seeking God’s Guidance
David’s request for God to teach him the way to walk underscores the need for divine direction. Believers should continually seek God’s wisdom in their daily decisions.

Lifting Up the Soul to God
Lifting up one’s soul signifies surrender and dependence on God. It is an act of worship and trust, acknowledging God as the source of life and guidance.

What Does Psalm 143:8 Mean
Let me hear Your loving devotion in the morning

• David starts the day longing to “hear” God’s ḥesed—His loyal, covenant-keeping love. This is more than an emotion; it is a request that God would actively make His steadfast care unmistakable at sunrise, when new mercies dawn (Lamentations 3:22-23; Psalm 90:14).

• By asking to hear it, David expects God to speak through His word and through providence. Just as Isaiah 50:4 pictures the Lord awakening His servant “morning by morning” to listen, the psalmist believes revelation is fresh and personal each day.

• The verse reminds us that spiritual vitality flows from meeting God first, before voices of the world crowd in.

for I have put my trust in You

• The plea rests on a settled choice: “I have put my trust.” Trust is not vague optimism; it is deliberate confidence in God’s character and promises (Proverbs 3:5-6; Psalm 5:11).

• Because faith anchors him, David reasons that God will answer. Divine faithfulness and human reliance work together—“He will cover you with His feathers… My God, in whom I trust” (Psalm 91:2-4).

• The line challenges the reader: have I transferred my weight from self-effort to the Rock that cannot move?

Teach me the way I should walk

• Having treasured God’s love and affirmed trust, David seeks practical direction. He wants the “way,” not merely information. Psalm 25:4-5 echoes the same heart: “Show me Your ways, O LORD… for You are the God of my salvation.”

• Guidance is relational; the God who loves also instructs. Isaiah 30:21 promises, “Your ears will hear a word behind you: ‘This is the way; walk in it.’”

• In the New Testament, Jesus fulfills this request by declaring, “I am the way” (John 14:6). Aligning with Him clarifies every step.

• Key takeaway: God’s will is discovered by disciples eager to obey, not merely curious to know (Micah 6:8).

for to You I lift up my soul

• The prayer closes with total surrender. To “lift up” the soul is to present the entire inner life—desires, fears, decisions—to the Lord (Psalm 25:1; 86:4).

• Lifting implies expectation; the psalmist raises his empty hands for God to fill. Jesus invites the same posture: “Come to Me, all you who are weary… and you will find rest for your souls” (Matthew 11:28-29).

• When soul, mind, and will are elevated toward God, guidance becomes more than a map; it turns into communion with the Guide.

summary

Psalm 143:8 paints a sunrise pattern for every believer: begin the day by experiencing God’s steadfast love, renew explicit trust, ask for clear direction, and yield the whole soul to Him. Such morning encounters cultivate confidence, obedience, and intimacy with the God who never fails to speak, lead, and uphold those who seek Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) In the morning.--Comp. Psalm 90:14. The expression either means "early," or is figurative of the dawn of hope and salvation.

The way wherein I should walk--i.e., the way at once of duty and safety.

I lift up my soul.--Or, my desire.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - Cause me to hear thy loving-kindness in the morning; i.e. early, speedily (comp. Psalm 46:5; Psalm 90:14). For in thee do I trust. His utter trust in God gives him a claim to be beard and helped. Cause me to know the way wherein I should walk; i.e. illumine me, so that I may perceive the course which I ought to follow (comp. Psalm 5:8, "Make thy way straight before my face"). For I lift up my soul unto thee. Again, a sort of claim seems to be urged, as in clause 2.
