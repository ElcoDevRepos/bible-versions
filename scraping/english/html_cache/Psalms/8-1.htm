Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For the choirmaster.
This phrase indicates that the psalm was intended for public worship and was directed to the leader of music in the temple. The role of the choirmaster was crucial in ancient Israelite worship, as music was a central part of their religious expression. This highlights the communal aspect of the psalm, meant to be sung and experienced collectively.

According to Gittith.
The term "Gittith" is believed to refer to a musical instrument or a tune associated with Gath, a Philistine city. This suggests a cultural exchange and the influence of neighboring regions on Israelite worship practices. It also reflects the universality of music as a form of worship, transcending cultural boundaries.

A Psalm of David.
David, the attributed author, was the second king of Israel and known for his musical and poetic skills. His authorship lends the psalm royal authority and a personal touch, as David often expressed his deep relationship with God through psalms. This connection to David also foreshadows the messianic lineage, as Jesus is often referred to as the Son of David.

O LORD, our Lord,
The use of "LORD" (YHWH) and "Lord" (Adonai) emphasizes both the covenantal name of God and His sovereign authority. This duality reflects the intimate yet reverent relationship believers have with God. It underscores the personal and communal acknowledgment of God's rule.

how majestic is Your name in all the earth!
The majesty of God's name signifies His reputation, character, and authority. This phrase celebrates God's universal sovereignty and the recognition of His greatness throughout the world. It echoes themes found in other scriptures, such as Isaiah 6:3, where God's glory fills the earth.

You have set Your glory above the heavens.
This statement elevates God's glory beyond the physical realm, suggesting His transcendence and supreme power. It aligns with biblical themes of God's creation and dominion over the universe, as seen in Genesis 1 and Psalm 19. This also points to the New Testament revelation of Christ, who embodies God's glory and reigns supreme.

What Does Psalm 8:1 Mean
For the choirmaster.

David begins with a musical instruction, signaling that this psalm is meant for public worship led by the temple choir. It reminds us that praise is not a private hobby but a congregational calling (Psalm 68:24–26). Like the Levites who “stood under the direction of their father to sing in the house of the LORD” (1 Chronicles 25:6), every generation is summoned to lift its voice so that God’s greatness is heard in the assembly.

According to Gittith.

The phrase also heads Psalm 81 and 84, both exuberant songs of celebration. Whether it points to a particular tune, instrument, or style associated with Gath, the idea is clear: worship should be joy-filled and skillfully expressed (Psalm 33:3; 149:3). By specifying the manner of praise, David shows that the God who orders the stars (Isaiah 40:26) cares about the order and excellence of our music.

A Psalm of David.

The shepherd-king who slew Goliath (1 Samuel 17:45-47) and ruled Israel (2 Samuel 5:1-5) also penned worship for God’s people. His life—marked by humble beginnings, victorious faith, and repentant devotion—models how personal experience fuels heartfelt praise (2 Samuel 22:1-4; Acts 13:22). When we sing Psalm 8, we stand with David, confessing that every success and every breath come from the Lord’s hand.

O LORD, our Lord

The first “LORD” is God’s covenant name (YHWH), declaring His self-existent, unchanging nature (Exodus 3:15). The second “Lord” affirms His rulership over us personally. Nothing about this address is remote; the Creator of galaxies is also the Shepherd who knows His sheep by name (Psalm 23:1; John 10:14). By pairing “LORD” and “our Lord,” David unites transcendence with intimacy, inviting us to bow in awe while resting in belonging.

how majestic is Your name in all the earth!

Majesty speaks of kingly splendor—strength, beauty, and authority on display everywhere we look (Psalm 104:1-2). God’s “name” sums up His character—holy, righteous, merciful, and faithful (Exodus 34:6-7). This majesty is not localized to Israel’s borders; it saturates “all the earth,” echoing Habakkuk 2:14, “the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD.” Ultimately, the universal praise David anticipates finds its fulfillment as “at the name of Jesus every knee should bow” (Philippians 2:9-11).

You have set Your glory above the heavens.

If earth brims with majesty, the heavens put God’s glory on a higher shelf altogether. The night sky is a showcase of His craftsmanship (Psalm 19:1), yet His splendor still surpasses what stars can display. He is exalted “above all blessing and praise” (Nehemiah 9:5), enthroned far beyond the visible cosmos (Isaiah 55:9; Revelation 4:11). The verse blends nearness (“in all the earth”) with exaltation (“above the heavens”), reminding us that while God is gloriously other, He is not distant—a truth confirmed when the infinite Word became flesh and dwelt among us (John 1:14).

summary

Psalm 8:1 opens with a call to corporate, joyful, and skillful worship, penned by David for the temple choir. It celebrates the covenant God who is both universally majestic and personally near. His name reveals perfect character; His glory eclipses the heavens yet fills the earth. As we join the song, we proclaim that the One who created and sustains all things reigns as our Lord, worthy of honor in every corner of creation and every part of our lives.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) O Lord our Lord.--Jehovah our Lord. For the first time in the Book of Psalms the personal feeling is consciously lost sight of in a larger, a national, or possibly human feeling. The poet recognises God's relation to the whole of mankind as to the whole material creation. Thus the hymn appropriately lent itself to the use of the congregation in public worship, though it does not follow that this was the object of its composition.

Excellent.--The LXX. and Vulg., "wonderful." Better, great or exalted.

Who hast set . . .--The. translation of this clause is uncertain. It must be determined by the parallelism, and by the fact that the poet, in Psalm 8:4, merely expands the thought he had before expressed. There is plainly some error in the text since it is ungrammatical. The proposed emendations vary considerably. The ancient versions also disagree. The Authorised Version may be retained, since it meets all the requirements of the context, and is etymologically correct; though, grammatically, Ewald's correction, which also agrees with the Vulg., is preferable, "Thou whose splendour is raised above the heavens." The precise thought in the poet's mind has also been the subject of contention. Some take the clause to refer to the praises raised in Jehovah's honour higher than the heavens, a thought parallel to the preceding clause; others, to the visible glory spread over the sky. Others see an antithesis. God's glory is displayed on earth in His name, His real glory is above the heavens. Probably only a general sense of the majesty of Him "that is higher than the highest" (Ecclesiastes 5:8), and "whom the heaven of heavens cannot contain" (1Kings 8:27), occupied the poet's mind.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - O Lord our Lord. In the original, Jehovah Adoneynu; i.e. "Jehovah, who art our sovereign Lord and Master." As David is here the mouthpiece of humanity, praising God for mercies common to all men, he uses the plural pronoun instead of the singular one. How excellent is thy Name in all the earth! or, "How glorious is thy Name!" (Kay, Cheyne). Who hast set thy glory above the heavens. It is difficult to obtain this sense from the present Hebrew text; but some corruption of the text is suspected.
