The wicked scheme against the righteous
This phrase highlights the ongoing conflict between the wicked and the righteous, a theme prevalent throughout the Psalms and the entire Bible. The term "wicked" refers to those who live in opposition to God's laws and principles. In biblical history, figures like Cain, who plotted against Abel, exemplify this behavior. The scheming of the wicked is often characterized by deceit and malice, as seen in the actions of figures like Haman in the Book of Esther. This conflict is not only physical but spiritual, reflecting the broader battle between good and evil. The righteous, those who follow God's ways, are often targets of such schemes, as their lives stand in contrast to the wicked. This tension is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus warns His followers of persecution for righteousness' sake (Matthew 5:10-12).

and gnash their teeth at them
Gnashing of teeth is a biblical expression of intense anger or frustration. In the cultural context of the Bible, it signifies a deep-seated animosity and a desire to harm. This imagery is used in other parts of Scripture, such as in Acts 7:54, where the crowd gnashes their teeth at Stephen before stoning him. It conveys the idea of the wicked being consumed by their hatred for the righteous. This phrase also foreshadows the ultimate judgment and separation of the wicked from the righteous, as described in Matthew 13:41-42, where gnashing of teeth is associated with the fate of those who reject God. The righteous, however, are called to respond with patience and trust in God's justice, as seen in the life of Jesus, who, despite being reviled, did not retaliate but entrusted Himself to God (1 Peter 2:23).

Teaching Points
Understanding the Conflict
Recognize that the tension between the wicked and the righteous is a recurring theme in Scripture, reflecting the spiritual battle between good and evil.

Trust in God's Justice
Despite the schemes of the wicked, believers are called to trust in God's ultimate justice and timing.

Responding to Persecution
Christians are encouraged to respond to persecution with faith and patience, knowing that God sees and will act.

Living Righteously
The call to live righteously remains, even in the face of opposition, as a testimony to God's transformative power.

Hope in God's Promises
Hold onto the promises of God for deliverance and vindication, which provide hope and strength in difficult times.

What Does Psalm 37:12 Mean
The wicked

• Scripture presents the “wicked” as those who live in stubborn rebellion against God, much like the chaff blown away in Psalm 1:4–6.

• Their character is marked by hostility toward truth—Proverbs 4:14–17 speaks of people who “cannot sleep unless they do evil.”

• Jesus identifies such hearts in John 3:19–20, where men love darkness rather than light.

• Here in Psalm 37 they stand in stark contrast to the righteous, illustrating the ongoing clash between those walking in unbelief and those trusting the Lord.

scheme against the righteous

• “Scheme” highlights intentional, calculated opposition—Psalm 21:11 says, “Though they intend You harm and devise a wicked plan, they will not prevail.”

• These plots can be social, legal, or personal, echoing what David experienced in 1 Samuel 23:9–14 when Saul laid traps for him.

• God’s people are not left unprotected; Psalm 37:12 appears within a larger promise that “the LORD laughs at him, for He sees his day is coming” (Psalm 37:13).

• The righteous—those justified by faith (Romans 5:1)—are often targets precisely because their lives expose evil (1 Peter 4:3–4).

and gnash their teeth at them

• “Gnash” portrays visceral anger and hatred. Acts 7:54 records that when Stephen spoke God’s truth, his accusers “gnashed their teeth at him.”

• Job 16:9 pictures similar hostility: “He gnashes at me with His teeth; my adversary sharpens his gaze on me.”

• Jesus warns of eternal judgment where there will be “weeping and gnashing of teeth” (Luke 13:28), tying this phrase to stubborn rejection of God.

• The verse therefore paints a vivid picture: the wicked are not merely annoyed; they are enraged, demonstrating that spiritual opposition can be fierce yet ultimately futile.

summary

Psalm 37:12 exposes the mindset of those who defy God: deliberate plotting and intense resentment aimed at the righteous. Yet the wider psalm reassures believers that the Lord sees every scheme, laughs at their empty rage, and secures the future of His people. Trusting Him, we need not fear the plots or the teeth of the wicked, for their hostility is temporary, while God’s justice and our inheritance are eternal.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - The wicked plotteth against the just (comp. Psalm 31:13; Psalm 35:4, 7, etc.). Wicked men commonly lay their plots against the righteous, as being less likely to suspect them, and perhaps as less likely to resist their machinations. And gnasheth upon him with his teeth (comp. Psalm 35:16).
