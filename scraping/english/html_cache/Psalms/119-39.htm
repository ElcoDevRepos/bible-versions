Turn away the disgrace I dread
This phrase reflects a plea for divine intervention to remove shame or dishonor. In the biblical context, disgrace often results from sin or failure to uphold God's laws. The psalmist's dread indicates a deep awareness of the consequences of sin, which in ancient Israel could lead to social ostracism or divine punishment. The request to "turn away" suggests repentance and a desire for restoration. This aligns with the broader biblical theme of God as a redeemer who removes shame, as seen in Isaiah 54:4, where God promises to take away the shame of His people. The concept of disgrace also ties into the New Testament, where Jesus bears the ultimate disgrace on the cross, providing a way for believers to be free from shame.

for Your judgments are good
Here, the psalmist acknowledges the inherent goodness of God's judgments, which are His decrees and laws. This reflects a trust in God's moral order and the belief that His commandments are designed for the well-being of His people. The term "judgments" can also refer to God's decisions in matters of justice, emphasizing His role as a righteous judge. This is consistent with the portrayal of God throughout the Old Testament, where His laws are seen as perfect and life-giving, as in Psalm 19:7-9. The goodness of God's judgments is further affirmed in the New Testament, where Jesus fulfills the law and embodies God's perfect will, as seen in Matthew 5:17. The psalmist's recognition of the goodness of God's judgments underscores a commitment to live according to divine principles, trusting in their ultimate benefit.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep longing for God's guidance and deliverance from disgrace.

2. God
The central figure to whom the psalmist directs his plea, acknowledging His righteous judgments.

3. Disgrace
The shame or dishonor that the psalmist fears, which could be due to personal sin, external persecution, or failure to uphold God's laws.

4. Judgments
Refers to God's decrees, laws, and decisions, which the psalmist affirms as inherently good and just.

5. Hebrew Context
The original Hebrew word for "disgrace" (cherpah) often implies public shame or reproach, while "judgments" (mishpatim) denotes God's legal decisions or ordinances.
Teaching Points
Understanding Disgrace
Recognize that disgrace can stem from both internal failures and external accusations. Seek God's help in overcoming both.

Value of God's Judgments
Embrace God's judgments as inherently good, trusting that His ways lead to righteousness and life.

Prayer for Deliverance
Regularly pray for God to turn away disgrace, acknowledging our dependence on His mercy and guidance.

Trust in God's Goodness
Cultivate a heart that trusts in the goodness of God's judgments, even when they challenge or correct us.

Living Without Fear of Shame
Live confidently in the assurance that God removes our disgrace through His grace and truth.

What Does Psalm 119:39 Mean
Turn away

The psalmist begins with a direct call for divine intervention. He is not asking for mere relief but for God Himself to act: “Turn away…”

• This request acknowledges that only the LORD has the authority and power to remove what burdens us (Psalm 25:20, “Guard my soul and deliver me; let me not be put to shame, for I take refuge in You,”).

• It is a faith–filled appeal, much like Peter’s cry, “Lord, save me!” when sinking (Matthew 14:30).

• At its core, the phrase models humble dependence on God rather than self-effort (Proverbs 3:5–6).

the disgrace

“Disgrace” points to shame or reproach that threatens the believer’s testimony.

• Scripture shows that disgrace may come from personal failure (Psalm 51:1), from enemies’ mockery (Psalm 69:7), or from life circumstances that appear to contradict God’s favor (Job 19:2-3).

• Whatever its source, disgrace is intolerable to the one who loves God’s name because it clouds His glory.

• God promises vindication: “Those who hope in Me will not be put to shame” (Isaiah 49:23).

I dread

The psalmist admits he “dreads” this disgrace—he feels real fear.

• Fear in itself is not sin; it is the launching point for trust (Psalm 56:3, “When I am afraid, I put my trust in You,”).

• He openly names the dread rather than hiding it, reflecting the honesty God invites (Psalm 62:8).

• By confessing fear, he shifts the weight onto God’s shoulders, echoing 1 Peter 5:7.

for Your judgments are good

Here is the anchor of his plea: God’s “judgments,” His decisions, decrees, and Word, “are good.”

• The psalmist reminds himself—and God—of the unchanging goodness of divine verdicts (Psalm 19:9, “The judgments of the LORD are true, being altogether righteous,”).

• Because God’s rulings are good, any disgrace ultimately cannot stand; His verdict of grace overrules all condemnation (Romans 8:1).

• Trust in God’s judgments fuels endurance under pressure (James 1:12) and breeds confidence that righteousness will prevail (Romans 7:12).

summary

The verse traces a simple yet powerful movement: a cry for rescue (“Turn away”), a naming of the burden (“the disgrace”), an honest admission of fear (“I dread”), and a firm grounding in God’s character (“for Your judgments are good”). The believer who clings to the goodness of God’s Word need not live under shame; the Lord Himself turns away every reproach and replaces dread with confident hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(39) My reproach which I fear.--The word for fear is an unusual one, used in Deuteronomy 9:19; Deuteronomy 28:60, for very strong dread. The reproach may be either the disgrace in God's sight of violating His commands, or, as the context (Psalm 119:42) suggests, a reproach from men for keeping God's law.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 39. - Turn away my reproach, which I fear; i.e. "turn away from me the only reproach of which I am afraid - the reproach of transgressing against thy Law" (comp. ver. 31). For thy judgments are good. Right in themselves, and conducive to man's happiness.
