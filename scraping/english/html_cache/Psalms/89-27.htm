I will indeed appoint him as My firstborn
The term "firstborn" in biblical context often signifies preeminence and special status rather than birth order. In ancient Near Eastern culture, the firstborn son held a position of privilege and authority. This phrase can be seen as a messianic prophecy, pointing to Jesus Christ, who is referred to as the "firstborn over all creation" (Colossians 1:15). The appointment by God indicates divine choice and favor, reminiscent of God's covenant with David, where God promises to establish his lineage and kingdom (2 Samuel 7:12-16). This appointment underscores the idea of a chosen leader who holds a unique relationship with God, reflecting the special status of Israel as God's "firstborn son" (Exodus 4:22).

the highest of the kings of the earth
This phrase emphasizes supremacy and unparalleled authority. In the historical context, it reflects the Davidic covenant, where God promises that David's line will endure forever and his throne will be established (Psalm 89:3-4). The "highest" king signifies a ruler above all earthly powers, a theme echoed in the New Testament with Jesus Christ, who is described as "King of kings and Lord of lords" (Revelation 19:16). This supremacy is not just political but spiritual, indicating a reign that transcends earthly kingdoms. The phrase also connects to the eschatological hope of a future messianic kingdom where Christ will reign supreme, fulfilling the promises made to David and establishing justice and righteousness on earth.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ethan the Ezrahite
The author of Psalm 89, who is believed to be a wise man during the time of King Solomon. He is credited with writing this psalm, which reflects on God's covenant with David.

2. David
The king of Israel to whom God made a covenant promising that his descendants would rule forever. This verse is part of a larger passage that speaks of God's promises to David.

3. God
The speaker in this verse, who is making a declaration about His chosen one, often understood as a reference to the Messiah in Christian theology.

4. Firstborn
A term used to denote preeminence and special status. In the context of this verse, it signifies a position of authority and honor.

5. Kings of the Earth
Refers to earthly rulers, highlighting the supremacy of the one appointed by God over all other kings.
Teaching Points
Understanding Firstborn
In biblical terms, "firstborn" signifies preeminence rather than birth order. It is a title of honor and authority, as seen in the context of Christ's supremacy.

Messianic Fulfillment
This verse is often seen as a prophecy pointing to Jesus Christ, who fulfills the role of the ultimate King and the firstborn of all creation.

God's Sovereignty
The appointment of the "firstborn" by God underscores His sovereign will and plan, reminding believers of His ultimate authority over all earthly powers.

Covenant Faithfulness
God's promise to David and its fulfillment in Christ demonstrates His faithfulness to His covenants, encouraging believers to trust in His promises.

Christ's Supremacy
Recognizing Jesus as the highest of the kings of the earth calls Christians to submit to His authority and live under His lordship.

What Does Psalm 89:27 Mean
I will indeed appoint him

• The statement underscores God’s personal action—He is the One who installs the king, echoing 2 Samuel 7:12-14 where He promises David an everlasting throne.

• “Indeed” stresses certainty; Numbers 23:19 reminds us God does not lie.

• Appointment by God conveys divine authority, not mere human coronation (Psalm 2:6; Acts 13:22-23).

• This pledge begins with David but looks forward to the Messiah, Jesus, whom God “has made both Lord and Christ” (Acts 2:36).

as My firstborn

• In Scripture “firstborn” speaks of rank and privilege (Exodus 4:22; Jeremiah 31:9).

• David, the youngest son, was elevated to firstborn status in royal privilege (Psalm 78:70-71).

• Ultimately the title points to Christ:

– Colossians 1:15, 18 calls Him “the firstborn of all creation” and “firstborn from the dead.”

– Romans 8:29 declares Him “firstborn among many brothers,” giving believers assurance of adoption.

• Being God’s “firstborn” guarantees inheritance rights—Psalm 2:8 promises the nations as His possession.

the highest of the kings of the earth

• The phrase proclaims universal supremacy: Revelation 19:16 names Jesus “King of kings.”

• Psalm 72:11 envisions all kings bowing before the Davidic ruler, fulfilled when “every knee will bow” (Philippians 2:10-11).

• “Highest” affirms absolute sovereignty; 1 Timothy 6:15 calls Him “the blessed and only Sovereign.”

• The promise is literal and future-oriented: Revelation 11:15 announces, “The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of His Christ, and He will reign forever and ever.”

summary

Psalm 89:27 guarantees that God Himself installs the chosen king, grants Him the unrivaled status of firstborn, and exalts Him above every earthly ruler. Rooted in the historic covenant with David, the verse reaches its fullest realization in Jesus Christ, whose divine appointment, inherited rights, and worldwide reign are certain, literal, and everlasting.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) Firstborn.--Jesse's youngest son became the firstborn, the favourite son of God. Here, of course, the epithet is extended to all the Davidic succession.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - Also I will make him my firstborn. There is but one true "Firstborn" - "the Only Begotten of the Father." All other so called "firstborns" - as Israel (Exodus 4:22), Ephraim (Jeremiah 31:9), David - are reflections or representatives, in some way or other, of the real and only true "Firstborn." Higher than the kings of the earth; literally, the most high above the kings of the earth; i.e. standing to the other "kings of the earth" as "the Most High" to his angelic ministers.
