Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You are good
This phrase acknowledges the inherent goodness of God, a central theme throughout the Bible. The goodness of God is foundational to His character and is reflected in His creation and actions. In Genesis 1, after each act of creation, God declares it "good," underscoring His nature. The goodness of God is also a source of comfort and assurance for believers, as seen in passages like Nahum 1:7, which states, "The LORD is good, a refuge in times of trouble." This goodness is not just moral perfection but also encompasses His kindness, mercy, and love.

and You do what is good
This part emphasizes that God's actions are consistent with His nature. His deeds are always righteous and just, as seen in Deuteronomy 32:4, which describes God as "a God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is He." The historical context of Israel's journey, including the Exodus and the giving of the Law, demonstrates God's good actions in guiding, protecting, and providing for His people. This assurance of God's good deeds is a call to trust in His plans and purposes, even when circumstances are challenging.

teach me Your statutes
This request reflects a desire for divine instruction and understanding of God's laws. The term "statutes" refers to the decrees and commandments given by God, particularly those found in the Torah. The psalmist's plea for teaching indicates a humble acknowledgment of the need for God's guidance in living a life that aligns with His will. This echoes the sentiment found in Psalm 25:4-5, where the psalmist asks God to "show me Your ways, LORD, teach me Your paths." The desire for instruction is also a recognition of the transformative power of God's Word, as seen in 2 Timothy 3:16-17, which speaks of Scripture's role in teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep love for God's law and a desire for understanding and obedience.

2. God
The central figure in this verse, described as inherently good and the source of all good actions.

3. The Law/Statutes
Refers to God's commandments and teachings, which the Psalmist seeks to learn and follow.
Teaching Points
Understanding God's Nature
Recognize that God's nature is inherently good. This understanding should shape our trust in Him and His plans for our lives.

Seeking God's Guidance
Like the Psalmist, we should earnestly seek to learn and understand God's statutes, knowing that His teachings are rooted in His goodness.

Reflecting God's Goodness
As followers of Christ, we are called to reflect God's goodness in our actions and interactions with others.

Trust in God's Goodness
In times of uncertainty or difficulty, remember that God is good and His actions are for our ultimate benefit.

Prayer for Instruction
Regularly pray for God to teach and guide you in His ways, trusting that His statutes are for your good.

What Does Psalm 119:68 Mean
You are good

The psalmist begins by stating a fact, not an opinion: God Himself is good.

• Goodness is an attribute of God’s very nature (Exodus 34:6; Psalm 34:8; Nahum 1:7).

• Jesus affirmed, “No one is good except God alone” (Mark 10:18), underscoring that all true goodness originates in Him.

• Because God is unchanging (James 1:17), His goodness is constant; believers can rely on it in every circumstance.

• Remembering God’s goodness guards the heart against doubting His character when life feels harsh or confusing (Psalm 27:13).

and You do what is good

What God is internally, He expresses externally.

• “The LORD is righteous in all His ways and kind in all His deeds” (Psalm 145:17).

• Even hard providences work for good to those who love Him (Romans 8:28), because His actions flow from His flawless wisdom and steadfast love (Deuteronomy 32:4).

• Seeing both God’s character and His works as good steadies faith: we interpret our experiences through who He is rather than judging Him by our experiences.

• This perspective encourages gratitude; every good gift comes from Him (James 1:17).

teach me Your statutes

Knowing God’s goodness leads naturally to wanting His guidance.

• If the Author of Scripture is good, His commands must also be good (Psalm 19:7-10).

• The psalmist asks not merely to read but to be taught—an ongoing, relational process (Psalm 25:4-5; John 14:26).

• Obedience becomes a response to grace, not a burdensome duty (1 John 5:3).

• God’s statutes equip believers “for every good work” (2 Timothy 3:16-17); learning them aligns life with His benevolent purposes.

summary

Psalm 119:68 links God’s unchanging goodness to His perfectly good actions and invites us to embrace His instruction. Because He is good and does good, His statutes are trustworthy. Confidence in His character fuels a desire to learn and live out His Word, anchoring our hearts in the certainty that every command and every circumstance is framed by His unfailing goodness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(68) It is characteristic of this psalm that the higher the conception of the Divine nature, the more earnest becomes the prayer for knowledge of His will in relation to conduct.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 68. - Thou art good, and doest good. Even chastening is a proof of thy goodness. By it thou "doest good" to thy servants (see Hebrews 12:10, 11). Teach me thy statutes. Impress thy Law upon me, even though it be by chastening.
