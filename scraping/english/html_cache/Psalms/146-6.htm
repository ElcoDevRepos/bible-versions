the Maker of heaven and earth, the sea, and everything in them
This phrase emphasizes God's role as the Creator of all things, a foundational belief in the Judeo-Christian tradition. The reference to God as the "Maker" aligns with Genesis 1:1, where God is described as creating the heavens and the earth. This creation account establishes God's sovereignty and power over the universe. The inclusion of "the sea" highlights the ancient Near Eastern understanding of the sea as a symbol of chaos, which God has dominion over, as seen in Psalm 89:9. The phrase "everything in them" underscores the comprehensive nature of God's creation, echoing Colossians 1:16, which speaks of all things being created through and for Christ. This reflects the belief in God's ongoing involvement and sustenance of creation, as seen in Acts 17:24-25.

He remains faithful forever
This statement affirms God's eternal faithfulness, a key attribute of His character. The Hebrew word for "faithful" (אֱמוּנָה, emunah) conveys a sense of steadfastness and reliability. This is echoed in Lamentations 3:22-23, which speaks of God's mercies being new every morning and His great faithfulness. The concept of God's eternal faithfulness is also seen in Deuteronomy 7:9, where God is described as keeping His covenant of love to a thousand generations. This assurance of God's unchanging nature provides comfort and hope, as seen in Hebrews 13:8, which declares Jesus Christ as the same yesterday, today, and forever. This phrase reassures believers of God's unending commitment to His promises and His people.

What Does Psalm 146:6 Mean
The Maker

Psalm 146:6 begins by identifying God as “the Maker.” Right away, the verse anchors our trust in the One who created everything that exists. Genesis 1:1 straightforwardly declares, “In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth,” while John 1:3 confirms, “Through Him all things were made.” Because God is the Maker, He has ultimate authority and power; nothing is outside His control. This truth invites us to place confident reliance on Him rather than on human leaders (Psalm 146:3-4).

of heaven and earth

The phrase expands the scope of His creative work: the “heavens” (everything above—sun, moon, stars, unseen spiritual realms) and the “earth” (everything below—mountains, fields, cities). Isaiah 45:12 echoes, “It is I who made the earth and created man upon it; My own hands stretched out the heavens.” Knowing God crafted both realms reminds us He rules over the spiritual and the physical alike. Our prayers and daily concerns matter to the One who speaks galaxies into being yet numbers every hair on our heads (Matthew 10:29-31).

the sea

Adding “the sea” highlights another realm often viewed as untamable and mysterious. Psalm 89:9 celebrates, “You rule the raging sea; when its waves mount up, You still them.” Jesus later demonstrates this authority in Mark 4:39 by commanding a storm to be still. For believers, the mention of the sea reassures us that no chaotic circumstance can overpower the Creator who set boundaries for the oceans (Job 38:8-11).

and everything in them

This sweeping clause leaves nothing out: every creature, every ecosystem, every molecule. Colossians 1:16-17 affirms, “All things were created through Him and for Him… in Him all things hold together.” Because God sustains “everything,” He can sustain you. When anxiety arises, Philippians 4:6-7 urges us to present every request to the One who holds every detail of creation together.

He remains faithful forever

The verse concludes by shifting from God’s power to His character: perpetual faithfulness. Numbers 23:19 reminds us God does not change His mind like humans. Lamentations 3:22-23 adds, “His mercies never fail; they are new every morning.” Knowing the all-powerful Creator is also endlessly faithful provides a solid foundation for hope. Hebrews 10:23 encourages, “Let us hold resolutely to the hope we profess, for He who promised is faithful.” His past acts of creation guarantee His present reliability and future promises.

summary

Psalm 146:6 paints a comprehensive picture: the God who made heaven, earth, sea, and every living thing is the same God who will never waver in His loyalty to His people. His limitless power and unending faithfulness invite us to trust Him fully, worship Him wholeheartedly, and rest securely in His unchanging care.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Truth.--Or, faithfulness. The connection of this feature of the Divine character with the creative act is worthy of notice. That act alone was for the universe a promise and pledge, just as the covenant was a peculiar promise to Israel. Tennyson has put the same thought into verse:

"Thou madest man, he knows not why;

He thinks he was not made to die;

And Thou hast made him: Thou art just."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Which made heaven, and earth, the sea, and all that therein is. Who is, therefore, an omnipotent Help, the very opposite of "the son of man, in whom is no help" at all (ver. 3) Which keepeth truth for ever; i.e. who keeps all his promises, and has promised his help to all such as call upon him faithfully (Psalm 145:18).
