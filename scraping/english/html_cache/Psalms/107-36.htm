He causes the hungry to settle there
This phrase highlights God's providence and care for those in need. The "hungry" can be understood both literally and spiritually, representing those who are in physical need and those who are spiritually seeking. In biblical history, God often provided for His people in times of need, such as when He provided manna for the Israelites in the wilderness (Exodus 16). The act of causing the hungry to settle suggests a divine intervention where God leads people to a place of sustenance and stability. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God as a provider and sustainer, as seen in passages like Psalm 23:1, "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want."

that they may establish a city
The establishment of a city signifies a transition from wandering or instability to permanence and community. In ancient times, cities were centers of security, culture, and economic activity. The biblical narrative often contrasts the nomadic life of the patriarchs with the settled life in the Promised Land. The idea of establishing a city can also be seen as a fulfillment of God's promises, such as the promise to Abraham that his descendants would inherit the land (Genesis 15:18-21). This phrase can also be seen as a type of the New Jerusalem, the ultimate city of God described in Revelation 21, where God's people will dwell eternally.

in which to dwell
Dwelling implies a sense of belonging and rest. In the biblical context, dwelling in a city provided protection and a sense of community. This phrase echoes the biblical promise of God providing a place for His people, as seen in Deuteronomy 12:10, where God promises to give His people rest from their enemies and a place to dwell. Spiritually, this can be seen as a metaphor for the believer's rest in Christ, who provides ultimate security and peace. Jesus' promise in John 14:2-3, where He speaks of preparing a place for His followers, resonates with this idea of dwelling in a secure and eternal home.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Hungry
Represents those in need, both physically and spiritually. In the context of the Psalm, they are those who have been wandering and are now provided for by God.

2. The City
Symbolizes a place of stability, community, and provision. It is a place where the previously wandering and hungry can find rest and establish their lives.

3. God's Provision
The event of God providing for the hungry and allowing them to establish a city is central. It highlights God's role as a provider and sustainer.
Teaching Points
God's Provision for Physical and Spiritual Needs
God is attentive to the needs of His people, providing both physical sustenance and spiritual fulfillment. We can trust Him to meet our needs in His timing and way.

The Importance of Community
Establishing a city signifies the importance of community and fellowship. As believers, we are called to build and be part of communities that reflect God's love and provision.

Trust in God's Timing
The process of moving from hunger to settlement requires patience and trust in God's timing. We are encouraged to remain faithful and hopeful during times of waiting.

God's Faithfulness in Transformation
God transforms desolate situations into places of abundance and stability. This transformation is a testament to His faithfulness and power.

Spiritual Hunger and Fulfillment
Just as God provides for physical hunger, He also satisfies spiritual hunger. We are invited to seek Him earnestly and find fulfillment in His presence.

What Does Psalm 107:36 Mean
He causes

• The verse opens with God as the active subject. Nothing unfolds by chance; “Every good and perfect gift is from above” (James 1:17).

• Psalm 23:2–3 reminds us that “He leads me … He restores my soul,” underscoring His hands-on direction.

• Isaiah 40:26 highlights His power: “By His great might and strength, not one is missing.” The same intentional care guides the homeless toward a home.

the hungry

• God’s heart is for those in need. Psalm 146:7 affirms He “gives food to the hungry.”

• Physical hunger is in view, yet spiritual longing is never far behind. Jesus echoes the promise: “Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness” (Matthew 5:6).

• Luke 1:53 celebrates the same theme: “He has filled the hungry with good things.”

to settle there

• “There” refers to the once-barren land God has just turned into “flowing springs” (Psalm 107:35).

• Settlement speaks of stability. Exodus 33:14 offers the assurance, “My Presence will go with you, and I will give you rest.”

• Psalm 68:6 adds, “God settles the lonely in families.” He doesn’t merely rescue; He roots His people.

that they may establish

• God’s provision empowers purposeful work. Isaiah 58:12 pictures rebuilt ruins: “You will raise up the foundations of many generations.”

• Genesis 12:2 shows the pattern: blessed in order to be a blessing.

• Jeremiah 29:5–7 urges exiles to “build houses and settle down … seek the prosperity of the city,” mirroring Psalm 107’s call to constructive living.

a city in which to dwell

• A city offers security, community, and culture anchored in God’s grace. Proverbs 11:11 notes, “By the blessing of the upright a city is exalted.”

• Hebrews 11:10 lifts our eyes higher: Abraham “was looking forward to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God.”

• Revelation 21:2 culminates the promise with “the Holy City … prepared as a bride adorned for her husband.” Earthly settlements foreshadow the eternal dwelling God is preparing.

summary

Psalm 107:36 paints a literal scene: God transforms wasteland into water-rich ground, brings hungry people there, plants them securely, and enables them to build a thriving city. He is the cause, the provider, the sustainer, and the visionary architect, turning desperate wanderers into settled builders whose community reflects His faithful love and anticipates the ultimate city He will reveal.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 36. - And there he maketh the hungry to dwell. God gives the laud, which he has thus blessed, to some previously famishing people; as he did Canaan to Israel after they had had but scant fare in the wilderness. That they may prepare a city for habitation; literally, and they prepare. It is naturally their first thought to prepare themselves a settled dwelling-place (comp. Genesis 4:17; Genesis 11:4; Genesis 25:16, etc.).
