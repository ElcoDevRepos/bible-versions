Remember, O LORD
This phrase is a plea for God to bring to mind His covenant promises. In the Hebrew context, "remember" often implies action based on a prior commitment. The psalmist is invoking God's faithfulness, as seen in His dealings with Israel throughout history. This reflects the covenant relationship established with figures like Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) and Moses (Exodus 6:7), where God promised to be their God and they His people.

Your compassion and loving devotion
The terms "compassion" and "loving devotion" are central to understanding God's character. "Compassion" (Hebrew: "rachamim") suggests a deep, tender mercy, akin to a parent's love for a child. "Loving devotion" (Hebrew: "chesed") is a steadfast, covenantal love that is loyal and enduring. These attributes are frequently highlighted in the Old Testament, such as in Exodus 34:6-7, where God describes Himself as "abounding in love and faithfulness." This steadfast love is a type of Christ, who embodies God's compassion and loving devotion through His sacrificial death and resurrection.

for they are from age to age
This phrase emphasizes the eternal nature of God's attributes. His compassion and loving devotion are not temporary or fleeting but have been evident throughout all generations. This is consistent with the biblical narrative, where God's faithfulness is demonstrated from creation (Genesis 1) through the patriarchs, the exodus, the monarchy, and the prophets. It points to the unchanging nature of God, as affirmed in Malachi 3:6, "For I the LORD do not change." This eternal aspect is fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who is the same yesterday, today, and forever (Hebrews 13:8), ensuring that God's compassion and loving devotion continue into the New Covenant.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 25, King David, is expressing his reliance on God's mercy and steadfast love. He is a central figure in the Old Testament, known for his deep relationship with God and his role as the second king of Israel.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God used in this verse, emphasizing His eternal nature and faithfulness to His promises.

3. Israel
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of David's plea is rooted in the covenant relationship between God and the nation of Israel.
Teaching Points
Understanding God's Character
God's compassion and loving devotion are central to His nature. As believers, we can trust in His unchanging character, knowing that His mercy is available to us at all times.

The Importance of Remembrance
David's plea for God to "remember" His compassion is a reminder for us to recall God's past faithfulness in our own lives. Reflecting on His goodness strengthens our faith and trust in Him.

Living in Light of God's Mercy
Recognizing God's enduring mercy should inspire us to live lives marked by gratitude and obedience. We are called to extend the same compassion and love to others that we have received from God.

The Eternal Nature of God's Love
God's loving devotion is not limited by time or circumstance. This assurance provides comfort and hope, especially during times of trial and uncertainty.

What Does Psalm 25:6 Mean
Remember

• David is not worried that God might forget; he is inviting the Lord to act on what He already knows.

• Scripture consistently shows that when God “remembers,” He moves in power—Genesis 8:1 “God remembered Noah… and the waters subsided,” Exodus 2:24 “God heard their groaning, and He remembered His covenant…,” Psalm 105:8 “He remembers His covenant forever….”

• By using this word, we are taught to pray boldly: ask the Lord to bring His promises to the forefront of His dealings with us, confident He delights to do so.

O LORD

• The prayer is addressed to the covenant name, the personal “I AM” of Exodus 3:14.

• David’s appeal rests on relationship; the One who spoke from the burning bush is the same who hears today—Psalm 100:3 “Know that the LORD is God.”

• Saying “O LORD” is a confession that no other help can compare—Psalm 16:2 “I said to the LORD, ‘You are my Lord; apart from You I have no good thing.’”

Your compassion

• Compassion is God’s tender, fatherly mercy—Psalm 103:13 “As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear Him.”

• It never fails—Lamentations 3:22 “Because of the LORD’s loving devotion we are not consumed, for His compassions never fail.”

• In Jesus this compassion takes visible form—Matthew 9:36 “When He saw the crowds, He was moved with compassion for them….”

• When we ask God to remember His compassion, we are leaning on a sure, unfailing attribute.

and loving devotion

• The phrase gathers up God’s steadfast, covenant-keeping love—Exodus 34:6 “abounding in loving devotion and faithfulness.”

• It is active and enduring—Psalm 136:1 “Give thanks to the LORD, for He is good. His loving devotion endures forever.”

• It binds God to His people—Hosea 2:19 “I will betroth you to Me forever… in loving devotion and compassion.”

• This devotion reaches its climax at the cross—Ephesians 2:4-5 “But because of His great love for us, God… made us alive with Christ….”

for they are from age to age

• The compassion and loving devotion just described are eternal, not temporary—Psalm 90:2 “From everlasting to everlasting You are God.”

• They do not flicker or fade—James 1:17 “…with whom there is no change or shifting shadow.”

• What sustained saints of old sustains us today—Psalm 103:17 “From everlasting to everlasting the loving devotion of the LORD extends to those who fear Him.”

• We rest on an unchanging Savior—Hebrews 13:8 “Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever.”

summary

Psalm 25:6 teaches us to pray with confidence: call on the covenant-keeping LORD, ask Him to act in line with His unfailing compassion and steadfast love, and rest assured these qualities are eternal. The God who remembered Noah and Israel remembers us; His mercy and loving devotion have no expiration date.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Remember, O Lord, thy tender mercies, and thy loving-kindnesses. Past mercies form a ground for the expectation of future blessings. God's character cannot change; his action as one time will always be consistent and harmonious with his action at another. If he has been kind and merciful to David in the past, David may count on his continuing the same in the future. For they have been ever of old. Not lately only, or to David only, have his mercies been shown, but through all past time, to all his servants, from of old.
