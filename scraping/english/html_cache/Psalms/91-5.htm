You will not fear the terror of the night
This phrase emphasizes divine protection and assurance. In biblical times, night was often associated with danger and uncertainty, as it was a time when enemies could attack under the cover of darkness. The "terror of the night" could refer to any unseen threats or fears that arise when visibility is low and vulnerability is high. This assurance of protection is reminiscent of God's promise to be a shield and refuge, as seen in Psalm 23:4, where the psalmist declares, "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for You are with me." The imagery of night also connects to spiritual darkness, where believers are assured that God's presence dispels fear and provides peace.

nor the arrow that flies by day
The "arrow" symbolizes direct and visible threats, such as warfare or conflict, which were common in ancient times. Arrows were a primary weapon in battles, representing danger that is both seen and immediate. This phrase assures believers of God's protection against such threats, echoing the promise found in Ephesians 6:16, where faith is described as a shield that can extinguish all the flaming arrows of the evil one. The mention of "day" contrasts with "night," suggesting that God's protection is comprehensive, covering both hidden and overt dangers. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God's sovereignty and care over all aspects of life, as seen in passages like Isaiah 54:17, which promises that no weapon formed against God's people will prosper.

What Does Psalm 91:5 Mean
You will not fear the terror of the night

- Darkness amplifies uncertainty, but God’s vigilance never dims. Psalm 121:3-4 promises, “He will not allow your foot to slip; your Protector will not slumber… neither slumber nor sleep.”

- Night terrors include both the physical (robbers, storms, sudden illness) and the spiritual (demonic oppression, anxious thoughts). Psalm 23:4 answers each: “Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for You are with me.”

- Proverbs 3:24 describes the outcome: “When you lie down, you will not be afraid; when you rest, your sleep will be sweet.” Sweet sleep stems from trusting God’s constant oversight.

- Job 5:19-21 widens the scope: “He will rescue you from six calamities… you will not fear destruction when it comes,” underscoring that divine protection covers every hour.

- The verse invites believers to exchange rehearsed worries for rehearsed promises, knowing that God keeps watch all night.

nor the arrow that flies by day

- Daylight exposes believers to open hostility—hostile words, unexpected crises, even literal violence. God sees every threat before it lifts off the bowstring.

- Isaiah 54:17: “No weapon formed against you shall prevail,” echoes the assurance that faith outlasts the enemy’s best weaponry.

- Ephesians 6:16 applies the metaphor spiritually: “Take up the shield of faith, with which you can extinguish all the flaming arrows of the evil one.”

- Psalm 34:7 harmonizes: “The angel of the LORD encamps around those who fear Him, and he delivers them.” Heavenly protection does not clock out at sunrise.

- The result is calm courage: alert but unafraid, confident that every arrow must pass through God’s hand first.

summary

Psalm 91:5 draws a 24-hour circle of safety. Whether dangers lurk in darkness or streak across the midday sky, God’s guardianship is literal, complete, and constant. Freed from paralyzing fear, His people are released to live each day and night in steady, unshakeable trust.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Terror by night.--Possibly a night attack by an enemy. (Comp. Song of Solomon 3:8; Proverbs 3:23-26.) Comp. Milton:

"To bless the doors from nightly harm."

In this case the arrow flying by day would refer to dangers of actual battle. But it is quite possible that the latter may be merely the Oriental expression for the pestilence, since it is still so called by Arabians. "I desired to remove to a less contagious air. I received from Solyman the emperor this message: that the emperor wondered what I meant in desiring to remove my habitation. Is not the pestilence God's arrow, which will always hit his mark?"--Quoted in Spurgeon's Treasury of David, from Busbequin's Travels.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Thou shalt not be afraid for the terror by night. Robbers constituted the chief "terror by night" (see Job 24:14-16; Jeremiah 49:9; Obadiah 1:5); but night attacks on the part of a foreign enemy were not uncommon (Song of Solomon 3:8; Isaiah 15:1). Nor for the arrow that fleth by day. Open war is probably intended, not sirocco, or pestilence, or "the arrows of the Almighty" (Job 6:4). The man who trusts in God will be specially protected in the peril of battle.
