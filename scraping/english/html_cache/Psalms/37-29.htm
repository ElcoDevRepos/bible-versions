The righteous will inherit the land
This phrase reflects a recurring biblical theme where the "righteous" are promised an inheritance. In the Old Testament, the land often symbolizes God's promise and blessing, initially given to Abraham and his descendants (Genesis 12:7). The concept of inheritance is not merely physical but also spiritual, indicating a deeper relationship with God. The righteous, those who live according to God's laws and maintain faithfulness, are assured of this divine promise. This promise is echoed in the Beatitudes, where Jesus states, "Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5), suggesting a future fulfillment in God's kingdom.

and dwell in it forever.
The promise of dwelling in the land "forever" extends beyond temporal possession to eternal security and peace. In the context of ancient Israel, dwelling in the land was associated with stability, prosperity, and God's favor. This eternal aspect points to eschatological hope, where the faithful will experience everlasting life in God's presence. The idea of eternal dwelling is further developed in the New Testament, where believers are promised a new heaven and a new earth (Revelation 21:1-3). This eternal dwelling is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who secures this promise through His death and resurrection, offering believers a place in the eternal kingdom.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Righteous
In the context of Psalm 37, the "righteous" refers to those who live in accordance with God's will, following His commandments and seeking to live a life that is pleasing to Him. They are contrasted with the wicked, who live in opposition to God's ways.

2. The Land
This refers to the Promised Land, a central theme in the Old Testament, symbolizing God's promise and blessing to His people. It represents not only a physical territory but also a place of spiritual rest and divine favor.

3. Inheritance
In biblical terms, inheritance often signifies the blessings and promises of God passed down to His people. It is a recurring theme that underscores God's faithfulness and the eternal nature of His promises.
Teaching Points
The Promise of Inheritance
God's promises are sure and steadfast. The inheritance of the land is a symbol of the eternal blessings that await those who are righteous in His sight.

Living Righteously
To be counted among the righteous, one must live a life that aligns with God's commandments and seeks His will above all else. This involves daily commitment and faithfulness.

Eternal Perspective
The promise of dwelling in the land forever encourages believers to maintain an eternal perspective, focusing on the everlasting rewards rather than temporary worldly gains.

Trust in God's Justice
In a world where the wicked often seem to prosper, Psalm 37:29 reassures believers that God's justice will prevail, and the righteous will ultimately receive their due reward.

What Does Psalm 37:29 Mean
The righteous

• Psalm 37:29 begins with a clear subject: “The righteous.” Scripture consistently paints righteousness as a life aligned with God’s will—both by trusting Him and by walking in obedience. “For the LORD knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish” (Psalm 1:6).

• This righteousness is not self-manufactured. Genesis 15:6 reminds us that Abraham “believed the LORD, and it was credited to him as righteousness,” a theme echoed in Romans 3:22 where righteousness comes “through faith in Jesus Christ to all who believe.”

• Practical expressions of righteousness include:

– Choosing integrity over compromise (Proverbs 11:3).

– Hungering for God’s ways (Matthew 5:6).

– Dependence on the Spirit’s power (Galatians 5:16).

Will inherit

• “Will inherit” speaks of a pledged gift, not a wage. Psalm 37:9 affirms, “Those who hope in the LORD will inherit the land”.

• Inheritance language runs through Scripture: Israel’s tribal allotments (Joshua 14–19), the “inheritance that is imperishable” kept for believers (1 Peter 1:4), and the Spirit as the “pledge of our inheritance” (Ephesians 1:14).

• Key takeaways:

– The promise rests on God’s faithfulness, not human merit.

– It carries both present assurance and future completion.

The land

• In David’s day, “the land” pointed first to the covenant territory given to Israel (Genesis 17:8). For believers today, it foreshadows the entire renewed earth. Romans 4:13 links Abraham’s promise to “heir of the world,” and Revelation 21:1–3 unveils “a new heaven and a new earth… and the dwelling of God is with men”.

• Practical implications:

– God values physical space and intends to redeem creation, not abandon it.

– Every foretaste—peace in your home, justice in your community—anticipates the full inheritance.

And dwell in it forever

• “Dwell” conveys settled, unhurried residence. Psalm 23:6 celebrates, “I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever”.

• Permanence replaces the instability of a fallen world. Psalm 37:18 says, “The inheritance of the blameless will endure forever,” echoed by Jesus’ pledge: “In My Father’s house are many rooms… I go to prepare a place for you” (John 14:2-3).

• The word “forever” stretches the promise into eternity:

– No eviction by enemies (Isaiah 60:21).

– No expiration date, for “they will reign for ever and ever” (Revelation 22:5).

– Complete rest—absence of fear, toil, or loss.

summary

Psalm 37:29 assures that those made right with God will receive a tangible, enduring inheritance. God Himself guarantees the gift, promises a restored creation as the setting, and secures an everlasting home where His people will live in unbroken fellowship with Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - The righteous shall inherit the land, and dwell therein for over (comp. vers. 3, 9, 11, 18, 22, 28, 34; and Proverbs 2:21). Bishop Butler sagaciously remarks that this is the natural tendency of things, if sufficient time be given, and accidental hindrances removed ('Analogy,' pt. 1. ch. 4.).
