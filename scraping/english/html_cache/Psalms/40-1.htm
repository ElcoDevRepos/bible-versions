For the choirmaster.
This phrase indicates that the psalm was intended for public worship and was to be directed by the choirmaster, a leader of music in the temple. The role of the choirmaster was significant in ancient Israelite worship, as music was a central part of their religious expression. This setting suggests that the psalm was not only a personal reflection but also a communal declaration of faith and trust in God.

A Psalm of David.
David, the second king of Israel, is traditionally credited with writing many of the psalms. His life was marked by both triumphs and trials, making his psalms deeply personal and relatable. David's experiences, from shepherd to king, provide a rich backdrop for understanding his reliance on God. This attribution also connects the psalm to the Davidic covenant, where God promised David that his throne would be established forever, a promise ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

I waited patiently for the LORD;
The act of waiting patiently signifies a deep trust and reliance on God's timing and sovereignty. In the Hebrew context, waiting is often associated with hope and expectation. This phrase reflects a posture of faith, acknowledging that God's plans are perfect and His timing is impeccable. The patience described here is not passive but active, involving prayer and trust in God's faithfulness.

He inclined to me and heard my cry.
This phrase illustrates God's responsiveness and compassion. The imagery of God inclining suggests a personal and attentive response, emphasizing His closeness and care for His people. The act of hearing the cry indicates that God is not distant or indifferent but is actively involved in the lives of those who call upon Him. This echoes other scriptures where God is depicted as a refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble (Psalm 46:1). It also foreshadows the New Testament revelation of Jesus Christ, who is the ultimate mediator and intercessor for humanity.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 40, David is expressing his personal experience of waiting on the Lord and receiving His response.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His faithfulness and relationship with His people.

3. The Psalmist's Cry
Represents a time of distress or need, where the psalmist seeks divine intervention.
Teaching Points
The Virtue of Patience
Waiting on the Lord requires patience, a fruit of the Spirit, which is cultivated through trust in God's timing and faithfulness.

God's Attentiveness
The phrase "He inclined to me" suggests that God is attentive and responsive to our cries, demonstrating His personal care and involvement in our lives.

The Power of Prayer
The psalmist's cry is a form of prayer, reminding us of the importance of bringing our needs and concerns before God, trusting that He hears us.

Faith in Action
Waiting patiently is an active expression of faith, where we continue to trust and serve God even when answers are delayed.

Hope in God's Faithfulness
The assurance that God hears and responds to our cries should inspire hope and confidence in His unchanging nature and promises.

What Does Psalm 40:1 Mean
For the choirmaster

David pens this psalm with public worship in mind, signaling that his personal experience is meant to strengthen the entire congregation. Similar superscriptions appear in Psalm 4 and Psalm 8, reminding us that:

• God’s past acts of deliverance are to be celebrated together (Psalm 22:22).

• Shared testimony fuels corporate faith and unity (Hebrews 10:24-25).

This opening phrase also hints that every victory story ultimately belongs to the Lord, not the individual.

A Psalm of David

David’s name cues us to read through the lens of his life—years of flight from Saul (1 Samuel 19-24), the ache of unmet promises, and seasons of sin and repentance (Psalm 32; 2 Samuel 12). The Spirit-inspired title assures us that:

• God works through real history, not myth (Acts 13:36).

• Personal struggle doesn’t disqualify us from proclaiming truth (2 Corinthians 12:9-10).

• The same faithful God who carried David carries every believer (Malachi 3:6).

I waited patiently for the LORD;

The Hebrew idea is prolonged, hope-filled expectancy, not passive resignation. David remembers:

• Waiting that is active—seeking, trusting, obeying (Psalm 37:7; Isaiah 40:31).

• Waiting that has a clear focus: “for the LORD,” not merely for changed circumstances (Lamentations 3:25-26).

• Waiting that is rewarded—God never wastes the pause (Romans 8:24-25).

Lessons for us:

– Patience is faith stretched over time.

– Delays refine motives and reveal God’s sufficiency (James 1:2-4).

He inclined to me and heard my cry.

God’s response is personal and intimate:

• “He inclined” pictures a father bending toward a child (Psalm 18:6).

• He “heard,” guaranteeing attention and action (1 John 5:14-15).

• David’s “cry” shows that honest, urgent prayer is welcomed by God (Psalm 34:17).

Encouragements:

– The Lord’s ear is never distant (Psalm 116:1-2).

– Prayer bridges the waiting and the rescue (Philippians 4:6-7).

– Every believer can expect divine intervention, though timing and methods vary (Ephesians 3:20).

summary

Psalm 40:1 testifies that patient, faith-filled waiting brings God’s gracious response. David’s personal story, offered for congregational worship, assures us that when we keep our eyes on the Lord, persistent prayer is met by His attentive, rescuing love.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) I waited patiently.--As the margin shows, this is expressed by the common Hebrew idiom the infinitive absolute with the preterite. We may nearly express it by repetition: I waited and waited.

Inclined . . .--Either intransitive (comp. Judges 16:30), or with ellipse of the word "ear," which usually is found with the verb in this conjugation. (See Psalm 17:6; Psalm 31:2.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - I waited patiently for the Lord; literally, waiting, 1 waited - a common Hebrew idiom, when an idea is to be emphasized. No writer enforces upon us more earnestly than David the duty of awaiting God's pleasure (Psalm 27:14; Psalm 37:7; Psalm 62:1, 5; Psalm 69:3, etc.). And he inclined unto me; literally, bent towards me - an anthropomorphism, but most expressive. And heard my cry; i.e. answered it - gave me what I prayed for.
