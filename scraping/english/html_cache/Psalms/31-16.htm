Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Make Your face shine on Your servant;
This phrase is a plea for God's favor and presence, echoing the priestly blessing in Numbers 6:24-26, where the Lord's face shining upon someone signifies divine approval and blessing. In ancient Near Eastern culture, the face of a king or deity shining upon a subject was a metaphor for favor and protection. The psalmist, likely David, seeks this divine favor, understanding that God's presence is the source of life and blessing. This request also reflects a deep personal relationship with God, where the psalmist identifies as God's servant, indicating humility and submission to God's will.

save me by Your loving devotion.
The plea for salvation is grounded in God's "loving devotion," a translation of the Hebrew word "chesed," which encompasses steadfast love, mercy, and covenant faithfulness. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where God's commitment to His promises is unwavering. The psalmist appeals to God's character, knowing that salvation is not earned but given out of God's faithful love. This concept is echoed throughout the Old Testament, such as in Exodus 34:6-7, where God describes Himself as abounding in steadfast love. In the New Testament, this loving devotion is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, whose sacrificial love provides salvation for all who believe.

Teaching Points
Seeking God's Favor
David's plea for God's face to shine upon him is a request for divine favor and presence. In our lives, we should continually seek God's favor through prayer and obedience.

Understanding God's Loving Devotion
The Hebrew word for "loving devotion" is "chesed," which encompasses God's steadfast love and mercy. Recognizing this attribute of God can deepen our trust in His salvation and care.

Dependence on God for Salvation
David's cry for salvation underscores the belief that true deliverance comes from God alone. We are encouraged to rely on God for both spiritual and physical salvation.

The Power of God's Presence
The shining of God's face symbolizes His presence and blessing. As believers, we should strive to live in the light of God's presence, which brings peace and guidance.

Humility in Prayer
David's identification as a servant reflects humility. Our prayers should be marked by a humble acknowledgment of our need for God's grace and intervention.

What Does Psalm 31:16 Mean
Make Your face shine

- David asks, “Make Your face shine” (Psalm 31:16). In Scripture, God’s shining face is a literal picture of His personal favor and pleasure. When the Lord’s face turns toward someone, blessing follows (Numbers 6:24-26).

- Darkness lifts under that light. Just as sunlight dispels night, God’s attentive presence scatters fear and confusion (Psalm 4:6; Psalm 80:3).

- This is not a vague wish; it is confidence that the Creator truly looks, listens, and responds (1 Peter 3:12).

on Your servant

- David identifies himself as “Your servant.” He stands in a real, covenant relationship with God, gladly acknowledging God’s authority (Psalm 119:125).

- Servant language reminds us that submission invites grace (James 4:6-10). Those who humble themselves under God’s mighty hand are lifted up.

- Because Scripture is accurate and literal, we know that the title “servant” is not empty; it carries the promise that the Master cares for His own (Isaiah 54:17; John 12:26).

save me

- The request is direct: “save me.” David expects tangible rescue—physical, emotional, and spiritual (Psalm 34:17-19).

- Salvation in the Old Testament often includes deliverance from enemies, but it points ahead to the fuller salvation God provides in Christ (Romans 10:13).

- The verse shows that calling on God for help is rooted in certainty, not wishful thinking (2 Samuel 22:3-4).

by Your loving devotion

- The ground of David’s plea is God’s “loving devotion.” This steadfast love is the unbreakable covenant mercy that never runs dry (Psalm 36:7; Lamentations 3:22-23).

- David is not appealing to his own merit. He anchors his hope in God’s proven faithfulness (Deuteronomy 7:9; Psalm 57:10).

- Because God’s love is immutable, the rescue requested in this verse is secure and sure (Psalm 13:5; Ephesians 2:4-5).

summary

Psalm 31:16 is a compact but powerful prayer. David, the Lord’s servant, seeks the literal, shining favor of God’s face, confident that such attention brings life and blessing. He asks for concrete salvation, resting entirely on God’s unwavering, covenant love. The verse teaches that believers today can call on the same faithful God, expecting His light and deliverance because His loving devotion never fails.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Make thy face to shine.--As in Psalm 4:6, an echo of the priestly blessing. (Numbers 6:24-26.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Make thy face to shine upon thy servant. This expression is first used in the blessing of Moses (Numbers 6:25). Its intrinsic beauty and poetry recommended it to the psalmists, with whom it recurs frequently (camp. Psalm 4:6; Psalm 67:1; Psalm 80:5, 7, 19; Psalm 119:135). It may be regarded as equivalent to "Be thou favourable and gracious unto thy servant." Save me for thy mercies' sake; literally, save me in thy mercy.
