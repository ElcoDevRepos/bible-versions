Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Now consider this
This phrase serves as a call to attention, urging the audience to reflect on the message being delivered. In biblical context, it is a common prophetic technique to demand the listener's focus before delivering a critical message. This mirrors the calls to repentance found in the prophetic books, such as Isaiah and Jeremiah, where God often calls His people to consider their ways and return to Him.

you who forget God
Forgetting God implies a deliberate neglect or abandonment of His commandments and presence. In the historical context of Israel, this often referred to the people's tendency to turn to idolatry and forsake the covenant relationship with Yahweh. This phrase echoes the warnings found in Deuteronomy, where Moses cautions the Israelites against forgetting the Lord after entering the Promised Land. It also reflects a broader biblical theme of spiritual amnesia, where God's people are reminded of His past deeds and faithfulness.

lest I tear you to pieces
This is a vivid metaphor illustrating the severity of God's judgment. The imagery of tearing to pieces is reminiscent of a predator attacking its prey, symbolizing the inescapable and destructive nature of divine retribution. This aligns with the prophetic warnings found in books like Hosea and Amos, where God’s judgment is depicted as fierce and unavoidable for those who persist in disobedience. It underscores the seriousness of turning away from God and the consequences that follow.

with no one to rescue you
This phrase emphasizes the totality of God's judgment, where no human intervention can save those who have forsaken Him. It reflects the biblical principle that salvation and deliverance come solely from God, as seen in passages like Psalm 62:1-2 and Isaiah 43:11. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, reliance on foreign alliances or idols for rescue was a common temptation, yet the prophets consistently warned that only God could provide true deliverance. This also foreshadows the ultimate rescue through Jesus Christ, who is portrayed in the New Testament as the sole mediator and savior.

Persons / Places / Events
1. God
The speaker in this verse, addressing those who have forgotten Him. He is portrayed as a righteous judge who warns of the consequences of neglecting His commandments.

2. The Forgetful
This refers to individuals or groups who have turned away from God, neglecting His laws and commands. They are warned of impending judgment if they do not repent.

3. Judgment
The event implied in this verse is the divine judgment that God will execute on those who forget Him. It serves as a warning of the severe consequences of spiritual neglect.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Forgetfulness
Spiritual forgetfulness leads to a life disconnected from God’s guidance and protection. Regularly engage in practices that remind you of God’s presence and commandments.

The Reality of Divine Judgment
God’s warnings are not idle threats. They are real and should prompt sincere reflection and repentance. Acknowledge the seriousness of divine judgment and live accordingly.

The Call to Repentance
God’s warning is also an invitation to return to Him. Embrace repentance as a daily practice, seeking God’s forgiveness and realigning your life with His will.

The Importance of Spiritual Vigilance
Stay vigilant in your faith to avoid spiritual complacency. Engage in regular prayer, Bible study, and fellowship to maintain a strong connection with God.

The Assurance of God’s Rescue
While the verse warns of destruction, it also implies that rescue is available for those who remember and turn back to God. Trust in God’s mercy and grace for deliverance.

What Does Psalm 50:22 Mean
Now consider this

“Now consider this” (Psalm 50:22) is an urgent divine summons. The LORD calls His audience to pause and weigh His words carefully.

• Psalm 4:4 urges, “Tremble in awe and do not sin; commune with your own heart on your bed and be still.”

• Deuteronomy 32:29 laments, “If only they were wise, they would understand; they would discern their latter end.”

The Almighty never warns without purpose; He lovingly commands reflection so that judgment may be averted.

you who forget God

Forgetting God is not mere absent-mindedness; it is willful neglect of His covenant, commands, and character.

• Judges 3:7 reports, “The Israelites did evil in the sight of the LORD; they forgot the LORD their God and served the Baals.”

• Jeremiah 2:32 asks, “Can a maiden forget her jewelry or a bride her wedding sash? Yet My people have forgotten Me for days without number.”

When life’s routines push Him to the margins, indifference mutates into rebellion. The verse exposes the heart condition behind external religion that Psalm 50 has just rebuked (vv. 8-13).

lest I tear you to pieces

God’s warning is stark and literal: judgment involves devastating, unstoppable force.

• Hosea 13:7-8 portrays the LORD “like a leopard…I will tear open their chests.”

• Hebrews 10:31 reminds, “It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God.”

The imagery of a predator underscores that divine justice is personal and active, shredding every false security.

with no one to rescue you

Once God executes judgment, there is no appeal beyond Him.

• Proverbs 29:1 cautions, “He who is often reproved, yet stiffens his neck, will suddenly be broken beyond healing.”

• Revelation 6:16 pictures rebels begging mountains to hide them “from the face of Him who sits on the throne,” yet escape is impossible.

Earthly allies, wealth, or ritual cannot intercede when the Judge Himself rises against the unrepentant.

summary

Psalm 50:22 is a merciful warning. The covenant God calls His forgetful people to sober reflection, urging them to remember Him before irrevocable judgment falls. His tearing is literal; His rescue is exclusive. The path of safety lies in remembering, repenting, and returning to the God who both judges and redeems.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Now consider this, ye that forget God. Having been "reproved," the wicked are now, in conclusion, exhorted and warned. "Consider this;" i.e. take it to heart, reflect upon it, let it sink deeply into your minds and consciences, and act upon it. Lest I tear you in pieces, and there be none to deliver. A most awful threat. To "tear in pieces" is the act of a wild beast (Psalm 7:2). Job declares that God "teareth him;" but otherwise the expression is scarcely used of Divine chastisements. Certainly, if God, in his anger, lays hold upon a man to punish him, there is no possible deliverance at the hand of any other man (Psalm 49:7, 8). Deliverance, if it comes at all, must come from the Redeemer within the Godhead.
