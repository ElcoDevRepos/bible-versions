The LORD upholds all who fall
This phrase emphasizes God's sustaining power and His role as a protector and supporter of those who stumble in life. Biblically, the concept of God upholding His people is seen throughout the Psalms (e.g., Psalm 37:24) and reflects His covenant faithfulness. The Hebrew word for "upholds" can also mean to support or sustain, indicating God's active involvement in the lives of His followers. Historically, this assurance would have been comforting to the Israelites, who often faced physical and spiritual challenges. Theologically, this can be seen as a type of Christ, who supports believers through His intercession (Hebrews 7:25).

and lifts up all who are bowed down.
This part of the verse highlights God's compassion and His ability to restore those who are oppressed or burdened. The imagery of being "bowed down" suggests a state of humility or distress, often due to sin, suffering, or external pressures. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, being bowed down could also refer to the physical posture of prayer or supplication, indicating a reliance on divine help. This lifting up is a recurring theme in Scripture, seen in passages like Isaiah 40:31, where God renews the strength of those who wait on Him. It also points to the work of Jesus, who offers rest to the weary and heavy-laden (Matthew 11:28).

Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness in Support
The LORD's commitment to uphold and lift those who fall or are bowed down demonstrates His unwavering faithfulness. Believers can trust in His constant support.

Divine Compassion
God's actions in this verse reflect His deep compassion for humanity. He is not distant or indifferent but actively involved in the lives of those who seek Him.

Encouragement in Trials
This verse offers encouragement to those facing trials. It assures believers that they are not alone and that God is ready to provide strength and relief.

Call to Humility
Recognizing our need for God's help requires humility. This verse encourages believers to acknowledge their dependence on God and seek His aid.

Role of the Community
As followers of Christ, we are called to emulate God's compassion by supporting and uplifting those around us who are struggling.

What Does Psalm 145:14 Mean
The LORD

- Psalm 145:14 opens by naming “the LORD,” the covenant God whose personal name (YHWH) signals His unchanging faithfulness (Exodus 3:14; Malachi 3:6).

- He is the same One who “is gracious and compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in loving devotion” just two verses earlier (Psalm 145:8).

- Throughout Scripture the Lord alone is portrayed as the steady Rock (Psalm 18:2) and the Shepherd who never abandons His flock (John 10:11).

upholds

- To “uphold” pictures firm, ongoing support: He does not merely catch us once; He keeps us standing afterward (Isaiah 41:10).

- The promise is personal and active, echoing God’s sustaining hand under His people in Deuteronomy 33:27.

- When we feel weak, this verse invites us to lean into the Lord’s sustaining power, the same power that “sustains all things by His powerful word” (Hebrews 1:3).

all who fall

- “All” leaves no faithful struggler out; the scope reaches every believer who stumbles (Psalm 37:23-24).

- Falling can be physical hardship, moral failure, or emotional collapse; whatever the fall, Proverbs 24:16 assures that “though a righteous man falls seven times, he rises again.”

- God’s upholding is not selective but universal to His people, reflecting the Good Shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine to rescue the one (Luke 15:4).

and lifts up

- The movement shifts from under-girding to raising: He not only steadies but actively elevates, restoring dignity (1 Peter 5:6).

- This lifting echoes the imagery of Psalm 40:2, where David says, “He lifted me from the pit of destruction.”

- It reminds us that resurrection power is not future only; it operates in daily life, bringing new strength (Isaiah 40:31).

all who are bowed down

- “Bowed down” pictures the crushed and burdened—those bent beneath sorrow, guilt, or oppression (Psalm 146:8).

- Jesus embodies this heart when He calls the weary to Himself for rest (Matthew 11:28-30).

- The verse guarantees that no weight is beyond God’s power to reverse; He “raises the poor from the dust” (1 Samuel 2:8).

summary

Psalm 145:14 assures us that the Lord’s faithful character results in real, present help: He steadies every believer who stumbles and personally lifts each one bent low by life’s burdens. The verse invites confident trust that the same God who spoke the world into being will keep holding us up and setting us upright today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) The Lord.--Comp. Psalm 37:24. It marks a grand step in theology when the first instance of majesty of the Divine Being is sought in His condescension to human weakness and pity for frailty and want. The heathen had seen that this was king-like--

"Regia (crede mihi) res est succurrere lapsis."

OVID: Ep. de Ponto 11., 9, 11.

But they had hardly seen that it was also god-like.

For "raiseth" and "bowed down," see Psalm 146:8.

Verse 14. - The Lord upholdeth all that fall. Lifts them up, i.e., and again "upholds" or supports them (comp. Psalm 37:24). And raiseth up all those that be bowed down (comp. Psalm 146:8).
