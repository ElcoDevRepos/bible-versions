Be exalted, O LORD, in Your strength;
This phrase calls for the elevation and glorification of the LORD, emphasizing His strength. The term "exalted" suggests lifting up or magnifying, which is a common theme in the Psalms, reflecting the desire to honor God above all else. The strength of the LORD is a recurring theme throughout the Old Testament, often associated with His ability to deliver and protect His people, as seen in Exodus 15:2 and Isaiah 12:2. The historical context of this psalm, attributed to David, may reflect a time of military victory, where God's strength was visibly demonstrated. Theologically, this strength is not just physical might but also encompasses His sovereign power and authority over creation, as seen in Genesis 1 and throughout the prophetic literature.

we will sing and praise Your power.
The response to God's exaltation and strength is communal worship, expressed through singing and praise. This reflects the cultural practice of using music and song as a means of worship, deeply rooted in Israelite tradition, as seen in the use of the Psalms in temple worship. The act of singing is not merely a ritual but an expression of heartfelt gratitude and acknowledgment of God's power. This power is not only seen in acts of deliverance but also in the sustaining and governing of the universe, as noted in passages like Colossians 1:16-17. The phrase also points forward to the New Testament, where the ultimate demonstration of God's power is seen in the resurrection of Jesus Christ, a fulfillment of the messianic hope that David often alludes to in his psalms. The communal aspect of this praise underscores the importance of corporate worship in the life of believers, a practice that continues in Christian communities today.

Teaching Points
Exalting God in Worship
Worship is a response to God's revealed strength and power. Believers are called to actively exalt God through song and praise.

Recognizing God's Strength
Acknowledge that God's strength is the source of our victories and deliverance. Reflect on personal experiences where God's power was evident.

Community in Worship
Worship is both personal and communal. Engage with others in praising God, fostering a sense of unity and shared faith.

Living in God's Power
Apply the understanding of God's strength to daily life challenges. Trust in His power to overcome obstacles and provide guidance.

Eternal Perspective
Remember that God's strength is not just for the present but is an eternal truth. This perspective can bring hope and assurance in uncertain times.

What Does Psalm 21:13 Mean
Be exalted, O LORD

The psalmist begins by lifting God high, calling attention to His supreme place above every earthly throne. Psalm 34:3 says, “Magnify the LORD with me; let us exalt His name together”, echoing this same longing to see God honored. Exalting the Lord is not adding to His greatness; it’s recognizing what is already true. We place Him at the center of our thoughts, our conversations, and our plans, just as Psalm 99:5 urges, “Exalt the LORD our God and worship at His footstool; He is holy”.

in Your strength

Rather than praising human ability, David zeroes in on God’s might. Victory, safety, and every good outcome flow from the Lord’s power. Psalm 20:6 affirms, “Now I know that the LORD saves His anointed; He answers him from His holy heaven with the saving strength of His right hand”. By celebrating God’s strength, we also admit our own weakness and depend on Him. Like Moses after the Red Sea crossing—“The LORD is my strength and my song” (Exodus 15:2)—we trace every triumph back to Him.

we will sing

The response to divine strength is joyful worship. Singing unites head and heart, truth and emotion. Psalm 95:1 invites, “Come, let us sing for joy to the LORD; let us shout to the Rock of our salvation”. Whether alone in a quiet room or gathered with a congregation, voices rise together as a testimony that God’s deeds matter. New Testament believers follow the same pattern: “Speak to one another with psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs” (Ephesians 5:19).

and praise Your power

Praise names the specific attributes of God—here, His power. Psalm 63:2 declares, “So I have seen You in the sanctuary and beheld Your power and Your glory”. Focusing on God’s power keeps fear at bay and fuels confidence for the future. Psalm 145:4–6 recounts, “One generation shall declare Your works to the next... I will meditate on the glorious splendor of Your majesty and on Your wondrous works”. Remembering past demonstrations of strength builds present faith and stirs worship that overflows into testimony.

summary

Psalm 21:13 calls us to lift God high, celebrate His unmatched strength, respond with heartfelt singing, and spotlight His power in our praise. As we do, we align our hearts with the eternal reality that the Lord reigns, and we find joy in declaring it.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Be thou exalted, Lord, in thine own strength. The psalm, as already remarked, ends, as it began, with the praise of God. "Be thou exalted" means, "Be thou lifted up, both in thyself, and in the praises of thy people" (comp. Psalm 18:46; Psalm 46:10). So will we sing and praise thy power. We, at any rate, will do our part to exalt thee. Our tongues shall ever sing of the great deeds thou doest for us.
