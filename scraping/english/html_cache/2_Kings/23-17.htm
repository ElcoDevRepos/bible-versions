Then the king asked, “What is this monument I see?”
This phrase occurs during King Josiah's reforms, as he seeks to purify the land of idolatry. The "monument" refers to a grave or tomb, indicating a place of significance. Josiah's inquiry shows his interest in understanding the historical and spiritual significance of the sites he encounters. This reflects his commitment to restoring true worship in Judah, aligning with his efforts to remove pagan altars and high places.

And the men of the city replied,
The men of the city are likely inhabitants of Bethel, a city with a complex religious history. Bethel was a center of idolatrous worship established by Jeroboam I, who set up golden calves there (1 Kings 12:28-29). The response of the city's men indicates their awareness of the site's historical and prophetic significance, suggesting that the memory of the "man of God" and his prophecy had been preserved over generations.

“It is the tomb of the man of God who came from Judah
The "man of God" refers to the unnamed prophet from Judah who confronted King Jeroboam at Bethel (1 Kings 13:1-2). This prophet's journey from Judah to Bethel underscores the division between the northern and southern kingdoms and highlights the ongoing conflict between true worship and idolatry. His actions and subsequent burial in Bethel serve as a testament to his faithfulness and the fulfillment of his prophecy.

and proclaimed these things that you have done to the altar of Bethel.”
The prophecy mentioned here is found in 1 Kings 13:2, where the man of God foretold the desecration of the altar at Bethel by a future king named Josiah. This prophecy is fulfilled in 2 Kings 23:15-16, as Josiah destroys the altar and burns human bones on it, defiling it. This act symbolizes the triumph of true worship over idolatry and serves as a type of Christ, who would ultimately fulfill the law and the prophets, bringing about spiritual renewal and restoration.

What Does 2 Kings 23:17 Mean
Setting the scene: Josiah’s reform in full stride

• Josiah has just renewed the covenant (2 Kings 23:1-3) and is tearing down every trace of idolatry, including the notorious altar at Bethel (2 Kings 23:15).

• Bethel’s altar was built by Jeroboam to lure Israel away from true worship (1 Kings 12:28-33), so dismantling it fulfills God’s long-standing judgment.

• This righteous zeal mirrors other kingdom-wide cleansings, such as Hezekiah’s earlier purge (2 Chronicles 31:1), underscoring a pattern: revival always demands action, not mere sentiment.

A curious monument catches the king’s eye

“Then the king asked, ‘What is this monument I see?’” (2 Kings 23:17a)

• Amid toppled idols and smashed stones, a solitary marker remains.

• Josiah’s question shows discernment: he will not treat every burial site alike but seeks to honor what God honors (compare Proverbs 14:8).

• Even in reform, righteous leaders pause to understand history before acting (Nehemiah 2:12-16).

The townspeople identify the tomb

“And the men of the city replied, ‘It is the tomb of the man of God who came from Judah…’” (2 Kings 23:17b)

• The “man of God” is the unnamed prophet who, 300 years earlier, confronted Jeroboam and foretold this very moment (1 Kings 13:1-2).

• Though centuries have passed, locals remember both the prophet and his message—evidence that God’s works are never forgotten (Psalm 111:4).

• This recall fulfills the promise that a faithful witness will be established forever (Isaiah 55:10-11).

Prophecy confirmed in detail

“…and proclaimed these things that you have done to the altar of Bethel.” (2 Kings 23:17c)

• “These things” refers to burning the altar’s bones and desecrating its high place, exactly as foretold (1 Kings 13:2; 2 Kings 23:16).

• Josiah’s actions line up point-for-point with the earlier prophecy, a vivid illustration of Numbers 23:19—God does not lie or change His mind.

• Such fulfillment bolsters confidence in every promise yet to come (Matthew 5:18; Revelation 21:5).

Respect for God’s faithful servant

• Josiah immediately protects the tomb, saying, “Let him rest; do not disturb his bones” (2 Kings 23:18).

• This echoes how the bones of Joseph were cherished generations later (Joshua 24:32), highlighting that God values even the remains of His servants.

• Honoring past faithfulness inspires present obedience (Hebrews 13:7).

Living lessons for today

• God’s Word always proves true—sometimes swiftly, sometimes across centuries.

• Righteous reform involves both tearing down evil and preserving what God has approved.

• Remembering and honoring faithful predecessors keeps us grounded in God’s unchanging purposes (Psalm 78:3-7).

summary

2 Kings 23:17 records a moment when Josiah’s zeal intersects with God’s centuries-old promise. By asking about the monument, the king discovers it is the tomb of the very prophet who foretold his actions. The scene showcases the precision of God’s Word, the honor due to His faithful messengers, and the certainty that every prophecy will stand fulfilled.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) What title is this?--What is yonder monument, or memorial stone? Ezekiel 39:15, "sign." Jeremiah uses the same term of a sign-post (Jeremiah 31:21, "waymarks"). (See 1Kings 13:29 seq.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - Then he said, What title is that that I see? rather, What pillar is that that I see? Josiah's eye caught sight of a "pillar" or obelisk (צִיון) among the tombs, or in their neighborhood, and he had the curiosity to ask what it was. And the men of the city told him, It is the sepulcher of the man of God, which earns from Judah (see 1 Kings 13:1). The "pillar" could not have been the actual "sepulcher," but was no doubt a monument connected with it. Many of the Phoenician excavated tombs are accompanied by monuments above ground, which are very conspicuous (see Renan's 'Mission de Phenicie,' pls. 11, et seq.). And proclaimed these things that thou hast done against the altar of Bethel (see 1 Kings 13:2). According to the present text of Kings, Josiah was prophesied of by name, as the king who would defile the altar; but it is possible that the words, "Josiah by name" (יאשִׁיָהוּ שְׂמו), have crept in from the margin.
