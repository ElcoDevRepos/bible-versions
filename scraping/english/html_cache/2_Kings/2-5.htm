Then the sons of the prophets at Jericho came up to Elisha and said,
The "sons of the prophets" refers to a group of prophetic disciples or a school of prophets, which were communities dedicated to learning and practicing the prophetic ministry. Jericho, a city with a rich biblical history, was one of the locations where these prophetic communities were established. This indicates the widespread influence and organization of prophetic ministry during this period. The approach of these prophets to Elisha suggests a shared understanding and respect for the prophetic office.

“Do you know that the LORD will take your master away from you today?”
This question highlights the prophetic insight given to the sons of the prophets, indicating that the impending departure of Elijah was known among the prophetic community. The phrase "the LORD will take your master away" suggests a divine intervention, as Elijah's departure was not a natural death but a supernatural event. This moment foreshadows the ascension of Christ, who was also taken up into heaven. The certainty of the event happening "today" underscores the immediacy and divine timing involved.

“Yes, I know,” he replied.
Elisha's response shows his awareness and acceptance of the situation. His knowledge of Elijah's impending departure indicates his close relationship with Elijah and his own prophetic insight. This acknowledgment also reflects Elisha's readiness to assume the prophetic mantle, similar to how the disciples were prepared for Jesus' ascension and the coming of the Holy Spirit.

“Do not speak of it.”
Elisha's request to "not speak of it" may reflect his emotional state, as he was about to lose his mentor and spiritual father. It could also indicate a desire to maintain focus on the task at hand without distraction. This mirrors moments in the New Testament where Jesus instructs his disciples to keep certain revelations private until the appropriate time. Elisha's response suggests a reverence for the solemnity of the moment and the divine nature of what was about to occur.

Teaching Points
The Role of Prophetic Community
The sons of the prophets represent a community of believers who are attuned to God's plans. This highlights the importance of being part of a faith community that encourages spiritual growth and awareness.

Acceptance of God's Will
Elisha's response, "do not speak of it," indicates his acceptance of God's will, even when it involves personal loss. Believers are encouraged to trust in God's plans, even when they are difficult to understand.

Mentorship and Succession
The relationship between Elijah and Elisha underscores the importance of mentorship in spiritual growth. Mature believers are called to invest in the next generation, passing on wisdom and faith.

Awareness of God's Timing
The knowledge of Elijah's impending departure shows the importance of being sensitive to God's timing in our lives. Believers should seek to discern and align with God's timing in their personal and communal lives.

What Does 2 Kings 2:5 Mean
Then the sons of the prophets at Jericho

• Scripture says, “Then the sons of the prophets at Jericho” (2 Kings 2:5).

• These “sons” were students in a prophetic guild, similar to those at Bethel (2 Kings 2:3).

• Their presence in Jericho, a city rebuilt in Ahab’s reign (1 Kings 16:34), shows that God had preserved a faithful remnant even in spiritually challenging surroundings.

• Their title points to mentorship under Elijah, echoing Samuel’s earlier prophetic schools (1 Samuel 19:20).

came up to Elisha

• Elisha, already anointed as Elijah’s successor (1 Kings 19:16), is moving in step with his master on this final day.

• The approach highlights Elisha’s recognized leadership among the prophetic communities (2 Kings 4:38).

• It underlines the continuity of God’s work: leadership changes, but the prophetic ministry endures (Joshua 1:1-2).

and said, “Do you know that the LORD will take your master away from you today?”

• The question assumes shared revelation; the prophets at Bethel had received the same insight earlier (2 Kings 2:3).

• “The LORD will take your master away” points to Elijah’s literal departure in a whirlwind (2 Kings 2:11), prefiguring Christ’s ascension (Acts 1:9-11).

• “Today” stresses God’s precise timetable, recalling how He announced Noah’s flood timing (Genesis 7:4) and Israel’s exodus night (Exodus 12:12).

“Yes, I know,” he replied.

• Elisha’s calm acknowledgment shows spiritual discernment; he has been walking closely with Elijah and with God (2 Kings 2:2,6).

• His response reflects humility—he knows, yet he does not flaunt the knowledge (Proverbs 17:27).

• It also indicates preparedness; like Joshua under Moses (Deuteronomy 31:23), Elisha is ready to step into leadership.

“Do not speak of it.”

• Elisha’s gentle rebuke protects Elijah’s final moments from distraction, honoring his mentor’s privacy (Philippians 2:3-4).

• He may also wish to guard the prophetic community from morbid speculation, keeping focus on obedience rather than curiosity (Deuteronomy 29:29).

• The phrase underscores reverence for holy matters, akin to Christ telling disciples to keep quiet until the right time (Matthew 17:9).

summary

2 Kings 2:5 records a brief, weighty exchange that reveals God’s active communication to His prophetic people, Elisha’s readiness to inherit Elijah’s mantle, and the importance of reverent silence before sacred events. The verse teaches that while God shares His plans with His servants (Amos 3:7), He also calls them to humility and respectful restraint as they await His unfolding purposes.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - And the sons of the prophets that were at Jericho came to Elisha, and said unto him; Knowest thou that the Lord will take away thy master from thy head today? And he answered, Yea, I know it . hold ye your peace. At Jericho, too, as well as at Bethel, there was a school of the prophets, though the two places were not more than about twenty miles apart. This would seem to imply the existence of a large number of such seminaries at this period. No doubt, when the secular power was most strongly opposed to true religion, the prophetical order had to make increased efforts to raise its numbers and multiply its schools. The prophets of Israel, it must be remembered, were, after the withdrawal of the priests and Levites (2 Chronicles 11:13, 14), the sole teachers of the people in true religion.
