Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And Elijah said to Elisha, “Please stay here, for the LORD has sent me on to the Jordan.”
Elijah, a prominent prophet in Israel, is nearing the end of his earthly ministry. His request for Elisha to stay behind is a test of Elisha's commitment and loyalty. The Jordan River holds significant historical and spiritual importance, being the site where the Israelites crossed into the Promised Land (Joshua 3:14-17). Elijah's journey to the Jordan symbolizes a transition, both for him and for Elisha, who is to succeed him. The LORD's directive to Elijah underscores the divine orchestration of events, as Elijah's departure is imminent.

But Elisha replied, “As surely as the LORD lives and as you yourself live, I will not leave you.”
Elisha's response demonstrates unwavering loyalty and determination to follow Elijah, reflecting the deep bond between the mentor and his disciple. His oath, invoking the life of the LORD and Elijah, emphasizes the seriousness of his commitment. This mirrors Ruth's declaration to Naomi (Ruth 1:16-17), highlighting themes of faithfulness and devotion. Elisha's insistence on staying with Elijah until the end signifies his readiness to inherit Elijah's prophetic mantle, a foreshadowing of the transfer of spiritual authority.

So the two of them went on.
The continuation of their journey together signifies Elisha's acceptance of his role as Elijah's successor. This moment is pivotal, as it leads to Elijah's ascension and Elisha's empowerment with a double portion of Elijah's spirit (2 Kings 2:9-10). The companionship of the two prophets also reflects the biblical principle of discipleship, where learning and growth occur through close relationship and shared experiences. This journey to the Jordan is a preparatory phase for Elisha, who will soon step into his prophetic ministry, continuing the work of God in Israel.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Elijah
A major prophet in Israel, known for his powerful ministry and miracles. He is nearing the end of his earthly journey and is about to be taken up to heaven.

2. Elisha
A devoted disciple of Elijah, who is determined to follow his mentor until the very end. He is being prepared to succeed Elijah as a prophet.

3. The Jordan River
A significant geographical and spiritual landmark in Israel's history, often associated with transitions and new beginnings.

4. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who directs the events and the journey of His prophets.

5. The Journey
The path Elijah and Elisha take, symbolizing the transition of prophetic authority and the faithfulness required in following God's call.
Teaching Points
Faithful Commitment
Elisha's refusal to leave Elijah demonstrates the importance of unwavering commitment to God's calling and to those He places in our lives as spiritual mentors.

Spiritual Perseverance
The journey to the Jordan signifies the perseverance required in our spiritual walk, even when the path is uncertain or challenging.

Divine Transition
Just as Elijah's ministry is about to transition to Elisha, God often prepares us for new roles and responsibilities. We must be ready to step into His plans with faith.

Mentorship and Discipleship
The relationship between Elijah and Elisha underscores the value of mentorship in spiritual growth and the passing on of wisdom and responsibility.

God's Sovereignty
The LORD's direction in sending Elijah to the Jordan reminds us of His sovereign control over our lives and the importance of obedience to His guidance.

What Does 2 Kings 2:6 Mean
And Elijah said to him, “Stay here, please,”

• Elijah repeats the same request he made at Bethel and Jericho (2 Kings 2:2, 4), giving Elisha the opportunity to choose comfort over commitment.

• The wording shows courtesy—“please”—yet it is also a test of resolve, similar to Jesus’ probing questions to would-be followers (Luke 9:57-62).

• True discipleship often involves invitations to turn back; perseverance reveals the heart (Hebrews 10:36).

“for the LORD has sent me on to the Jordan.”

• Elijah’s obedience is immediate and specific; he goes exactly where the LORD directs (1 Kings 17:3-5).

• The Jordan is a place of transition—Israel’s entrance to the land (Joshua 3), Naaman’s cleansing (2 Kings 5:14), and now Elijah’s departure. God frequently uses geographic markers to highlight new eras in His redemptive plan.

• Elijah’s statement underscores divine initiative: ministry movements originate with God, not human ambition (Proverbs 16:9; Acts 16:6-10).

But Elisha replied, “As surely as the LORD lives and as you yourself live, I will not leave you.”

• Elisha binds his loyalty with a double oath, similar to Ruth’s vow to Naomi (Ruth 1:16-17) and Jonathan’s covenant with David (1 Samuel 20:17).

• His steadfastness fulfills the charge Elijah laid on him years earlier—“Elisha…went after Elijah and served him” (1 Kings 19:21).

• Persistence positions Elisha to receive the promised “double portion” (2 Kings 2:9). God rewards those who refuse to let go until the blessing is secured (Genesis 32:26; Luke 11:9-10).

So the two of them went on.

• Both prophet and protégé advance together, illustrating that effective kingdom work is generational (2 Titus 2:2).

• The shared journey pictures disciples walking with their master until the very end, just as Jesus led the Eleven to the Mount of Olives before His ascension (Acts 1:9-12).

• Unity of purpose brings them to the miraculous moment ahead; obedience today sets the stage for God’s intervention tomorrow (John 14:21).

summary

2 Kings 2:6 presents Elijah testing, Elisha clinging, and God directing. Elijah obeys the LORD’s command toward the Jordan, while Elisha proves unwavering devotion, refusing to separate from his mentor. Their continued partnership prepares the way for the handing of the prophetic mantle. The verse teaches that steadfast loyalty to God-appointed leaders and relentless pursuit of God’s path secure us for the transitions and inheritances the LORD has planned.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Said unto him.--Syriac adds, "unto Elisha;" Arabic, as 2Kings 2:4, and so three MSS.

And he said.--LXX., "and Elisha said"--an improvement.

"Not only Elisha, the intimate companion and future successor of Elijah, but all the disciples of the different 'schools of the prophets,' have the presentiment of the loss which threatens them. The Spirit has warned them all; they communicate their fears, but Elisha forbids them to give free course to their sorrow. A respectful silence, a resignation not exempt from foreboding, suits this condition of things. Elisha clings to his master, as though he could keep him back; the disciples follow them with their eyes. The monotony of the successive scenes adds to the solemn effect of the total description" (Reuss).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - And Elijah said unto him, Tarry, I pray thee, here; for the Lord hath sent me to Jordan. Elijah makes a third effort to detach his follower from him, or a third trial of his fidelity. He is ordered, not to a town, where his follower might find lodging and refreshment and companionship, but into the open country - to the Jordan. And then, who can say whither? Will it not be best for Elisha to leave him now, and not continue a wandering which threatens to be endless? But the follower is staunch; nothing daunts him; and he makes the same reply as before. And he said, As the Lord liveth, and as thy soul liveth, I will not leave thee. And they two went on.
