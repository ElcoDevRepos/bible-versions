But as for the king of Judah
This phrase refers to King Josiah, who reigned from approximately 640 to 609 BC. Josiah was known for his religious reforms and efforts to restore the worship of Yahweh in Judah. His reign marked a significant departure from the idolatrous practices of his predecessors. Josiah's reforms were motivated by the discovery of the Book of the Law in the temple, which led to a national renewal of covenant faithfulness. His actions are reminiscent of the ideal kingship described in Deuteronomy, where the king is to lead the people in obedience to God's law.

who sent you to inquire of the LORD
Josiah sent a delegation to inquire of the LORD after the discovery of the Book of the Law. This reflects his humility and desire to seek divine guidance, a characteristic of a righteous leader. Inquiring of the LORD was a common practice in ancient Israel, often involving prophets or priests who would seek God's will on behalf of the king or the people. This act demonstrates Josiah's recognition of the authority of God's word and his commitment to aligning his reign with divine expectations.

tell him that this is what the LORD, the God of Israel, says
This phrase underscores the prophetic authority of the message being delivered. The LORD, the God of Israel, is the covenant name of God, emphasizing His relationship with His chosen people. The use of "the God of Israel" highlights the continuity of God's promises and His faithfulness to the covenant established with the patriarchs. The prophetic message serves as a direct communication from God, affirming His sovereignty and the importance of obedience to His commands.

‘As for the words that you heard’
This refers to the words of the Book of the Law that were read to Josiah, likely a portion of Deuteronomy. The hearing of these words had a profound impact on Josiah, leading to his repentance and reform efforts. The phrase emphasizes the power of God's word to convict and transform. It also connects to the broader biblical theme of hearing and responding to God's voice, as seen in passages like Deuteronomy 6:4-9 and James 1:22-25. The words heard by Josiah were not just historical records but living words with the power to guide and direct the nation of Judah.

Persons / Places / Events
1. King Josiah
The king of Judah who initiated religious reforms and sought to restore the worship of Yahweh. He is known for his dedication to God and his efforts to rid Judah of idolatry.

2. Huldah the Prophetess
A prophetess in Jerusalem who delivered God's message to King Josiah's delegation. Her role highlights the presence and influence of female prophets in Israel.

3. Jerusalem
The capital city of Judah, where the temple was located and where King Josiah reigned. It was the center of religious and political life in Judah.

4. The Book of the Law
Discovered during temple renovations, this book (likely Deuteronomy) prompted Josiah's reforms and the inquiry to the LORD.

5. The LORD, the God of Israel
The covenant God of Israel, who communicates His will and judgment through prophets and His written word.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Seeking God
Josiah's decision to inquire of the LORD demonstrates the importance of seeking divine guidance in times of uncertainty and decision-making.

The Role of Scripture in Revival
The discovery of the Book of the Law was a catalyst for spiritual renewal, highlighting the power of God's Word to convict and transform.

God's Response to Humility
Josiah's humble response to God's Word led to a favorable message from the LORD, illustrating that God honors those who approach Him with a contrite heart.

The Influence of Godly Leadership
Josiah's reforms show how a leader's commitment to God can lead to national spiritual renewal and blessing.

The Value of Prophetic Voices
Huldah's role underscores the importance of listening to God's messengers, who provide guidance and insight into His will.

What Does 2 Kings 22:18 Mean
But as for the king of Judah

- God singles out King Josiah personally. Though judgment is coming on the nation (2 Kings 22:16–17), the Lord notices one faithful man in the palace.

- Scripture often highlights individual accountability within national issues (Ezekiel 18:20; 2 Chronicles 16:9).

- Josiah’s identity as “king of Judah” ties him to David’s line, reminding us of God’s covenant faithfulness (2 Samuel 7:12–16; 1 Kings 13:2).

who sent you to inquire of the LORD

- Josiah did not rely on political advisers; he sought divine counsel through Huldah the prophetess (2 Kings 22:14–15).

- Seeking God first is the mark of a godly leader (2 Chronicles 34:21; Psalm 27:8).

- Scripture urges believers to “ask God” for wisdom (James 1:5; Proverbs 3:5–6).

• Leaders today—parents, pastors, officials—still model reliance on the Lord by prayer and consultation with His Word.

tell him that this is what the LORD, the God of Israel, says:

- The message carries ultimate authority because it comes from “the LORD,” the covenant name Yahweh, and “the God of Israel,” the national Redeemer (Exodus 20:2).

- Prophets spoke only as moved by God (2 Peter 1:21); therefore, Josiah can trust the report completely.

- God’s word always accomplishes His purpose (Isaiah 55:11) and is not subject to negotiation (Numbers 23:19).

• Our confidence rests on the same unchanging voice preserved in Scripture (2 Timothy 3:16–17).

‘As for the words that you heard,

- Josiah had just heard the rediscovered Book of the Law (2 Kings 22:10–11). The phrase points back to that convicting moment.

- Hearing opened his heart, leading to repentance—illustrating Romans 10:17 and James 1:22.

- The Lord acknowledges Josiah’s response: tearing his clothes and weeping (2 Kings 22:19). God sees not only the hearing but the heart’s reaction (Hebrews 4:12–13).

• Scripture heard and believed still brings life-changing conviction and comfort (Psalm 119:25,50).

summary

2 Kings 22:18 assures Josiah that the God who judges nations also notices individual faith. Because the king humbly sought divine guidance, listened to God’s word, and responded with genuine repentance, the Lord sends him a personal message of grace amid national disaster. The verse underscores God’s personal attention, the necessity of inquiring of Him, the absolute authority of His spoken word, and the transforming power of hearing Scripture with a responsive heart.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 18. - But to the King of Judah which sent you to inquire of the Lord, thus shall ye say to him (see the comment on ver. 15), Thus saith the Lord God of Israel. As touching the words which thou hast heard; i.e. the words that were read to thee by Shaphan (ver. 10) - the awful threats which caused thee to rend thy clothes and to make inquiry of me.
