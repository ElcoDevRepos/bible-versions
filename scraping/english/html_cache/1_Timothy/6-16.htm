Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He alone is immortal
This phrase emphasizes the unique and eternal nature of God. In biblical context, immortality is an attribute solely ascribed to God, distinguishing Him from all creation. Unlike humans, who are mortal and subject to death, God possesses life in Himself (John 5:26). This aligns with the Old Testament understanding of God as the "Everlasting God" (Genesis 21:33). The concept of immortality here underscores God's sovereignty and eternal existence, which is a foundational belief in Christian theology.

and dwells in unapproachable light
The imagery of "unapproachable light" conveys God's holiness and purity. Light is often used in Scripture to symbolize God's presence and truth (1 John 1:5). The idea of God dwelling in light that is unapproachable by humans highlights the separation between God's holiness and human sinfulness. This is reminiscent of Moses' encounter with God on Mount Sinai, where the glory of God was so overwhelming that Moses could not see His face (Exodus 33:20). The light signifies God's majesty and the reverence due to Him.

No one has ever seen Him, nor can anyone see Him
This statement reflects the biblical teaching that God is spirit (John 4:24) and invisible (Colossians 1:15). Throughout Scripture, there are instances where God's presence is revealed through manifestations, such as the burning bush (Exodus 3:2) or the pillar of cloud and fire (Exodus 13:21), but His full essence remains unseen. This aligns with the understanding that God's nature is beyond human comprehension and visibility, reinforcing the need for faith and reverence.

To Him be honor and eternal dominion!
This doxology ascribes glory and authority to God, acknowledging His supreme power and rule over all creation. The call for honor and dominion reflects the biblical theme of God's kingship and sovereignty, as seen in passages like Psalm 145:13, which speaks of God's everlasting kingdom. This phrase serves as a reminder of the proper response to God's majesty—worship and submission to His will.

Amen
The term "Amen" is a declaration of affirmation, meaning "truly" or "so be it." It is used throughout Scripture to express agreement with God's truth and promises. In this context, it serves as a fitting conclusion to the doxology, affirming the eternal truths about God's nature and reign. The use of "Amen" connects this passage to the broader biblical tradition of affirming God's faithfulness and the certainty of His word.

Teaching Points
God’s Unique Immortality
God alone possesses immortality inherently. Unlike humans, who may receive eternal life as a gift, God’s immortality is part of His essence.

The Unapproachable Light
The imagery of unapproachable light signifies God’s absolute holiness and purity. It reminds us of the reverence and awe we should have in approaching God.

Invisibility of God
The fact that no one has seen God underscores His transcendence and the mystery of His divine nature. It calls us to faith and trust in the unseen.

Honor and Eternal Dominion
The doxology at the end of the verse reminds believers to ascribe honor and recognize God’s eternal rule over all creation. It encourages worship and submission to His authority.

What Does 1 Timothy 6:16 Mean
He alone is immortal

Paul begins with a breathtaking statement: “He alone is immortal” (1 Timothy 6:16).

• God possesses life in Himself; no one granted it to Him, and it can never be taken away (John 5:26).

• All created beings—angels and people alike—receive life from Him and therefore can die or be destroyed apart from His sustaining power (Romans 6:23; 1 Corinthians 15:53-54).

• This sets the Lord apart from every idol or human ruler who may claim permanence (Psalm 90:2; 1 Timothy 1:17).

dwells in unapproachable light

The verse continues: He “dwells in unapproachable light.”

• Light pictures purity and holiness: “God is Light, and in Him there is no darkness at all” (1 John 1:5).

• “He wraps Himself in light as with a garment” (Psalm 104:2), emphasizing His majesty.

• The word “unapproachable” reminds us of Sinai, when even Moses trembled and the people were warned not to touch the mountain (Exodus 19:16-24; Hebrews 12:18-21). God’s glory is so intense that, apart from His grace, it would consume us.

No one has ever seen Him

Scripture is crystal clear: “No one has ever seen God” (John 1:18).

• Mortal eyes cannot behold the full, unveiled essence of the Almighty (Exodus 33:20).

• Even prophetic visions—Isaiah’s temple scene (Isaiah 6:1-5) or Ezekiel’s chariot-throne (Ezekiel 1:26-28)—were mediated glimpses, not direct sightings.

nor can anyone see Him

Paul doubles down: it is not merely that no one has seen God; no one “can” see Him.

• The limitation lies with us, not with Him. Our finite, fallen nature cannot withstand infinite holiness (1 Kings 8:27).

• Yet God still desires relationship, so “the Word became flesh and dwelt among us” (John 1:14). In Jesus, we behold “the image of the invisible God” (Colossians 1:15), the only safe and saving revelation.

To Him be honor

Because of who He is, honor flows naturally to God.

• Heavenly creatures cry, “Honor and glory and thanks to Him who sits on the throne” (Revelation 4:9).

• Our earthly response mirrors heaven’s: we attribute worth, praise, and reverence to the One whose character is flawless and whose actions are always righteous (Psalm 115:1; Revelation 5:12-13).

and eternal dominion!

God’s rule is not limited by time or challenge.

• Nebuchadnezzar learned to confess, “His dominion is an everlasting dominion” (Daniel 4:34).

• Unlike earthly empires that rise and fall, the Lord’s kingdom endures forever (Psalm 145:13; Revelation 11:15).

• Paul’s phrase assures Timothy—and every believer—that no earthly power can ultimately threaten God’s sovereign plan.

Amen

This single word seals the doxology.

• “Amen” means “so be it,” underscoring certainty and agreement (2 Corinthians 1:20; Revelation 7:12).

• We echo Paul’s “Amen,” joining the chorus of saints who trust God’s eternal, unchangeable glory.

summary

1 Timothy 6:16 is a compact anthem of praise. It proclaims God’s unique immortality, His blazing holiness, His invisibility to fallen eyes, and His unending kingship. Each truth draws worship from us, reminding us that life, light, honor, and authority belong exclusively to Him. Our rightful, joyful response is the same as Paul’s: Honor and eternal dominion be His—Amen.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Who only hath immortality.--The holy angels--the souls of men--are immortal. "But one alone, 'God,' can be said to have immortality," because He, unlike other immortal beings who enjoy their immortality through the will of another, derives it from His own essence.

Dwelling in the light which no man can approach unto.--This should be rendered, dwelling in light unapproachable. The Eternal is here pictured as dwelling in an atmosphere of light too glorious for any created beings (not only men) to approach. (See Psalm 104:2, where the Eternal is addressed as covering himself with light as with a garment; see too Daniel 2:22, where light is spoken of as dwelling with God.) The symbolism of the old covenant teaches the same truth, the unapproachable glories in which God dwells; for instance, the guarding of the bounds of Sinai in the giving of the Law; the covering of the faces of the Seraphim in the year that King Uzziah died, when Isaiah saw the divine vision; the veiled darkness of the Holy of holies in the Tabernacle and the Temple, where ever and anon the visible glory dwelt.

Whom no man hath seen, nor can see.--The Old Testament teaches the same mysterious truth--"For there shall no man see me, and live" (Exodus 33:20, and also Deuteronomy 4:12). John 1:18 repeats this in very plain words--"No man hath seen God at any time." The Greek word here includes all created beings. The English translation, "no man," utterly fails to reproduce the meaning of the original. (See also 1John 4:12.) . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Light unapproachable for the light which no man can approach unto, A.V.; eternal for everlasting, A.V. Unapproachable (ἀπρόσιτον); only here in the New Testament, but found occasionally in. the later classics, corresponding to the more common ἄβατος. Whom no man hath seen, nor can see (comp. 1 Timothy 1:17 (where see note) and Exodus 33:20-23). The appearance of the "God of Israel" to Moses and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel, related in Exodus 34:9-11, was that of the Son in anticipation of the Incarnation. The invisibility of the essential Godhead is also predicated in our Lord's saying, "God is a Spirit" (John 4:24). This whole passage is a magnificent embodiment of the attributes of the living God, supreme blessedness and almighty power, universal dominion, and unchangeable being, inscrutable majesty, radiant holiness, and glory inaccessible and unapproachable by his creatures, save through the mediation of his only begotten Son.
