Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then his father Zechariah
Zechariah, a priest from the division of Abijah, is the father of John the Baptist. His role as a priest places him in a position of spiritual authority and knowledge of the Scriptures. His experience in the temple, where he was struck mute for doubting the angel's message, highlights the importance of faith and obedience. Zechariah's lineage connects him to the priestly traditions of Israel, emphasizing the fulfillment of God's promises through his family.

was filled with the Holy Spirit
The filling of the Holy Spirit signifies divine inspiration and empowerment. In the Old Testament, the Holy Spirit would come upon individuals for specific purposes, such as prophecy or leadership. This moment marks a transition to the New Testament era, where the Holy Spirit plays a central role in the lives of believers. Zechariah's filling is reminiscent of the prophets of old, who spoke God's truth under the Spirit's influence.

and prophesied:
Prophecy in the biblical sense involves speaking forth God's message, often with a focus on future events or divine truths. Zechariah's prophecy is a declaration of God's redemptive plan, highlighting the coming of the Messiah and the role of his son, John, in preparing the way. This act of prophecy connects Zechariah to the prophetic tradition of Israel, where God communicated His will through chosen individuals. The content of his prophecy, which follows in the subsequent verses, aligns with Old Testament prophecies concerning the Messiah and the salvation of God's people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Zechariah
A priest from the division of Abijah, husband to Elizabeth, and father of John the Baptist. He was initially struck mute for doubting the angel Gabriel's message but regained his speech after naming his son John.

2. Holy Spirit
The third person of the Trinity, who empowers and inspires believers. In this context, the Holy Spirit fills Zechariah, enabling him to prophesy.

3. Prophecy
A divinely inspired utterance. Zechariah's prophecy, often called the Benedictus, praises God for His faithfulness and foretells the role of his son, John, in preparing the way for the Messiah.

4. John the Baptist
The son of Zechariah and Elizabeth, who would grow up to be the forerunner of Jesus Christ, calling people to repentance and baptizing them in preparation for the coming Messiah.

5. Israel
The nation chosen by God, to whom the promises of the Messiah were given. Zechariah's prophecy reflects God's faithfulness to His covenant with Israel.
Teaching Points
The Role of the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit empowers believers to speak and act according to God's will. Just as Zechariah was filled with the Spirit to prophesy, we too can seek the Spirit's guidance in our lives.

Faithfulness of God
Zechariah's prophecy reminds us of God's unwavering faithfulness to His promises. We can trust that God will fulfill His word in our lives as He did for Israel.

The Importance of Prophecy
Prophecy serves to edify, encourage, and comfort believers. It is a reminder of God's active involvement in history and His plans for the future.

Preparation for Christ
John's role was to prepare the way for Jesus. Similarly, we are called to prepare our hearts and the hearts of others for Christ's return.

Overcoming Doubt
Zechariah's initial doubt was transformed into faith and praise. This encourages us to move from doubt to trust in God's promises.

What Does Luke 1:67 Mean
Then

• The opening word ties Zechariah’s experience to the immediate context. Just moments earlier his tongue was loosed after writing, “His name is John” (Luke 1:63–64), a fulfillment of Gabriel’s promise (Luke 1:20).

• Scripture often uses “then” to mark a divinely appointed turning point—much like Acts 2:1, “When the day of Pentecost had come.” God never wastes chronology; every event unfolds on His schedule (Galatians 4:4).

his father Zechariah

• Luke identifies him as John the Baptist’s father, emphasizing God’s work through ordinary people who trust Him. Zechariah was a priest serving at the altar (Luke 1:5, 8–9), reminding us of God’s pattern of calling servants in the midst of obedience—compare Moses tending sheep (Exodus 3:1) or David caring for flocks (1 Samuel 16:11–13).

• Though he once doubted (Luke 1:18–20), Zechariah now stands as a witness to God’s faithfulness, echoing the encouragement found in Psalm 145:13, “The LORD is faithful in all His words.”

was filled with the Holy Spirit

• The filling is a sovereign act of God, empowering believers for specific service. We see similar moments with Elizabeth (Luke 1:41), Peter (Acts 4:8), and Paul (Acts 13:9).

• Filling is distinct from New Testament indwelling; the Spirit had already been at work in Zechariah’s life, yet now He overtakes every faculty for proclamation—anticipating Ephesians 5:18, “Be filled with the Spirit.”

• This verse affirms that prophetic utterance is Spirit-driven, lining up with 2 Peter 1:21, “Prophecy never had its origin in the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit.”

and prophesied

• Prophecy here is more forth-telling than fore-telling: Zechariah announces God’s redemptive plan in Luke 1:68-79, blessing the coming Messiah and outlining John’s role. It resembles the Spirit-prompted praises of Miriam (Exodus 15:20–21) and Hannah (1 Samuel 2:1–10).

• The content centers on covenant faithfulness—Abrahamic promises (Genesis 22:16–18) and Davidic hope (2 Samuel 7:12–16)—demonstrating that true prophecy magnifies God’s Word rather than human imagination.

• By linking Spirit-filling to prophecy, Luke underscores that Scripture’s message is both supernatural and trustworthy, echoing Jesus’ words in John 16:13: “When the Spirit of truth comes, He will guide you into all truth.”

summary

Luke 1:67 shows the seamless movement of God’s plan: at just the right moment, God takes a once-doubtful priest, fills him with His Spirit, and turns him into a mouthpiece of redemptive praise. “Then” signals perfect timing, “his father Zechariah” reminds us God uses willing servants, “was filled with the Holy Spirit” highlights divine empowerment, and “and prophesied” affirms that Spirit-given words reveal and celebrate the faithful purposes of God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(67) Was filled with the Holy Ghost, and prophesied.--The latter word appears to be used in its wider sense of an inspired utterance of praise (as, e.g., in 1Samuel 19:20; 1Corinthians 14:24-25). The hymn that follows appears as the report, written, probably, by Zachariah himself, of the praises that had been uttered in the first moments of his recovered gift of speech. As such, we may think of it as expressing the pent-up thoughts of the months of silence. The fire had long been kindling, and at last he spake with his tongue.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 67. - His father Zacharias was filled with the Holy Ghost, and prophesied, saying. The inspired hymn which follows - thought out, no doubt, with the Holy Spirit's help in the course of the long enforced seclusion which his first want of faith had brought upon him - holds a prominent place in all Western liturgics. Like the Magnificat, it is believed to have been first introduced into the public worship of the Church about the middle of the sixth century by St. Csesarius of Aries. It may be briefly summarized as a thanksgiving for the arrival of the times of Messiah.
