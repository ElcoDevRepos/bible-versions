Her parents were astounded
The reaction of the parents reflects the miraculous nature of the event. In the context of first-century Jewish culture, the raising of the dead was an extraordinary act that only God could perform, highlighting Jesus' divine authority. This miracle is one of several instances where Jesus demonstrates His power over life and death, reinforcing His identity as the Messiah. The astonishment of the parents can be compared to the reactions of others who witnessed Jesus' miracles, such as the disciples in the calming of the storm (Luke 8:25) and the crowd at the raising of the widow's son in Nain (Luke 7:16).

but Jesus ordered them not to tell anyone what had happened
This command for silence is part of what is known as the "Messianic Secret," a theme in the Gospels where Jesus often instructs those He heals or those who witness His miracles to keep them private. This could be to prevent misunderstandings about His mission, as many expected a political Messiah rather than a spiritual Savior. Additionally, Jesus may have wanted to avoid drawing premature attention from the authorities, which could hinder His ministry. This instruction is similar to other instances, such as when He healed the leper (Mark 1:44) and when He was transfigured (Mark 9:9). The command also emphasizes the personal nature of faith and the importance of internalizing the miracle rather than broadcasting it for public acclaim.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is the one who performs the miracle of raising Jairus' daughter from the dead. His authority over life and death is a key theme.

2. Jairus
A synagogue leader whose faith in Jesus leads him to seek help for his dying daughter. His role highlights the importance of faith and desperation in seeking Jesus.

3. Jairus' Daughter
The young girl who is raised from the dead by Jesus. Her resurrection is a testament to Jesus' divine power and compassion.

4. Jairus' Wife
Present at the miracle, she shares in the astonishment and joy of her daughter's restoration to life.

5. The House of Jairus
The setting for this miraculous event, symbolizing a place of transformation from mourning to joy.
Teaching Points
The Power of Faith
Jairus' approach to Jesus in faith, despite his position, teaches us the importance of humility and belief in Christ's power.

Jesus' Authority Over Death
This miracle underscores Jesus' divine authority, reminding us that He is sovereign over life and death.

The Role of Witnesses
Jesus instructs the parents not to tell anyone, which may seem counterintuitive. This highlights the importance of timing and God's plan in sharing testimonies.

Astonishment and Worship
The parents' astonishment should lead us to worship and reverence for Jesus' miraculous works in our lives.

Confidentiality and Obedience
Jesus' command for silence teaches us about obedience and the importance of following His instructions, even when we don't fully understand them.

What Does Luke 8:56 Mean
Her parents

• Jairus and his wife had just watched Jesus take their dead daughter by the hand and say, “Child, arise” (Luke 8:54; cf. Mark 5:22-24, 40).

• Scripture presents them as real, historical parents whose faith brought Jesus into their home (Luke 8:41-42).

• Their presence highlights God’s concern for family, echoing promises like Psalm 103:17 and reminding us that parental faith can open doors for divine intervention (Acts 16:31).

were astounded

• “Astonished out of their minds” captures the moment—an unmistakable, literal resurrection (Luke 7:16; John 11:44).

• Awe before God’s power is a proper response (Psalm 66:5).

• This amazement verifies the miracle; it wasn’t staged or symbolic, but an event that left eyewitnesses overwhelmed (Luke 5:26).

but Jesus ordered them

• Jesus immediately exercises authority, guiding the aftermath of His work (Luke 4:41; John 2:4).

• His commands are as purposeful as His miracles, revealing that obedience should follow revelation (John 14:15).

• By directing the parents, He protects the girl and the mission, showing shepherd-like care (John 10:11).

not to tell anyone

• Similar instructions appear in Luke 5:14 and Matthew 9:30. Jesus often limited publicity to keep crowds from forcing a political agenda (John 6:15) and to prevent premature confrontation with authorities (Luke 9:21-22).

• Silence guarded the girl’s privacy and allowed her to grow up without being exploited as a curiosity (Isaiah 42:2).

• The focus remained on faith, not fame; spreading the news could shift attention from the message to the miracle (Matthew 12:16-21).

what had happened

• “What had happened” refers to the literal raising of a child from death—an undeniable sign that “God has visited His people” (Luke 7:16).

• It previews Jesus’ own resurrection, assuring believers that He truly holds “the keys of death and Hades” (Revelation 1:18).

• The phrase also underscores eyewitness testimony; Luke’s orderly account (Luke 1:3-4) invites us to trust the historical reliability of gospel events.

summary

Luke 8:56 shows astonished parents witnessing the literal resurrection of their daughter, then receiving Jesus’ firm instruction to keep the event quiet. Their wonder confirms the miracle’s reality, while His command protects His mission from misguided publicity. The verse teaches that genuine encounters with Christ evoke awe, demand obedience, and ultimately point to His supreme power over death.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 56. - He charged them that they should tell no man what was done. The enthusiasm in Galilee just then needed no extra spur. The crowds which followed him were increasing. The excitement, the Master felt, was unreal and evanescent; he wished rather to calm it than to increase it.
