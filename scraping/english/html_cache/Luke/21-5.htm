As some of the disciples were remarking
The disciples often engaged in discussions about their surroundings, reflecting their human curiosity and the cultural significance of the temple. This moment captures their awe and admiration for the temple's grandeur, a central place of worship and national pride for the Jewish people.

how the temple was adorned with beautiful stones
The temple in Jerusalem, known as Herod's Temple, was renowned for its architectural magnificence. Herod the Great had expanded and beautified the Second Temple, using large, white stones and gold decorations. These stones were not only functional but also symbolized the strength and permanence of the Jewish faith and nation.

and consecrated gifts
The temple was adorned with offerings and gifts dedicated to God, including gold and silver vessels, tapestries, and other valuable items. These gifts were expressions of devotion and were meant to honor God, reflecting the religious practices and priorities of the Jewish people.

Jesus said,
Jesus' response to the disciples' remarks often provided deeper spiritual insights or prophetic revelations. In this context, His words would soon shift the focus from the physical beauty of the temple to the impending destruction and the transient nature of earthly structures, emphasizing the need for spiritual preparedness and reliance on God rather than on material or national symbols.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Disciples
Followers of Jesus who were present with Him and observed the temple's adornment. They often sought to understand Jesus' teachings and were key figures in spreading His message.

2. Temple
The Jewish temple in Jerusalem, a central place of worship and a symbol of Jewish faith and identity. It was known for its grandeur and beauty, adorned with precious stones and gifts.

3. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, who uses this moment to teach about the transient nature of earthly things and the coming destruction of the temple.

4. Beautiful Stones
Refers to the impressive and costly materials used in the construction and decoration of the temple, symbolizing human achievement and religious devotion.

5. Consecrated Gifts
Offerings made to the temple, often valuable and given as acts of worship or dedication to God.
Teaching Points
The Transience of Earthly Glory
Earthly achievements and structures, no matter how grand, are temporary. Our focus should be on eternal values and the kingdom of God.

True Worship and Devotion
While the temple was adorned with gifts, true worship is not about external beauty but about the heart's devotion to God.

Prophetic Fulfillment
Jesus' prediction of the temple's destruction was fulfilled in 70 AD, reminding us of the reliability of His words and the importance of being prepared for future prophetic events.

Spiritual Temples
As believers, we are called to be living temples of God, emphasizing purity, holiness, and dedication to His service.

Eternal Perspective
In a world focused on material success, Christians are encouraged to maintain an eternal perspective, investing in what lasts beyond this life.

What Does Luke 21:5 Mean
As some of the disciples were remarking

The disciples are tourists in their own city, marveling aloud at the splendor before them. Their reaction mirrors common human tendencies:

• We can be easily captivated by what our eyes see (1 John 2:16).

• Even those who walk closely with Jesus can slip into earthly-minded admiration (Philippians 3:19–20).

Cross references such as Matthew 24:1 and Mark 13:1 show the same scene, confirming its historicity and underscoring how widespread the sense of awe was among the Twelve.

how the temple was adorned with beautiful stones

Herod’s temple was famous for its massive white limestone blocks and gold accents that caught the sun. The disciples’ comments remind us:

• Magnificent structures can point to God’s past acts (2 Chronicles 3:1–2), yet they are still temporary (Hebrews 9:11).

• Outward beauty, no matter how grand, is not what ultimately pleases the Lord (1 Samuel 16:7).

The stones that impressed the disciples would, within one generation, be thrown down (Luke 21:6), validating Jesus’ words and highlighting the transience of earthly glory (Psalm 102:25–26).

and consecrated gifts

Offerings dedicated to God—golden vines, votive plaques—decorated the complex (Exodus 35:22). These gifts illustrate:

• The instinct to honor God tangibly (Proverbs 3:9).

• The danger of equating physical offerings with genuine devotion (Isaiah 1:13–15).

Acts 7:48 reminds us, “the Most High does not dwell in houses made by human hands,” echoing Jesus’ coming correction of misplaced confidence in religious trappings.

Jesus said

Before the disciples finish admiring, Jesus answers with sober prophecy (Luke 21:6). His response teaches:

• He sees beyond surface beauty to coming judgment (Revelation 1:14).

• He redirects focus from man-made glory to the eternal kingdom (Hebrews 12:26–28).

Jesus’ words are fulfilled in A.D. 70, proving the reliability of every promise He speaks (John 14:29). His statement also sets the stage for the wider Olivet Discourse, where He counsels vigilance, not sentimentality (Luke 21:34–36).

summary

Luke 21:5 captures a momentary fascination with architectural splendor, only to have Jesus reveal its impending ruin. The verse warns believers not to anchor faith in physical beauty or religious monuments. Instead, Christ calls His followers to look past what dazzles the eyes and cling to what endures—His word, His kingdom, and His righteous reign.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5, 6) And as some spake of the temple.--See Notes on Matthew 24:1-2; Mark 13:1-2, where the "some" are identified with the disciples.

Goodly stones.--These were probably so called, either as being sculptured, or as being of marble, or porphyry, or other of the more precious materials used in building.

Gifts.--St. Luke uses the more strictly classical word for "offerings," according to some of the best MSS., in the self-same form as the Anath?ma (1Corinthians 12:3; 1Corinthians 16:12), which elsewhere in the New Testament is confined to the idea of that which is set apart, not for a blessing, but a curse. The fact that he is the only writer to use it in its good sense is characteristic of his Gentile and classical training. Other MSS., however, give the more usual term, Anath?ma, as if it had been found necessary to distinguish the form of the word according to its uses.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 5-7. - The temple - its impending ruin. The disciples questions. Verse 5. - And as some spake of the temple. After the Lord's remark upon the alms-giving of the rich men and the poor widow to the treasury of the temple, the Master left the sacred building for his lodging outside the city walls. As far as we know, his comment upon the widow's alms was his last word of public teaching. On their way home, while crossing the Mount of Olives, they apparently halted for a brief rest. It was then that some of his friends called attention to the glorious prospect of the temple, then lit up by the setting sun. It was, no doubt, then in all its perfect beauty, a vast glittering mass of white marble, touched here and there with gold and color. Whosoever had not gazed on it, said the old rabbis, had not seen the perfection of beauty. It is possible that the bystander's remark was suggested by the memory of the last bit of Divine teaching they had listened to. "Lord, is not the house on Zion lovely? But if only such gifts as those you have just praised with such unstinting praise had been made, never had that glorious pile been raised in honor of the Eternal King." More probable, however, the sight of the great temple, then bathed in the golden glory of the fast-setting sun, recalled some of the Master's sayings of that eventful day, notably such as, "Your house is left unto you desolate," which occurred in the famous twice-spoken apostrophe, "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, thou that killest the prophets!" (Matthew 23:38; Luke 13:35). "What, Lord I will that house, so great, so perfect in its beauty, so loved, the joy of the whole earth, - will that house be left desolate and in shapeless ruins?" With goodly stones. The enormous size of the stones and blocks of marble with which the temple of Jerusalem was built excited the surprise of Titus when the city fell. Josephus mentions ('Bell. Jud.,' v. 5) that some of the levelled blocks of marble or stone were forty cubits long and ten high. And gifts; better rendered, sacred offerings, such as the "golden vine," with its vast clusters, the gift of Herod - which probably suggested the discourse, "I am the true Vine" (reported in John 15.) - such as crowns, shields, vessels of gold and silver, presented by princes and others who visited the holy house on Zion. The temple was rich in these votive offerings. The historian Tacitus, for instance, calls it "a temple of vast wealth" ('Hist.,' 5. 8).
