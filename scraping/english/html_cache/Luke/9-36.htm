After the voice had spoken
This phrase refers to the voice of God the Father, which had just declared Jesus as His chosen Son during the Transfiguration (Luke 9:35). This divine affirmation echoes the baptism of Jesus (Luke 3:22) and fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah being the Son of God (Psalm 2:7). The voice signifies divine approval and authority, emphasizing Jesus' unique relationship with the Father.

only Jesus was present with them
Following the divine voice, Moses and Elijah, who appeared with Jesus, disappeared. This highlights Jesus' supremacy over the Law and the Prophets, represented by Moses and Elijah, respectively. It underscores the New Covenant, with Jesus as the central figure, fulfilling the Law and the Prophets (Matthew 5:17). The focus on Jesus alone points to His sufficiency and preeminence in salvation and revelation.

The disciples kept this to themselves
The disciples' silence can be attributed to Jesus' instruction not to reveal the vision until after His resurrection (Matthew 17:9). This reflects the theme of the "Messianic Secret" in the Gospels, where Jesus often instructs His followers to keep His identity and works concealed until the appropriate time. It also indicates their lack of full understanding of the event's significance at that moment.

and in those days they did not tell anyone what they had seen
This secrecy aligns with the cultural and historical context of the time, where premature revelation of Jesus' divine nature could have led to misunderstanding or political unrest. The disciples' silence also suggests a period of reflection and growth in their understanding of Jesus' mission. The eventual sharing of this event, post-resurrection, would serve as a powerful testimony to Jesus' divine nature and the fulfillment of His mission.

Teaching Points
The Revelation of Jesus' Divinity
The Transfiguration serves as a powerful revelation of Jesus' divine nature, affirming His identity as the Son of God.

The Importance of Witness
The disciples' role as witnesses to the Transfiguration underscores the importance of bearing witness to Christ's glory and truth.

The Call to Silence and Reflection
The disciples' initial silence about the event invites us to reflect on the mysteries of God and the appropriate timing for sharing spiritual experiences.

The Voice of God
The voice from heaven emphasizes the authority of Jesus and the necessity of listening to Him, aligning with the call to heed God's Word.

Transformation through Encounter
Just as the disciples were transformed by witnessing Jesus' glory, believers are called to be transformed by encounters with Christ through prayer and Scripture.

What Does Luke 9:36 Mean
After the voice had spoken

• The cloud’s voice—“This is My Son, whom I have chosen; listen to Him” (Luke 9:35)—had just affirmed Jesus’ divine Sonship, echoing earlier revelations at His baptism (Luke 3:22) and foreshadowing the Father’s future testimony (John 12:28).

• The cessation of the voice signals that heaven has spoken its final word on Jesus’ identity; there is nothing more to add. Compare Matthew 17:5-6 where the disciples fall facedown, showing the proper response to God’s direct revelation.

• Like Israel at Sinai who trembled when God spoke (Exodus 19:19), the disciples experience fear and awe, underscoring that what follows carries the full authority of God.

only Jesus was present with them

• Moses and Elijah vanish, leaving Christ alone. This visually proclaims His supremacy over the Law and the Prophets (Colossians 1:18; Hebrews 3:3).

• The scene drives home Hebrews 12:2: “Let us fix our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of our faith.” When all other voices fade, He remains the focal point.

• Practically, the moment calls believers to:

– Center worship on Christ rather than spiritual experiences.

– Trust that in every circumstance “only Jesus” is sufficient (John 14:6).

The disciples kept this to themselves

• Jesus had already instructed them not to broadcast the vision until after His resurrection (Matthew 17:9). Their silence is obedience, not reluctance.

• This restraint mirrors Luke 2:19, where Mary “treasured up all these things.” Sometimes revelation is meant to be pondered before it is proclaimed.

• The event becomes a reservoir of faith that will later bolster their witness (2 Peter 1:17-18).

and in those days they did not tell anyone what they had seen

• Timing matters in God’s plan (Ecclesiastes 3:7). Sharing the Transfiguration prematurely could have fueled political messianic fervor (John 6:15).

• After the resurrection, silence gives way to bold testimony (Acts 1:8); until then, secrecy protects the mission.

• The pattern encourages believers to seek the Spirit’s guidance on when to speak and when to wait (Proverbs 15:23).

summary

Luke 9:36 shows the Father’s final word of approval, the Son’s unrivaled centrality, the disciples’ obedient silence, and the strategic timing of God’s revelation. When the cloud lifts, only Jesus remains—sufficient, supreme, and worthy of our unwavering attention.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 36. - And they kept it close, and told no man in those days any of those things which they had seen. The reasons of this silence for the present have been already discussed. The scene, doubtless, had done its work in the education of the three. Without telling their companions what they had seen and heard on the mount, we may assume that the sight of the serene confidence and renewed trust on the part of Peter, James, and John did its effectual work in strengthening their brethren. No doubt directly after the Resurrection, possibly during the days of darkness and gloom which followed the day of the cross, the chosen three related at length their experience of the Transfiguration mystery. The narrative of the Transfiguration and its attendant circumstances, as might have been expected, has been a favourite subject for hostile criticism. It does not, however, lend itself to any probable, or even possible, explanation which refers the story to some exaggerated report of a mistaken natural phenomenon. The whole story, as we have it thrice - with very slight variation in the details - repeated in the synoptical Gospels, must stand as we have it, or else must be wholly rejected as a myth. But, if a myth, whence did it spring? for nothing in the Jewish expectation of Messiah could possibly have suggested the "legend." The strange and even childish interruption of Peter could never have been invented. No one friendly to the apostle would have chronicled such a saying had there been any doubt resting on its authenticity; and a writer hostile to the apostle would scarcely have invented a narrative which treated of the Divine glory of the apostle's adored Master. If it be an invention, whence comes it? in whose interest was it composed? and how did it find its way into the very heart of the three synoptical Gospels? for there we find it woven into that marvellous tapestry of revelation and teaching which has at once charmed and influenced so many millions of men and women now for more than eighteen hundred years. Something of the purpose which the Transfiguration was intended to serve in the education of the twelve has been already discussed in the foregoing notes. Dr. Lange, who has made this difficult passage in the story of Jesus a subject of deep and earnest study, has given us some beautiful thoughts on the real signification of the Lord's transfiguration. This scholar and divine considers that, just at this period of his public ministry, Jesus had reached the zenith of his power. This is indicated by the grandeur of his recent miracles. There was nothing higher and more sublime to be reached by him. From this moment, therefore, earthly existence became too narrow a sphere. There only remained death; but death is, as St. Paul says, the wages of sin. For the sinless Man the issue of life is not the sombre passage of the tomb, rather is it the. royal road of a glorious transformation. Had the hour of this glorification struck for Jesus? and was the Transfiguration the beginning of the heavenly renewal? Gess, quoted by Godet - from whose precis of Lange's note these observations are derived - gives expression to Lange's thoughts in these words: "This event (the Transfiguration) indicates the ripe preparation of Jesus for immediate entrance upon eternity." "Had not Jesus himself," goes on Godet to say, thus con-eluding this very beautiful and suggestive, if somewhat fanciful note, "voluntarily suspended this change which was on the point of being wrought in him, this moment, the moment of his glorious transfiguration would have become the moment of his ascension."
