Then Jesus told them a parable
In the Gospel of Luke, parables are a key teaching method used by Jesus to convey spiritual truths. Parables are simple stories used to illustrate a moral or spiritual lesson. Jesus often used them to reveal truths to those who were open to understanding while concealing them from those who were not receptive. This particular parable is unique to Luke's Gospel, emphasizing the importance of prayer and perseverance.

about their need to pray at all times
Prayer is a central theme in the teachings of Jesus, and this phrase underscores the necessity of constant communication with God. The idea of praying "at all times" suggests a lifestyle of prayer, not limited to specific times or places. This aligns with Paul's exhortation in 1 Thessalonians 5:17 to "pray without ceasing." The cultural context of the time included set times for prayer, but Jesus is advocating for a more continuous and personal relationship with God.

and not lose heart:
The phrase "not lose heart" addresses the human tendency to become discouraged, especially when prayers seem unanswered. In the historical context, the Jewish people were under Roman occupation, and many were longing for deliverance. Jesus encourages persistence in prayer, reflecting the belief that God is faithful and will respond in His timing. This teaching is echoed in other scriptures, such as Galatians 6:9, which encourages believers not to grow weary in doing good. The call to perseverance in prayer is a reminder of the hope and trust believers should have in God's promises.

What Does Luke 18:1 Mean
Then Jesus told them a parable

• “Then Jesus told them a parable” (Luke 18:1) signals the Lord’s deliberate use of story to shape His disciples’ outlook.

• Parables are more than illustrations; they reveal truth to believers while sifting the unresponsive (Luke 8:10; Matthew 13:34).

• Jesus the Master Teacher speaks with divine authority (Matthew 7:29), so the instruction that follows is not optional advice but God’s will.

• Throughout Luke, parables press hearers toward decision and faithfulness (Luke 10:36-37; 12:41-48), reminding us that revelation always asks for response.

About their need to pray at all times

• The purpose statement is explicit: “about their need to pray at all times.” Prayer is presented as a necessity, not a luxury.

• Scripture consistently calls believers to continual prayer:

– “Pray without ceasing” (1 Thessalonians 5:17).

– “Pray in the Spirit at all times” (Ephesians 6:18).

– “Devote yourselves to prayer, being watchful and thankful” (Colossians 4:2).

• Reasons persistence matters:

– It keeps our hearts aligned with God’s purposes (Psalm 25:4-5).

– It acknowledges ongoing dependence on the Father for daily needs (Matthew 6:11).

– It sustains spiritual alertness amid temptation (Luke 21:36).

• The widow’s tenacious petitions in the verses that follow (Luke 18:3-7) illustrate how unrelenting prayer moves even an unjust judge—how much more the righteous Judge of all the earth.

And not lose heart

• Jesus couples constant prayer with the command “not lose heart.” Discouragement is a real threat in a fallen world (Proverbs 13:12).

• Scripture counters weariness with promises:

– “Let us not grow weary in well-doing, for in due time we will reap if we do not give up” (Galatians 6:9).

– “Therefore we do not lose heart... our inner self is being renewed day by day” (2 Corinthians 4:16).

– “Consider Him who endured such hostility... so that you will not grow weary and lose heart” (Hebrews 12:3).

• Practical helps to remain encouraged:

– Recall God’s past faithfulness (Psalm 77:11-12).

– Anchor hope in His character of justice and mercy (Luke 18:7-8).

– Stay connected to the body of Christ for mutual support (Hebrews 10:24-25).

• Persistent prayer and steadfast courage are inseparable; prayer fuels hope, and hope keeps prayer vibrant.

summary

Luke 18:1 presents Jesus’ clear directive: believers must keep praying and keep believing. By couching the lesson in a parable, He underscores both the urgency and the accessibility of persistent prayer. Constant communion with God safeguards hearts from discouragement, while confidence in His just nature sustains perseverance. The verse calls every follower to live in continual, trusting conversation with the Father, certain that He hears and will act in His perfect time.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XVIII.

(1) That men ought always to pray, and not to faint.--The latter of the two verbs is noticeable as being used in the New Testament by St. Luke and St. Paul only (2Corinthians 4:1; 2Corinthians 4:16; Galatians 6:9; 2Thessalonians 3:13). The whole verse is remarkable as being one of the few instances (Luke 18:9 being another) in which a parable is introduced by a distinct statement as to its drift and aim. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-14. - The Lord speaks the two parables on prayer - the importunate widow, and the Pharisee and publican. Verse 1. - And he spake a parable unto them to this end, that men ought always to pray, and not to faint. The formnla ἕλεγε δὲ καί, literally, "and he spake also," calls attention to the fact that the parable-teaching immediately to follow was a continuation of what had preceded. Indeed, the connection between the first of the two parables, which urges restless continued prayer, and the picture which the Lord had just drawn of men's state of utter forgetfulness of God, is obvious. "The Son of man has been rejected; he has gone from view; the masses are plunged in gross worldliness; men of God are become as rare as, in the days of Abraham, they were in Sodom. What, then, is the position of the Church? That of a widow whose only weapon is incessant prayer. It is only by means of this intense concentration that faith will be preserved. But such is precisely the disposition which Jesus fears may not be found even in the Church at his return" (Godet).
