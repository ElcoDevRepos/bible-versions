Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But Jesus overheard them
In this context, Jesus is responding to a report that Jairus's daughter has died. The term "overheard" suggests that Jesus is aware of the situation even when others might think it is beyond hope. This reflects His omniscience and His ability to intervene in human affairs. The setting is in Galilee, where Jesus performed many miracles, demonstrating His authority over life and death.

and said to Jairus
Jairus is a synagogue leader, a position of significant religious and social standing. His approach to Jesus indicates a level of desperation and faith, as religious leaders were often skeptical of Jesus. This interaction highlights the breaking of social and religious barriers, as Jesus ministers to those in need regardless of their status.

Do not be afraid
Fear is a common human response to death and hopeless situations. Jesus's command to not fear is a recurring theme in Scripture, emphasizing trust in God's power and sovereignty. This echoes God's assurances to His people throughout the Old Testament, such as in Isaiah 41:10, where God tells Israel not to fear because He is with them.

just believe
Faith is central to Jesus's ministry and teachings. Here, belief is not just intellectual assent but a deep trust in Jesus's power and authority. This call to faith is consistent with Hebrews 11:6, which states that without faith, it is impossible to please God. Jesus often required faith as a prerequisite for miracles, as seen in other healing accounts.

and she will be healed
This promise of healing is a testament to Jesus's divine authority over sickness and death. It foreshadows the resurrection power that Jesus would later demonstrate in His own resurrection. The healing of Jairus's daughter is a type of the ultimate healing and resurrection that believers will experience through Christ. This miracle also fulfills prophecies about the Messiah bringing restoration and life, as seen in passages like Isaiah 35:5-6.

Teaching Points
Faith Over Fear
Jesus' words to Jairus, "Do not be afraid; just believe," highlight the importance of faith over fear. In moments of crisis, believers are called to trust in God's power and promises.

The Power of Jesus
This passage demonstrates Jesus' authority over life and death, encouraging believers to rely on His power in seemingly impossible situations.

The Role of Community
The presence of the crowd reminds us of the influence of societal pressures. Believers are encouraged to focus on Jesus' words rather than the doubts of others.

Hope in Desperation
Jairus' approach to Jesus in desperation teaches that in our most hopeless moments, turning to Christ can bring about miraculous change.

The Assurance of Healing
Jesus' assurance of healing to Jairus' daughter serves as a reminder of the spiritual and physical restoration available through faith in Christ.

What Does Luke 8:50 Mean
But Jesus overheard them

• The messengers came with grim news: Jairus’s daughter was dead (Luke 8:49). Before Jairus could even respond, Jesus “overheard,” proving He is never out of touch with our circumstances (Psalm 139:1-4; 2 Chronicles 16:9).

• Nothing escapes His notice; His perfect awareness sets the stage for His intervention, reminding us that He both hears and cares (John 1:48; Hebrews 4:13).

and said to Jairus

• Jesus addresses Jairus personally, showing that faith is not an impersonal formula but a relationship. He knows Jairus by name, just as He later calls Zacchaeus down from the tree (Luke 19:5) and describes Himself as the Shepherd who “calls His own sheep by name” (John 10:3).

• By speaking directly, Christ takes leadership of the moment, inviting Jairus to follow His word rather than the fearful report.

Do not be afraid;

• Fear is the natural first reaction to loss, yet Jesus immediately forbids it. Throughout Scripture God’s people hear the same command: “Do not fear, for I am with you” (Isaiah 41:10; Joshua 1:9).

• Fear focuses on circumstances; faith focuses on Christ. Jesus knows fear and faith cannot coexist comfortably, so He calls Jairus to choose (2 Timothy 1:7).

just believe,

• The Greek text is continuous—keep believing. Jairus had already shown faith by seeking Jesus (Luke 8:41), and now he must persevere.

• Faith is simple trust in the person and promise of Jesus (John 3:16; Hebrews 11:6). Jesus does not ask Jairus to perform or to understand; He asks him to cling to confidence in His word (Mark 9:23).

and she will be healed.

• The promise is specific and unconditional: Jairus’s daughter “will” be restored. Christ’s authority extends over sickness and death alike (Luke 7:14-15; John 11:25-26).

• Healing here is literal—her life is returned. Yet it also foreshadows the ultimate healing every believer receives in resurrection life (Revelation 21:4; 1 Corinthians 15:52-57).

• Jesus’ word alone secures the outcome, echoing the centurion’s insight that a single command from Christ is enough (Matthew 8:8-13).

summary

Luke 8:50 records Jesus stepping into Jairus’s grief with omniscient awareness, personal concern, a clear command to reject fear, an invitation to steadfast faith, and a guaranteed promise of healing. The verse assures believers that Christ sees every crisis, speaks directly into it, calls us to courageous trust, and possesses absolute power to fulfill His word.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(50) Believe only.--There is a slight difference in the shade of meaning of the Greek tense as compared with the like command in St. Mark's report, the latter giving "Believe" as implying a permanent state--Be believing--St. Luke's report laying stress on the immediate act of faith.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 50. - But when Jesus heard it, he answered him, saying, Fear not: believe only, and she shall be made whole. No shadow of hesitation crossed the Redeemer's mind; with unruffled calmness he whispered his words of cheer to the grief-stricken father, and bade him fear nothing, for that all would yet be well with the child. Then follows the well-known, often-read story told in such few words, yet are they so vivid, so dramatic, that we seem to be looking on the scene. The grief-stricken household, the hired mourners, the still death-room, the white motionless form of the dead girl - the ruler's only child - lying on her little bed, the group of the six with tear-dimmed eyes standing round; the loving Master bending over the little dead, his smile as for a moment he took back the all-power he had laid aside a little season for our sakes; the far-off look in his eyes as for a moment his vision ranged over his old home of peace and grandeur; and then the two words spoken in the familiar Aramaic (Hebrew), which Mark, or rather Mark's master, Peter, remembered so well, "Talitha, kumi!" and the dead child rose up again, the spirit had returned to its frail tenement.
