Go!
This command is a direct and urgent instruction from Jesus to His disciples. It reflects the immediacy and importance of their mission. The imperative "Go" is reminiscent of the Great Commission in Matthew 28:19, where Jesus commands His followers to make disciples of all nations. The urgency of the mission underscores the need for obedience and action in spreading the Gospel.

I am sending you out
Jesus is the one commissioning the disciples, indicating His authority and the divine nature of their mission. This sending mirrors the sending of the prophets in the Old Testament, such as Isaiah (Isaiah 6:8) and Jeremiah (Jeremiah 1:7), who were also sent by God to deliver His message. The disciples are not acting on their own accord but are representatives of Christ, carrying His message and authority.

like lambs among wolves.
This phrase highlights the vulnerability and danger the disciples will face. Lambs are gentle and defenseless animals, while wolves are predators, symbolizing the hostility and opposition the disciples will encounter. This imagery is consistent with the persecution faced by early Christians, as seen in Acts and the epistles. It also reflects Jesus' own experience, as He was "the Lamb of God" (John 1:29) who faced opposition and sacrifice. The contrast between lambs and wolves emphasizes the need for reliance on God's protection and wisdom in the face of adversity.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker of this verse, Jesus is commissioning His disciples to go out and spread the message of the Kingdom of God. He is the central figure in this passage, providing guidance and instruction.

2. The Disciples
The immediate audience of Jesus' command. They are being sent out to preach and perform miracles in His name, representing the early Christian mission.

3. Lambs
Symbolic of innocence, vulnerability, and purity. The disciples are compared to lambs, indicating their need for reliance on God amidst their mission.

4. Wolves
Represent danger, hostility, and opposition. The world into which the disciples are sent is depicted as threatening and adversarial.

5. The Mission
The event of sending the disciples out to preach the Gospel, heal the sick, and proclaim the Kingdom of God.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God
As lambs among wolves, disciples must rely on God's protection and guidance. This teaches us to trust in God's provision and strength in the face of adversity.

Awareness of Spiritual Opposition
Recognize that following Christ involves spiritual warfare. Believers should be prepared for opposition and remain steadfast in their faith.

Innocence and Purity
Like lambs, Christians are called to maintain purity and innocence, avoiding the corrupting influences of the world while engaging with it.

Courage in Mission
Despite the dangers, Jesus commands His followers to "Go!" This encourages believers to be bold and courageous in sharing their faith, trusting in God's sovereignty.

Community and Support
The imagery of lambs suggests the need for community and mutual support among believers as they face challenges together.

What Does Luke 10:3 Mean
Go!

“Go!” (Luke 10:3) is a command, not a suggestion. Jesus calls His followers to move outward, leaving comfort for mission.

• The urgency echoes Matthew 28:19-20 and Mark 16:15, where the risen Lord sends disciples to “make disciples” and “preach the gospel.”

• Acts 1:8 shows the radius expanding—Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and “to the ends of the earth.” Every believer is part of this outward flow.

• The Lord never separates salvation from service. We are saved, then sent.

I am sending you out

Our going rests on His sending.

• Authority: In John 20:21 Jesus says, “As the Father has sent Me, I also send you.” We move under His commission, not our own initiative.

• Personal involvement: The “I” highlights Christ’s active role; He remains the Shepherd directing His flock (Psalm 23:1; John 10:4).

• Provision: Because He sends, He supplies (Philippians 4:19). The seventy-two in Luke 10 lacked purse, bag, or sandals, yet nothing essential was missing (Luke 10:4; 22:35).

• Representation: 2 Corinthians 5:20 calls us “ambassadors.” Our words and actions reflect the King’s character.

Like lambs among wolves

Jesus paints a vivid, literal picture of vulnerability and hostility.

• Lambs are gentle and defenseless; wolves are predatory. Christians should expect opposition (2 Timothy 3:12; John 15:18-20).

• The image does not license fear but dependence. The Good Shepherd protects His flock (John 10:11; Psalm 23:4).

• Method matters: We meet aggression with innocence and wisdom (Matthew 10:16—“shrewd as snakes and innocent as doves”).

• Spiritual warfare is real. Ephesians 6:11-18 urges us to “put on the full armor of God” because unseen forces prowl like wolves (1 Peter 5:8).

• Perseverance is promised. Though Paul warned of “savage wolves” invading the church (Acts 20:29), Christ secures ultimate victory (Romans 8:37).

summary

Luke 10:3 blends urgency with realism. Jesus commands His followers to go, assures them He is the One sending, and prepares them for a world often hostile to the gospel. Our confidence rests not in personal strength but in the Shepherd who sends and safeguards His lambs, empowering us to advance His kingdom with courage, clarity, and compassion.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Go your ways: behold, I send you forth as lambs among wolves. These first missionaries were to go forth unarmed and unprovided. They were to be a type of the strange, seemingly weak Christian preachers of the next two hundred years, before whose simple words and unarmed presence the great system of paganism was to go down. One of the rare but beautiful traditional sayings of the Lord is referred to the first occasion of his speaking the words of this third verse. Peter is said to have asked him, "But how, then, if the wolves tear the lambs?" And the Lord said, "Let not the lambs fear the wolves, when the lambs are once dead;" and then added again the words of Matthew 10:28, "Fear not them which kill the body," etc.
