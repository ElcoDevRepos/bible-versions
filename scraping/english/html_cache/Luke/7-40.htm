Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But Jesus answered him
In this context, Jesus is responding to Simon the Pharisee, who had been silently judging Jesus for allowing a sinful woman to anoint His feet. This demonstrates Jesus' omniscience, as He perceives Simon's thoughts without them being spoken. This ability to know the hearts and minds of individuals is a divine attribute, aligning with passages such as John 2:24-25, where it is noted that Jesus knew what was in man.

“Simon, I have something to tell you.”
Jesus addresses Simon directly, indicating a personal and intentional engagement. The use of Simon's name suggests a personal relationship and a call to attention. This approach is reminiscent of how God often calls individuals by name in Scripture, such as Moses in Exodus 3:4, indicating the importance of the message to follow. Jesus' method of teaching through parables and direct conversation is a hallmark of His ministry, as seen throughout the Gospels.

“Tell me, Teacher,” he said.
Simon’s response acknowledges Jesus as a "Teacher," a term of respect that recognizes Jesus' authority and wisdom. However, it also reflects a limited understanding of Jesus' true identity as the Messiah. The title "Teacher" is frequently used in the Gospels, often by those who recognize Jesus' authority in teaching but do not fully grasp His divine nature. This interaction sets the stage for the parable of the two debtors, which follows, illustrating themes of forgiveness and grace.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who is teaching and revealing truths about the Kingdom of God.

2. Simon the Pharisee
A Pharisee who invited Jesus to his house. His interaction with Jesus provides a backdrop for a lesson on forgiveness and love.

3. The Event
This verse is part of the account where a sinful woman anoints Jesus' feet with perfume at Simon's house, leading to a teaching moment about forgiveness and gratitude.
Teaching Points
Understanding Forgiveness
Jesus uses this moment to teach about the depth of God's forgiveness. Just as the woman was forgiven much, we too are called to forgive others.

Recognizing Jesus' Authority
Simon addresses Jesus as "Teacher," acknowledging His authority. We should also recognize and submit to Jesus' authority in our lives.

Responding to Jesus' Teachings
Like Simon, we are invited to listen to Jesus' teachings. Our response should be one of openness and willingness to learn.

The Role of Gratitude
The woman's actions demonstrate profound gratitude for forgiveness. We should cultivate a heart of gratitude for the grace we receive.

Judgment vs. Compassion
Simon's initial judgment of the woman contrasts with Jesus' compassion. We are called to show compassion rather than judgment.

What Does Luke 7:40 Mean
But Jesus answered him

• Even before Simon verbalizes a question, Jesus responds, showing His divine awareness of hearts and thoughts (see “Jesus, knowing their thoughts…” in Matthew 9:4; also John 2:24-25).

• The conjunction “But” indicates a contrast with the silent judgment Simon has just formed about the woman anointing Jesus’ feet (Luke 7:39). Christ steps in to correct Simon’s inward attitude, emphasizing that our unspoken reasoning is fully exposed to Him (Hebrews 4:13).

• By initiating the conversation, Jesus models proactive grace—He does not wait for Simon to seek clarity; He seeks Simon for correction and redemption (Luke 19:10).

Simon

• Jesus addresses the Pharisee by name, making the encounter personal. Scripture often records God calling people by name to draw them into relationship (Exodus 3:4; John 20:16).

• Naming him highlights accountability. Simon cannot hide behind his religious status; he must face the truth person-to-person (Romans 2:1).

• The personal call underscores that faith is never generic; it is specific and individual (John 10:3).

I have something to tell you

• Christ’s statement signals a purposeful teaching moment. Whenever Jesus says He has “something” to convey, it carries eternal weight (John 16:12-13).

• This approach invites listening rather than condemning. Jesus offers revelation before rebuke, mirroring James 1:19’s call to be “quick to listen.”

• We learn that Jesus desires dialogue that penetrates the heart, not mere external conformity (Isaiah 1:18).

Tell me, Teacher

• Simon’s reply sounds respectful—he calls Jesus “Teacher”—yet the narrative soon exposes his limited understanding. Respectful titles without surrendered hearts fall short (Matthew 7:21-23).

• Still, Simon’s openness to hear gives Jesus room to speak a parable of the two debtors (Luke 7:41-43), illustrating grace. Even tentative willingness can become a gateway for truth (Acts 17:11-12).

• The exchange reminds believers to invite Christ’s correction regularly. When we say, “Tell me, Teacher,” we position ourselves for growth (Psalm 139:23-24).

summary

Luke 7:40 shows Jesus lovingly confronting hidden judgment. He calls Simon by name, invites attentive listening, and sets the stage for a transforming lesson on forgiveness. The verse reveals Christ’s heart to address inner attitudes, value personal engagement, and lead His listeners from mere respect to genuine understanding and grace.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(40) And Jesus answering said . . .--The answer was, as the context shows, to the unspoken thoughts of the Pharisee.

Simon, I have somewhat to say unto thee.--The name of the Pharisee is thus given to us, but it was too common to suggest any identification. It is a somewhat singular coincidence that the two anointings should have happened (comp. Matthew 26:6) each of them in the house of a Simon, but it cannot be looked on as more than accidental.

Master, say on.--The term used is 'one which implied recognition of our Lord's character as a teacher or Rabbi.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 40. - And Jesus answering said unto him, Simon, I have somewhat to say unto thee. And he saith, Master, say on. How accurately did the Master read Simon's heart. Not a real Prophet because he was in ignorance of the character and life of the woman whom he suffered without rebuke to pour the fragrant ointment over him! We almost see the half-sad smile flickering on the Teacher's lips as he turned and spoke to his host. Such a parable-story as Jesus was about to give utterance to was no uncommon form of teaching on such an occasion when a well-known Rabbi like Jesus was Guest at a festal gathering.
