Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Her spirit returned
This phrase indicates the miraculous nature of the event, as it signifies the restoration of life. In biblical terms, the "spirit" often refers to the life force or breath given by God (Genesis 2:7). The return of the spirit signifies divine intervention, affirming Jesus' authority over life and death. This miracle is a testament to Jesus' power, similar to the raising of Lazarus (John 11:43-44) and the widow's son at Nain (Luke 7:14-15). It fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah bringing life (Isaiah 26:19).

and at once she got up
The immediacy of her action underscores the completeness of the miracle. There is no period of recovery or weakness, which highlights the totality of Jesus' healing power. This immediate response is consistent with other miracles performed by Jesus, where healing is instantaneous (Mark 5:29, John 5:9). It also reflects the cultural understanding of resurrection, where the restored individual is fully functional, as seen in Elijah and Elisha's miracles (1 Kings 17:22, 2 Kings 4:35).

And He directed that she be given something to eat
This instruction serves multiple purposes. Practically, it demonstrates Jesus' care for the girl's physical needs, ensuring her well-being. Culturally, eating is a sign of life and normalcy, confirming the reality of her resurrection. It also serves as a proof to the witnesses that she is truly alive and not a ghost, as spirits do not eat (Luke 24:41-43). This act parallels Jesus' post-resurrection appearances, where He eats with His disciples to prove His physical resurrection (Luke 24:42-43, John 21:12-13).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is performing a miracle by raising a young girl from the dead. His authority over life and death is a key theme.

2. The Young Girl
The daughter of Jairus, a synagogue leader, who had died but was brought back to life by Jesus. Her resurrection is a testament to Jesus' divine power.

3. Jairus
A synagogue leader who sought Jesus' help for his dying daughter. His faith in Jesus' ability to heal is significant in this account.

4. The Crowd
Those who were present at Jairus' house, including mourners and possibly skeptics, who witnessed the miracle.

5. The Disciples
Jesus' followers who were likely present and witnessed this miracle, further solidifying their understanding of His divine nature.
Teaching Points
The Power of Jesus Over Death
Jesus' ability to raise the dead confirms His divine authority and power, offering hope of eternal life to believers.

Faith in Action
Jairus' approach to Jesus in faith, despite his daughter's death, serves as a model for believers to trust in Jesus' power and timing.

The Importance of Physical Needs
Jesus' instruction to give the girl something to eat highlights the importance of caring for physical needs, even after spiritual or miraculous events.

Witnessing Miracles
The presence of witnesses to the miracle underscores the importance of sharing testimonies of God's work in our lives to strengthen the faith of others.

Compassion and Restoration
Jesus' actions demonstrate His compassion and desire to restore not just life, but wholeness, to individuals and families.

What Does Luke 8:55 Mean
Her spirit returned

- Scripture affirms that at death the spirit departs the body (Ecclesiastes 12:7; James 2:26). Here, “her spirit returned” (Luke 8:55) states plainly that life was literally restored to Jairus’s daughter.

- Jesus is revealed as Lord over life and death. This miracle anticipates His own resurrection and foreshadows the promise of bodily resurrection for believers (John 11:25-26; 1 Corinthians 15:20-22).

- No incantations or medical procedures appear—only Christ’s authority. Like in 1 Kings 17:22, where Elijah prayed and a child’s life returned, the power behind the miracle is the living God Himself, now present in Jesus.

and at once she got up

- The immediacy underscores the completeness of the healing. There is no recovery period, just instant vitality, echoing other instantaneous miracles such as the paralytic in Luke 5:25 and Peter’s mother-in-law in Luke 4:39.

- Physical evidence silences doubt. Everyone in the room—parents and the three disciples (Mark 5:37)—saw the transformation, supporting the eyewitness credibility Luke emphasizes (Luke 1:3-4).

- The girl’s rising points to the new life granted to all who hear Christ’s call (Ephesians 2:4-6). Resurrection power is not theoretical; it produces visible change.

And He directed that she be given something to eat

- A practical instruction proves the reality of the miracle. Ghosts don’t eat (Luke 24:41-43). Jesus offers an ordinary action to validate an extraordinary event.

- The tenderness of Christ shines: He cares not only about triumphant moments but about a hungry twelve-year-old’s next meal (Psalm 103:13-14; Philippians 4:19).

- Providing food also engages the parents, inviting them to act in faith and resume normal family life—similar to Jesus involving servants at Cana (John 2:7-8).

summary

Luke 8:55 shows Jesus literally restoring life, instantly reviving the girl, and thoughtfully attending to her needs. The verse proclaims His sovereign power over death, offers tangible proof of resurrection life, and reminds us that the Lord who conquers the grave also cares for everyday necessities.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(55) Her spirit came again.--The precise form of expression is peculiar to St. Luke, and is, perhaps, characteristic of the more accurate phraseology that belonged to him as a physician.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 55. - He commanded to give her meat. She had been grievously ill, sick, we know, even to death; and now that the old strength and health had come back again, the Master felt she would at once, after her long abstinence, need food. Even the child's mother was not so motherly as Jesus.
