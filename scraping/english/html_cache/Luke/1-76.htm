Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And you, child, will be called a prophet of the Most High;
This phrase refers to John the Baptist, the son of Zechariah and Elizabeth. The title "prophet of the Most High" signifies John's role as a divinely appointed messenger. In the Jewish tradition, prophets were seen as spokespersons for God, delivering His messages to the people. The term "Most High" is a title for God, emphasizing His supreme authority and sovereignty. This prophecy aligns with the Old Testament tradition, where prophets like Elijah and Isaiah were called to prepare the people for God's work. John's prophetic role is further confirmed in Matthew 11:9-11, where Jesus Himself acknowledges John as more than a prophet.

for you will go on before the Lord
This phrase indicates John's mission to precede the coming of the Lord, who is identified as Jesus Christ. The concept of going "before the Lord" is rooted in the prophetic tradition, where a forerunner prepares the way for a significant divine intervention. This echoes the prophecy in Malachi 3:1, which speaks of a messenger preparing the way before the Lord. John's ministry of baptism and repentance was a preparatory act, calling people to turn back to God in anticipation of the Messiah's arrival.

to prepare the way for Him,
The imagery of preparing the way is drawn from ancient practices where roads were cleared and leveled for the arrival of a king or dignitary. Spiritually, this preparation involved calling people to repentance and readiness for the coming of Jesus. Isaiah 40:3-5 is a key Old Testament reference, where a voice cries out to prepare the way of the Lord, making straight paths in the wilderness. John's role was to fulfill this prophecy, making the people ready to receive Jesus' message of salvation. This preparation was not just physical but spiritual, emphasizing the need for a heart transformation to welcome the Messiah.

Persons / Places / Events

The father of John the Baptist, a priest who prophesied over his son after regaining his speech.

2. John the Baptist
The child referred to in this verse, who would grow up to be the forerunner of Jesus Christ, preparing the way for the Lord.

3. The Most High
A title for God, emphasizing His supreme authority and sovereignty.

4. The Lord
Refers to Jesus Christ, whose way John the Baptist was to prepare.

5. The Prophecy
Zechariah's prophecy, known as the Benedictus, which speaks of the roles and missions of both John the Baptist and Jesus.
Teaching Points
The Role of a Prophet
John the Baptist was called to be a prophet, a role that involves speaking God's truth and preparing people for His coming. We, too, are called to share God's truth in our lives.

Preparation for the Lord
Just as John prepared the way for Jesus, we are called to prepare our hearts and the hearts of others for His return. This involves repentance, faith, and living a life that reflects Christ's teachings.

The Importance of Obedience
Zechariah's prophecy highlights the importance of obedience to God's calling. John the Baptist's life was marked by obedience, and we are encouraged to follow his example.

God's Sovereign Plan
The prophecy over John the Baptist shows God's sovereign plan in action. Trusting in God's plan for our lives, even when we don't fully understand it, is crucial for our faith journey.

The Power of Prophecy
Prophecy in the Bible serves to guide, warn, and encourage God's people. Understanding and applying biblical prophecy can strengthen our faith and provide direction.

What Does Luke 1:76 Mean
And you, child

• These words come from Zechariah’s Spirit-filled prophecy over his newborn son, John (Luke 1:67-79).

• By addressing the infant directly, Zechariah highlights John’s personal calling, showing that God’s purposes are woven into an individual’s life from the very beginning (Jeremiah 1:5; Psalm 139:13-16).

• The “child” is clearly John the Baptist, whose birth had already been announced by Gabriel (Luke 1:13) and whose arrival brought great joy to his parents and community (Luke 1:57-58).

Will be called a prophet of the Most High

• John is given the unique title “prophet of the Most High,” pointing to the majesty and sovereignty of God (Psalm 47:2).

• Unlike the long-silent prophetic voice in Israel since Malachi, John would break the silence and speak God’s words directly to the people (Matthew 11:9-10).

• Jesus later confirms this identity: “Yes, I tell you, and more than a prophet” (Luke 7:26-28), underscoring John’s special role in redemptive history.

• Being “called” a prophet implies public recognition; John’s ministry drew crowds from all Judea (Luke 3:2-3), and even Herod feared him, knowing he was righteous and holy (Mark 6:20).

For you will go on before the Lord

• “The Lord” here refers to the Messiah—Jesus Himself—showing that John’s mission is directly tied to the arrival of God in the flesh (John 1:29-30).

• This fulfills Isaiah 40:3, “A voice of one calling in the wilderness, ‘Prepare the way for the LORD; make straight paths for Him,’” and Malachi 3:1, “I will send My messenger, and he will prepare the way before Me.”

• John’s life literally precedes Jesus’ public appearance by about six months (Luke 1:36) and his ministry by only a short season (Luke 3:1-6).

• Going “before the Lord” signifies a herald’s task—announcing royal arrival—rooted in ancient Near Eastern practice where roads were cleared for visiting kings.

To prepare the way for Him

• John’s preparation involved preaching repentance and administering baptism “for the forgiveness of sins” (Luke 3:3), softening hearts to receive the Savior.

• He called people to ethical fruitfulness—sharing possessions, honest dealings, contentment (Luke 3:10-14)—illustrating genuine repentance.

• His message pointed away from himself and toward Christ: “He must increase, I must decrease” (John 3:30).

• By identifying Jesus as “the Lamb of God” (John 1:29, 36), John directed seekers to the One who would take away sin, thereby smoothing spiritual obstacles and clarifying God’s redemptive plan.

summary

Luke 1:76 celebrates God’s deliberate design for John the Baptist: a divinely appointed child who would become the long-awaited prophet, step onto history’s stage ahead of the Messiah, and ready hearts for Jesus’ saving work. Each phrase underscores John’s identity, his relationship to the Lord, and his pivotal task of spiritual road-building so that when Christ appeared, the people would recognize and receive their King.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(76) Thou, child, shalt be called the prophet of the Highest.--Note the recurrence of the same divine name that had appeared in Luke 1:32; Luke 1:35.

Thou shalt go before the face of the Lord.--The verse is, as it were, an echo of two great prophecies, combining the "going before Jehovah" of Malachi 3:1, with the "preparing the way" of Isaiah 40:3.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 76. - And thou, child; literally, little child. Here the father breaks forth into an expression of gladness at the thought of the great part his baby-son was to bear in this great national deliverance. His son, too - oh, joy undreamed of! - is to be ranked among the glorious company of the prophets of the Highest.
