What does it profit a man
This phrase introduces a rhetorical question that challenges the listener to consider the true value of worldly success. In biblical context, profit often refers to material gain or advantage. The question implies that there is a deeper, more significant measure of success than material wealth. This echoes the wisdom literature of the Old Testament, such as Ecclesiastes, which questions the ultimate value of earthly pursuits.

to gain the whole world
"Gaining the whole world" symbolizes achieving the highest level of worldly success and possession. Historically, this could refer to the vast empires and wealth of rulers like Alexander the Great or the Roman Caesars, who were known for their extensive conquests and riches. The phrase suggests the ultimate achievement of earthly power and material wealth, yet it is presented as insufficient in the grand scheme of spiritual reality.

yet lose or forfeit his very self?
This part of the verse highlights the spiritual and eternal consequences of prioritizing worldly gain over one's soul. The terms "lose" and "forfeit" suggest a voluntary exchange or sacrifice of something invaluable. In the cultural context of Jesus' time, the soul was understood as the essence of a person, their true identity and eternal being. This teaching aligns with other scriptural warnings about the dangers of wealth and the importance of spiritual integrity, such as in Matthew 6:19-21, where Jesus advises storing up treasures in heaven rather than on earth. The concept of losing one's soul is also a stark reminder of the eternal perspective that Jesus consistently taught, emphasizing the kingdom of God over earthly kingdoms.

2. Disciples

5. The Soul (or Self)

Teaching Points
The Value of the Soul
The soul is of infinite worth, surpassing all material wealth. Prioritize spiritual health and eternal life over temporary gains.

The Illusion of Worldly Success
Worldly success is fleeting and cannot satisfy the deepest needs of the human heart. True fulfillment is found in Christ.

The Cost of Discipleship
Following Jesus requires a willingness to sacrifice worldly ambitions. Evaluate what you are willing to give up to follow Him.

Eternal Perspective
Cultivate an eternal perspective that values what lasts forever over what is temporary. Make decisions with eternity in mind.

Self-Examination
Regularly examine your priorities and align them with God's will. Are you investing in what truly matters?

What Does Luke 9:25 Mean
What does it profit

• Jesus poses the value statement: “What does it profit…” (Luke 9:25).

• The word profit speaks of bottom-line gain; it weighs outcomes, just as Proverbs 11:4 reminds that “Riches do not profit in the day of wrath.”

• In Matthew 16:26 and Mark 8:36 the same warning appears, underscoring that this is a non-negotiable principle, not a passing comment.

• Paul echoes the thought in 1 Timothy 6:6-10, where he contrasts fleeting riches with true gain found in godliness.

A man

• The Lord addresses every person—no one is exempt (Romans 3:23).

• Psalm 49:7-9 stresses that no man can redeem another’s life; each must face this verse personally.

• Hebrews 9:27 reminds that “people are destined to die once, and after that to face judgment,” so the appeal is urgent and individual.

To gain the whole world

• Jesus exaggerates deliberately: even if someone captured every ounce of earthly success, it would still be a shaky investment.

• Solomon’s experiment in Ecclesiastes 2:4-11 shows that palaces, vineyards, and treasures left him empty.

• The parable of the rich fool in Luke 12:16-21 depicts a man piling up barns yet dying that very night.

• Satan offered Jesus “all the kingdoms of the world” in Matthew 4:8-10; Christ refused, proving that worldly gain is never worth spiritual compromise.

Yet lose or forfeit

• “Lose” pictures ultimate ruin; “forfeit” stresses a voluntary trade-off. People can knowingly exchange eternal life for temporal advantage, as Judas did (Acts 1:16-20).

• Hebrews 2:3 warns against “neglecting so great a salvation,” showing that loss can come by simple indifference.

• Romans 6:23 states that “the wages of sin is death,” identifying the true cost behind the bargain.

His very self

• The phrase points to the soul—your conscious, eternal person (Mark 8:37).

• Jesus speaks of the everlasting dimension addressed again in Matthew 25:46, where destinies are “eternal life” or “eternal punishment.”

• Revelation 20:11-15 shows the finality of the Great White Throne judgment, when anyone not in the Book of Life is cast into the lake of fire; that is the ultimate forfeiture.

• In hopeful contrast, Psalm 49:15 declares, “God will redeem my soul from Sheol,” a promise secured through Christ (1 Peter 1:9).

summary

Luke 9:25 weighs temporal success against eternal destiny and declares that the exchange rate is disastrously lopsided. Earthly gain, even at its widest possible extent, vanishes next to the worth of a single soul. The verse summons every individual to place faith in Christ, hold the world loosely, and treasure the life that lasts forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(25) And lose himself, or be cast away.--Better, destroy himself, or suffer loss. The first word expresses a more direct act, as of self-destruction, and the second (see Note on Matthew 16:16) implies the thought of the forfeiture of something precious rather than of being absolutely rejected. It presents, so to speak, a slightly softened aspect of the previous words.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - For what is a man advantaged, if he gain the whole world, and lose himself, or be cast away? Godet's comment here is pithy and quaint: "Jesus supposes, in this twenty-fifth verse, the act of saving one's own life accomplished with the most complete success... amounting to a gain of the whole world. But in this very moment, the master of this magnificent domain finds himself condemned to perish! What gain to draw in a lottery a gallery of pictures... and at the same time to become blind!" "O flesh," writes Luther (quoted by Dr. Morrison), "how mighty art thou, that thou canst still throw darkness over those things, even to the minds of the holy!"
