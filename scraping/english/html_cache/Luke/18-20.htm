Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You know the commandments:
This phrase indicates that Jesus is speaking to someone familiar with the Jewish Law, specifically the Ten Commandments given to Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 20:1-17). It underscores the importance of the Law in Jewish life and its role as a moral guide. Jesus often referenced the commandments to highlight their enduring significance and to point out deeper spiritual truths.

‘Do not commit adultery:
This commandment, found in Exodus 20:14, emphasizes the sanctity of marriage and the importance of faithfulness. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, adultery was not only a personal sin but also a social offense that could disrupt family and community stability. Jesus later expands on this commandment in Matthew 5:27-28, teaching that even lustful thoughts violate this law, thus deepening its moral implications.

do not murder:
This commandment, from Exodus 20:13, prohibits the unlawful taking of human life, reflecting the value God places on life. In the historical context, murder was seen as a grave sin against both God and society. Jesus further expounds on this in Matthew 5:21-22, equating anger with murder, thereby addressing the heart's intent and not just the physical act.

do not steal:
Exodus 20:15 commands against taking what does not belong to you, highlighting the importance of respecting others' property. In the ancient world, theft could lead to severe consequences, disrupting social order. This commandment is echoed throughout scripture, emphasizing integrity and trustworthiness (Ephesians 4:28).

do not bear false witness:
This commandment, found in Exodus 20:16, prohibits lying, especially in legal settings. In ancient Israel, truthful testimony was crucial for justice. Bearing false witness could lead to wrongful punishment and societal discord. Jesus emphasizes truthfulness in all communication (Matthew 5:37), reflecting God's nature as truth.

honor your father and mother:
Exodus 20:12 commands respect and obedience to parents, promising long life as a reward. This reflects the family structure's importance in Israelite society, where honoring parents was foundational to social stability. Paul reiterates this commandment in Ephesians 6:1-3, emphasizing its continued relevance for Christian families.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker of this verse, Jesus is addressing a rich young ruler who inquires about eternal life. Jesus uses this opportunity to teach about the commandments and the heart's posture towards God and wealth.

2. Rich Young Ruler
A man of wealth and status who approaches Jesus with a question about inheriting eternal life. His encounter with Jesus highlights the challenge of prioritizing spiritual wealth over material wealth.

3. The Commandments
Jesus refers to the Ten Commandments, which are foundational laws given by God to Moses on Mount Sinai. These commandments are central to Jewish and Christian ethical teachings.
Teaching Points
The Importance of the Commandments
Jesus emphasizes the commandments as a moral foundation. They are not just rules but a guide to living a life that honors God and respects others.

Heart Over Legalism
While the rich young ruler knew the commandments, Jesus challenges him to look beyond mere legalistic adherence and examine his heart's true allegiance.

Wealth and Spiritual Priorities
The encounter highlights the potential conflict between material wealth and spiritual devotion. Jesus calls for a heart that prioritizes eternal values over temporal riches.

Honoring Relationships
The commandments Jesus mentions focus on relationships—both with God and others. This underscores the importance of integrity and respect in our interactions.

Self-Examination
Jesus' response invites us to examine our own lives: Are we truly living out the commandments in spirit and truth, or are we merely following them outwardly?

What Does Luke 18:20 Mean
Do not commit adultery

“You shall not commit adultery.” (Exodus 20:14)

• God designed marriage as the sole context for sexual intimacy (Genesis 2:24).

• Jesus deepens the command by addressing lust in the heart (Matthew 5:27-28).

• Faithfulness pictures Christ’s covenant love for His people (Ephesians 5:31-32).

Living it out:

– Guard heart and eyes (Job 31:1; 1 Thessalonians 4:3-4).

– Cultivate contentment and affection within marriage (Proverbs 5:15-18; Hebrews 13:4).

do not murder

“You shall not murder.” (Exodus 20:13)

• Human life bears God’s image (Genesis 9:6).

• Jesus equates unrighteous anger with murder’s root (Matthew 5:21-22; 1 John 3:15).

• Love fulfills the law by protecting life (Romans 13:9-10).

Living it out:

– Reject hatred, resentment, and violence (Ephesians 4:31).

– Defend the vulnerable and promote life (Proverbs 24:11-12; James 1:27).

do not steal

“You shall not steal.” (Exodus 20:15)

• God entrusts possessions as stewardship, not entitlement (Psalm 24:1).

• Taking what is not ours violates neighborly love (Leviticus 19:11).

• Transformation in Christ turns takers into givers (Ephesians 4:28).

Living it out:

– Practice honest work and fair dealings (Proverbs 11:1).

– Cultivate generosity toward need (Acts 20:35; 2 Corinthians 9:7).

do not bear false witness

“You shall not give false testimony against your neighbor.” (Exodus 20:16)

• Truth reflects God’s character (Numbers 23:19; John 14:6).

• Lies destroy trust and community (Proverbs 12:22).

• Believers are called to transparent speech (Colossians 3:9-10; Ephesians 4:25).

Living it out:

– Refuse gossip, slander, exaggeration.

– Speak words that edify and align with reality (Proverbs 15:4).

honor your father and mother

“Honor your father and your mother.” (Exodus 20:12)

• First command with a promise of blessing (Ephesians 6:2-3).

• Respect for parental authority trains the heart for submission to God-ordained order (Hebrews 12:9).

• Care for parents in their need displays genuine faith (1 Timothy 5:4).

Living it out:

– Show gratitude, obedience, and practical care.

– Speak honorably of parents even when disagreeing (Proverbs 23:22).

summary

Jesus cites these five commands to reveal the rich young ruler’s true spiritual condition (Luke 18:21-23). Far from a checklist, each word exposes the heart’s need for repentance and total reliance on Christ. The passage invites us to examine our own obedience, recognize where we fall short, and cling to the Savior who perfectly kept every command on our behalf (2 Corinthians 5:21; Romans 8:3-4).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) Thou knowest the commandments.--St. Luke here agrees with St. Matthew in omitting the "defraud not," which we find in St. Mark.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - Thou knowest the commandments. The report in St. Matthew is somewhat fuller. There the ruler, when directed to the commandments, replies by asking "which?" expecting most likely to be referred to some of the elaborate traditional laws of the rabbinic schools, which were difficult to keep even by men in the position of a wealthy Pharisee; but to his surprise Jesus mentions the most general and best-known of the ancient ten.
