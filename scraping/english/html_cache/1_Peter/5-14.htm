Greet one another with a kiss of love.
In the early Christian church, the "kiss of love" was a common greeting among believers, symbolizing unity, fellowship, and mutual affection. This practice is rooted in the cultural customs of the time, where a kiss was a sign of respect and friendship. The "kiss of love" is mentioned in other New Testament passages, such as Romans 16:16 and 1 Corinthians 16:20, indicating its widespread acceptance among early Christians. It served as a physical expression of the spiritual bond shared by those in the faith, transcending social and ethnic barriers. This greeting reflects the deep sense of community and love that characterized the early church, as believers were called to demonstrate Christ's love to one another.

Peace to all of you who are in Christ.
The concept of "peace" in this context goes beyond the absence of conflict; it encompasses a sense of wholeness, well-being, and spiritual tranquility that comes from being in a right relationship with God through Jesus Christ. This peace is a fulfillment of Jesus' promise in John 14:27, where He assures His followers of a peace that the world cannot give. The phrase "in Christ" signifies the believers' union with Jesus, highlighting their identity and security in Him. This peace is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22) and is available to all who have accepted Christ as their Savior. The blessing of peace is a common benediction in the New Testament, underscoring its importance in the life of a believer and the community of faith.

Teaching Points
Expression of Christian Love
The "kiss of love" is a cultural expression of the deep, familial love that should characterize Christian relationships. While cultural expressions may change, the underlying principle of genuine love and affection remains timeless.

Unity in Christ
The greeting signifies the unity and equality of all believers in Christ, transcending cultural and social barriers. This unity is a powerful testimony to the world of the transformative power of the Gospel.

Peace as a Gift from Christ
The peace Peter speaks of is a profound, spiritual peace that comes from being in Christ. It is a peace that sustains believers through trials and unites them in purpose and mission.

Cultural Context and Modern Application
While the specific practice of greeting with a kiss may not be common in all cultures today, the principle of warmly and sincerely greeting fellow believers is universally applicable. Modern equivalents might include a handshake, hug, or verbal greeting.

Living Out the Gospel
The call to greet one another with love and to live in peace is a practical outworking of the Gospel. It challenges believers to actively demonstrate the love and peace of Christ in their interactions with others.

What Does 1 Peter 5:14 Mean
Greet one another with a kiss of love

Peter’s closing line begins with a very tangible instruction: “Greet one another with a kiss of love” (1 Peter 5:14).

• In the first-century church this “holy kiss” (Romans 16:16; 1 Corinthians 16:20; 2 Corinthians 13:12; 1 Thessalonians 5:26) was an open, brotherly sign of unity across social and ethnic lines.

• The literal act underscored that the family of God is bound together by real affection, not mere words.

• While cultural forms of greeting differ today, the principle remains unchanged: believers are to show warm, sincere, visible love for each other (John 13:34-35).

• Practical applications include:

– Welcoming newcomers personally rather than leaving them on the fringe.

– Restoring strained relationships quickly so warmth is genuine (Matthew 5:23-24).

– Choosing expressions—handshake, hug, encouraging words—that communicate the same heartfelt acceptance the “kiss of love” once did.

Peace to all of you who are in Christ

Peter then blesses the readers: “Peace to all of you who are in Christ” (1 Peter 5:14).

• Peace is more than the absence of conflict; it is the wholeness Jesus promised: “Peace I leave with you; My peace I give you” (John 14:27).

• This peace comes only “in Christ.” Through faith we are united with Him, justified, and therefore “have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ” (Romans 5:1).

• Because God’s peace guards hearts and minds (Philippians 4:7), Peter’s benediction carries real power, not polite sentiment.

• The blessing is corporate: “all of you.” Peace should characterize the entire fellowship, reflecting the reconciling work of the cross (Ephesians 2:14-16).

summary

Peter’s final sentence reminds believers to embody affectionate, tangible love toward one another and to rest in the comprehensive peace granted to every person united with Christ. Warm fellowship and God-given peace together display the gospel to the watching world and strengthen the church within.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) Kiss of charity.--Not only does he wish them to receive the greetings of the Roman Church, but to display their brotherly love to each other as well. On the kiss of charity, see 1Thessalonians 5:26. The "peace" which he wishes to them includes, though it is not limited to, peace amongst themselves.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Greet ye one another with a kiss of charity. St. Paul gives the same direction in four places (Romans 16:16; 1 Corinthians 16:20; 2 Corinthians 13:12; 1 Thessalonians 5:26). The practice seems to have been universal in early times; it is mentioned by Justin Martyr, Tertullian, Chrysostom, Augustine, and other ancient writers (see Bingham's 'Antiquities,' 15. 3. 3). It is now used only in the Coptic Church of Egypt. Rites and ceremonies may be changed "according to the diversities of countries, times, and men's manners;" the sacred duty of brotherly love remains unchanged forever. Peace be with you all that are in Christ Jesus. Amen. The most ancient manuscripts omit the word "Jesus" here and the "Amen? St. Paul's blessing at the end of his Epistles is usually "grace" (in the Epistle to the Ephesians he adds "peace"). St. Peter ends his Epistle with the benediction which he had so often heard from the Savior's lips. That blessed gift of peace is granted to all who are "in Christ," who is our Peace (Ephesians 2:14).
