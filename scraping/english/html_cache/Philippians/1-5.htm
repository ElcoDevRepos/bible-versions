Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
because of your partnership in the gospel
The term "partnership" here is translated from the Greek word "koinonia," which implies a deep fellowship and sharing. This partnership is not merely financial support but a spiritual and communal involvement in the mission of spreading the gospel. The Philippians were known for their generosity and support of Paul's ministry, as seen in 2 Corinthians 8:1-5, where Paul commends the Macedonian churches, including Philippi, for their giving despite their own poverty. This partnership reflects the early church's communal nature, as described in Acts 2:42-47, where believers shared everything in common and supported one another in their mission.

in the gospel
The gospel, or "good news," refers to the message of Jesus Christ's death, burial, and resurrection for the salvation of humanity. This message was central to Paul's ministry and the early church's mission. The Philippians' partnership in the gospel indicates their active role in evangelism and discipleship, aligning with the Great Commission in Matthew 28:19-20. Their involvement would have included prayer, financial support, and possibly even sending members to assist Paul, as seen with Epaphroditus in Philippians 2:25.

from the first day
This phrase highlights the immediacy and consistency of the Philippians' support. From the moment Paul first preached in Philippi, as recorded in Acts 16:11-40, the church there was actively involved in his ministry. Lydia, a prominent convert, immediately opened her home to Paul and his companions, demonstrating the early and enthusiastic support of the Philippian believers. This initial response set the tone for their ongoing partnership.

until now
The phrase "until now" underscores the enduring nature of the Philippians' commitment. Despite the passage of time and the challenges faced by both Paul and the Philippian church, their support remained steadfast. This continuity is a testament to their faithfulness and dedication to the gospel mission. It also reflects the broader theme of perseverance found throughout the New Testament, as seen in Hebrews 12:1-2, encouraging believers to run the race with endurance. The Philippians' unwavering support serves as a model for Christian commitment and partnership in ministry.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Partnership in Ministry
The Philippians' partnership with Paul highlights the importance of collaboration in spreading the gospel. Believers are called to support one another in ministry efforts, whether through prayer, financial support, or active participation.

Generosity as a Reflection of Faith
The Philippians' generosity serves as a model for Christians today. Their willingness to give, even in difficult circumstances, reflects a deep trust in God and commitment to His work.

Consistency in Faith and Action
From the "first day until now," the Philippians remained steadfast in their support. This consistency is a testament to their faith and dedication, encouraging believers to remain faithful in their commitments.

The Role of Gratitude in Christian Relationships
Paul's expression of gratitude for the Philippians' partnership underscores the importance of recognizing and appreciating the contributions of others in the faith community.

What Does Philippians 1:5 Mean
because of

- “I thank my God every time I remember you. In every prayer for all of you, I always pray with joy, because of your partnership in the gospel from the first day until now” (Philippians 1:3-5).

- Paul’s gratitude is rooted in an observable fact, not sentiment. Their actions caused his thanksgiving, echoing the pattern of 1 Thessalonians 1:2-3 and 2 Thessalonians 1:3, where thanksgiving flows from clear evidence of grace.

your partnership

- The Philippians shared tangibly and sacrificially:

• Financially – “no church but you partnered with me in the matter of giving and receiving” (Philippians 4:15-16).

• Personally – Epaphroditus risked his life to deliver their gift (Philippians 2:25-30).

• Spiritually – “you all share in the grace with me” (Philippians 1:7).

- Similar fellowship appears in 2 Corinthians 8:1-5, where Macedonian believers “gave beyond their ability” to advance the ministry.

in the gospel

- The partnership’s focus was not merely Paul but the message: “the gospel … is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes” (Romans 1:16).

- Paul had earlier defined that gospel: “Christ died for our sins … was buried … was raised on the third day” (1 Corinthians 15:3-4).

- Guarding that message mattered; Galatians 1:8-9 warns against any distortion. The Philippians put their resources behind the true, saving proclamation.

from the first day

- “First day” points to Acts 16, when Lydia believed and immediately invited the missionaries into her home (Acts 16:14-15).

- The Philippian jailer likewise “set a meal before them, and rejoiced with his whole household” (Acts 16:32-34).

- From those opening hours the church embraced generosity, meeting at Lydia’s house to encourage Paul and Silas before their departure (Acts 16:40).

until now

- Years later, while Paul was “in chains” (Philippians 1:7), their support had not waned.

- They sent aid repeatedly (2 Corinthians 11:9) and most recently by Epaphroditus (Philippians 4:18).

- Their consistency mirrors Hebrews 6:10, where God notes labor “shown toward His name … having ministered and still ministering to the saints.”

summary

Philippians 1:5 celebrates a church that stepped into gospel work the moment they believed and never stopped. Their financial gifts, personal risk, and spiritual solidarity formed a living partnership that advanced the unchanging, saving message of Christ. Paul’s joy rises from that steadfast track record, and the verse reminds believers today that real fellowship means lifelong, practical investment in the spread of the gospel.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) For your fellowship in the gospel.--More properly, towards the gospel, or, as affecting the gospel. The construction is illustrated by the more limited use of the same Greek word (as in Romans 15:26; 2Corinthians 9:13) in the sense of "contribution"; in which case the word "towards" introduces the objects of the almsgiving there specified. Accordingly St. Paul must be taken here to mean the fellow-working of the Philippians in the ministry of the gospel, of which he speaks still more distinctly in Philippians 1:7. That fellow-working had been shown (see Philippians 4:15) even "in the beginning of the gospel," by a contribution to St. Paul's needs--not perhaps his personal needs only--which from them, and (so far as we know) from them only, he consented to accept.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - For your fellowship in the gospel from the first day until now; rather, as R.V., for your fellowship in furtherance of the gospel. This verse should be taken in connection with Ver. 3. St. Paul thanks God for their help, their co-operation towards the work of the gospel. They helped forward the work by their prayers, their labors, and their liberal bounty. This fellowship began "in the beginning of the gospel," when the Philippians sent aid to the apostle at Thessalonica and Corinth; it continued "until now" ten years; they had just sent their alms to St. Paul at Rome by phroditus (Philippians 4:10).
