because you know that the Lord will reward each one
This phrase emphasizes the certainty of divine justice and reward. In the biblical context, the assurance of God's reward is a recurring theme, as seen in passages like Hebrews 11:6, which states that God rewards those who earnestly seek Him. The concept of divine reward is rooted in the Old Testament, where God promises blessings for obedience (Deuteronomy 28:1-14). This assurance is meant to encourage believers to remain steadfast in their faith and good works, knowing that their efforts are not in vain.

for whatever good he does
The focus here is on the universality of good deeds, regardless of their scale or visibility. This aligns with the teachings of Jesus in the Gospels, such as in Matthew 25:31-46, where acts of kindness and service are highlighted as criteria for divine reward. The phrase underscores the biblical principle that God values the intentions and actions of the heart, as seen in 1 Samuel 16:7, where God looks at the heart rather than outward appearances. This encourages believers to pursue righteousness in all aspects of life.

whether he is slave or free
This phrase addresses the social context of the early Christian church, where both slaves and free individuals were part of the community. The inclusion of both groups highlights the radical equality of all believers before God, as also expressed in Galatians 3:28, which states that there is neither slave nor free in Christ. This teaching would have been countercultural in the Roman Empire, where slavery was a common institution. The message is that one's social status does not affect their standing before God or their ability to receive His rewards. This reflects the transformative nature of the Gospel, which breaks down societal barriers and unites all believers in Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of the letter to the Ephesians, Paul was a key figure in the early Christian church, known for his missionary journeys and theological teachings.

2. Ephesus
A major city in Asia Minor, Ephesus was a center of commerce and culture. The Ephesian church was one of the early Christian communities to which Paul wrote.

3. Slaves and Masters
In the context of Ephesians 6, Paul addresses relationships between slaves and masters, a common social structure in the Roman Empire, emphasizing mutual respect and service as unto the Lord.
Teaching Points
Understanding Divine Reward
The promise of reward from the Lord is not based on social status but on the goodness of one's actions. This underscores the impartiality of God's justice.

Service as Worship
Every act of service, whether in a position of authority or submission, is an opportunity to worship God. Our work should be done with the mindset of serving Christ.

Equality in Christ
The verse emphasizes the equality of all believers before God, regardless of earthly status. This calls for a community where love and respect transcend social hierarchies.

Motivation for Good Works
Believers are encouraged to do good not for human recognition but with the assurance that God sees and rewards all good deeds.

Living with Eternal Perspective
Understanding that our actions have eternal significance helps us to live with a focus on pleasing God rather than seeking temporary rewards from people.

What Does Ephesians 6:8 Mean
Because you know

• Paul speaks to believers who already possess settled confidence in God’s promises (see Hebrews 11:6; 1 John 5:13).

• This “knowing” flows from faith in the inerrant Word; we take God at His word that He sees, cares, and will act (Psalm 33:13–15; 2 Chronicles 16:9).

• Such assurance changes daily attitudes—work is no longer drudgery but worship offered to the Lord (Colossians 3:23–24).

that the Lord will reward

• Scripture repeatedly teaches that God “will repay each one according to his deeds” (Romans 2:6; 2 Corinthians 5:10).

• The reward is both present (peace, joy, usefulness—Proverbs 11:18; Mark 10:29–30) and future at Christ’s judgment seat (1 Corinthians 3:12–14).

• Because “the Lord” Himself rewards, no good work is ever overlooked, even if people ignore or oppose us (Matthew 6:4; Hebrews 6:10).

each one

• The promise is individual and personal—God knows every name, face, and circumstance (John 10:3; Luke 12:7).

• No believer can ride on another’s coattails; likewise, no one is disqualified by obscurity (Galatians 6:4–5; Revelation 22:12).

• This truth motivates private integrity as much as public service (Matthew 25:21; Philippians 2:12–13).

for whatever good he does

• “Whatever” covers every act springing from love and obedience, however small—giving a cup of water, a kind word, faithful labor (Matthew 10:42; Ephesians 2:10).

• Good works are the evidence of genuine faith, not the basis of salvation (Ephesians 2:8–9; Titus 3:5).

• The Lord defines what is good; we discern it in His Word and by His Spirit (Micah 6:8; Hebrews 13:20–21).

whether he is slave or free

• First-century believers included household slaves and free citizens; God’s promise levels every social distinction (1 Corinthians 12:13; Galatians 3:28).

• Earthly status neither limits opportunity nor guarantees privilege before God (James 2:1–9).

• Modern parallels apply to every workplace, class, or culture—Christ honors faithful service regardless of position (1 Peter 2:18–19; Colossians 4:1).

summary

Ephesians 6:8 assures every believer that the Lord personally observes and will unfailingly reward every act of Spirit-empowered goodness, without regard to social standing. Knowing this, we serve Him wholeheartedly, confident that nothing done for Christ is ever in vain.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) The same shall he receive of the Lord.--This verse clenches the previous exhortations by the inculcation of a sense of responsibility and hope. The phrase itself is emphatic--not "he shall receive the reward of his deed," but "he shall receive the deed itself," considered as a thing still living and returning on his head, both in the judgments of life and in what we rightly call the "Last Judgment" of the Great Day. A slave in the eye of the law had no rights, and therefore no responsibility or hope. St. Paul therefore bids him, as a Christian, lift his thoughts to a region in which all, bond and free alike, may hear the blessing, "Well done, good and faithful servant."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - Knowing that whatsover good thing each man shall have done, the same shall he receive from the Lord, whether he be bond or free. The hope of reward is brought in to supplement the more disinterested motive, such addition being specially useful in the case of slaves (as of children, vers. 2, 3). For the slave the hope of reward is future - it is at the Lord's coming that he will have his reward.
