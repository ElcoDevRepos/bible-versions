Baalah
Baalah is a town mentioned in the allotment of land to the tribe of Judah. It is also known as Kiriath-jearim in other parts of the Bible (Joshua 15:9, 1 Chronicles 13:6). This location is significant as it was one of the places where the Ark of the Covenant was kept before being moved to Jerusalem by King David. The name Baalah is derived from the Canaanite deity Baal, indicating the syncretism and influence of Canaanite culture in the region. The town's association with the Ark highlights its importance in Israel's religious history and foreshadows the centralization of worship in Jerusalem.

Iim
Iim is a lesser-known location in the territory of Judah. The name Iim means "ruins" or "heaps," suggesting it may have been a site of ancient destruction or abandonment. Its mention in the list of towns allotted to Judah indicates the comprehensive nature of the tribal inheritance, encompassing even small or desolate places. The inclusion of such sites underscores the thoroughness of the land distribution and the fulfillment of God's promise to give the Israelites the land of Canaan.

Ezem
Ezem is another town within the territory of Judah. Its exact location is uncertain, but it is listed among the southern towns of Judah, indicating its position in the Negev region. The Negev was a semi-arid area, requiring the inhabitants to adapt to a challenging environment. The mention of Ezem reflects the diversity of the land allotted to Judah, from fertile plains to arid deserts. This diversity required the tribe to rely on God's provision and guidance, mirroring the spiritual journey of dependence on God.

Persons / Places / Events

A town in the southern part of the territory allotted to the tribe of Judah. The name "Baalah" is derived from the Hebrew root "Baal," which means "lord" or "master." This reflects the historical context where Canaanite deities were often worshipped, and places were named after them.

2. Iim
Another town within the territory of Judah. The name "Iim" is thought to mean "ruins" or "heaps," indicating a place that may have been desolate or abandoned at some point.

3. Ezem
A town also located in the southern region of Judah's inheritance. The name "Ezem" is believed to mean "bone" or "strength," possibly indicating a place of fortitude or resilience.

What Does Joshua 15:29 Mean
Baalah

“Baalah, Iim, Ezem” (Joshua 15:29) appears in the long catalog of southern Judean settlements.

• Baalah sits in the Negev, the dry southern region bordering Edom, anchoring Judah’s extreme frontier (cf. Joshua 15:21).

• The same town later shows up in Simeon’s inheritance nested inside Judah’s larger territory (Joshua 19:3; 1 Chronicles 4:29). God kept His word to Judah while also providing room for Simeon when Judah’s share proved “too large for them” (Joshua 19:9).

• Every named place—no matter how obscure—proves that “not one of the good promises which the LORD had made to the house of Israel failed” (Joshua 21:45). The listing underscores a concrete, geographic fulfillment, reminding us that God’s faithfulness reaches even the smallest village on the map.

Iim

• Iim (“heaps” or “ruins”) is another Negev hamlet, probably perched on a ridge of rocky knolls. Its mention with Baalah and Ezem links it to the same defensive chain stretching toward the wilderness.

• Though tiny, Iim mattered for the security of the border and for sustaining herders and travelers moving between Beersheba and the Arabah (see the travel corridor hinted at in Numbers 20:17–19; Deuteronomy 2:8).

• Isaiah later pictures cities reduced to “ruins” (Isaiah 17:1), an echo that reminds us how fragile human settlements are compared with the enduring word of God (Isaiah 40:8). The verse quietly invites trust in the One whose covenant, not bricks, guarantees permanence.

Ezem

• Ezem rounds out the triad, another Negev village reassigned to Simeon (Joshua 19:3; 1 Chronicles 4:29).

• The handover illustrates cooperative stewardship within God’s people: Judah holds the larger allotment, yet shares resources so every tribe thrives (compare Paul’s principle of mutual provision in 2 Corinthians 8:13–15).

• Positioned near desert pasturelands, Ezem would have served shepherding families—an echo of the patriarchs who once wandered these same plains (Genesis 13:1–4). God’s promise embraces both the famous and the forgotten, securing livelihoods in unfriendly terrain.

summary

Joshua 15:29 may read like a footnote, but it testifies to the meticulous faithfulness of God. Baalah, Iim, and Ezem remind us that:

• Every promise has a pinpoint location; God’s word lands on real soil.

• Small places and ordinary people share in the inheritance just as surely as great cities and famous leaders.

• The covenant community flourishes when members steward God-given space together, mirroring the generosity of the Lord who apportions it.

Pulpit Commentary
