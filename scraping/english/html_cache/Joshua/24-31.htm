Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Israel had served the LORD
This phrase highlights the faithfulness of the Israelites during a specific period. Serving the LORD implies adherence to the covenant established at Sinai, where the Israelites committed to obey God's commandments. This service is a reflection of their loyalty and dedication to Yahweh, contrasting with periods of rebellion and idolatry seen in other parts of their history.

throughout the days of Joshua
Joshua, as Moses' successor, led Israel into the Promised Land. His leadership is marked by military conquests and the distribution of land among the tribes. Joshua's influence and commitment to God were pivotal in maintaining the nation's faithfulness. His leadership style and decisions are often seen as a type of Christ, leading God's people into their inheritance.

and of the elders who outlived him
The elders mentioned here were leaders who had firsthand experience of God's miraculous works, such as the crossing of the Jordan River and the fall of Jericho. Their leadership helped maintain the spiritual and social order after Joshua's death. This period of stability underscores the importance of godly leadership in guiding a nation.

and who had experienced all the works that the LORD had done for Israel
This phrase emphasizes the importance of witnessing God's acts, such as the plagues in Egypt, the parting of the Red Sea, and the provision of manna. These events were foundational in shaping Israel's identity and faith. The elders' personal experiences with these divine acts provided them with the authority and conviction to lead the people in faithfulness to God. This experiential knowledge is crucial for passing down faith to subsequent generations, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:6-9.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joshua
The leader of Israel after Moses, who led the Israelites into the Promised Land and oversaw the distribution of the land among the tribes.

2. The Elders
Leaders who outlived Joshua and had firsthand experience of God's works, helping to maintain the faithfulness of Israel.

3. Israel
The nation chosen by God, consisting of the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who served the LORD during this period.

4. The LORD
The covenant God of Israel, who performed mighty works on behalf of His people.

5. The Works of the LORD
The miraculous acts and guidance provided by God, including the crossing of the Jordan River, the fall of Jericho, and the conquest of Canaan.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Godly Leadership
Joshua and the elders provided spiritual guidance that helped Israel remain faithful. Godly leaders are crucial in guiding communities to serve the LORD.

The Power of Witnessing God's Works
Those who experienced God's miracles firsthand were more likely to remain faithful. Personal experiences with God can strengthen our faith and commitment.

The Role of Community in Faithfulness
The collective memory and shared experiences of a community can help sustain faith across generations.

The Danger of Forgetfulness
As seen in later generations, forgetting God's works can lead to spiritual decline. Regular remembrance and teaching of God's deeds are vital.

Legacy of Faith
The faithfulness of one generation can impact the next. We should strive to leave a legacy of faith for those who follow us.

What Does Joshua 24:31 Mean
Israel had served the LORD throughout the days of Joshua

• Joshua’s leadership set a clear, godly direction for the nation. He consistently called Israel to wholehearted obedience, reminding them of God’s faithfulness (Joshua 1:7-9; 23:6).

• His personal resolve—“But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15)—modeled uncompromising devotion. Under such leadership, the people responded in kind.

• The statement underscores continuity: from the conquest of Canaan (Joshua 6–12) to the land allotments (Joshua 13–22), corporate worship and covenant loyalty were maintained.

• This verse confirms that God’s covenant promises are experienced most fully when His people remain under godly, Scripture-anchored leadership (Deuteronomy 17:18-20).

and of the elders who outlived him

• After Joshua died, a generation of elders—eyewitnesses of God’s mighty acts—kept Israel aligned with the covenant (Judges 2:7).

• Leadership did not end with one man; God raised up faithful successors who preserved Joshua’s memory, teachings, and example (Exodus 18:21; Deuteronomy 31:9-13).

• Their unified testimony provided a stabilizing bridge between the conquest generation and those who would follow. It illustrates how a leadership team, grounded in God’s Word, can extend spiritual momentum beyond a single lifetime (2 Timothy 2:2).

and who had experienced all the works that the LORD had done for Israel

• Firsthand experience of God’s miracles—crossing the Jordan (Joshua 3-4), Jericho’s fall (Joshua 6), the sun standing still (Joshua 10)—fueled unwavering loyalty.

• Personal witness turns doctrinal truth into living conviction; these elders could say, “We have seen with our eyes” (Deuteronomy 4:9; Psalm 78:3-7).

• Their testimony guarded against the drift that often follows when memory of God’s acts fades (Judges 2:10-12).

• The verse reminds every generation to recount God’s works so faith remains vibrant and historical, not theoretical (Psalm 145:4-6).

summary

Joshua 24:31 highlights a golden era when Israel flourished under leaders who personally knew God’s mighty deeds and kept His Word central. Godly leadership, shared testimony, and living memory of divine intervention combined to safeguard covenant faithfulness. The passage encourages believers today to value Scripture-anchored leadership, preserve the stories of God’s works, and pass them to the next generation so that wholehearted service to the LORD endures.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) Israel served the Lord all the days of Joshua, and . . . of the elders that overlived Joshua.--It cannot surprise us that the personal influence of the man and of the events of his day was so difficult to efface. There was a primitive Church in Canaan as well as in the Roman Empire. The short duration of the one seems to have an analogy in the case of the other.

(32) The bones of Joseph, and also of his brethren, as appears by Acts 7:16. The precedent set by Joseph is exceedingly likely to have been followed.

And it became the inheritance of the children of Joseph.--It may be that this fact helped to fix the position of Ephraim and Manasseh in the centre of the country.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - And Israel served the Lord (cf. Judges 2:10). We see here the value of personal influence. Nor is such influence altogether unnecessary among us now. The periods of great religious movements in the Christian Church are in many ways very like to the time of the Israelitish conquest of Palestine by Joshua. They are times when God visibly fights for His Church, when miracles of grace are achieved, when the enemies of God are amazed and confounded at the great things God has done. The successes, so clearly due to the interposition of a Higher Power, have a sobering rather than an intoxicating effect, and the influence of the grave, wise, earnest men at the head of the movement is great with their enthusiastic followers. But with the removal of these leaders in Israel a reaction sets in. The fervour of the movement declines, the era of slackness and compromise succeeds, and a generation arises which "knows not the Lord, nor yet the works which He had done for Israel." In our times such reactions, living as we do in the full blaze of gospel light, are far more transient and less fatal than in the days of Israel. But in our measure we continue to experience the working of that law by which intense energy is apt to be followed by coldness, and every earnest movement for good needs a continual rekindling at the altar of God of the fire which first set it at work. That overlived Joshua. Literally, that lengthened out their days after Joshua.
