Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
These are their numbers according to the houses of their fathers:
This phrase indicates a detailed census or military organization based on tribal and familial lines, a common practice in ancient Israel. The division by "houses of their fathers" reflects the patriarchal structure of Israelite society, where lineage and heritage were crucial for identity and inheritance. This method of organization ensured that each tribe maintained its distinct identity and responsibilities within the nation.

From Judah, the commanders of thousands:
Judah, being the largest and most prominent tribe, often took a leading role in military and political matters. The "commanders of thousands" suggests a well-structured military hierarchy, with leaders responsible for large groups of soldiers. This structure is reminiscent of the organization seen during the Exodus (Exodus 18:21), where leaders were appointed over thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens.

Adnah the commander, and with him 300,000 mighty men of valor;
Adnah is noted as a significant military leader, and his name means "pleasure" or "delight." The mention of "300,000 mighty men of valor" highlights the strength and preparedness of Judah's forces. The term "mighty men of valor" is used throughout the Old Testament to describe warriors of exceptional bravery and skill (e.g., David's mighty men in 2 Samuel 23). This large number indicates a period of military strength and stability under King Jehoshaphat's reign, reflecting God's blessing and the fulfillment of His promises to protect and prosper His people when they followed His commandments.

What Does 2 Chronicles 17:14 Mean
These are their numbers

God does not deal in vague generalities; He records exact figures. This tally reminds us of other divinely sanctioned numberings, such as Numbers 1:2–3 and 1 Chronicles 21:5, where counting served to organize and mobilize God’s people for specific purposes. Here, the list underscores the solid, measurable strength He granted to King Jehoshaphat’s kingdom.

according to the houses of their fathers

• The census is arranged by family lines, echoing the tribal structure instituted in Exodus 6:14 and reaffirmed in Numbers 26:55.

• Lineage conveyed identity, inheritance, and responsibility. By highlighting “houses,” the writer shows that both spiritual heritage and civic duty flowed through families (see Deuteronomy 6:6-7).

• This organization fostered unity; each clan could see its contribution to Judah’s defense, much like Nehemiah 4:13-14 later rallies families to build the wall.

From Judah

• Judah, the royal tribe (Genesis 49:8-10), supplied the first contingent.

• After the kingdom divided, Judah carried David’s covenant promise (2 Samuel 7:16). Naming Judah first affirms that God’s redemptive line remained intact despite national upheaval (compare 2 Chronicles 13:5).

• The emphasis also reminds us that spiritual leadership should be matched by practical readiness (Psalm 78:70-72).

the commanders of thousands

• Military structure mirrors Moses’ system of leaders over “thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens” (Exodus 18:25; Deuteronomy 1:15).

• Such order prevents chaos and models the principle that God values disciplined stewardship of gifts and people (1 Corinthians 14:40).

• In practical terms, it assures that every soldier had clear oversight—critical for the victories recorded later in 2 Chronicles 20:15-22.

Adnah the commander

• Though little else is said of Adnah, his placement at the top of Judah’s roster implies proven faithfulness, similar to the mighty men surrounding David in 2 Samuel 23:8-39.

• God often elevates the faithful obscure (consider 1 Samuel 16:11-12 with David). Adnah’s mention tells us that God notices and records steadfast service.

and with him 300,000 mighty men of valor

• “Mighty men of valor” is the same phrase used of Gideon’s troops (Judges 6:12) and David’s warriors (1 Chronicles 12:28); it blends courage with skill.

• Three hundred thousand is no idle statistic—it represents God’s abundant provision for national security (compare Asa’s 300,000 from Judah in 2 Chronicles 14:8).

• The huge force also contrasts with later prophets’ warnings against relying solely on armies (Psalm 33:16-17; Isaiah 31:1), reminding readers that strength is a gift to be stewarded under God’s authority.

summary

2 Chronicles 17:14 records a precise, clan-based muster of Judah’s troops under Adnah, totaling 300,000 seasoned warriors. The verse highlights God’s orderly provision for His people, the enduring significance of family heritage, the central role of Judah in redemptive history, and the worth He places on faithful leadership. Ultimately it assures us that the Lord equips those who walk in His ways with everything needed—spiritually and practically—to fulfill their calling.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) And these . . . their fathers.--And this is their muster (or census), according to their father-houses (clans), 1Chronicles 24:3. The warriors were marshalled in the army according to clans, so that men of the same stock fought side by side with their kindred. Perhaps in the original document this heading was followed by a much more detailed scheme of names and divisions than that which the text presents.

Of Judah.--To Judah (belonged) captains of thousands, viz., the three enumerated in 2Chronicles 17:14-16 : Adnah, Jehohanan, and Amasiah. They were the principal officers, or generals, of the entire forces of Judah.

Adnah the chief--To wit, the captain Adnah. That Adnah was commander-in-chief is implied by his being named first, and his corps being the largest.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - This verse, with the following four, gives us the names of five captains, chiefs, princes, or military officers for the kingdom's service, with the numbers of the troops they severally commanded. The numbers of them (see note under 1 Chronicles 23:11); Hebrew, פקֻדָּתָם. The better English rendering to carry at once the signification would be, The muster of them, etc. The captains... the chief, In both cases the Hebrew is the familiar word for" prince" (שָׂר); in the former without article, in the latter with article. The numbers of this and following four verses are not only absolutely unreliable, but in themselves impossible. According to the house of their fathers; i.e. the quotation is drawn from an army catalogue, arranged carefully by fathers' houses (Numbers 1:18, 22, etc.).
