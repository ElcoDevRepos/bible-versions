Now that faith has come
This phrase signifies a pivotal transition in the believer's relationship with God. The coming of faith refers to the arrival of the new covenant through Jesus Christ, which fulfills the promises made in the Old Testament. This faith is not merely intellectual assent but a deep trust in the redemptive work of Christ. The concept of faith here is rooted in the Abrahamic covenant, where Abraham's belief was credited to him as righteousness (Genesis 15:6). The coming of faith marks the end of the law's role as a custodian, as faith in Christ now provides the means for justification and righteousness (Romans 3:22).

we are no longer under a guardian
The term "guardian" refers to the law, which served as a tutor or custodian until Christ came. In the Greco-Roman world, a guardian was responsible for the care and discipline of a child until they reached maturity. Similarly, the Mosaic Law was intended to guide and protect God's people, revealing sin and pointing towards the need for a Savior (Galatians 3:24). With the advent of Christ, believers are no longer under the law's supervision because they have reached spiritual maturity through faith. This transition from law to grace is a central theme in Paul's epistles, emphasizing that salvation is by faith alone, not by works of the law (Ephesians 2:8-9). The liberation from the law's guardianship underscores the believer's new identity as a child of God, fully adopted into His family through Christ (Galatians 4:4-7).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of the letter to the Galatians, addressing the early Christian communities in the region of Galatia.

2. Galatians
The recipients of the letter, a group of early Christians in the region of Galatia, who were struggling with the influence of Judaizers insisting on adherence to the Mosaic Law.

3. The Law (Torah)
Refers to the Mosaic Law, which served as a "guardian" or "tutor" until the coming of Christ and the establishment of faith.

4. Faith in Christ
The new covenant established through Jesus Christ, which supersedes the old covenant of the Law.

5. Judaizers
A group within the early church advocating for the necessity of following Jewish laws and customs, including circumcision, for salvation.
Teaching Points
Freedom from the Law
Believers are no longer bound by the ceremonial and civil aspects of the Mosaic Law. This freedom is not a license to sin but a call to live by the Spirit.

Role of Faith
Faith in Christ is the means by which we are justified and made righteous before God. It is the foundation of our relationship with Him.

Spiritual Maturity
Just as a child matures and no longer needs a guardian, Christians are called to mature in their faith, relying on the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

Unity in Christ
The removal of the law as a guardian emphasizes the unity of all believers in Christ, regardless of ethnic or cultural backgrounds.

Living by the Spirit
With the law no longer as our guardian, we are called to live by the Spirit, producing the fruit of the Spirit in our lives.

What Does Galatians 3:25 Mean
Now that faith has come

- Paul is speaking of the arrival of saving faith centered in Jesus Christ—the trust in His finished work that justifies sinners (Galatians 3:22-24; Romans 3:22).

- This “coming” is tied to the historical reality of Christ’s death and resurrection (Romans 4:25) and the personal moment each believer believes the gospel (Ephesians 2:8-9).

- Faith ushers believers into full sonship, no longer merely anticipating promise but living in it (Galatians 3:26; John 1:12).

- The shift is from a system that exposed sin to a relationship that provides righteousness (Romans 1:17; Hebrews 11:1).

We are no longer under a guardian

- The “guardian” (paidagógos) was the Mosaic Law functioning like a tutor over minors (Galatians 3:23-24).

• It pointed out sin (Romans 7:7).

• It restrained wrongdoing (1 Timothy 1:8-9).

• It foreshadowed Christ through sacrifices and ceremonies (Colossians 2:16-17).

- Now, in Christ, believers are released from the Law’s custody and condemnation (Romans 6:14; 7:6).

- Freedom from the guardian is not license to sin; the indwelling Spirit now leads and empowers holiness (Galatians 5:16-18; Titus 2:11-12).

summary

Galatians 3:25 celebrates the transition from Law-bound tutelage to Spirit-empowered sonship. Faith in Christ has arrived, so the believer no longer lives under the Law’s guardianship but enjoys the full rights and responsibilities of God’s redeemed children.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(25-29) But now the Law has been exchanged for the dispensation of faith. Henceforth the old state of pupilage is at an end. We are no longer like children, but adult members of the divine family--sons of God. We have entered into this relation by faith in Christ. For to be baptised into Christ is to enter into the closest possible relation to Him. It is to be identified with Him entirely. Nor is any excluded. The old barriers of race, status, and even sex, are done away. Through their relation to Christ, all Christians, as it were, unite to form a single man. They are a body animated by a single personality and will. And their relation to Christ stamps them as the true descendants of Abraham. In them is the promise of the Messianic blessing fulfilled.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - But after that faith is come (ἐλθούσης δὲ τῆς πίστεως); but now that Faith hath come; this white-robed, joy-bringing angel of deliverance! (see note on the words, in ver. 23, "before faith came"). We are no longer under a schoolmaster (οὐκέτι ὐπὸ παιδαγωγόν ἐσμεν); we are no longer under a keeper of our childhood. When a child becomes of age, as determined by his father's arrangement, the paedagogus's function, of course, ceases; so also when we(God's collective people)became believers in Christ, we had reached the era appointed by our Father for our coming of age, and the Law lost all hold upon us. This triumphant conclusion is based upon the premiss that the Law was the paeda-gogus of God's people, and nothing more. This premiss is itself proved true to the apostle's conviction, by the very nature of the case.
