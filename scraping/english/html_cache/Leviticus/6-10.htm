Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And the priest shall put on his linen robe and linen undergarments
The linen garments signify purity and holiness, essential for approaching God. Linen, a fabric made from flax, was common in ancient Israel and symbolized righteousness (Revelation 19:8). The priest's attire reflects the need for cleanliness and separation from sin, as seen in Exodus 28:39-43, where specific garments are prescribed for priestly duties. This attire also prefigures Christ, our High Priest, who is pure and without sin (Hebrews 4:15).

and he shall remove from the altar the ashes of the burnt offering that the fire has consumed
The removal of ashes signifies the completion of the sacrifice, a daily ritual emphasizing the need for continual atonement and cleansing. The burnt offering, or "olah," was entirely consumed by fire, symbolizing total dedication to God (Leviticus 1:9). This act foreshadows Christ's complete sacrifice on the cross, fulfilling the need for ongoing offerings (Hebrews 10:10-14).

and place them beside it
Placing the ashes beside the altar indicates respect for what has been offered to God. It also signifies the transition from one sacrifice to the next, maintaining the altar's readiness for continual offerings. This practice underscores the perpetual nature of worship and atonement in the Old Covenant, pointing to the eternal efficacy of Christ's sacrifice, which does not need repetition (Hebrews 9:24-28).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Priest
The individual responsible for performing the duties described in this verse. In the context of Leviticus, the priest is a mediator between God and the people, performing sacred rituals.

2. Linen Robe and Undergarments
Specific garments worn by the priest, symbolizing purity and holiness. Linen was chosen for its purity and suitability for sacred service.

3. Burnt Offering
A type of sacrifice in the Old Testament, symbolizing atonement and dedication to God. The offering was completely consumed by fire, representing total surrender to God.

4. The Altar
The place where sacrifices were made, representing the presence of God and the place of atonement.

5. Ashes
The remains of the burnt offering, which the priest was instructed to remove, signifying the completion of the sacrifice and the need for continual cleansing.
Teaching Points
Holiness in Service
The priest's garments symbolize the need for purity and holiness in serving God. As believers, we are called to live lives set apart for God's purposes.

The Role of the Priest
The priest's duties highlight the importance of mediating between God and humanity. In Christ, we have the ultimate High Priest who intercedes for us.

Symbolism of the Burnt Offering
The burnt offering represents total dedication to God. We are called to offer our lives as living sacrifices, fully surrendered to His will.

Continual Cleansing
The removal of ashes signifies the need for ongoing cleansing and renewal in our spiritual lives. We must regularly seek God's forgiveness and purification.

What Does Leviticus 6:10 Mean
And the priest shall put on his linen robe

• Linen signals purity and separation for God. Exodus 28:42–43 shows the same requirement so that “they will not incur guilt and die.”

• The robe reminds us that ministry begins with being clothed in righteousness provided by God, not self-made efforts (Isaiah 61:10; Revelation 19:8).

• Only the priest puts it on—leadership carries unique responsibilities, echoed later in James 3:1.

and linen undergarments

• Even unseen layers matter to God. The command reaches beneath public appearance to private integrity (1 Timothy 4:16).

• Modesty before a holy God protects both priest and people from irreverence (Exodus 20:26).

• Layered linen also insulates the priest from sweat, picturing service that relies on God’s grace rather than fleshly striving (Ezekiel 44:18).

and he shall remove from the altar the ashes of the burnt offering that the fire has consumed

• Ashes represent what is left after total consecration; the offering was completely given to God (Leviticus 1:9).

• Clearing the ashes keeps the fire burning continuously (Leviticus 6:12–13), illustrating ongoing devotion that is never allowed to die out (Romans 12:1).

• The same fire that consumes also purifies; Hebrews 12:29 connects God’s consuming fire with holiness.

and place them beside it

• Setting the ashes “beside” the altar treats what is holy with respect until the priest changes garments and carries them outside the camp (Leviticus 6:11).

• This interim placement keeps the altar area orderly for fresh sacrifices—order is an expression of God’s character (1 Corinthians 14:33).

• The movement from altar to outside the camp foreshadows Christ, who “suffered outside the gate” to bear our sin (Hebrews 13:11–12).

summary

Leviticus 6:10 portrays a priest prepared in pure linen, tending God’s perpetual fire, faithfully clearing away yesterday’s ashes, and handling even residue with reverence. The verse teaches that ministry springs from holiness, integrity, and disciplined devotion, all pointing ahead to Christ’s perfect priesthood and the believer’s call to continual, wholehearted worship.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) And the priest shall put on his linen garment.--The officiating priest was to put on his sacerdotal garments, which consisted of four pieces--viz., (1) the tunic, which was a long close robe of fine linen, with sleeves but without folds, covering the whole body, and reaching down to the feet; (2) linen breeches--better, linen drawers--which, according to the authorities during the second Temple, reached to the knees and were fastened by ribbons above the flanks; (3) a linen girdle, which, according to the same authorities, was three fingers wide and thirty-two cubits. long, and, like the veil of the court and of the sanctuary, was embroidered with figures; and (4) a mitre, or better, turban, which was likewise of fine linen, and was fastened to the head by means of ribbons, to prevent its falling off (Exodus 28:4; Exodus 28:40; Exodus 29:5-10; Leviticus 8:13). Though the second and third only are here mentioned, there can hardly be any doubt that all the four garments were meant, and that the third and fourth are either omitted for the sake of brevity, or because they are included in the first term, which is the reason why some of the ancient versions have it in the plural.

Take up the ashes which the fire had consumed with the burnt offering.--Better, take up the ashes into which the fire had consumed the burnt offering. That is, the ashes into which the consuming fire had converted the victim.

He shall put them beside the altar.--During the second Temple, a priest was appointed by lot to take off from the altar every morning at least a shovelful of ashes and carry it without the camp, and when the ashes accumulated they were entirely removed to the same place.
