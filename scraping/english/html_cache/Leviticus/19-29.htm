You must not defile your daughter by making her a prostitute
This command is part of the Holiness Code, a section of Leviticus that outlines ethical and moral conduct for the Israelites. The prohibition against prostitution is rooted in the understanding of sexual purity and the sanctity of family. In ancient Near Eastern cultures, temple prostitution was sometimes practiced as part of pagan worship, but Israel was called to be distinct and separate from such practices. The act of making one's daughter a prostitute would not only degrade her but also violate the covenantal relationship with God, who called His people to holiness. This command underscores the importance of protecting the dignity and purity of individuals, especially within the family unit.

or the land will be prostituted
The land of Israel was seen as a gift from God, a place where His presence dwelled among His people. The concept of the land being "prostituted" suggests that moral corruption and idolatry defile not just individuals but the entire community and the land itself. This reflects the biblical principle that sin has communal consequences, affecting the spiritual and physical well-being of the nation. The land was to remain holy, as it was the inheritance of God's people, and any defilement would jeopardize their covenantal blessings.

and filled with depravity
Depravity here refers to moral corruption and wickedness. The warning is that allowing such practices would lead to a society overwhelmed by sin, moving further away from God's standards. This phrase highlights the ripple effect of sin, where individual actions contribute to the moral decay of the entire community. The call to holiness in Leviticus is a call to resist such depravity and to live in a way that reflects God's character. This principle is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are urged to live pure and blameless lives, shining as lights in a dark world (Philippians 2:15).

Teaching Points
Value of Purity
God calls His people to live in purity, both physically and spiritually. This commandment underscores the importance of maintaining moral integrity within the family and community.

Protection of Women
The verse highlights the need to protect and honor women, ensuring they are not exploited or degraded. This reflects God's heart for justice and dignity for all individuals.

Holiness in the Land
The commandment warns against allowing sin to permeate the community, which can lead to widespread wickedness. Believers are called to be a light in the world, promoting righteousness.

Family Responsibility
Parents have a responsibility to guide their children in the ways of the Lord, teaching them to uphold God's standards and values.

Cultural Influence
The Israelites were to be distinct from the surrounding nations. Similarly, Christians are called to be in the world but not of it, resisting cultural practices that contradict God's Word.

What Does Leviticus 19:29 Mean
You must not defile your daughter

• The command is addressed first to fathers, highlighting their responsibility to protect their children’s purity (see Proverbs 22:6; Ephesians 6:4).

• “Defile” carries the idea of treating someone as common or unclean (cf. Leviticus 18:24–25). God calls parents to honor the sacred trust of raising a child made in His image, not to cheapen that life for personal gain.

• By placing this warning inside a chapter filled with “You shall be holy” commands (Leviticus 19:2), the Lord shows that family holiness is inseparable from national holiness.

by making her a prostitute

• Forced or permitted prostitution was a real temptation in the surrounding Canaanite culture, where temple prostitution was tied to fertility rites (Deuteronomy 23:17; Hosea 4:13–14).

• God’s law refuses to let economic hardship or cultural pressure justify sexual exploitation. The daughter is not merchandise; she is covenant family.

• The New Testament echoes this principle: “Do you not know that your bodies are members of Christ? … Therefore glorify God in your body” (1 Corinthians 6:15–20).

or the land will be prostituted

• Personal sin never stays private; it seeps into the soil of society (Joshua 7:1, 11–12).

• Scripture often personifies the land, showing how moral pollution invites divine judgment (Jeremiah 3:1–3; Isaiah 24:5–6).

• Israel’s calling was to inhabit a land set apart for God; tolerating sexual exploitation would make the whole nation resemble the pagan cultures they were to displace.

and filled with depravity

• When sexual sin is normalized, every other form of corruption follows (Romans 1:24–28).

• Judges 19 offers a grim historical picture: unchecked immorality led to brutality, civil war, and near‐annihilation of a tribe.

• The verse warns that if parents surrender their duty, society will slide from isolated acts of immorality into widespread moral chaos.

summary

Leviticus 19:29 is God’s clear, protective boundary: parents are never to sacrifice their children’s purity for profit or acceptance. Violating that boundary defiles the individual, corrodes the family, and ultimately infects the whole community with depravity. Obeying the command upholds God’s holiness, honors the inherent worth of every child, and safeguards the moral health of the land.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(29) Do not prostitute thy daughter.--This refers to the degrading worship of Astarte which prevailed in ancient times, and which at times also broke out among the Jews.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - Do not prostitute thy daughter. This is a peremptory prohibition, applying to every Jewish maiden, introduced in this place with a primary relation to the sanctification of lust by the dedication of young girls at some heathen temples; but by no means confined in its application to such practices. All legal sanction of the sin of prostitution is forbidden, for whatever purpose it may be given; and the certain result of such sanction is indicated in the final words of the verse, lest the land fall to whoredom, and the land become full of wickedness (cf. Deuteronomy 23:17).
