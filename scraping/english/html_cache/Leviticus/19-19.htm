Leviticus 19
1. A repetition of various laws














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You are to keep My statutes.
This command emphasizes the importance of obedience to God's laws, which were given to the Israelites as part of the covenant relationship established at Mount Sinai. The statutes are divine decrees meant to set Israel apart as a holy nation. This phrase underscores the broader biblical theme of holiness and separation from pagan practices, as seen in Exodus 19:5-6, where God calls Israel to be a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation." The call to keep God's statutes is a recurring theme throughout the Pentateuch, highlighting the covenantal relationship between God and His people.

You shall not crossbreed two different kinds of livestock;
This prohibition against crossbreeding animals reflects the broader biblical principle of maintaining the order of creation as established by God. In Genesis 1, God creates each living creature "according to its kind," and this command reinforces the idea of preserving the distinctiveness of each species. The prohibition may also serve as a symbolic reminder of Israel's call to remain distinct and separate from the surrounding nations and their practices. This command can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the fulfillment of the law and the one who perfectly maintains the order and purpose of creation.

you shall not sow your fields with two kinds of seed;
The instruction against mixing seeds in a field is another example of maintaining purity and order in creation. This command may have practical agricultural implications, as mixing seeds could lead to reduced crop yields or other issues. However, it also carries a symbolic meaning, emphasizing the importance of purity and separation in the life of the Israelites. This principle of separation is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are called to live lives distinct from the world (2 Corinthians 6:17). The command also points to the purity and holiness required in the kingdom of God, which is ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

and you shall not wear clothing made of two kinds of material.
The prohibition against wearing garments made of mixed materials, such as wool and linen, serves as a daily reminder of the need for purity and separation. This command may have practical reasons, as mixing materials could affect the durability or quality of the clothing. However, it also symbolizes the broader theme of holiness and the distinct identity of God's people. In the New Testament, the concept of spiritual purity and being clothed in righteousness is fulfilled in Christ, who provides believers with garments of salvation (Isaiah 61:10). This command, like the others, points to the ultimate fulfillment of the law in Jesus, who embodies perfect holiness and separation from sin.

Teaching Points
Holiness and Separation
The command to not mix different kinds of livestock, seeds, or fabrics symbolizes the call for the Israelites to be distinct and separate from the surrounding nations. This principle of separation is a call for believers today to live lives that are distinct in purity and holiness.

Obedience to God's Statutes
The verse emphasizes the importance of adhering to God's commands, even in seemingly small or mundane matters. This teaches us that every aspect of our lives should be submitted to God's will.

Symbolism of Purity
The prohibition against mixing different kinds of materials can be seen as a metaphor for spiritual purity. Believers are called to maintain purity in their thoughts, actions, and relationships.

Respect for Divine Order
The laws reflect God's order in creation. Respecting this order is a way of honoring God and acknowledging His sovereignty over all aspects of life.

Practical Holiness
The specific instructions in Leviticus 19:19 remind us that holiness is not just a spiritual concept but involves practical, everyday decisions that reflect our commitment to God.

What Does Leviticus 19:19 Mean
You are to keep My statutes.

God begins with a sweeping call to obedience, anchoring every specific command that follows.

• This is not a suggestion but a mandate grounded in His authority and character (Leviticus 19:37).

• Jesus echoes the same principle: “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments” (John 14:15).

• Obedience is the believer’s practical response to God’s holiness (1 Peter 1:15-16).

You shall not crossbreed two different kinds of livestock;

The Creator set fixed boundaries in Genesis 1, repeatedly stating that creatures reproduce “according to their kinds” (Genesis 1:24-25).

• Mixing species blurs those God-ordained lines, so Israel was told to refrain.

• A related command forbids yoking an ox and a donkey together (Deuteronomy 22:10), underscoring that differing natures produce hardship and disorder.

• Spiritually, the principle warns against alliances that compromise purity (2 Corinthians 6:14).

you shall not sow your fields with two kinds of seed;

Agriculturally, mixed seed complicates harvest and weakens yield. God tied this prohibition to the health and holiness of the land (Deuteronomy 22:9).

• Practically, an Israelite farmer’s field testified to set-apart living; neighbors could see the difference.

• Jesus’ parable of the wheat and the weeds (Matthew 13:24-30) portrays the danger of mingling truth and error—an echo of this same concern for purity.

and you shall not wear clothing made of two kinds of material.

Garments symbolized identity; mixed fabrics blurred visual distinctions established by God (Deuteronomy 22:11).

• Linen and wool, for instance, come from entirely different sources—plant and animal—mirroring the larger theme of separation.

• Revelation 19:8 pictures the church clothed in “fine linen, bright and pure,” illustrating that righteousness is unmixed and undefiled.

summary

Leviticus 19:19 strings together ordinary-sounding rules that point to an extraordinary truth: God calls His people to visible, tangible holiness. From livestock and seed to fabric and daily obedience, He insists on clear lines that honor His created order, protect community health, and model spiritual integrity. The timeless lesson remains: wholehearted devotion avoids compromising mixtures, cherishes God’s boundaries, and delights in keeping His statutes.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) Ye shall keep my statutes--that is, the following ordinances, which though not of the same high moral nature as the precepts laid down in the preceding verses, are yet necessary to attain to holiness. The Holy God has made everything "after its kind" (Genesis 1:11-12; Genesis 1:21; Genesis 1:24-25, &c.), and has thus established a physical distinction in the order of His creation. For man to bring about a union of dissimilar things is to bring about a dissolution of the Divine laws and to act contrary to the ordinances of Him who is holy, and to whose holiness we are to attain.

Cattle gender with a diverse kind.--Such commixtures would not only contravene the Divine order of things, but would lessen the abhorrence of the crime prohibited in Leviticus 18:22-23. The use, however, of animals produced from such mixtures was not forbidden. Hence we find that mules were largely employed by the Jews (2Samuel 13:29; 2Samuel 18:9; 1Kings 1:33; 1Kings 1:38; 1Kings 10:25; 1Kings 18:5; Ezra 2:66, &c.). These hybrids were either the issue of parents voluntarily coming together without the aid of the Israelites, or were imported from other countries. This law is binding upon the Jews to this day in every country where they happen to live, whether in Palestine or out of it. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - Ye shall keep my statutes. Having arrived at the general conclusion, "Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself, in the previous verse, the legislator pauses, and then presents a collection of further laws, arranged as before in no special order. The first is a mystical injunction against the confusion of things which are best kept apart, illustrated in three subjects - diverse kinds of cattle in breeding, mingled seeds in sowing a field, and mixed materials in garments. In Deuteronomy 22:10, a fresher illustration is added, "Thou shalt not plow with an ox and an ass together." The existence of mules, which we find frequently mentioned in the' later history (2 Samuel 13:29; 2 Samuel 18:9; 1 Kings 1:33), may be accounted for by supposing that the positive precept with regard to breeding cattle here laid down was transgressed, or that the mules were imported from abroad (see 1 Kings 10:25). The word used here and in Deuteronomy 22:11 for a garment mingled of linen and woolen, is shaatenez, an Egyptian word, meaning probably mixed. The difficulty raised on this verse by the allegation that the high priest's dress was made of mixed materials, is met by the answer that, if it were of mixed materials (which is uncertain, for wool is not mentioned in Exodus 28, nor is it quite determined that shesh means linen), the mixture was not such as is here forbidden. The moral meaning of the whole of this injunction is exhibited in the following passages from the New Testament, "Ye cannot drink the cup of the Lord, and the cup of devils" (1 Corinthians 10:21). "Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers: for what fellowship hath righteousness with unrighteousness? and what communion hath light with darkness? and what concord hath Christ with Belial? or what part hath he that believeth with an infidel? and what agreement hath the temple of God with idols?" (2 Corinthians 6:14-16). "He cannot love the Lord Jesus with his heart," says Hooker, "who lendeth one ear to his apostles and another to false teachers, and who can brook to see a mingle-mangle of religion and superstition' ('Serm.' 5:7, quoted by Wordsworth).
