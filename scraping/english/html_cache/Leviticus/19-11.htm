Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You must not steal.
This command is part of the Holiness Code, a section of Leviticus (chapters 17-26) that outlines how the Israelites are to live as God's holy people. Stealing violates the trust and community integrity that God desires for His people. In the ancient Near Eastern context, theft was a serious offense that disrupted social harmony and economic stability. The command not to steal is reiterated in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:15) and is foundational for maintaining justice and righteousness. In the New Testament, Paul echoes this command in Ephesians 4:28, urging believers to work honestly and share with those in need, reflecting a transformed life in Christ.

You must not lie or deceive one another.
Lying and deception are condemned because they undermine relationships and community trust. In the biblical context, truthfulness is a reflection of God's character, who is described as a God of truth (Deuteronomy 32:4). The prohibition against lying is also found in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:16), emphasizing its importance in maintaining justice and social order. Deception is seen as a tool of the enemy, as illustrated in the story of the serpent in Genesis 3. In the New Testament, Jesus identifies Himself as "the way, the truth, and the life" (John 14:6), and believers are called to speak the truth in love (Ephesians 4:15). This command highlights the call to live in integrity and honesty, reflecting the nature of Christ in our interactions with others.

What Does Leviticus 19:11 Mean
You must not steal

“ You must not steal ” (Leviticus 19:11a) comes straight from God’s own mouth, underscoring how seriously He values personal property and honest dealings.

• Exodus 20:15 echoes the same command in the Ten Commandments, showing this is not a passing suggestion but a foundational principle for covenant living.

• Ephesians 4:28 brings the mandate into New-Testament community life: “He who has been stealing must steal no longer, but rather labor, doing good with his own hands, so that he may have something to share with the one in need.” Here, stealing is replaced by diligent work and generosity—a complete turnaround of motives.

• Malachi 3:8-10 reminds Israel that withholding tithes was robbing God Himself. Stealing isn’t limited to taking material items; it encompasses any unfair gain.

• Practical takeaway:

– Respect other people’s possessions, time, and intellectual property.

– Replace selfish acquisition with a stewardship mindset, gladly working and giving.

You must not lie or deceive one another

“ You must not lie or deceive one another ” (Leviticus 19:11b) joins two related offenses—falsehood and manipulation—because both undermine trust.

• Proverbs 12:22: “Lying lips are detestable to the LORD, but those who deal faithfully are His delight.” The contrast is stark: God hates deceit, delights in truth.

• Colossians 3:9-10: “Do not lie to one another, since you have taken off the old self… and put on the new.” In Christ, truth-telling is a marker of the new life.

• John 8:44 identifies the devil as “the father of lies,” so whenever we distort truth we echo his character rather than God’s.

• Deception corrodes community: Acts 5:1-11 shows how Ananias and Sapphira’s lie to the church brought swift judgment, underscoring that honesty protects the unity of God’s people.

• Practical takeaway:

– Speak truth even when it costs.

– Reject half-truths, exaggerations, and hidden motives.

– Build relationships on transparency so others can rely on your word.

summary

Leviticus 19:11 calls believers to live visibly different lives: no stealing, no lying, no deception. God’s people honor others’ property, speak truth, and foster trust because these actions mirror His righteous, faithful character. Walking in this integrity blesses our neighbors, safeguards our communities, and glorifies the Lord who never steals, never lies, and never deceives.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) Ye shall not steal.--This injunction, which forms the eighth commandment of the Decalogue (Exodus 20:15), most probably has here a primary reference to the conduct of the owners of fields and vineyards. They are cautioned that by depriving the poor of his prescribed right to the corner of the fields, and to the gleanings of the harvest and vintage, they commit theft. Hence the Jewish canonists laid it down that he who puts a basket under a vine at the time of gathering grapes robs the poor.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - Stealing, cheating, and lying are classed together as kindred sins (see chapter Leviticus 6:2, where an example is given of theft performed by means of lying; cf. Ephesians 4:25; Colossians 3:9).
