Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The Spirit of the LORD came upon him
This phrase indicates divine empowerment, a recurring theme in the Book of Judges where God raises leaders to deliver Israel. The Spirit of the LORD coming upon someone signifies God's direct intervention and blessing, enabling them to accomplish tasks beyond human capability. This empowerment is seen in other judges like Gideon (Judges 6:34) and Samson (Judges 14:6). It foreshadows the New Testament outpouring of the Holy Spirit on believers (Acts 2:4).

and he became Israel’s judge
Othniel, the first judge mentioned in the Book of Judges, is raised by God to lead and deliver Israel. The role of a judge in this context is not merely judicial but also military and spiritual leadership. Judges were seen as deliverers and saviors, prefiguring the ultimate deliverer, Jesus Christ, who judges and saves His people (John 5:22).

and went out to war
This phrase highlights the military aspect of Othniel's judgeship. The judges often led Israel in battle against oppressors. The act of going to war under divine guidance underscores the theme of God fighting for Israel, as seen in Exodus 14:14. It also reflects the spiritual warfare believers face, as described in Ephesians 6:12.

And the LORD delivered Cushan-rishathaim king of Aram into the hand of Othniel
The deliverance of Cushan-rishathaim, whose name means "double wickedness," signifies God's judgment against oppressors and His faithfulness to Israel. Aram, often associated with regions in modern-day Syria, was a significant threat. This victory demonstrates God's sovereignty over nations and His ability to use chosen leaders to fulfill His purposes, as seen in Isaiah 45:1 with Cyrus.

who prevailed against him
Othniel's victory is attributed to divine intervention rather than human strength, emphasizing the biblical principle that success comes from the LORD (Proverbs 21:31). This victory serves as a reminder of God's promise to deliver His people when they repent and turn to Him, a theme echoed throughout the Old Testament and fulfilled in Christ's ultimate victory over sin and death (1 Corinthians 15:57).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Spirit of the LORD
Refers to the divine presence and empowerment given by God to individuals for specific tasks or roles. In Hebrew, "Ruach Yahweh" signifies God's active and dynamic presence.

2. Othniel
The first judge of Israel after the death of Joshua. He is Caleb's younger brother and is empowered by the Spirit of the LORD to deliver Israel from oppression.

3. Israel
The nation chosen by God, often falling into cycles of sin, oppression, repentance, and deliverance during the time of the Judges.

4. Cushan-rishathaim
The king of Aram-Naharaim, who oppressed Israel for eight years before being defeated by Othniel.

5. Aram-Naharaim
A region in Mesopotamia, indicating the geographical and political context of Israel's oppressors.
Teaching Points
Empowerment by the Spirit
Just as Othniel was empowered by the Spirit of the LORD, believers today are called to rely on the Holy Spirit for strength and guidance in their daily lives.

God's Faithfulness in Deliverance
Despite Israel's repeated failures, God remains faithful to deliver them when they repent. This underscores God's mercy and faithfulness to His covenant people.

The Role of Leadership
Othniel's leadership was marked by divine empowerment and obedience. Christian leaders are encouraged to seek God's guidance and strength in their roles.

Victory Over Oppression
The deliverance from Cushan-rishathaim symbolizes God's power to free His people from spiritual and physical oppression. Believers can trust in God's ability to deliver them from their struggles.

What Does Judges 3:10 Mean
The Spirit of the LORD came upon him

- “The Spirit of the LORD came upon him” (Judges 3:10).

- God Himself initiates Othniel’s mission; power is not self-generated (Judges 6:34; 1 Samuel 16:13).

- The pattern is consistent: whenever God’s people face overwhelming odds, His Spirit equips a willing servant (Acts 1:8; Zechariah 4:6).

- Under the old covenant this empowerment was task-specific and temporary, foreshadowing the permanent indwelling promised in the new covenant (John 14:16–17).

…and he became Israel’s judge

- With the Spirit’s arrival, Othniel steps into leadership: “he became Israel’s judge.”

- A judge was more than a civil arbiter; he was a deliverer raised up by God (Judges 2:18; Exodus 18:26).

- God often calls ordinary people to extraordinary roles, equipping them in the moment of need (1 Corinthians 1:26-29).

- Othniel’s role brings stability after Israel’s repentance (Judges 3:9), illustrating how confession and divine appointment go hand in hand (1 John 1:9).

…and went out to war

- Spiritual empowerment moves quickly to practical action: Othniel “went out to war.”

- Faith is never passive; it acts on God’s direction (James 2:17; Deuteronomy 20:1).

- Israel’s battles are the LORD’s battles; stepping onto the field is an act of trust (1 Samuel 17:47; Psalm 60:12).

- Obedience positions believers to witness God’s power firsthand.

And the LORD delivered Cushan-rishathaim king of Aram into the hand of Othniel

- The outcome is settled before the first sword is drawn: “the LORD delivered” the enemy (Joshua 24:11; Psalm 44:3).

- Cushan-rishathaim’s intimidating name and eight-year oppression (Judges 3:8) are no match for God’s sovereignty (Exodus 15:3).

- Deliverance underscores divine faithfulness to covenant promises despite Israel’s earlier unfaithfulness (Deuteronomy 7:9).

…who prevailed against him

- “Othniel… prevailed” because God fights for His people (Romans 8:31; 1 John 5:4).

- Victory leads to forty years of peace (Judges 3:11), showing how God’s solutions are thorough and restorative (Judges 8:28).

- The story encourages believers today to rely on God’s Spirit for every challenge, confident that His purposes cannot fail (Romans 8:37).

summary

Judges 3:10 records a straightforward sequence: God’s Spirit empowers Othniel, who then steps into his God-given role, engages the enemy in obedient action, and experiences decisive victory granted by the LORD. The verse highlights divine initiative, human obedience, and certain triumph—an enduring pattern inviting every believer to trust the Spirit’s power, follow God’s call, and watch Him bring deliverance.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) The Spirit of the Lord came upon him.--Here the Targum has "the spirit of prophecy" (comp. Isaiah 61:1), perhaps with reference to Numbers 11:25. They render the same phrase in Judges 6:34, "spirit of courage from Jehovah." This expression constantly recurs in this book (Judges 6:34; Judges 11:29; Judges 13:25). For "came upon him" (literally "was upon him"), a stronger phrase is "clothed him" (Judges 6:34; 1Chronicles 12:18; 2Chronicles 24:20). The Jews, however, placed Othniel highest among the judges, and applied to him the words of Song of Solomon 4:7, "Thou art all fair; there is no spot in thee," because he alone of the judges is represented as irreproachable. Further than this, they followed some dim traditional data in identifying him with Jabez (1Chronicles 4:10), and regarding him as a learned teacher of the law. (See Judges 1:13.) . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - And the Spirit, etc. This marks Othniel as one of the extraordinary Shophetim, or judges, Divinely commissioned to save Israel (see Judges 6:34; Judges 11:29; Judges 13:25; Judges 14:6, 19).
