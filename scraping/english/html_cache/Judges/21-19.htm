But look,
This phrase indicates a call to attention or a suggestion to consider something important. It reflects the urgency and significance of the message being conveyed to the Israelites, who were seeking a solution to the problem of providing wives for the Benjamites.

they said,
The speakers are the leaders or elders of the Israelite tribes, who are deliberating on how to address the near extinction of the tribe of Benjamin after the civil war described in the preceding chapters. Their collective decision-making reflects the communal nature of leadership in ancient Israel.

there is a yearly feast to the LORD
This refers to one of the three major pilgrimage festivals commanded in the Torah, likely the Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot), Passover, or the Feast of Weeks (Shavuot). These feasts were times of communal gathering, worship, and celebration, emphasizing the covenant relationship between God and Israel.

in Shiloh,
Shiloh was an important religious center in Israel during the period of the Judges. It housed the Tabernacle and the Ark of the Covenant before the establishment of Jerusalem as the religious capital. Archaeological evidence supports Shiloh's significance, with remains indicating a long period of occupation and religious activity.

which is north of Bethel
Bethel was a significant city in Israel's history, associated with patriarchal narratives such as Jacob's dream (Genesis 28:10-19). Its mention here helps to geographically locate Shiloh for the audience, indicating its proximity to other well-known sites.

east of the road that goes up from Bethel to Shechem,
This description provides a clear geographical marker, as roads were crucial for travel and trade. Shechem was another significant city, associated with Abraham and Jacob, and later a center of political and religious activity. The road's mention highlights the interconnectedness of these key locations in Israel's history.

and south of Lebonah.
Lebonah is less prominent in biblical narratives, but its mention here serves to further pinpoint Shiloh's location. The detailed geographical description underscores the importance of Shiloh as a central and accessible place for the Israelites to gather for worship and decision-making.

What Does Judges 21:19 Mean
But look,

The phrase signals a sudden, urgent shift of attention. In the face of Israel’s crisis—an entire tribe on the brink of extinction (Judges 21:6–7)—the leaders refuse to surrender to despair. They look for God-given solutions rather than human resignation (cf. Jeremiah 32:27: “Behold, I am the LORD... Is anything too difficult for Me?”).

Key ideas:

• God’s people are called to lift their eyes in faith when situations appear impossible (Numbers 14:8–9).

• “Look” invites immediate obedience, echoing other moments when quick action was vital (1 Samuel 14:8–12).

they said,

These are the elders of Israel, the same assembly that earlier swore an oath not to give their daughters to the Benjamites (Judges 21:1). Their collective speech underscores corporate responsibility. Proverbs 15:22 reminds us, “Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed”.

Highlights:

• The covenant community must resolve problems together (Acts 15:6).

• Words spoken in unity can point toward God’s provision (Joshua 1:16–18).

there is a yearly feast to the LORD in Shiloh,

Shiloh was Israel’s worship center before Jerusalem (Joshua 18:1). An annual festival offered the needed setting where unmarried women would gather, providing opportunity for wives for Benjamin without breaking the oath. Similar gatherings occurred at other feasts (1 Samuel 1:3–7).

Connections:

• Feasts celebrated God’s faithfulness (Exodus 23:14–17).

• God often weaves deliverance into worship settings (2 Chronicles 20:21–23).

which is north of Bethel

Geography matters. By placing Shiloh “north of Bethel,” Scripture roots the event in verifiable space, reinforcing historical accuracy (Genesis 12:8). The phrase also recalls Jacob’s earlier encounters with God at Bethel, linking past promises to present solutions (Genesis 28:19).

Truths:

• God’s acts occur in real places and times (Luke 2:1–7).

• Past spiritual milestones strengthen current faith (Psalm 77:11).

east of the road that goes up from Bethel to Shechem,

This well-traveled ridge route straddled central Canaan. Mentioning the road helps readers visualize the scene and recognize the accessibility of Shiloh. Roads in Scripture often symbolize the path of obedience (Isaiah 30:21).

Points to note:

• Clear directions show God does not hide His provision (Psalm 25:8–10).

• The road between Bethel and Shechem ties Abrahamic history (Genesis 12:6–7) to the current generation, reminding Israel of covenant continuity.

and south of Lebonah.

The southern marker, Lebonah, further pinpoints Shiloh. Such detail silences any charge of myth or legend; the narrative invites investigation. Likewise, Luke lists cities to anchor the nativity (Luke 2:4).

Takeaways:

• Precise detail bolsters trust in Scripture’s reliability (John 19:41–42).

• God’s guidance is specific, not vague (Acts 9:11).

summary

Judges 21:19 is more than a map reference. Each descriptive phrase reveals God’s faithfulness to preserve His people through worship, community counsel, and concrete guidance. In Israel’s lowest moment, the leaders turn eyes of faith toward a God-appointed feast at a real location, trusting that the Lord who wrote their geography also scripted their rescue.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) A feast of the Lord in Shiloh.--It is unlikely that the reference is to a local feast; but it is impossible to say which of the three yearly feasts is meant. The most natural would be the Feast of Tabernacles. We see from 1Samuel 1:3 that even among pious families the trying custom of going up to the Tabernacle three times a year had fallen into complete abeyance.

A place which is on the north side of Beth-el . . .--This elaborate description of the site of Shiloh, a place which is so often mentioned elsewhere without any addition, is extremely curious. There can be little doubt that it is due to the marginal gloss of some Masoretic scribe, perhaps in the editing of the sacred books by Ezra. That it is a gloss seems clear, because it comes in as a parenthesis in the speech of the elders, and, of course, in their day such a description was needless. Indeed, it was spoken at Shiloh itself, and the site was well known to all Israel. But by the time that the story was committed to writing in the days of the kings, or finally edited in the days of Ezra, Shiloh had long been desolate, and probably the very site was unknown to thousands. Hence this very valuable and interesting description was added, which has alone enabled us to identify Shiloh in the modern Seil-n.

South of Lebonah.--Lebonah, now Lubban, is not mentioned elsewhere.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - There is a feast of the Lord in Shiloh yearly. Compare the exactly similar description, 1 Samuel 1:3, 7. There is a great difference of opinion among commentators as to what feast is here meant. Hengstenberg, Keil, Delitzsch, and others think it was the passover; Bishop Patrick and others think it was the feast of tabernacles, a more joyous feast; Rosenmuller and others think it was a festival peculiar to Shiloh, after the analogy of the yearly sacrifice of the family of Jesse at Bethlehem (1 Samuel 20:29), and more or less in accordance with Deuteronomy 12:10-12. It is not easy to say which view is right, but the last seems not improbable, In a place which is on the north side, etc. The words in a place are not in the Hebrew, and do not seem to be implied by the context. But the description is that of the situation of Shiloh itself, which is very exact (see 'Palestine Exploration Fund,' Map of West Palestine). Lebonah survives in el-Lubbun, about two miles north-west of Seilun, and to the west of the road to Shechem or Nablus. It seems strange that so particular a description of the situation of Shiloh should be given; but it may probably indicate that the writer lived after the tabernacle had been moved to Jerusalem, and Shiloh had relapsed into an obscure village (see Judges 20:27, note). The situation of the descriptive words in the Hebrew, with the pronoun which, separated from Shiloh by the word yearly, indicates that they are an explanation added by the narrator.
