Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But the man was unwilling to spend the night.
This phrase highlights the Levite's determination to continue his journey despite the late hour. In the context of ancient Israel, traveling at night was dangerous due to the threat of bandits and wild animals. His unwillingness to stay may reflect a sense of urgency or distrust of the local hospitality, which is a recurring theme in the narrative of Judges, where the moral and social fabric of Israel is depicted as deteriorating.

He got up and departed,
The Levite's decision to leave immediately suggests a proactive approach to his journey. This action can be seen as a reflection of the chaotic period of the Judges, where individuals often acted independently without centralized leadership. The phrase also indicates a transition in the narrative, moving the story forward and setting the stage for the events that follow.

and arrived opposite Jebus (that is, Jerusalem),
Jebus, known later as Jerusalem, was at this time a Canaanite city not yet conquered by the Israelites. This geographical reference is significant as it underscores the incomplete conquest of the Promised Land, a recurring issue in the Book of Judges. Jerusalem would later become the political and spiritual center of Israel under King David, highlighting the city's future importance in biblical history.

with his two saddled donkeys and his concubine.
The mention of two saddled donkeys indicates preparation for a long journey, suggesting the Levite's intent to travel efficiently and with necessary provisions. The presence of his concubine is central to the narrative, as her subsequent mistreatment and death become a catalyst for the events that lead to civil strife within Israel. The concubine's role also reflects the complex social and familial structures of the time, where concubinage was a common practice, though it often led to familial discord and moral complications.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Discernment
The Levite's decision to avoid Jebus due to its non-Israelite inhabitants reflects a need for discernment in choosing where to seek refuge. Believers today must also discern wisely in their associations and decisions.

Consequences of Choices
The Levite's choice to continue traveling rather than staying in Jebus leads to dire consequences. This highlights the importance of seeking God's guidance in decision-making.

Moral Decay and Its Impact
The events in Judges 19 illustrate the moral decay of Israel during this period. Believers are reminded of the importance of upholding God's standards in a fallen world.

What Does Judges 19:10 Mean
But the man was unwilling to spend the night.

– The Levite has already lingered five days with his father-in-law (Judges 19:4-8) and senses urgency to resume the journey.

– His refusal underscores a concern for purposeful stewardship of time (Ephesians 5:15-16) and a desire to keep obligations to serve in Ephraim.

– It also reveals caution toward lingering in a Canaanite locale where covenantal hospitality might be lacking, recalling Israel’s call to remain distinct (Deuteronomy 7:3-6).

He got up and departed

– Initiative replaces passivity; the Levite chooses movement rather than comfort, echoing Abraham’s obedience to press on after pauses (Genesis 12:4).

– The text shows an exercise of headship: he “got up” first, leading those with him (compare Joshua 24:15, “as for me and my house”).

– Spiritual takeaway: decisive obedience often guards against compromise (James 4:17).

And arrived opposite Jebus (that is, Jerusalem)

– The writer notes the Canaanite name Jebus to remind readers that Jerusalem was not yet in Israelite control (Judges 1:21).

– Choosing to bypass a pagan city rather than sleep there mirrors Lot’s earlier reluctance to remain in Sodom once warned (Genesis 19:15-17).

– The narrative sets up the tragic events that follow in Gibeah, highlighting how seeking fellowship among God’s people should be safer—though in this case Israel’s moral decay proves otherwise (Judges 19:12-15).

With his two saddled donkeys and his concubine

– The provisioned donkeys indicate preparedness (Luke 14:28), reinforcing the Levite’s responsibility for his household and animals (Proverbs 27:23).

– Mentioning the concubine again reminds us of restored relationship after her earlier departure (Judges 19:2-3), yet foreshadows her vulnerability amid Israel’s lawlessness.

– The detail demonstrates Scripture’s literal historical reporting, setting the stage for a sober illustration of covenant breakdown (Hosea 4:1-2).

summary

Judges 19:10 records a decisive but perilous choice: the Levite refuses another night’s delay, leads his household away from questionable surroundings, and presses toward what he expects will be safer Israelite territory. His practical diligence and separation from pagan influence are commendable, yet the verse also foreshadows Israel’s internal corruption that will surface in Gibeah. The passage challenges readers to combine prompt obedience with discernment, recognizing that true safety is found only where God’s covenant standards are honored.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - Jebus. See Judges 1:21, note. Jerusalem is numbered among Joshua's conquests at Joshua 10:23; Joshua 12:10. But from this verse it would appear that the Israelite population had withdrawn and left the city to be entirely occupied by the Jebusites, who held it till the time of David (2 Samuel 5:6). Jerusalem is only about two hours from Bethlehem.
