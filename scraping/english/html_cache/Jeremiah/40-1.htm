This is the word that came to Jeremiah from the LORD
This phrase emphasizes the divine origin of Jeremiah's message, underscoring the prophet's role as a mouthpiece for God. Throughout the book of Jeremiah, the phrase "the word of the LORD" is a recurring theme, highlighting the authority and authenticity of the prophetic message. This divine communication is consistent with the prophetic tradition in the Old Testament, where prophets receive direct revelations from God (e.g., Isaiah 1:10, Ezekiel 1:3).

after Nebuzaradan captain of the guard had released him at Ramah
Nebuzaradan was a high-ranking Babylonian official, the captain of the guard, responsible for overseeing the deportation of the Jewish captives. His actions reflect the fulfillment of God's judgment on Judah due to their persistent disobedience. Ramah, located near Jerusalem, served as a staging area for the exiles before their journey to Babylon. This location is significant in biblical history, as it was also the site where Rachel wept for her children (Jeremiah 31:15), symbolizing the sorrow of exile.

having found him bound in chains among all the captives of Jerusalem and Judah
Jeremiah, though a prophet of God, was initially treated like the other captives, bound in chains. This reflects the indiscriminate nature of the Babylonian conquest and the suffering endured by the people of Judah. Jeremiah's experience parallels that of Jesus Christ, who, despite His innocence, was bound and led away (Matthew 27:2). The prophet's release signifies God's providential care and the fulfillment of His promise to protect Jeremiah (Jeremiah 1:8).

who were being exiled to Babylon
The exile to Babylon was a pivotal event in Jewish history, marking the beginning of the Babylonian Captivity. This period was a direct consequence of Judah's unfaithfulness to the covenant with God, as prophesied by Jeremiah and other prophets (Jeremiah 25:11-12). The exile served as both punishment and purification, ultimately leading to a renewed commitment to God upon the return to the land. The Babylonian Captivity is also a type of spiritual exile, representing the separation from God due to sin, with the promise of restoration through repentance and faith.

What Does Jeremiah 40:1 Mean
This is the word that came to Jeremiah from the LORD

The verse opens with a familiar refrain, underscoring that the message originates with God Himself, not human speculation (cf. 2 Timothy 3:16; Jeremiah 1:4–5).

• The phrase “the word … from the LORD” reminds us that, even amid national catastrophe, God is still speaking—just as He had spoken through Moses in Deuteronomy 28 and later through prophets like Isaiah (Isaiah 55:10-11).

• It signals continuity: what Jeremiah hears now aligns perfectly with earlier prophecies of judgment and eventual restoration (Jeremiah 29:10-14).

• Because the source is divine, the accuracy of the coming message is guaranteed (Numbers 23:19).

after Nebuzaradan captain of the guard had released him at Ramah

Historical detail grounds the verse in a real event recorded elsewhere (2 Kings 25:11-12; Jeremiah 39:11-12).

• Nebuzaradan, Babylon’s military official, is a surprising agent of Jeremiah’s freedom—illustrating Proverbs 21:1, where “the king’s heart is in the hand of the LORD.”

• Ramah, just north of Jerusalem, served as a transit camp for deportees (cf. Jeremiah 31:15).

• God’s word reaches Jeremiah not in the temple, but in a holding area administered by a pagan army. The setting shows that divine revelation is not confined to “sacred” spaces (Acts 7:48-49).

having found him bound in chains

Jeremiah is discovered “bound in chains,” a striking contrast to the spiritual freedom he carries within (Jeremiah 20:2; Acts 16:25-26).

• The prophet suffers physically for faithfully preaching God’s truth, reflecting Jesus’ later teaching in Matthew 5:11-12.

• His chains highlight God’s sovereignty: the man restrained by soldiers is the one through whom God will speak to nations (Jeremiah 1:10).

• The detail reassures believers that hardship does not negate calling; instead, it often confirms it (2 Corinthians 4:7-9).

among all the captives of Jerusalem and Judah

Jeremiah is lumped in with the exiles, sharing the fate of his people even though he had warned them (Jeremiah 25:3-11).

• This solidarity mirrors Moses choosing “to suffer affliction with the people of God” (Hebrews 11:25).

• It also anticipates Christ, who identified with sinners though He was sinless (Philippians 2:7-8).

• The presence of a faithful remnant within the larger judged nation fulfills God’s pattern seen in 1 Kings 19:18 and Isaiah 10:20-22.

who were being exiled to Babylon

The deportation completes warnings sounded for decades (Jeremiah 7:12-15; 25:11).

• Babylon is both a place of judgment and a crucible for future hope—Daniel, Ezekiel, and the eventual return (Ezra 1:1).

• The exile proves God keeps His word precisely, fulfilling Deuteronomy 28:36, yet it also sets up His promise of restoration after seventy years (Jeremiah 29:10).

• Thus, Jeremiah 40:1 anchors God’s faithfulness on both sides of the covenant: He disciplines, yet He preserves His plan.

summary

Jeremiah 40:1 shows that God’s authoritative word reaches His servant in the bleakest context, after a pagan commander frees a chained prophet amid deportees bound for Babylon. The verse certifies that:

• Divine revelation continues even in judgment.

• God directs rulers and circumstances to fulfill His purposes.

• Personal suffering often accompanies faithful ministry.

• The prophet identifies with the people he serves.

• Exile is both judgment and the prelude to eventual restoration—demonstrating God’s unwavering commitment to His promises.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XL.

(1) The word that came to Jeremiah from the Lord.--It is noticeable that this introduction is not followed by any specific utterance of prophecy until we come to Jeremiah 42:7. It is a natural conclusion that it stands as a kind of heading to the section of the collected prophecies subsequent to the capture of the city.

Had let him go from Ramah.--The town so named was in the tribe of Benjamin (Joshua 18:25), about six miles from Jerusalem, and retains its old name in the form Er-Ram. It was used on this occasion as a depot for the prisoners who were brought to it from Jerusalem, to await the orders of Nebuzaradan as to their ultimate disposal. The captain of the guard and the prophet had apparently not met before, and the latter had been brought in chains (literally, manacles, chains fastened to the wrists, Jeremiah 40:4), like the other captives.

(2?4) The Lord thy God . . .--It is significant that the Chaldaean general speaks as if recognising Jehovah as the God of Israel, and the prophet's mission from Him. Such a recognition did not, however, imply more than the belief of the polytheist, that each nation had its own guardian deity. We find language of a like kind, though spoken with a tone of sarcasm, coming even from the lips of Rab-shakeh (2Kings 18:25). As a prophet, however, Jeremiah is treated with marked respect--in part, perhaps, due to the policy he had advocated; in part, possibly, to the influence of men like Daniel and his friends at Babylon--and offered the option of going, with the promise of honourable treatment. to that city, from which, however, it is assumed, that he would not return, or remaining in Judaea, to go where he will. The prophet obviously chooses the second alternative, but before he acts on it another plan occurs to Nebuzar-adan. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-6. - The liberation of Jeremiah. Verse 1. - The word that came to Jeremiah. The formula seems to announce a prophecy; but no prophecy follows. It is not allowable to suppose, with Keil and others, that "the word" describes the entire body of prophetic utterance in ch. 40-45. (in spite of the fact that ch. 44. and 45. have special headings). The use would be unexampled; and a prologue of forty verses (see Jeremiah 42:7) is equally contrary to prophetic analogy. Apparently the "word," or prophecy, which originally followed the heading has been lost or removed to some other place. Had let him go from Ramah. Here is an apparent discrepancy with the account in Jeremiah 39:14. The brevity of the latter seems to account for it. No doubt the more precise statement in our passage is to be followed. After the capture of the city, a number of captives, including Jeremiah, were probably conducted to Ramah (see on Jeremiah 31:15), where they had to wait for the royal decision as to their fate. Jeremiah, however, had already been in custody in the "court of the watch," and the writer of Jeremiah 29:14 simply omits the second stage of his captivity (Keil). In chains. See ver. 4, "The chains which were upon thine hand."
