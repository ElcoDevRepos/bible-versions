The word of the LORD came to Jeremiah
This phrase indicates divine revelation, a common theme in prophetic literature. The "word of the LORD" signifies a direct communication from God, emphasizing the authority and authenticity of the message. Jeremiah, like other prophets, serves as a mouthpiece for God, highlighting the role of prophets in conveying God's will to His people. This divine communication underscores the importance of listening to God's guidance and the serious nature of the prophetic office.

in the thirteenth year of the reign of Josiah
The thirteenth year of Josiah's reign places this event around 627 BC. Josiah was a reforming king of Judah, known for his efforts to restore the worship of Yahweh and eliminate idolatry. This period was marked by significant religious and political changes, as Josiah sought to bring the nation back to covenant faithfulness. The timing of Jeremiah's call during Josiah's reforms suggests a divine endorsement of these efforts and a call to further spiritual renewal.

son of Amon king of Judah
Amon was Josiah's father and reigned for a short period before being assassinated. His reign was characterized by idolatry and a departure from the ways of his father, Manasseh. The mention of Amon highlights the contrast between the idolatrous practices of previous kings and Josiah's reforms. It also situates Jeremiah's ministry within a specific historical context, emphasizing the continuity of God's work through different generations and the ongoing need for repentance and return to God.

king of Judah
Judah, the southern kingdom, was the remnant of the united monarchy after the division following Solomon's reign. The mention of Judah situates Jeremiah's ministry geographically and politically, as he prophesied during a time of national decline and impending judgment. The focus on Judah underscores the covenant relationship between God and His people, as well as the consequences of their disobedience. Jeremiah's messages often called for repentance and warned of the coming Babylonian exile, reflecting the themes of judgment and hope that permeate his prophetic work.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah. Known for his perseverance and faithfulness amidst opposition.

2. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who communicates His will and purpose through His prophets.

3. Josiah
The king of Judah during whose reign Jeremiah began his prophetic ministry. Known for his reforms and efforts to return Judah to the worship of Yahweh.

4. Amon
The father of Josiah and a former king of Judah, whose reign was marked by idolatry and was assassinated by his own officials.

5. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which was the primary audience of Jeremiah's prophecies. It was a time of political and spiritual turmoil.
Teaching Points
Divine Calling
God calls individuals for specific purposes. Like Jeremiah, we should be attentive to God's voice and ready to respond to His call in our lives.

Historical Context
Understanding the historical and cultural context of biblical events enriches our comprehension of Scripture. Josiah's reforms set the stage for Jeremiah's ministry, reminding us of the importance of spiritual leadership.

Faithfulness in Adversity
Jeremiah's ministry began in a time of reform but continued through much opposition. We are called to remain faithful to God's word, even when it is unpopular or challenging.

God's Sovereignty
The timing of God's word to Jeremiah during Josiah's reign underscores God's control over history. We can trust that God is sovereign over our circumstances and timing.

What Does Jeremiah 1:2 Mean
The word of the LORD

• “The word of the LORD” (Jeremiah 1:2) reminds us that the message originates with God Himself, not human speculation.

• Scripture consistently presents God’s word as living and authoritative—“All Scripture is God-breathed” (2 Timothy 3:16).

• Just as “the word of the LORD came to Abram” in Genesis 15:1 and to Jonah in Jonah 1:1, Jeremiah’s prophecy carries the same divine weight and certainty.

• Because God cannot lie (Numbers 23:19; Titus 1:2), every prophecy delivered under this phrase is utterly reliable.

came to Jeremiah

• God initiates the conversation; Jeremiah does not volunteer or invent a message.

• Like Moses in Exodus 3:4 and Samuel in 1 Samuel 3:21, Jeremiah is personally addressed and commissioned, underscoring the relational nature of revelation.

• This calling answers the need of Judah for a faithful messenger, paralleling Isaiah’s “Here am I. Send me!” moment (Isaiah 6:8).

in the thirteenth year

• God ties His word to a precise moment in history, confirming that biblical events unfold in real time, not myth.

• The thirteenth year of Josiah’s reign (about 627 BC) lands before Josiah’s sweeping reforms fully take hold (2 Chronicles 34:3-8).

• By timestamping the call, Scripture invites us to verify the chronology, reinforcing its trustworthiness (cf. Luke 3:1-2).

of the reign of Josiah

• Josiah is remembered as one who “did what was right in the eyes of the LORD” (2 Kings 22:2).

• God raises prophets during seasons of reform to sustain and deepen renewal—just as He did with Haggai and Zechariah during the post-exilic rebuild (Ezra 5:1-2).

• Jeremiah’s ministry would both support Josiah’s revival and warn of judgment once the people drifted again.

son of Amon

• Mentioning Josiah’s father, Amon—an evil king (2 Kings 21:19-22)—highlights God’s grace in raising a godly son from an ungodly lineage.

• This echoes how He brought good Hezekiah from wicked Ahaz (2 Chronicles 28:22-27) and later righteous Josiah from Amon, proving that heritage need not dictate destiny.

• The detail also anchors Jeremiah’s call in verifiable royal genealogy, demonstrating Scripture’s careful historical record-keeping.

king of Judah

• Jeremiah is commissioned within the southern kingdom after the northern kingdom (Israel) has already fallen to Assyria (2 Kings 17:6).

• Judah still holds the Davidic throne promised in 2 Samuel 7:16, so Jeremiah’s messages will revolve around covenant faithfulness, impending exile, and eventual restoration (Jeremiah 29:10-14).

• By identifying the reigning monarch, the verse prepares readers for the national focus of Jeremiah’s prophetic burden.

summary

Jeremiah 1:2 roots the prophet’s call in a specific historical setting, under a real king, at an exact year, demonstrating that God’s word breaks into humanity’s timeline with unassailable authority. Every phrase underscores reliability: the divine source, the personal recipient, the datable moment, the reforming monarch, the surprising lineage, and the covenant kingdom. Together they assure us that when God speaks, His message is precise, verifiable, and eternally trustworthy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) In the thirteenth year of his reign.--If we take the data of 2 Kings 22, Josiah was at that time in his twentieth or twenty-first year, having grown up under the training of Hilkiah. His active work of reformation began five years later. The images of Baal and Asherah (the groves) were thrown down, and the high places desecrated. The near coincidence of the commencement of Jeremiah's work as prophet with that of the king must not be forgotten. As Josiah reigned for thirty-one years, we have to place eighteen years of the prophet's ministry as under his rule.
