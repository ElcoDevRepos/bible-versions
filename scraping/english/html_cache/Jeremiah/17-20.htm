Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Say to them
This phrase indicates a direct command from God to the prophet Jeremiah. It emphasizes the role of the prophet as a messenger who conveys God's words to the people. Prophets in the Old Testament were often tasked with delivering messages of warning, instruction, or encouragement, and their authority came directly from God.

‘Hear the word of the LORD
The call to "hear" underscores the importance of listening and obeying God's message. In Hebrew culture, hearing was closely linked to obedience. The phrase "word of the LORD" signifies divine revelation, which carries ultimate authority and truth. This is a common prophetic formula used to assert that the message is not of human origin but from God Himself.

O kings of Judah
The address to the "kings of Judah" highlights the responsibility of the leaders to heed God's word. The kings were expected to lead the nation in covenant faithfulness. Historically, the kings of Judah were often judged by their adherence to God's laws, and their leadership had significant spiritual and political implications for the nation.

all people of Judah and Jerusalem
This phrase expands the audience to include not just the leaders but all inhabitants of the region. It reflects the communal nature of the covenant relationship with God, where both leaders and people are accountable. Jerusalem, as the capital city and spiritual center, held particular significance in the religious life of the nation.

who enter through these gates
The reference to "these gates" likely points to the gates of Jerusalem, which were central to the city's life. Gates were places of commerce, legal transactions, and public discourse. They symbolize the entry point to the city and, metaphorically, to the presence of God in the temple. This phrase underscores the accessibility of God's message to all who come into the city, emphasizing the universal call to repentance and obedience.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah and Jerusalem. Known for his perseverance in the face of opposition.

2. Kings of Judah
The ruling monarchs of the southern kingdom of Judah, responsible for leading the people in accordance with God's laws.

3. People of Judah
The inhabitants of the southern kingdom, often called to repentance by the prophets due to their disobedience.

4. Jerusalem
The capital city of Judah, a central place of worship and political power, often symbolic of the spiritual state of the nation.

5. Gates of Jerusalem
The entry points to the city, symbolizing the threshold between the secular and the sacred, and a place of judgment and decision-making.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Listening to God
The call to "hear the word of the LORD" is a reminder that God speaks to His people and expects them to listen attentively. In our daily lives, we must prioritize time to read and meditate on Scripture, seeking to understand God's will.

Leadership Accountability
The mention of the "kings of Judah" highlights the responsibility of leaders to guide their people according to God's commands. Whether in church, family, or community, leaders are accountable to God for their influence and decisions.

The Role of the Community
The address to "all people of Judah and Jerusalem" underscores the collective responsibility of the community to uphold God's standards. As believers, we are called to support one another in faith and obedience.

The Significance of the Gates
The "gates" symbolize decision points in our lives where we choose to follow God's path or our own. We must be vigilant at these crossroads, ensuring our choices align with God's word.

Repentance and Obedience
The broader context of Jeremiah's message is a call to repentance and obedience. True repentance involves a change of heart and actions, aligning our lives with God's commands.

What Does Jeremiah 17:20 Mean
Say to them

Jeremiah receives a direct charge. The imperative “Say” places the prophet under divine orders, not personal inclination.

• Jeremiah acts as a mouthpiece, echoing Jeremiah 1:7, 17 (BSB: “You must go to everyone I send you and speak whatever I command you”).

• Ezekiel 3:11 shows the same pattern—prophets speak only what God puts in their mouths.

• This reminds us that God still speaks with authority today through His written Word; our task mirrors Jeremiah’s—simply pass it on without alteration.

Hear the word of the LORD

The call is not to admire or debate but to listen with the intent to obey.

• Deuteronomy 6:4–5 begins the Shema the same way: “Hear, O Israel,” linking true hearing with wholehearted love.

• Isaiah 1:10 rebukes rulers who “hear the word of the LORD” yet ignore it; James 1:22 urges believers to be doers, not merely hearers.

• In Jeremiah 11:2 God says, “Hear the words of this covenant,” underscoring that covenant life hinges on receptive hearts.

O kings of Judah

Leadership is addressed first, because responsibility starts at the top.

• Jeremiah 22:2 instructs the king to “hear the word of the LORD, O king of Judah…you and your officials and your people.”

• 2 Kings 23:1-3 shows King Josiah modeling obedience by reading the Book of the Covenant publicly.

• Psalm 2:10-12 counsels kings to “be wise” and “serve the LORD with fear,” reminding civil leaders that they are under divine authority.

all people of Judah and Jerusalem

The message embraces everyone, rejecting any notion that holiness is for clergy alone.

• Deuteronomy 29:10-11 calls “all of you, your leaders and tribes…your women and children” to stand in covenant.

• Joel 2:15-17 gathers elders, infants, and newlyweds alike for national repentance.

• Acts 2:39 extends the promise “to you and your children and to all who are afar off,” showing God’s inclusive heart through every era.

who enter through these gates

The setting is the city gates—places of worship, commerce, and justice. The audience is about to step into the public square or the temple; God meets them at the threshold.

• Jeremiah 17:19 locates the prophet “in the gate of the people,” signaling strategic positioning where decisions are made.

• Jeremiah 7:2 commands him to “stand in the gate of the LORD’s house” and proclaim truth to worshipers.

• Psalm 24:7 calls, “Lift up your heads, O gates,” anticipating the King of Glory; righteous conduct at the gates honors that coming King.

summary

Jeremiah 17:20 pictures God halting every rank of society—rulers, citizens, worshipers—at the very entry point of daily life and communal worship. Through His prophet He insists, “Listen to My Word.” The verse underscores three timeless truths: God’s message is authoritative, obedience is universal, and accountability begins the moment we step into the public sphere.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - Jeremiah addresses himself first of all to the kings of Judah. As it would be very unnatural for a public orator to appeal to the yet unborn members of the reigning dynasty, and as there are several indications that the "house of David" was able at this period, as also in that of Isaiah, to exercise a decisive political and civil influence, even, as appears from Jeremiah 21:11, 12, monopolizing the judicial functions, it is natural to suppose that "kings of Judah" is here used in a very special sense, via. of the members of the various branches of the royal family ("The sons of the king," Zephaniah 1:8; comp. Jeremiah 36:26, "Jerahmeel, a king's son"), and their descendants, who received the royal title by courtesy (parallels for this will be found in Gesenius's 'Hebrew Thesaurus,' s.v.me'lek). The queen-mother was probably the leader of this plan; "the mistress," as she was called (see on Jeremiah 13:18), and the royal princes (among whom the "house of Nathan," Zechariah 12:12, would doubtless be reckoned), constituted in fact a body almost as numerous as they did (according to Brugsch Bey) in Egypt, and politically much more influential; so much so indeed that only a king of unusual force of character, like Hezekiah or Josiah, could venture, and that timidly, to oppose them. The weak-principled Zedekiah seems to have been entirely dominated by this powerful caste, and to have been little more than a maire du palais (the same sense of the phrase is required in Jeremiah 19:8, and probably in Jeremiah 25:18).
