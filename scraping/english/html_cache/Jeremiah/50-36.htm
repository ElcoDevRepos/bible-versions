Jeremiah 50
1. The judgment of Babylon and the redemption of Israel














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
A sword is against her false prophets
This phrase indicates divine judgment against the false prophets of Babylon. In the biblical context, false prophets were those who claimed to speak for God but led people astray with lies and deceit. The sword symbolizes God's instrument of judgment. In Jeremiah's time, false prophets often contradicted true prophets like Jeremiah, offering false hope and security (Jeremiah 23:16-17). The judgment against them underscores the seriousness of misleading God's people. This aligns with other scriptures where false prophets face severe consequences (Deuteronomy 18:20, Ezekiel 13:9).

and they will become fools
The outcome of the false prophets' judgment is that they will be exposed as fools. In biblical terms, a fool is someone who rejects wisdom and truth, often leading to their downfall (Proverbs 1:7, Psalm 14:1). This phrase highlights the futility of opposing God's truth and the inevitable exposure of deceit. Historically, Babylon was known for its diviners and astrologers, who were ultimately powerless against God's plans (Isaiah 47:12-15).

A sword is against her warriors
The warriors of Babylon, known for their might and military prowess, are also subject to God's judgment. The sword here represents the impending defeat and destruction of Babylon's military forces. This reflects the broader theme of God's sovereignty over nations and their armies, as seen in other prophetic books like Isaiah and Ezekiel. Babylon's warriors, despite their strength, cannot withstand God's decrees (Isaiah 13:17-19).

and they will be filled with terror
The terror that fills the warriors signifies the fear and panic that accompany divine judgment. This is a reversal of their usual role as instillers of fear in others. The historical context of Babylon's fall to the Medes and Persians illustrates this terror, as the once-mighty empire crumbled unexpectedly (Daniel 5). This phrase also serves as a reminder of the ultimate futility of relying on human strength and power against God's will (Psalm 33:16-17).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Jeremiah is known for his prophecies concerning the judgment of God upon Judah and other nations, including Babylon.

2. Babylon
A powerful empire during the time of Jeremiah, known for its conquest of Judah and the destruction of Jerusalem. In this chapter, Babylon is the subject of God's impending judgment.

3. Diviners
Individuals in Babylon who practiced divination, attempting to predict the future or gain insight through supernatural means, often contrary to God's will.

4. Warriors
The military forces of Babylon, known for their strength and conquests, but here prophesied to face defeat and terror.

5. Sword
Symbolic of God's judgment and the instrument of His wrath against Babylon, representing both literal and metaphorical destruction.
Teaching Points
The Futility of False Wisdom
The diviners of Babylon, despite their claims to wisdom, are rendered foolish by God's judgment. This serves as a reminder that human wisdom, apart from God, is ultimately futile.

The Power of God's Judgment
The prophecy against Babylon's warriors illustrates that no earthly power can stand against the judgment of God. This encourages believers to trust in God's sovereignty over all nations and powers.

The Consequences of Pride
Babylon's reliance on its own strength and wisdom leads to its downfall. This warns against the dangers of pride and self-reliance, urging believers to seek humility and dependence on God.

The Certainty of God's Word
The fulfillment of prophecies against Babylon demonstrates the reliability of God's Word. Believers can have confidence in the promises and warnings found in Scripture.

Spiritual Warfare
The imagery of the sword against Babylon's diviners and warriors can be applied to the spiritual battles believers face, reminding them to rely on God's truth and strength.

What Does Jeremiah 50:36 Mean
A sword is against her false prophets, and they will become fools

“ A sword is against her diviners, and they will become fools” (Jeremiah 50:36a).

• God targets the very people who claimed to speak in His name yet led Babylon deeper into idolatry. Their proud predictions will be exposed as empty when the invader’s sword arrives (Jeremiah 27:9–10; Ezekiel 13:6–9).

• The term “fools” points to moral and spiritual bankruptcy (Psalm 14:1). When judgment falls, their supposed wisdom collapses, echoing the showdown on Mount Carmel where false prophets were shamed before Elijah’s God (1 Kings 18:38–40).

• The “sword” symbolizes literal military conquest (Isaiah 13:17–19) and God’s decisive Word that severs truth from deception (Jeremiah 23:29; Hebrews 4:12).

• Babylon once silenced Judah’s prophets; now God silences Babylon’s. This reversal reminds us that every boast opposed to divine truth is temporary (2 Corinthians 10:4–5).

A sword is against her warriors, and they will be filled with terror

“ A sword is against her mighty men, and they will be dismayed” (Jeremiah 50:36b).

• The empire’s elite troops—proud of their victories over nations like Judah—will panic when God directs the Medo-Persian forces against them (Jeremiah 51:11; Daniel 5:30–31).

• Fear overtakes the strongest when God removes courage (Jeremiah 46:5; Isaiah 13:7–8). Babylon’s warriors, fearless in their own strength, crumble because the Lord of hosts fights against them (Jeremiah 50:29).

• This fulfills earlier warnings that trust in chariots and human might fails (Psalm 20:7; Isaiah 31:3). God alone grants security; without Him, even fortified walls and seasoned soldiers offer no refuge (Proverbs 21:31).

summary

Jeremiah 50:36 announces double judgment: deceiving voices are unmasked as foolish, and defiant strength melts into terror. God’s sword—both His spoken verdict and the literal armies He sends—cuts down lies and pride alike. The verse reassures believers that the Lord defends truth and humbles every power that exalts itself against Him, proving again that His Word never fails.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) A sword is upon the liars; and they shall dote.--The Hebrew word for "liars"--literally, boastings--implies the falsehood of folly rather than of purpose. Better, perhaps, the prating fools. The marginal readings "chief stays" and "bars" rest on no adequate authority. Here the word applies to the diviners and magicians (comp. Isaiah 44:25).
