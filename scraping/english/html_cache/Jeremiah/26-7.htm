Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Now the priests and prophets and all the people
This phrase highlights the diverse audience present during Jeremiah's proclamation. The priests and prophets were religious leaders, often seen as mediators between God and the people. However, during Jeremiah's time, many were corrupt or false, leading the people astray (Jeremiah 23:11-14). The inclusion of "all the people" indicates a public setting, emphasizing the widespread impact of Jeremiah's message. This gathering reflects the communal nature of worship and religious instruction in ancient Israel.

heard Jeremiah speaking
Jeremiah's role as a prophet was to deliver God's messages, often involving calls to repentance and warnings of judgment. His speaking was not just a casual conversation but a divinely inspired proclamation. The act of hearing implies accountability; those who heard were responsible for responding to the message. This echoes the biblical theme that faith comes by hearing the word of God (Romans 10:17).

these words
The specific words Jeremiah spoke were warnings of impending judgment due to the people's disobedience and idolatry. This message was consistent with the covenantal themes found throughout the Old Testament, where blessings and curses were contingent upon Israel's faithfulness (Deuteronomy 28). Jeremiah's words were not his own but were given by God, underscoring the authority and seriousness of the message.

in the house of the LORD
The house of the LORD refers to the temple in Jerusalem, the central place of worship and sacrifice for the Israelites. It was considered the dwelling place of God's presence among His people. The setting in the temple underscores the gravity of Jeremiah's message, as it was delivered in the most sacred space, where the people were expected to be most attuned to God's voice. This location also highlights the irony of the people's unfaithfulness, as they were defiling the very place meant for worship and communion with God (Jeremiah 7:9-11).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah. Known for his boldness and faithfulness in delivering God's often unpopular messages.

2. The Priests
Religious leaders responsible for temple worship and maintaining the spiritual life of the people. They often held significant influence over the religious and social life of Judah.

3. The Prophets
Other individuals claiming to speak on behalf of God. In Jeremiah's time, many were false prophets who contradicted God's true messages.

4. The People
The general population of Judah who were present in the temple. They were the audience of Jeremiah's prophetic message.

5. The House of the LORD
Refers to the temple in Jerusalem, the central place of worship for the Israelites and a symbol of God's presence among His people.
Teaching Points
The Courage to Speak God's Truth
Jeremiah's example challenges us to speak God's truth boldly, even when it is unpopular or met with resistance.

Discernment in Leadership
The presence of false prophets highlights the need for discernment in whom we listen to and follow, ensuring they align with God's Word.

The Role of the Church
Just as the temple was central to worship, the church today should be a place where God's truth is proclaimed and lived out.

The Importance of Genuine Worship
God desires true worship from His people, not just outward rituals. Our hearts and actions must align with His commands.

Responding to God's Message
The people's reaction to Jeremiah's message prompts us to consider how we respond to God's Word in our lives.

What Does Jeremiah 26:7 Mean
Now the priests

The men charged with guarding God’s law are the first to be mentioned.

• Their duty: teach truth and preserve holiness (Deuteronomy 31:9–13; Malachi 2:7).

• Their history: previous priests had already opposed Jeremiah’s earlier preaching (Jeremiah 20:1–2).

• Application: when spiritual leaders reject God’s word, the nation soon follows (Hosea 4:6).

and the prophets

Side-by-side with the priests stand those who claimed to speak for God.

• Most were offering soothing lies of peace (Jeremiah 23:16–17; 28:1-4).

• True prophets like Jeremiah were a minority and faced fierce pushback (1 Kings 22:6-8).

• Their presence in this verse highlights the clash between genuine revelation and popular “prophetic” optimism.

and all the people

The entire congregation is included.

• Earlier, God told Jeremiah to stand “in the courtyard of the LORD’s house and speak to all the cities of Judah who come to worship” (Jeremiah 26:2).

• The crowd’s response will swing from curiosity (v. 7) to fury and a death sentence (vv. 8–11).

• This shows how public opinion can be swayed quickly when hearts are unrepentant (Matthew 27:20-23).

heard Jeremiah speaking

Nothing was done in secret.

• Jeremiah’s voice rang out in the very center of national worship, fulfilling God’s command: “Do not shrink back… speak all I command you” (Jeremiah 1:17).

• Hearing truth brings accountability (Romans 10:17; Hebrews 4:2).

• Their later rejection is therefore willful, not from ignorance.

these words

The “words” refer to the temple sermon just delivered (Jeremiah 26:4-6):

“If you do not listen to Me… I will make this house like Shiloh and this city an object of cursing.”

• The message was specific, covenantal, and unmistakably severe.

• God ties obedience to blessing and rebellion to judgment (Leviticus 26; Deuteronomy 28).

• Rejecting these words would equal rejecting God Himself.

in the house of the LORD

The setting intensifies the moment.

• The temple was supposed to be “a house of prayer for all nations” (Isaiah 56:7), yet it had become a place of false security (Jeremiah 7:4).

• Declaring impending destruction while standing in that sacred precinct exposed the emptiness of outward religion divorced from obedience (Micah 3:11; Matthew 23:27).

• God often confronts sin at the very altar people try to hide behind (1 Samuel 3:11-14; Matthew 21:12-13).

summary

Jeremiah 26:7 pictures every level of Judah’s society—priests, prophets, and common people—gathered in the temple and audibly confronted with God’s warning. Their hearing makes them fully accountable. The verse underscores that when God’s servants faithfully proclaim His word, the truth reaches the leadership, the influencers, and the masses alike. Whether they heed or resist will determine their destiny, but the messenger has delivered the message in the heart of national worship, leaving no room for excuse.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) The priests and the prophets.--The mention of the latter is significant. Jeremiah had to separate himself from both the orders to which he belonged, in the one case, by birth, in the other, by a special vocation. His bitterest foes were found among those who claimed to speak as he did, in the name of the Lord, but who tuned their voice according to the time, and prophesied deceits. See Notes on Jeremiah 23:9-40.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 7-11. - To all devout Jews this prediction of the destruction of the temple must have been startling; but to those who placed their confidence in the mere exist-once of a consecrated building (Jeremiah 7:4), it was like a blow aimed at their very life. Besides, were not the majority of the prophets of Jehovah of entirely another way of thinking? Did they not promise peace? And what could justify Jeremiah in announcing not merely war, but the downfall of the Divine habitation itself? Hence no sooner had the prophet concluded his discourse, than he was arrested, accused, and condemned to death.
