Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I will make an everlasting covenant with them:
This phrase refers to God's promise of a new and enduring covenant with His people. The concept of an "everlasting covenant" is rooted in the Abrahamic covenant (Genesis 17:7) and is fulfilled in the New Covenant through Jesus Christ (Hebrews 8:6-13). This covenant is characterized by its permanence and divine origin, contrasting with the temporary and conditional nature of the Mosaic covenant. It signifies a relationship that is not based on human merit but on God's unchanging faithfulness.

I will never turn away from doing good to them:
Here, God assures His people of His unwavering commitment to their welfare. This promise echoes the blessings outlined in Deuteronomy 28 for obedience to God's commands. It also reflects God's character as inherently good (Psalm 100:5) and His desire to bless His people (Jeremiah 29:11). This assurance is a source of hope and comfort, especially during times of exile and hardship, as it underscores God's benevolent intentions.

and I will put My fear in their hearts:
This phrase indicates a transformation of the inner being, where God instills a reverential awe and respect for Himself within His people. This internal change is a hallmark of the New Covenant, where the law is written on the hearts of believers (Jeremiah 31:33). The "fear of the Lord" is foundational to wisdom (Proverbs 9:10) and is essential for a life that honors God. It signifies a deep, relational understanding of God's holiness and sovereignty.

so that they will never turn away from Me:
The result of God's work in the hearts of His people is their steadfast faithfulness to Him. This phrase highlights the security and perseverance of the saints, a theme echoed in the New Testament (John 10:28-29). It assures believers of God's sustaining grace, which enables them to remain faithful. This promise is fulfilled in the life of Jesus Christ, who perfectly embodies faithfulness and secures the faith of His followers through the Holy Spirit.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of both judgment and hope to the people of Judah during a time of great turmoil and impending exile.

2. God
The covenant-making and covenant-keeping God who promises to establish an everlasting covenant with His people.

3. The People of Judah
The recipients of God's promise, who are facing the consequences of their disobedience but are also given hope for future restoration.

4. Babylonian Exile
The historical context in which Jeremiah prophesied, where the people of Judah were taken captive by Babylon as a result of their unfaithfulness to God.

5. Everlasting Covenant
A divine promise of unending commitment and relationship between God and His people, characterized by God's unwavering goodness and the instilling of reverence in their hearts.
Teaching Points
The Nature of God's Covenant
God's covenants are everlasting, reflecting His unchanging nature and eternal commitment to His people.

God's Unwavering Goodness
God promises never to turn away from doing good to His people, highlighting His benevolent character and faithfulness.

The Fear of the Lord
The fear of the Lord is a divine gift that keeps believers aligned with God's will, fostering a relationship of reverence and obedience.

Heart Transformation
True transformation comes from God placing His fear in our hearts, leading to a life that does not turn away from Him.

Hope in Restoration
Even in times of judgment or discipline, God offers hope and restoration through His promises and covenants.

What Does Jeremiah 32:40 Mean
I will make an everlasting covenant with them

God Himself pledges a covenant that will never expire. This is no mere renewal of Israel’s past agreements; it is an unbreakable bond initiated and guaranteed by the Lord.

• Genesis 17:7 shows God promising Abraham an “everlasting covenant,” foreshadowing this moment.

• Isaiah 55:3 speaks of the “everlasting covenant” tied to the sure mercies of David, confirming continuity in God’s redemptive plan.

• Hebrews 13:20 calls Jesus “the great Shepherd of the sheep” who secures “the eternal covenant,” revealing Christ as the covenant’s guarantor.

Because Scripture is accurate and literal, we take “everlasting” at face value—God’s commitment extends beyond time, ensuring His people’s security forever.

I will never turn away from doing good to them

God’s heart toward His covenant people is permanently tilted toward blessing.

• James 1:17 reminds us that “every good and perfect gift is from above… who does not change like shifting shadows”, confirming His unwavering goodness.

• Psalm 23:6 celebrates, “Surely goodness and mercy will follow me all the days of my life”, echoing the promise of unending benevolence.

• Romans 8:28 assures that “all things work together for good to those who love God,” revealing this pledge in practical terms: even hardships become instruments of blessing.

The literal promise leaves no room for fear that God might abandon or harm His people; His goodness is a steady, unchanging flow.

I will put My fear in their hearts

God promises to implant reverence within His people so they respond to Him rightly.

• Jeremiah 31:33 anticipates the new covenant: “I will put My law in their minds and inscribe it on their hearts”, indicating an internal transformation.

• Ezekiel 36:26–27 expands this: God gives a new heart and spirit, causing obedience.

• Proverbs 1:7 teaches that “the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge,” so this implanted fear is foundational for wise, holy living.

This is not terror but awe-filled devotion, a Spirit‐wrought change that aligns desires with God’s will.

So that they will never turn away from Me

The result of God’s action is enduring faithfulness from His people.

• John 10:28–29 declares that no one can snatch Christ’s sheep from His hand—divine preservation mirrors Jeremiah’s promise.

• Philippians 1:6 comforts believers that He who began a good work will finish it, underscoring God-given perseverance.

• Jude 24 praises the One “who is able to keep you from stumbling,” affirming that the security rests in God’s power, not human resolve.

Because God implants reverence and vows unending goodness, His people remain steadfast. Apostasy is prevented by divine initiative, not human grit.

summary

Jeremiah 32:40 presents a fourfold chain of grace: an unending covenant, a guarantee of continual goodness, an inward transformation, and an everlasting fidelity. Each link is forged by God Himself, ensuring our security, blessing, and perseverance. The verse invites confident trust: the God who binds Himself to us also keeps us bound to Him forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(40) I will make an everlasting covenant . . .--The "covenant" thus promised is, it must be remembered, identical with that of Jeremiah 31:31--the "new covenant," which shall never wax old and decay, but shall abide for ever. "My fear" is identical with "the fear of the Lord," which is "the beginning of wisdom." The curse of Israel had been that they had been without that fear to restrain them
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 40. - An everlasting covenant. It is the "new covenant" of Jeremiah 31:31, etc., which is meant (for the phrase, comp. Isaiah 55:3; Ezekiel 37:26). That I will not turn... to do them good. The comma in the Authorized Version impairs the sense. The prophet means, "That I will not cease to show them favour" (comp. Isaiah 54:10).
