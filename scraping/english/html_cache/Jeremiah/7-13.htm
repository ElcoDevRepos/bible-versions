Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And now, because you have done all these things, declares the LORD
This phrase indicates a transition from past actions to present consequences. The "things" refer to the idolatry and disobedience of the Israelites, as detailed in the preceding verses. The phrase "declares the LORD" emphasizes the authority and finality of God's pronouncement. This is a common prophetic formula used to assert divine authority, seen throughout the Old Testament, such as in Isaiah 1:18 and Ezekiel 18:30. The historical context is the reign of King Josiah and his successors, a time of religious reform that was ultimately superficial for many in Judah.

and because I have spoken to you again and again but you would not listen
This highlights God's persistent attempts to reach His people through prophets like Jeremiah. The repetition of God's efforts underscores His patience and mercy, as seen in 2 Chronicles 36:15-16, where God repeatedly sent messengers to His people. The phrase "you would not listen" indicates a willful disobedience and hardening of hearts, reminiscent of the Israelites' behavior in the wilderness (Exodus 32:9). This refusal to listen is a recurring theme in Jeremiah, illustrating the people's stubbornness and the resulting judgment.

and I have called to you but you would not answer
This phrase emphasizes the relational aspect of God's communication with His people. The imagery of calling and not receiving an answer suggests a broken relationship, akin to a parent calling out to a wayward child. This is reflective of God's desire for a covenant relationship, as seen in Hosea 11:1-4, where God expresses His love and longing for Israel. The refusal to answer is symbolic of the people's rejection of God's covenant, leading to the inevitable consequences of their actions. This also foreshadows the ultimate call of God through Jesus Christ, who invites all to respond to His message of salvation (Matthew 11:28-30).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of warning and hope to the people of Judah. His ministry was marked by his deep emotional connection to his people and his unwavering commitment to God's truth.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is speaking through Jeremiah. He is portrayed as a patient and persistent communicator, reaching out to His people despite their disobedience.

3. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which is the primary audience of Jeremiah's prophecies. At this time, Judah is steeped in idolatry and social injustice, leading to God's impending judgment.

4. The Temple Sermon
The context of Jeremiah 7 is often referred to as the "Temple Sermon," where Jeremiah stands at the gate of the LORD's house to deliver God's message of warning to those who falsely trust in the temple for security.

5. Repeated Warnings
The event of God repeatedly reaching out to His people through prophets like Jeremiah, emphasizing His desire for their repentance and return to covenant faithfulness.
Teaching Points
God's Persistent Call
God is relentless in His pursuit of His people, demonstrating His love and desire for relationship. We should be attentive to His voice in our lives, recognizing His patience and grace.

The Danger of Ignoring God
Ignoring God's call leads to spiritual peril. We must be vigilant in listening and responding to God's Word, avoiding the complacency that leads to judgment.

The Role of Prophets and Messengers
God uses individuals to communicate His truth. We should value and heed the messages delivered by those God has placed in our lives to guide us.

Repentance and Return
The call to repentance is central to God's message. We are invited to turn from our ways and return to God, experiencing His forgiveness and restoration.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Disobedience to God's call results in consequences. Understanding this should motivate us to live in alignment with His will.

What Does Jeremiah 7:13 Mean
And now

The Lord shifts from recounting Judah’s history of rebellion to announcing the consequence in real time. The weight of the moment mirrors earlier warnings: just as God once told Israel, “Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts” (Hebrews 3:15), He now confronts them with an urgent “now.” The link to the previous verse (Jeremiah 7:12) reminds us of Shiloh’s downfall—proof that God’s patience has limits (1 Samuel 4:10–11).

because you have done all these things, declares the LORD

Judah’s tangible sins—idolatry, social injustice, temple hypocrisy (Jeremiah 7:8–10)—cannot be brushed aside. God’s declaration is final and authoritative, echoing His verdict on Manasseh’s atrocities (2 Kings 21:9–15) and the nation’s collective guilt (2 Chronicles 36:14). Scripture consistently ties moral choice to divine response (Isaiah 5:7); deeds carry covenant consequences.

and because I spoke to you again and again but you did not listen

God’s repeated appeals (“rising early and speaking,” Jeremiah 25:3–4) reveal remarkable long-suffering. Prophets were sent “persistently” (2 Chronicles 24:19), yet the people tuned them out. Proverbs 1:24–25 pictures wisdom’s call going unheeded, and Stephen later charges, “You always resist the Holy Spirit” (Acts 7:51). Ignoring divine instruction is not mere negligence; it is active rebellion.

and I called to you but you did not answer

The imagery shifts from speaking to calling—an even more personal summons. Refusal to respond repeats the pattern condemned in Isaiah 65:12 and foretold in Zechariah 7:13: “They would not listen, so when they called, I would not listen.” Jesus evokes the same grief when He laments over Jerusalem’s rejection (Matthew 23:37; cf. Matthew 22:3). Silence toward God results in His judicial silence toward the unrepentant.

summary

Jeremiah 7:13 underscores a sober sequence: God’s people sinned, God patiently warned, they refused to listen, and now judgment is inevitable. The verse reveals both the relentless mercy of repeated calls and the righteous certainty of consequences when those calls are ignored.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Rising up early and speaking.--A characteristic phrase of Jeremiah's, and used by him only (Jeremiah 13:25, Jeremiah 25:4; Jeremiah 26:5; Jeremiah 29:19). In its bold anthropomorphism it takes the highest form of human activity, waking from sleep and beginning at the dawn of day, to represent the like activity in God.

I called you, but ye answered not.--An echo of earlier complaints from prophets and wise men (Proverbs 1:24; Isaiah 65:12; Isaiah 66:4), destined itself to be used again by One greater than the prophets (Matthew 23:37). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Rising up early and speaking; i.e. speaking zealously and continually (so ver. 25; Jeremiah 25:4; Jeremiah 26:5; Jeremiah 29:19). It is an expression peculiar to Jeremiah.
