Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
So now, this is what the LORD God of Hosts, the God of Israel, says:
This phrase emphasizes the authority and sovereignty of God. "LORD God of Hosts" refers to God's command over the heavenly armies, underscoring His power and majesty. The title "God of Israel" highlights His covenant relationship with the nation of Israel, reminding the people of their unique identity and responsibilities as His chosen people. This introduction sets the stage for a divine pronouncement, demanding attention and reverence.

Why are you doing such great harm to yourselves:
The rhetorical question points to the self-destructive behavior of the people. Historically, the Israelites often turned to idolatry and disobedience, leading to their downfall. This phrase suggests that their actions are not only against God but also detrimental to their own well-being. It reflects the biblical principle that sin leads to suffering and separation from God, as seen throughout the Old Testament.

by cutting off from Judah man and woman, child and infant:
This phrase indicates the severe consequences of the people's actions, leading to the decimation of the population. Historically, the Babylonian conquest resulted in significant loss of life and exile. The mention of "man and woman, child and infant" underscores the totality of the destruction, affecting all demographics. This echoes the covenant curses outlined in Deuteronomy 28, where disobedience leads to national calamity.

leaving yourselves without a remnant:
The concept of a "remnant" is significant in biblical theology, often referring to a faithful group preserved by God despite widespread judgment. Here, the warning is that their continued rebellion could result in the absence of such a remnant, threatening the survival of the nation. This connects to prophecies in Isaiah and other prophets where a remnant is preserved for future restoration. The absence of a remnant would mean the loss of hope for renewal and fulfillment of God's promises to Israel.

Teaching Points
The Consequences of Disobedience
Disobedience to God leads to self-destruction. The people of Judah are warned that their actions are causing great harm to themselves.

The Importance of a Remnant
God desires to preserve a remnant. The warning against cutting off man, woman, child, and infant highlights the importance of maintaining a faithful group who can carry on God's promises.

God's Relentless Pursuit
Despite their rebellion, God continues to reach out to His people. This demonstrates His relentless love and desire for their repentance and restoration.

The Danger of Idolatry
Idolatry leads to spiritual and physical ruin. The people of Judah are reminded that their idolatrous practices are the root of their problems.

The Call to Self-Examination
Believers are encouraged to examine their own lives for areas of disobedience and idolatry, understanding that these lead to spiritual harm.

What Does Jeremiah 44:7 Mean
So now

“So now” (Jeremiah 44:7a) signals an urgent turning point. After recounting Judah’s long rebellion (Jeremiah 44:1-6), the Lord refuses to let His people drift further without confrontation—much like His immediate “now” in Jeremiah 7:3 and Isaiah 1:18. The moment demands decision; delay only deepens disaster.

This is what the LORD God of Hosts, the God of Israel, says

The triple title underscores absolute authority.

• “LORD” (Yahweh) reminds them of the covenant name first revealed in Exodus 3:15.

• “God of Hosts” points to His command over angelic armies (1 Samuel 17:45; Psalm 46:7), stressing that no earthly power—Egypt included—can shield disobedience.

• “God of Israel” highlights His personal relationship with this nation (Jeremiah 31:1), making their rebellion not mere law-breaking but betrayal.

When the Commander-in-Chief of heaven speaks, the only faithful response is submissive obedience (Jeremiah 32:27).

Why are you doing such great harm to yourselves

Sin is self-destructive. The people thought idol worship in Egypt would secure prosperity (Jeremiah 44:17), yet God lays bare the irony: “you are doing great harm to yourselves.” Scripture repeatedly exposes this boomerang effect:

• Proverbs 8:36—“he who fails to find me harms himself.”

• Ezekiel 18:31—“why should you die, O house of Israel?”

• Romans 6:23—“the wages of sin is death.”

God’s prohibitions protect, not imprison; refusing them guarantees personal and collective ruin.

By cutting off from Judah man and woman, child and infant

Disobedience doesn’t stay private; it slashes through generations. Earlier prophecies warned of indiscriminate judgment (Jeremiah 6:11; 2 Chronicles 36:17). Here the Lord exposes the cost in vivid categories:

• “man and woman” – adult leadership eliminated

• “child and infant” – future hope erased

This mirrors covenant curses such as Deuteronomy 28:62, “you who were as numerous as the stars…will be left few in number.” Sin’s collateral damage spares no age or gender.

Leaving yourselves without a remnant

God consistently preserves a remnant to keep His promises alive (Isaiah 1:9; Jeremiah 23:3). By persisting in rebellion, the exiles threaten even that mercy: “leaving yourselves without a remnant.” Earlier, He had promised survival if they remained in Judah (Jeremiah 42:10-12). Their flight to Egypt—and continued idolatry—places them outside the shelter of that promise, aligning with the grim forecast of Jeremiah 42:17: “they will perish…they will have no remnant or survivor.” The warning is as stark as it is loving: turn back, or lose everything.

summary

Jeremiah 44:7 is a loving yet severe wake-up call. The covenant God, Commander of heaven’s armies, confronts His wayward people with the truth that their sin is self-inflicted injury. By clinging to idolatry, they endanger every demographic, risk wiping out the God-preserved remnant, and jeopardize the very promises meant to bless them. The verse pleads: recognize who is speaking, grasp the lethal cost of rebellion, and return before the remnant disappears.
