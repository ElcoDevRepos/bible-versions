Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But now, behold, I am freeing you today from the chains that were on your wrists.
This phrase signifies a pivotal moment of liberation for Jeremiah, who had been bound in chains. The context is the aftermath of Jerusalem's fall to Babylon in 586 BC. Jeremiah, though a prophet of God, was caught up in the general deportation of the people. The chains symbolize the physical and spiritual bondage of the Israelites due to their disobedience. This act of freeing Jeremiah can be seen as a type of Christ's mission to liberate humanity from the bondage of sin.

If it pleases you to come with me to Babylon, then come, and I will take care of you.
The offer to accompany the Babylonian captain, Nebuzaradan, to Babylon reflects the favor Jeremiah found even among his captors. Babylon, a symbol of exile and judgment, also represents a place where God’s people would be refined and preserved. The promise of care suggests God's providence and protection, even in foreign lands. This echoes God's assurance to His people that He will be with them wherever they go (Genesis 28:15).

But if it seems wrong to you to come with me to Babylon, go no farther.
Jeremiah is given a choice, highlighting the respect and freedom granted to him. This choice underscores the theme of free will in the biblical narrative, where God allows His people to make decisions while guiding them towards His purposes. It also reflects the respect Jeremiah commanded due to his faithfulness to God's message.

Look, the whole land is before you.
This phrase recalls God's promise to Abraham in Genesis 13:9, where the land was set before him to choose. It signifies a new beginning and the opportunity for Jeremiah to continue his prophetic mission. The land, though devastated, remains a place of potential restoration and hope for the remnant of Israel.

Wherever it seems good and right to you, go there.
Jeremiah is entrusted with the autonomy to choose his path, a rare privilege in the context of captivity. This reflects the biblical principle that God’s servants are often given the freedom to discern and follow His will. It also emphasizes the importance of seeking what is good and right, aligning with the moral and ethical teachings found throughout Scripture.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet of God who warned Judah of impending judgment and was later freed by the Babylonians.

2. Nebuzaradan
The captain of the guard under King Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon, who released Jeremiah from his chains.

3. Babylon
The empire that conquered Judah and Jerusalem, leading to the Babylonian exile.

4. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which faced destruction and exile due to its disobedience to God.

5. The Land
Refers to the land of Judah, which Jeremiah is given the freedom to choose to stay in or leave.
Teaching Points
Divine Liberation
God orchestrates our freedom, both spiritually and physically. Just as Jeremiah was freed from his chains, believers are freed from the bondage of sin through Christ.

Choice and Guidance
God provides us with choices and the wisdom to make them. Jeremiah's freedom to choose where to go reflects the free will God grants us, along with His guidance.

Trust in God's Provision
Nebuzaradan's offer to care for Jeremiah in Babylon is a reminder that God provides for His people, even in foreign lands or difficult circumstances.

Faithfulness in Uncertainty
Jeremiah's situation teaches us to remain faithful and trust God, even when the future is uncertain or when we are given difficult choices.

God's Sovereignty
The unfolding events show God's control over nations and individuals, reminding us that He is sovereign over our lives and circumstances.

What Does Jeremiah 40:4 Mean
I am freeing you today

Jeremiah had literally been dragged away in chains when Jerusalem fell, yet the very pagan official who oversaw the conquest now removes those shackles.

• God vindicates His servant just as He promised (Jeremiah 39:11-12; Jeremiah 15:11).

• The image of broken bonds recalls other divine jailbreaks—Acts 12:7; Psalm 107:14—and anticipates the Messiah’s liberating work (Isaiah 61:1).

• The release occurs “today,” underscoring that God’s timing is precise and personal (Galatians 4:4).

If it pleases you to come with me

Nebuzaradan’s offer is astonishing: “Come with me to Babylon, and I will take care of you.”

• Provision—Jeremiah would enjoy court protection much like Jehoiachin later did (2 Kings 25:28-30).

• Purpose—Babylon was the new mission field where God’s people were heading (Jeremiah 29:4-7).

• Peace—The captain’s words fulfill God’s earlier assurance that Jeremiah’s life would be “as a prize of war” (Jeremiah 39:18).

But if it seems wrong to you to come

Jeremiah is not coerced; he is free to obey conscience and calling.

• The principle of holy choice—Deut 30:19; Ruth 1:16.

• God values willing service above forced compliance—2 Cor 9:7.

• Even earthly powers must bow to God’s higher purpose for His prophet (Proverbs 21:1).

Look, the whole land is before you

Staying in Judah also lies open.

• A remnant remains; they will soon need pastoral guidance (Jeremiah 40:6; 42:2-3).

• God often keeps a witness right where judgment has fallen—Gen 19:29; Acts 17:34.

• The phrasing echoes Genesis 13:9, inviting faith to survey God’s promises despite ruins.

Wherever it seems good and right to you, go there

Nebuzaradan closes with a sweeping grant of freedom.

• “Good and right” calls for moral discernment (Proverbs 3:5-6; Psalm 32:8).

• True liberty is the ability to follow God’s directive, not merely personal whim (Romans 8:14; James 1:25).

• Jeremiah chooses Mizpah, a strategic place for shepherding survivors (Jeremiah 40:6), illustrating that freedom finds its highest expression in service.

summary

Jeremiah 40:4 shows the Lord breaking literal chains, providing unexpected favor through a conqueror, and granting His servant genuine freedom to pursue the path that aligns with divine calling. Whether cared for in Babylon or ministering in a devastated Judah, Jeremiah’s options highlight God’s sovereignty, tender provision, and respect for willing obedience. The verse invites every believer to trust the Lord who liberates, guides, and equips us to walk wherever it is “good and right” in His sight.
