And Judah and all its cities will dwell together in the land
This phrase reflects the promise of restoration and unity for the people of Judah. Historically, the Babylonian exile had scattered the inhabitants of Judah, leading to a period of displacement and longing for their homeland. The promise of dwelling together signifies a return to peace and stability, reminiscent of the united kingdom under David and Solomon. This restoration is not just physical but also spiritual, as it implies a return to covenant faithfulness. Theologically, this can be seen as a foreshadowing of the ultimate gathering of God's people, as seen in prophecies like Ezekiel 37:21-22, where God promises to gather the Israelites from among the nations and bring them back to their own land.

the farmers and those who move with the flocks
This phrase highlights the agrarian society of ancient Judah, where agriculture and pastoralism were central to the economy and daily life. Farmers and shepherds represent the backbone of the community, emphasizing the return to normalcy and prosperity. The mention of both farmers and shepherds indicates a comprehensive restoration, where all aspects of society are revitalized. This imagery also connects to the pastoral themes found throughout Scripture, such as Psalm 23, where God is depicted as a shepherd caring for His flock. In a broader sense, it points to the Messianic hope of Jesus Christ, the Good Shepherd, who gathers and cares for His people, as seen in John 10:11-16.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Judah
One of the twelve tribes of Israel, representing the southern kingdom after the division of Israel. It is significant as the lineage of King David and ultimately Jesus Christ.

2. Cities of Judah
These are the various urban centers within the territory of Judah, each with its own unique role and significance in the life of the nation.

3. Farmers
Individuals who work the land, representing the agrarian aspect of society, which was crucial for sustenance and economic stability.

4. Those who move with the flocks
Refers to shepherds or nomadic herders, highlighting the pastoral lifestyle that was common in ancient Israel.

5. The Land
The Promised Land given by God to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, symbolizing God's covenant and faithfulness.
Teaching Points
God's Promise of Restoration
Jeremiah 31:24 highlights God's faithfulness in restoring His people to their land. This serves as a reminder of His unchanging promises and the hope we have in His plans for restoration in our lives.

Unity and Community
The verse emphasizes the unity of Judah and its cities, encouraging us to seek unity and community within the body of Christ, working together for the common good.

The Value of Work
The mention of farmers and shepherds underscores the dignity of work and the importance of each role in society. We are called to honor God through our vocations, recognizing that all work is valuable in His sight.

Living in God's Provision
The promise of dwelling in the land points to living in God's provision and care. We are encouraged to trust in His provision and to be good stewards of the resources He has given us.

What Does Jeremiah 31:24 Mean
And Judah

“Judah” identifies the covenant people who had endured exile because of unbelief. Here God assures them of a literal return.

• Jeremiah 30:3 speaks of bringing “My people Israel and Judah back to the land that I gave to their fathers.”

• Zechariah 10:6 promises, “I will strengthen the house of Judah … they shall be as though I had not cast them off.”

• God’s faithfulness to Judah underscores His unwavering commitment to His promises, encouraging us to trust every word He speaks.

and all its cities

Not merely Jerusalem but every town and village is included. The blessing is comprehensive, reaching the outskirts as well as the capital.

• Jeremiah 33:13 pictures “the towns of the hill country, the foothills, and the Negev” once again ringing with life.

• Isaiah 61:4 portrays ruined cities rebuilt, confirming that no community is overlooked.

• God’s restoration touches every corner, reminding believers that His grace extends to every sphere of life.

will dwell together in the land

The people live securely and in unity on the very soil God promised to Abraham. Exile is reversed; stability replaces scattering.

• Jeremiah 23:6 declares, “In His days Judah will be saved, and Israel will dwell securely.”

• Micah 4:4 envisions each person “under his vine and under his fig tree, with no one to frighten him.”

• Ezekiel 28:25–26 foretells Israel “dwelling securely in their own land.”

God’s promise of a safe homeland looks ahead to the ultimate messianic kingdom when peace will be permanent.

the farmers

Agriculturists symbolize settled, fruitful life. The soil once ravaged by war will flourish again.

• Jeremiah 31:5 anticipates vineyards being planted and enjoyed.

• Amos 9:13 describes mountains dripping with new wine and plowman overtaking reaper.

• Isaiah 65:21 pictures people building houses and planting vineyards, enjoying long-term rewards.

The Lord restores both livelihood and joy, proving that His redemption is practical as well as spiritual.

and those who move with the flocks

Shepherds and nomadic herders are equally blessed, showing that every vocation thrives under God’s favor.

• Ezekiel 34:23 promises one Shepherd who will tend the flock.

• Micah 5:4 portrays the Messiah shepherding His people “in the strength of the Lord.”

• Psalm 23:1-2 reflects the Shepherd providing green pastures and still waters.

From settled farmers to wandering shepherds, all find provision and protection in the land God restores.

summary

Jeremiah 31:24 paints a vivid picture of total restoration: the covenant people, every city, secure dwelling, flourishing farms, and thriving flocks. God’s promise, taken literally, assures that He will re-establish His people in peace and prosperity. This strengthens faith today, reminding us that the same faithful Lord will fully accomplish every word He has spoken.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) Husbandmen, and they that go forth with flocks.--The prophet's ideal of the restored life of Israel is that it should combine the best features of the patriarchal and the kingly life. A people pastoral, yet not nomadic--agricultural, yet sharing in the culture and safety of cities--this was the picture that rose up in Jeremiah's thoughts, in sharp contrast to the facts that actually surrounded him in the shape of devastated fields and pastures, with no flocks and herds (Jeremiah 4:26-29).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - The ideal of outward life exhibited by the prophets is still the agricultural and pastoral. Jeremiah puts this more forcibly than the Authorized Version represents. Instead of, And there shall dwell in Judah, etc., he says, And there shall dwell therein (viz. in the land) Judah and all his cities together as husbandmen, and they shall go about with flocks, i.e. they shall attend to their ancient pursuits without let or hindrance from invaders (comp. Isaiah 32:20). "Go about" (literally, break up) is the regular word for the periodical journeying of the nomad life.
