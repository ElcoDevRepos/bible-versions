Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
This is the word of the LORD
This phrase emphasizes the divine origin of the message. In the Old Testament, prophets were often the mouthpieces of God, delivering His messages to the people. The phrase underscores the authority and authenticity of the prophecy, indicating that it is not merely human speculation but a revelation from God Himself. This aligns with other prophetic introductions, such as in Isaiah and Ezekiel, where the divine source of the message is highlighted.

that came to Jeremiah the prophet
Jeremiah, known as the "weeping prophet," was called by God to deliver messages of judgment and hope to the people of Judah. His ministry spanned the reigns of several kings, and he often faced opposition and persecution. Jeremiah's role as a prophet was to communicate God's will, often warning of impending judgment due to the people's disobedience. His prophecies are characterized by their emotional depth and personal involvement, reflecting his deep concern for his people.

about the Philistines
The Philistines were a significant group in the ancient Near East, often in conflict with Israel. They occupied the coastal region of Canaan and were known for their military prowess. The Philistines are frequently mentioned in the Old Testament, notably in the stories of Samson and David. This prophecy against them indicates God's judgment on the nations surrounding Israel, reflecting the broader theme of divine sovereignty over all nations.

before Pharaoh struck down Gaza
Gaza was one of the principal cities of the Philistines, located along the Mediterranean coast. The mention of Pharaoh suggests a historical context where Egypt, under Pharaoh's leadership, was exerting influence or military action in the region. This event likely occurred during the late 7th or early 6th century BC, a time of significant geopolitical shifts. The prophecy's timing "before" the event indicates God's foreknowledge and control over historical events, reinforcing the theme of divine sovereignty.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, called by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah and surrounding nations. His ministry spanned over 40 years during a tumultuous period in Israel's history.

2. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who communicates His will and judgments through His prophets.

3. Philistines
An ancient people who were often in conflict with Israel. They inhabited the coastal regions of Canaan, including cities like Gaza.

4. Pharaoh
The title for the king of Egypt. The specific Pharaoh is not named here, but he represents the power of Egypt, a significant force in the region.

5. Gaza
A major city of the Philistines, located on the Mediterranean coast. It was a strategic and fortified city, often mentioned in the context of military campaigns.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nations
The prophecy against the Philistines demonstrates God's control over all nations, not just Israel. He holds every nation accountable for their actions.

The Role of Prophets
Jeremiah's role as a prophet underscores the importance of being faithful in delivering God's message, regardless of the audience or the content of the message.

Judgment and Mercy
While the prophecy speaks of judgment, it also serves as a call to repentance. God's warnings are opportunities for nations and individuals to turn back to Him.

Historical Context and Relevance
Understanding the historical context of these prophecies helps us see the relevance of God's word in our own time. Nations today are still under God's authority and judgment.

The Importance of Obedience
The Philistines' fate serves as a reminder of the consequences of disobedience and rebellion against God. It calls us to examine our own lives and align with God's will.

What Does Jeremiah 47:1 Mean
This is the word of the LORD

– The verse opens by declaring that what follows is not human opinion but divine revelation.

– Such phrasing underscores perfect authority and absolute reliability, echoing 2 Timothy 3:16, “All Scripture is God-breathed,” and 2 Peter 1:21, where prophecy is said to come as men “spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit.”

– Jeremiah regularly introduces messages this way (Jeremiah 1:4; 2:1), reminding readers that every warning or comfort he delivers carries the weight of God’s own voice.

that came to Jeremiah the prophet

– The message arrives through a man specifically called and set apart (Jeremiah 1:5).

– Jeremiah’s faithfulness in relaying hard truths, even under persecution (Jeremiah 20:7–9), models obedience for all who receive God’s word today.

– Amos 3:7 affirms the pattern: “Surely the Lord GOD does nothing without revealing His plan to His servants the prophets,” showing God’s consistent practice of speaking through chosen messengers.

about the Philistines

– God’s concern extends beyond Israel; He addresses surrounding nations, proving His universal sovereignty (Isaiah 14:29–32; Ezekiel 25:15–17).

– The Philistines, long-time foes of Israel (1 Samuel 5; 2 Samuel 5:17–25), are not forgotten by the LORD; their deeds matter to Him, and He will judge righteously.

– This oracle reminds readers that no nation stands outside God’s moral jurisdiction, fulfilling Genesis 18:25, “Will not the Judge of all the earth do right?”

before Pharaoh struck down Gaza

– God time-stamps the prophecy to a real, verifiable event, anchoring it in history and demonstrating His foreknowledge (Isaiah 46:9-10).

– Likely referring to Pharaoh Neco’s campaign prior to Babylon’s dominance (compare Jeremiah 46:2; 2 Kings 24:7), the notation shows the LORD’s alertness to geopolitical shifts that seem, on the surface, unrelated to Israel.

– Other prophecies anticipated calamity for Gaza (Zephaniah 2:4; Amos 1:6-8), reinforcing that God orchestrates the timing and agents of judgment.

summary

Jeremiah 47:1 announces a God-given message, channeled through His proven prophet, targeting the Philistines and dated just before Pharaoh’s assault on Gaza. The verse highlights four truths: the absolute authority of God’s word, the responsibility of His messengers, the reach of His rule over every nation, and His precise control over world events. The LORD speaks, His prophet records, the nations are accountable, and history unfolds exactly as He has ordained.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XLVII.

(1) Against the Philistines.--Here also we have, as in the preceding chapter, a message connected with Jeremiah 25:20. The Gaza of this verse is the Azzah of that, and the date is fixed at a time prior to Necho's attack on that city. Writers who, like Hitzig, identify the Oadytis of Herod. ii. 159, 3:5, with Gaza, suppose his attack to have been made on his return from his victory at Carchemish. The date of the prophecy is thus fixed in the interval between the two events. Ezekiel 25:15 should be compared as a contemporary and parallel prediction.
