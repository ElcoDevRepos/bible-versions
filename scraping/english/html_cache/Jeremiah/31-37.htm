Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
This is what the LORD says:
This phrase introduces a divine proclamation, emphasizing the authority and sovereignty of God. It is a common prophetic formula used to assert that the message is directly from God, not the prophet's own words. This underscores the seriousness and reliability of the message.

Only if the heavens above could be measured
The heavens symbolize the vastness and incomprehensibility of God's creation. In ancient times, the heavens were seen as immeasurable, reflecting the infinite nature of God. This phrase suggests the impossibility of the task, highlighting God's unchanging commitment to His promises.

and the foundations of the earth below searched out
This refers to the depths of the earth, which were considered mysterious and beyond human understanding. The imagery of searching out the earth's foundations conveys the idea of an impossible task, reinforcing the certainty of God's covenant with Israel.

would I reject all of Israel’s descendants
God's covenant with Israel is central to biblical theology. Despite Israel's unfaithfulness, God promises not to utterly reject them. This reflects the theme of divine mercy and faithfulness, as seen in other scriptures like Romans 11:1-2, where Paul affirms that God has not rejected His people.

because of all they have done
This acknowledges Israel's sin and rebellion, which are well-documented throughout the Old Testament. Despite their actions, God's grace prevails. This echoes the theme of redemption and forgiveness found throughout the Bible, pointing to the ultimate redemption through Christ.

declares the LORD.
The repetition of this phrase bookends the passage, reinforcing the divine origin and authority of the message. It serves as a reminder of God's ultimate control and the certainty of His promises, encouraging trust and faith in His word.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who speaks through the prophet Jeremiah, affirming His unchanging commitment to His people.

2. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his messages of both judgment and hope to the people of Judah during a time of great turmoil and impending exile.

3. Israel
The descendants of Jacob, representing God's chosen people, who are assured of God's enduring covenant despite their transgressions.

4. Heavens and Earth
Symbolic representations of the vastness and complexity of God's creation, used here to illustrate the impossibility of God rejecting Israel.

5. Covenant Context
The broader context of Jeremiah 31, which includes the promise of a new covenant, highlighting God's plan for redemption and restoration.
Teaching Points
God's Unfailing Commitment
Despite Israel's failures, God's covenant promises remain unbroken. This reassures believers of God's steadfast love and faithfulness.

The Impossibility of Measuring God's Creation
The imagery of measuring the heavens and earth serves to remind us of God's infinite power and wisdom, encouraging trust in His divine plan.

Hope in Restoration
Even in times of judgment, God provides hope for restoration. This encourages believers to seek repentance and trust in God's redemptive work.

The New Covenant Fulfillment
Jeremiah's prophecy points to the new covenant in Christ, inviting believers to live in the reality of this fulfilled promise through faith and obedience.

God's Sovereignty and Mystery
The passage invites reflection on the mystery of God's ways, urging believers to embrace humility and awe in their relationship with Him.

What Does Jeremiah 31:37 Mean
This is what the LORD says

- The prophet introduces a direct oracle, signaling absolute authority and certainty (Jeremiah 1:9; Isaiah 40:8).

- The speaker is the covenant-keeping LORD who has just promised the new covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34), so every word that follows rests on His unchanging character (Malachi 3:6; Hebrews 6:17-18).

Only if the heavens above could be measured

- Measuring the heavens was—and remains—humanly impossible (Job 38:31-33; Psalm 147:4-5).

- God uses the immeasurable sky to illustrate an unattainable condition, underscoring the permanence of His commitment to Israel (Genesis 15:5; Jeremiah 33:22).

- The image invites awe: as the stars exceed human calculation, so God’s faithfulness exceeds human limits (Psalm 103:11).

And the foundations of the earth below searched out

- “Foundations” evoke the hidden depths no one can fully explore (Job 38:16-18; Proverbs 8:29).

- Pairing heaven’s vastness with earth’s depths creates a total picture of the created order, reinforcing that the condition for God’s rejection is beyond reach (Psalm 89:11; Colossians 1:16-17).

- God ties His promise to the very architecture of creation, just as He did with Noahic stability (Isaiah 54:9-10).

Would I reject all of Israel’s descendants

- The verb points to absolute rejection—something God asserts He will never do under normal conditions (Leviticus 26:44-45; Romans 11:1-2, 28-29).

- “All of Israel’s descendants” confirms a national scope, assuring continued ethnic and covenant identity (Genesis 17:7; Jeremiah 46:27-28).

- This pledge safeguards every preceding promise: land, seed, blessing, and now the new covenant (Genesis 12:2-3; Ezekiel 37:21-28).

Because of all they have done, declares the LORD

- Israel’s sin is not minimized; judgment had fallen (Jeremiah 29:14; Lamentations 1:8).

- Yet even accumulated rebellion cannot nullify divine grace once the stipulated impossible conditions are set (Psalm 106:43-45; Hosea 14:4).

- The clause magnifies mercy: God acknowledges the offense yet reaffirms irrevocable election (Deuteronomy 4:30-31; 2 Timothy 2:13).

summary

Jeremiah 31:37 anchors God’s promise to Israel in the very fabric of creation. Unless humanity can chart the limitless heavens or probe earth’s deepest foundations—an impossibility—God will never cast off the nation He chose. Their sin invited discipline, but not extinction. The verse therefore guarantees the enduring place of Israel in God’s redemptive plan, testifying to His unfailing faithfulness and the certainty of every covenant pledge.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) If heaven above can be measured . . .--The thought of the preceding verse is reproduced with a slight modification of meaning. Over and above the idea, as stated above, that the stability of nature is a parable of the steadfastness of God's laws and purposes in the spiritual world, there is implied a feeling, like that of Romans 11:33, that man's finite intellect cannot fathom His modes of working out that purpose any more than it can measure what to the prophet's mind were the illimitable heaven and the unfathomable earth.
