Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
As for you
This phrase is directed specifically to the prophet Jeremiah, indicating a personal command from God. Jeremiah, as a prophet, often served as an intermediary between God and the people of Judah. This direct address underscores the gravity of the message and the unique role Jeremiah plays in delivering God's word.

do not pray for these people
God instructs Jeremiah not to intercede for the people of Judah. This is significant because intercessory prayer was a common practice among prophets. The command suggests that the people's sins have reached a point where divine judgment is inevitable. This reflects a broader biblical theme where persistent disobedience leads to a withdrawal of divine favor, as seen in other instances like 1 Samuel 15:35, where God regrets making Saul king.

do not offer a plea or petition on their behalf
The repetition of the command emphasizes the seriousness of the situation. Pleas and petitions were forms of supplication that prophets and priests would make to seek God's mercy. The prohibition indicates that the people's actions have severed their relationship with God to such an extent that even the prophet's intercession is unwelcome.

and do not beg Me
This further intensifies the prohibition against intercession. Begging implies a deep, earnest appeal, often made in desperation. The use of this term highlights the finality of God's decision regarding the people's fate. It reflects a moment where divine patience has been exhausted, similar to the hardening of Pharaoh's heart in Exodus.

for I will not listen to you
God's refusal to listen signifies a judicial hardening, where the opportunity for repentance has passed. This echoes other biblical instances where God chooses not to hear prayers due to persistent sin, such as in Isaiah 1:15. It serves as a sobering reminder of the consequences of turning away from God. This phrase also foreshadows the ultimate judgment that will come upon Judah, leading to the Babylonian exile.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of warning and judgment to the people of Judah. His ministry was marked by deep personal struggle and opposition from his own people.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which had fallen into idolatry and disobedience to God. The people of Judah were the primary audience of Jeremiah's prophecies.

3. The Temple
The central place of worship in Jerusalem, which had become a symbol of false security for the people who believed that God's presence there would protect them despite their sins.

4. Idolatry
The worship of false gods and idols, which was rampant in Judah and a primary reason for God's impending judgment.

5. God's Judgment
The divine response to the persistent sin and rebellion of Judah, which included the eventual destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple by the Babylonians.
Teaching Points
The Limits of Intercession
While intercessory prayer is powerful, there are times when God may instruct us to refrain from praying for certain outcomes, especially when His judgment is set due to persistent sin.

The Consequences of Idolatry
Idolatry leads to spiritual blindness and separation from God. We must examine our lives for modern-day idols that may hinder our relationship with Him.

The Importance of Genuine Repentance
God desires a heart that is truly repentant. Rituals and religious practices are empty without a sincere turning away from sin.

Understanding God's Justice
God's refusal to hear prayers in this context highlights His justice. It serves as a reminder that God is holy and cannot tolerate sin indefinitely.

The Role of the Prophet
Jeremiah's role was to deliver God's message, even when it was difficult. We are called to speak truth in love, even when it is unpopular or challenging.

What Does Jeremiah 7:16 Mean
As for you

Jeremiah, the faithful prophet, is singled out. God distinguishes him from the rest of Judah’s population.

• This personal address underscores responsibility: when God reveals judgment, His servants must relay it without compromise (Ezekiel 3:17; Amos 3:7).

• The phrase also comforts the obedient remnant; even amid national rebellion, God still speaks personally to His own (2 Kings 19:20; Malachi 3:16-17).

• For believers today, the call is similar—stand apart in obedience while culture drifts (Romans 12:2).

do not pray for these people

A startling prohibition: intercession is withheld.

• Normally, God urges prayer for others (1 Samuel 12:23; 1 Timothy 2:1-3). Here, however, persistent sin cancels that privilege (Isaiah 1:15).

• Judah had repeatedly rejected prophetic warnings (Jeremiah 6:16-17; 7:13). Continued intercession would suggest their hearts might yet soften, but God declares the opposite—they are fixed in rebellion (Proverbs 29:1).

• This boundary emphasizes divine holiness: mercy is abundant, yet not limitless when repentance is refused (Genesis 6:3).

do not offer a plea or petition on their behalf

God widens the ban: no supplication of any kind.

• “Plea” points to asking for favor; “petition” implies a formal request. Both are futile here because judgment is already decreed (Jeremiah 14:11-12).

• Such finality mirrors moments with Saul (1 Samuel 15:35-16:1) and with Israel at Kadesh-barnea (Numbers 14:11-23).

• The lesson: continual refusal to heed God’s voice can seal a verdict that even godly intercessors may not overturn (Hebrews 3:7-13).

and do not beg Me

Intense language—no earnest imploring.

• God’s patience has a terminus when sin is willful and prolonged (2 Chronicles 36:15-16).

• Begging assumes there is still room for negotiation, but here God’s decision is irrevocable (Lamentations 3:42-44).

• This underscores the seriousness of apostasy: grace spurned grows into wrath stored (Romans 2:4-5).

for I will not listen to you

Divine refusal is explicit.

• The issue is not Jeremiah’s standing—God loves and listens to him elsewhere (Jeremiah 15:1). Rather, the subject of prayer is closed.

• Similar refusals appear with those cherishing sin (Psalm 66:18), practicing injustice (Micah 3:4), or treating repentance lightly (Proverbs 1:24-28).

• God remains sovereign over prayer; when He says “no,” His glory and justice are upheld (Job 40:1-8; 2 Corinthians 12:8-9).

summary

Jeremiah 7:16 reveals a sobering boundary: persistent, unrepentant rebellion can reach a point where God forbids further intercession. The passage exalts God’s holiness, warns against hard-heartedness, and reminds believers to respond promptly to conviction. While God delights in mercy, He also executes justice; therefore, heed His Word today and urge others to do the same while the door of grace remains open.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Pray not thou.--The words imply that a prayer of intercession, like that which Moses had offered of old (Exodus 32:10), was rising up in the heart of the prophet. He is told that he must check it. Judgment must have its way. The discipline must be left to do its work. A like impulse met by a like repression is found in Jeremiah 11:14; Jeremiah 14:11. It is obvious that the utterance of the conflict between his human affections and the Divine will made the sentence which he pronounced more terrible than ever.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 16-20. - The hypocrisy of the worship of Jehovah proved; its punishment. Verse 16. - Pray not thou for this people. Abraham prayed for Sodom (Genesis 18:23-32); Moses and Samuel for Israel (Exodus 32:11-14; Exodus 17:11; Numbers 14:13-20; Psalm 106:23; 1 Samuel 7:9, 10; 1 Samuel 12:17, 18, 23); and Jeremiah would fain perform the same pious duty to his people. We have a specimen of his intercession in Jeremiah 14:19-22 (comp. Jeremiah 18:20), followed immediately by a rejection of his prayer, parallel in thought to the present passage. Verbal parallels are Jeremiah 11:14; Jeremiah 14:11. Cry; i.e. cry for help (see on Jeremiah 14:12); parallel with "prayer," as Jeremiah 11:14; Psalm 17:1 61:1.
