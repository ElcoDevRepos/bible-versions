Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
O land, land, land
The repetition of "land" emphasizes the urgency and importance of the message. In biblical literature, repetition is often used to draw attention to a critical point. The land here refers to the nation of Judah, which is facing impending judgment due to its disobedience and idolatry. This echoes the covenantal relationship between God and Israel, where the land is a significant aspect of the promise given to Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3). The land is not just a physical territory but represents the people and their spiritual state. The triple repetition can also be seen as a call to the entire nation, including its leaders and inhabitants, to heed the warning.

hear the word of the LORD!
This phrase is a direct call to listen and obey the divine message. In the prophetic tradition, the "word of the LORD" is authoritative and demands a response. The prophets were God's messengers, and their words carried the weight of divine authority. In the context of Jeremiah, this call to hear is a plea for repentance and a return to covenant faithfulness. The concept of hearing in Hebrew culture goes beyond mere auditory reception; it implies obedience and action (Deuteronomy 6:4-5). This call to hear is reminiscent of the Shema, where Israel is commanded to listen and love the LORD with all their heart. The urgency of this message is underscored by the dire consequences of ignoring God's word, as seen in the subsequent Babylonian exile.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, called by God to deliver messages of warning and hope to the people of Judah. His ministry spanned the reigns of several kings, and he is known for his lamentations over Jerusalem's impending destruction.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which was facing imminent judgment due to its persistent idolatry and injustice. Jeremiah's prophecies were directed primarily at this kingdom.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is calling the land to listen to His word. This emphasizes His authority and the seriousness of the message being delivered.

4. The Land
Symbolically represents the people of Judah and their society. The repetition of "land" underscores the urgency and importance of the message.

5. The Word of the LORD
The divine message delivered through Jeremiah, calling for repentance and warning of consequences if the people do not heed God's commands.
Teaching Points
The Urgency of God's Word
The repetition of "land" three times signifies the critical nature of the message. We must prioritize listening to God's word in our lives, recognizing its authority and urgency.

God's Sovereignty Over Creation
By addressing the land, God demonstrates His sovereignty over all creation. We are reminded that everything is under His control and must respond to His commands.

Call to Repentance
The context of Jeremiah's message is a call to repentance. We should examine our lives for areas where we need to turn back to God and align with His will.

The Role of Prophets
Jeremiah's role as a prophet highlights the importance of those who speak God's truth. We should value and heed the messages of those who faithfully proclaim God's word today.

The Consequences of Disobedience
The warnings in Jeremiah serve as a reminder of the consequences of ignoring God's word. We must take seriously the call to live according to His commands to avoid spiritual and moral decline.

What Does Jeremiah 22:29 Mean
O land

The opening cry “O” pulls the reader in with urgency and affection. God is not speaking into a vacuum; He is addressing a real, physical realm that He Himself created (Genesis 1:1).

• The exclamation shows the Creator’s deep concern for what happens in His creation, echoing Isaiah 1:2—“Hear, O heavens, and give ear, O earth.”

• It reminds us that the Lord’s warnings are always rooted in love (2 Peter 3:9).

• Like Deuteronomy 30:19, the appeal places responsibility on the hearer: life and death are set before the land.

land

The first repetition intensifies the plea. Repetition in Scripture is God’s highlighter.

• The land had been blessed with covenant privileges (Exodus 3:17) yet was sliding into covenant curses (Deuteronomy 28:15-68).

• Jeremiah had already warned that the soil itself would feel the weight of judgment (Jeremiah 12:4, 10-11).

• This second call is a gracious pause, offering space for repentance before consequences fall, much like Joel 2:12-13.

land

A third call underlines finality—time is running out.

• The ground that once “flowed with milk and honey” (Exodus 13:5) is about to become desolate (Jeremiah 25:11).

• Romans 8:22 pictures creation groaning under sin; here the groaning is about to turn into devastation if the people remain stubborn.

• God’s tripled appeal recalls Jesus’ thrice-repeated “Simon, son of John” (John 21:15-17), showing that repetition is mercy before reckoning.

hear the word of the Lord!

Everything hinges on hearing and obeying.

• “Hear” in Scripture always implies doing (James 1:22).

• The “word of the Lord” is living and active (Hebrews 4:12); ignoring it invites disaster, while embracing it brings life (Psalm 119:25).

• Jeremiah’s generation refused to listen (Jeremiah 22:21), yet the invitation extends to every generation (Matthew 7:24-27).

Practical takeaways:

– Keep a soft heart; the same word that judges can heal (Hosea 14:4).

– Let Scripture, not culture, shape response (Colossians 2:8).

– Act promptly; delayed obedience is disobedience (Psalm 119:60).

summary

Jeremiah 22:29 is a fourfold wake-up call. God urgently addresses His land, repeats the summons to underscore the danger, and commands immediate attention to His unchanging word. The verse reminds us that the whole created order is accountable to its Maker, that repetition is mercy, and that life or ruin turns on one simple issue: Will we truly hear and obey the word of the Lord?

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(29) O earth, earth, earth.--The solemnity of the mystic threefold repetition expresses the certainty of the Divine decree (comp. Jeremiah 7:4). So in our Lord's most solemn utterances we have the twice-repeated "Simon, Simon" (Luke 22:31), and the recurring "Verily, verily" of St. John's Gospel (John 8:51 et al.).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - O earth, earth, earth. The repetition is for solemnity's sake (comp. Jeremiah 7:4).
