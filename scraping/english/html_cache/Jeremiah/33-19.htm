And the word of the LORD came to Jeremiah:
This phrase indicates divine communication, a common occurrence in the prophetic books of the Old Testament. Jeremiah, a major prophet, often received messages from God to deliver to the people of Judah. This underscores the role of prophets as intermediaries between God and His people. The phrase "the word of the LORD" emphasizes the authority and divine origin of the message, distinguishing it from human opinion or speculation. In the context of Jeremiah's ministry, this communication often involved warnings of judgment and promises of restoration. The phrase also connects to other instances in the Bible where God speaks directly to His prophets, such as with Isaiah, Ezekiel, and Daniel, reinforcing the continuity of God's revelation throughout the Scriptures.
Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his prophecies concerning the destruction and eventual restoration of Jerusalem. He is often called the "weeping prophet" due to his deep sorrow over the sins of his people and the coming judgment.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who communicates His will and promises through His prophets. In this context, He is reaffirming His covenant promises to His people.

3. The Word of the LORD
This phrase signifies divine revelation. It is the message that God imparts to His prophets, which they are to deliver to the people.

4. Jerusalem
The capital city of Judah, which is central to the prophecies of Jeremiah. It is both the site of impending judgment and the future hope of restoration.

5. The Covenant
Although not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the context of Jeremiah 33 involves God's covenant with David and the promise of a righteous Branch, pointing to the Messiah.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness
God's word to Jeremiah underscores His unwavering faithfulness to His promises. Despite the circumstances, God remains true to His covenant.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Jeremiah serve as God's mouthpieces, delivering messages of both judgment and hope. Their role is crucial in guiding God's people back to Him.

Hope in Restoration
Even in times of despair, God provides hope for restoration. This is a reminder that God's plans for His people are ultimately for their good and His glory.

The Importance of Covenant
Understanding God's covenants is key to grasping the overarching account of the Bible. They reveal God's character and His redemptive plan through history.

Application of God's Word
Just as Jeremiah received the word of the LORD, believers today are called to receive and apply God's word in their lives, trusting in His promises.

What Does Jeremiah 33:19 Mean
And the word

“Word” signals God’s personal, living revelation, not a vague impression. As in Genesis 15:1, 1 Samuel 3:21, and Isaiah 55:11, the Lord speaks with the same authority that created the universe.

• Scripture reports this activity repeatedly (Hebrews 1:1–2), underscoring that God still chooses verbal communication to disclose His purposes.

• Because the Word is living and active (Hebrews 4:12), what follows is trustworthy and demands attention.

of the LORD

The phrase points to Yahweh, the covenant-keeping God who never changes (Exodus 3:14; Malachi 3:6).

• His covenant name anchors the message in His unchanging character.

• It reminds us that the promises about to be given are as sure as the One giving them (Numbers 23:19).

• Since He is holy and truthful (Psalm 99:3; John 17:17), the word recorded here is infallible and must be embraced literally.

came to

God initiates; Jeremiah does not conjure or imagine the revelation (Jeremiah 1:4; Amos 3:7).

• The verb “came” pictures the Word pursuing the prophet, showing divine eagerness to make Himself known (Isaiah 65:1).

• This underscores grace: the Lord stoops to speak, just as He later “sent His word and healed them” (Psalm 107:20).

• 2 Peter 1:21 confirms that such coming results in prophecy carried along by the Holy Spirit.

Jeremiah

The specific recipient matters. Jeremiah, called from the womb (Jeremiah 1:5), stands as a proven, persevering prophet (Jeremiah 20:9).

• His track record—warning of judgment (Jeremiah 7:34) and affirming hope (Jeremiah 31:31)—proves his reliability.

• God’s choice of Jeremiah ensures the message will be declared despite opposition (Jeremiah 15:20-21).

• Through this faithful messenger, the Lord will unveil the everlasting covenant promises that follow (Jeremiah 33:20-26).

summary

Jeremiah 33:19 affirms that the living, covenant-keeping God took the initiative to deliver an authoritative, trustworthy message through His chosen prophet. The verse anchors the promises that follow in God’s unchanging character and reminds us that every word He speaks is literal, reliable, and demands our confidence and obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19-22) And the word of the Lord came unto Jeremiah, saying . . .--The new introduction here and in Jeremiah 33:23 indicates a fresh message borne in on the prophet's mind after an interval of time. In substance it repeats the promise of Jeremiah 33:17-18, but it reproduces them with yet greater solemnity. The covenant of Jehovah with David and with the Levites the priests is placed on the same level of permanence as the ordered succession of day and night. If the old order ultimately gave way to the new, it was only because the new was the transfigured and glorified reproduction of the old. Whatever may have been the thoughts of the prophet, we are authorised in looking for the seed of David and of the Levites in those who, by virtue of their union with Christ, are made both kings and priests unto the Father (Revelation 1:6). Just as the promise to the seed of Abraham is fulfilled in those who are spiritually the children of the faith of Abraham (Romans 9:7-8), so in this sense only can it be true that the seed of David and the Levites shall out number the host of the heaven and the sand of the sea.
