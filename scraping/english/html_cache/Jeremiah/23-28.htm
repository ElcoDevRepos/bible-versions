Let the prophet who has a dream retell it
In the context of Jeremiah's time, many false prophets claimed to have dreams and visions from God. These dreams were often self-serving and misleading, not aligned with God's true message. The cultural context of the ancient Near East placed significant value on dreams as a means of divine communication, but this was often exploited by false prophets. The Bible frequently warns against false prophets (Deuteronomy 13:1-5, Matthew 7:15), emphasizing the need for discernment. This phrase suggests that while dreams can be shared, they must be weighed against the truth of God's word.

but let him who has My word speak it truthfully
This emphasizes the importance of fidelity to God's message. In contrast to the false prophets, Jeremiah was called to deliver God's word accurately, regardless of personal cost or public reception. The historical context shows that true prophets often faced persecution (Jeremiah 20:1-2, 1 Kings 22:27). The call to speak truthfully aligns with the broader biblical theme of truth (John 17:17) and the responsibility of those who are entrusted with God's word (James 3:1).

For what is straw compared to grain?”
This metaphor contrasts the emptiness of false prophecies (straw) with the nourishing and sustaining truth of God's word (grain). Straw, while part of the harvest, is ultimately useless for sustenance, symbolizing the empty promises of false prophets. Grain, on the other hand, represents the life-giving and sustaining power of God's word (Matthew 4:4). This imagery is consistent with other biblical passages that use agricultural metaphors to distinguish between true and false teachings (Matthew 13:24-30).

declares the LORD
This phrase underscores the authority and finality of God's pronouncement. Throughout the Bible, when God declares something, it is a statement of divine authority and truth (Isaiah 55:11). The use of "declares the LORD" serves as a reminder that the message comes from God Himself, not from human invention or interpretation. This reinforces the call to heed God's word above all else, as His declarations are trustworthy and eternal (Psalm 119:89).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, called by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah. He is known for his prophecies of judgment and calls for repentance.

2. False Prophets
Individuals in Jeremiah's time who claimed to speak for God but delivered messages that were not from Him. They often spoke of peace and prosperity contrary to God's true warnings.

3. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel during Jeremiah's ministry. It was facing impending judgment due to its disobedience and idolatry.

4. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who speaks through His true prophets and contrasts His word with the falsehoods of the false prophets.

5. Dreams and Visions
Common means through which prophets claimed to receive divine messages. In this context, they are contrasted with the true word of God.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Truthful Speech
God's word is to be spoken truthfully and faithfully. Believers are called to discern and uphold the truth in a world filled with falsehoods.

Discernment Between True and False Messages
Just as grain is more valuable than straw, God's true word is more valuable than false dreams. Christians must seek discernment through prayer and study of the Scriptures.

The Responsibility of Those Who Speak for God
Those who claim to speak for God must ensure they are conveying His true message, not their own ideas or desires.

The Value of God's Word
God's word is likened to grain, which sustains and nourishes. Believers should prioritize the study and application of Scripture in their lives.

The Consequences of False Prophecy
False teachings can lead people astray and have serious spiritual consequences. The church must guard against such influences.

What Does Jeremiah 23:28 Mean
Let the prophet who has a dream retell it

“Let the prophet who has a dream retell it” (Jeremiah 23:28a).

• God acknowledges that some claim revelations through dreams (Jeremiah 23:25–27).

• He permits them to speak, yet the phrase “retell it” underscores their limited authority; they may only share what they have experienced, nothing more (cf. Deuteronomy 13:1-3; 1 Kings 22:22).

• Dreams can be real, but they are fallible and easily distorted by human imagination (Ecclesiastes 5:7).

• The Lord’s tolerance here is not endorsement; it exposes the difference between human impressions and divine revelation.

But let him who has My word speak it truthfully

“but let him who has My word speak it truthfully” (Jeremiah 23:28b).

• “My word” points to Scripture’s inspiration and inerrancy (2 Timothy 3:16; 2 Peter 1:19-21).

• Truthful speaking involves:

– Accuracy—no alteration or embellishment (Proverbs 30:5-6).

– Boldness—declaring the whole counsel of God despite opposition (Acts 20:27; Jeremiah 26:2).

– Integrity—living consistently with what is proclaimed (James 1:22).

• God’s Word must override every other source of guidance (Isaiah 8:20; Galatians 1:8).

• The contrast invites listeners to weigh messages by Scripture rather than by charisma or novelty (1 Thessalonians 5:20-21).

For what is straw compared to grain?

“For what is straw compared to grain?” declares the LORD (Jeremiah 23:28c).

• Straw: lightweight, combustible, nutritionally empty—symbolizing human dreams and false prophecies (Isaiah 44:20).

• Grain: substantial, nourishing, life-sustaining—symbolizing God’s Word (Matthew 4:4).

• The imagery stresses:

– Value: only grain feeds; straw cannot (Psalm 19:7-10).

– Permanence: straw burns; grain endures (1 Peter 1:24-25).

– Judgment: straw will be consumed in God’s fire (Jeremiah 23:29; 1 Corinthians 3:12-13).

• The Lord invites His people to discern the life-giving message from chaff (Psalm 1:4; Luke 3:17).

summary

Jeremiah 23:28 draws a sharp line between humanly sourced dreams and the divinely given Word. God allows dreamers to speak, but His command is that those entrusted with Scripture proclaim it faithfully. Like straw versus grain, only the pure Word possesses substance, nourishment, and eternal worth. Believers are called to prize, proclaim, and live by that unfailing Word, testing every message against it and finding their spiritual sustenance in its truth.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) Let him tell a dream.--The point of the words lies in the contrast between the real and the counterfeit revelation. Let the dreamer tell his dream as such, let the prophet speak the word of Jehovah truly, and then it will be seen that the one is as the chaff and stubble, and the other as the wheat--one worthless, the other sustaining life. What have they in common? What has one to do with the other?

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - Let him tell a dream; rather, let him tell it as a dream; let him tell his dreams, if he will, but not intermix them with Divine revelations. Jeremiah, then, does not deny that there is a measure of truth in what these prophets say; he only demands a distinct declaration that their dreams are but dreams, and not equal in authority to the Divine word. For, as he continues, What is the chaff to the wheat? What right have you to mix the worthless chaff with the pure, winnowed grain? How, he implies, can such an adulterated message produce the designed effect of a prophetic revelation? (St. Paul has a somewhat similar figure, 1 Corinthians 3:10-13.) So Naegelsbach. Keil, however, denies that there is any thought of an adulteration of the Divine word by the "false prophets." According to him, the question in this verse is simply meant to emphasize the contrast between the false, dream-born prophecy of Jeremiah's opponents and the true revelations. How can the false prophecy pretend to be the true? They are as different as chaff and wheat. Both views are admissible. Naegelsbach introduces a new element by suggesting the intermixture of false and true in the utterances of the "false prophets;" but his view is not inconsistent with what the prophet has stated before, and it is favored by ver. 30 and by the command, Let him speak my word faithfully; i.e. in its genuine form; comp. Jeremiah 2:21, "A faithful or trustworthy [i.e. a genuine] seed;" also, for the general sense, 2 Corinthians 2:17.
