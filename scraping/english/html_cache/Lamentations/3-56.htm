You heard my plea:
This phrase reflects the personal and intimate relationship between the speaker and God. In the context of Lamentations, traditionally attributed to the prophet Jeremiah, it underscores the belief that God is attentive to the cries of His people. The historical backdrop is the Babylonian siege and destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BC, a time of immense suffering and despair. The plea signifies a deep trust in God's ability to hear and respond, reminiscent of other biblical instances where God hears the cries of His people, such as in Exodus 2:24, where God hears the groaning of the Israelites in Egypt.

Do not ignore my cry for relief:
This part of the verse is a direct appeal for God's intervention and mercy. The cultural context of ancient Israel placed a strong emphasis on communal lament and individual supplication during times of distress. The plea for relief is a common theme in the Psalms, such as Psalm 28:2, where the psalmist asks God not to be deaf to his cry. Theologically, this reflects the human condition of dependence on divine grace and the hope for deliverance. It also foreshadows the New Testament understanding of Jesus as the ultimate source of relief and salvation, as seen in Matthew 11:28, where Jesus invites those who are weary to come to Him for rest.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
Traditionally considered the author of Lamentations, Jeremiah is known as the "weeping prophet." He laments the destruction of Jerusalem and the suffering of his people.

2. Jerusalem
The city that has been destroyed by the Babylonians, leading to the lament and sorrow expressed in the book.

3. Babylonian Exile
The event during which the Israelites were taken captive by the Babylonians, leading to the context of the lament.
Teaching Points
God Hears Our Cries
Just as God heard Jeremiah's plea, He hears our cries for help and relief. We can be confident that our prayers reach Him, even in our darkest moments.

Persistent Prayer
The verse encourages believers to persist in prayer, trusting that God is attentive to our needs and concerns.

God's Compassion
The plea for God not to close His ears highlights His compassionate nature. We are reminded of His willingness to listen and respond to our suffering.

Faith in Times of Distress
In times of distress, our faith is tested. This verse encourages us to maintain our faith and trust in God's ability to deliver us.

Relief Through Relationship
Our relationship with God is central to finding relief. By maintaining a close relationship with Him, we can experience His peace and comfort.

What Does Lamentations 3:56 Mean
You heard my plea

– The writer looks back and affirms, “You heard my plea” (Lamentations 3:56). That little word “heard” is loaded with hope.

• It means God actually listened, not just noticed. Psalm 34:17 echoes the same confidence: “The righteous cry out, and the LORD hears.”

• It proves their relationship is intact. Even in the wreckage of Jerusalem, the covenant God still bends His ear (Psalm 116:1-2).

• It silences despair. If the Lord has heard once, He will hear again (Micah 7:7).

• It motivates continued prayer. Hebrews 4:16 invites believers to “approach the throne of grace with confidence,” precisely because He is a listening God.

• It reminds us that hearing is followed by action. Psalm 34:4 links the two: “I sought the LORD, and He answered me; He delivered me from all my fears.”

Do not ignore my cry for relief

– The second half is still a plea: “Do not ignore my cry for relief.” The prayer hasn’t ended; faith keeps talking.

• Honest lament: Like Psalm 28:1, “do not be deaf to me,” the speaker admits he feels the need for reassurance.

• Persistence encouraged: Jesus commends perseverance in Luke 18:1-8. The petitioner here does the same—refusing silence until help comes.

• Confidence in God’s character: Isaiah 59:1 reminds us, “Surely the arm of the LORD is not too short to save, nor His ear too dull to hear.”

• A pattern for us: When affliction presses, James 5:13 simply says, “Is any one of you suffering? Let him pray.”

• Future-focused hope: Psalm 102:17 promises, “He will not despise their plea.” The writer in Lamentations banks on that pledge.

summary

Lamentations 3:56 models a faith that remembers and a faith that still asks. First, the sufferer testifies, “You heard my plea,” anchoring his heart in God’s proven attentiveness. Then he continues, “Do not ignore my cry for relief,” showing that previous answers fuel present petitions. Together the two lines invite us to do the same: recall God’s past faithfulness, approach Him again with confidence, and expect His ear—and His hand—to move.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(56) Thou hast heard . . . hide not thine . . .--There is something eminently suggestive in the sequence of the two clauses. The recollection that prayer was answered in the past, prompts its utterance in the present. Historically, the words may point to the intervention of Ebed-melech in Jeremiah 38:7.

At my breathing--i.e., the "sighs" or "sobs" of the mourner.
