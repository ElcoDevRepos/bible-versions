So Moses and Eleazar the priest
Moses, the leader of the Israelites, and Eleazar, the son of Aaron and the high priest, are central figures in this passage. Moses represents the civil authority, while Eleazar represents the religious authority. This dual leadership reflects the theocratic nature of Israelite society, where governance and worship were intertwined. Eleazar's role as high priest is significant, as he succeeded his father Aaron and was responsible for maintaining the spiritual purity of the people. This partnership between Moses and Eleazar underscores the importance of both civil and religious obedience to God's commands.

did as the LORD had commanded Moses
This phrase emphasizes the obedience of Moses and Eleazar to God's instructions. In the context of Numbers 31, God commanded the Israelites to take vengeance on the Midianites, who had led them into idolatry and immorality (Numbers 25). The obedience of Moses and Eleazar is a recurring theme in the Pentateuch, highlighting the importance of following divine directives. This obedience is also a type of Christ, who perfectly fulfilled the will of the Father (John 6:38). The phrase serves as a reminder of the necessity of adhering to God's commands, as disobedience often led to dire consequences for the Israelites.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites, chosen by God to deliver His people from Egypt and guide them through the wilderness. In this passage, Moses is executing God's commands regarding the division of the spoils of war.

2. Eleazar the Priest
The son of Aaron and the high priest of Israel at this time. Eleazar plays a crucial role in religious leadership and in ensuring that the commands of God are followed precisely.

3. The LORD's Command
The directive given by God to Moses concerning the distribution of the spoils taken from the Midianites. This command reflects God's sovereignty and His concern for justice and order among His people.

4. The Israelites
The people of God, who are in the process of conquering the land promised to them. This event is part of their journey and preparation to enter the Promised Land.

5. The Midianites
A nomadic people who opposed Israel. The conflict with the Midianites is part of the broader account of Israel's journey to the Promised Land and their encounters with various nations.
Teaching Points
Obedience to God's Commands
Moses and Eleazar's actions demonstrate the importance of following God's instructions precisely. This obedience is a model for believers today, emphasizing the need to adhere to God's Word in all aspects of life.

Role of Spiritual Leadership
The involvement of Eleazar highlights the role of spiritual leaders in guiding and ensuring the community's faithfulness to God's commands. This underscores the importance of godly leadership in the church.

God's Sovereignty and Justice
The division of the spoils as commanded by God reflects His sovereign rule and His concern for justice and order. Believers are reminded of God's ultimate authority and His righteous standards.

Community and Stewardship
The distribution of spoils among the community illustrates principles of stewardship and communal responsibility. Christians are called to manage resources wisely and share with others in need.

Faithfulness in Small and Large Matters
The meticulous obedience in dividing the spoils shows that faithfulness in small tasks is as important as in larger ones. Believers are encouraged to be diligent in all areas of life.

What Does Numbers 31:31 Mean
So

- The little word links us back to the previous instructions (Numbers 31:25-30) where God detailed how the Midianite plunder was to be divided.

- “So” signals immediate follow-through—there is no gap between revelation and response, echoing Genesis 6:22 where “Noah did everything that God commanded him.”

Moses

- God’s chosen leader (Exodus 3:10) steps into action yet again.

- Moses models consistent faithfulness; Hebrews 3:5 commends him as “faithful in all God’s house.”

- His involvement displays that leadership in Scripture is servant-oriented, accountable first to God, then to the people.

and Eleazar the priest

- Eleazar, successor to Aaron (Numbers 20:28), represents priestly mediation.

- His presence shows that civil and spiritual leaders work together in Israel, a pattern repeated in Joshua 14:1 where Joshua and Eleazar allot the land.

- Partnership safeguards obedience: two witnesses ensure the command is carried out precisely (Deuteronomy 17:6).

did

- Obedience moves from hearing to doing—“Be doers of the word and not hearers only” (James 1:22).

- Action validates faith; compare John 2:5 where servants fill the jars “just as” Jesus said and witness the miracle.

- The verb is singular in purpose—unity of effort: Moses and Eleazar act with one heart.

as the LORD

- Yahweh remains the ultimate authority; His name guarantees the righteousness of the command (Psalm 19:9).

- Every directive carries divine backing; Deuteronomy 12:32 warns not to add or subtract.

- The text keeps God central, preventing any credit from shifting solely to human leaders.

had commanded Moses

- The chain of command is clear: from the LORD to Moses, then to the people (Exodus 40:16).

- Repeated phrasing throughout the Pentateuch (“just as the LORD commanded Moses”) underscores trustworthiness and consistency (Numbers 27:21).

- God’s word is final; Moses’ role is transmission, not negotiation—an enduring lesson on prophetic responsibility.

summary

Numbers 31:31 captures a snapshot of seamless obedience: God speaks, Moses receives, spiritual leadership unites, and action follows without delay. It reminds us that blessed outcomes flow when leaders and people alike align their steps “just as the LORD commanded.”
