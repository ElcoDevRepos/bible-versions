and these were their names:
This phrase introduces the list of the twelve spies sent by Moses to explore the land of Canaan. Naming individuals in biblical texts often signifies the importance of their roles and the historical accuracy of the account. Names in the Bible frequently carry meanings that reflect the character or destiny of the person.

From the tribe of Reuben,
Reuben was the firstborn son of Jacob and Leah. The tribe of Reuben held a significant position as the firstborn, though Reuben himself lost his birthright due to his transgression (Genesis 49:3-4). The tribe settled east of the Jordan River, an area known for its pastoral land, which aligns with their request to Moses for this territory (Numbers 32:1-5).

Shammua son of Zaccur;
Shammua, whose name means "heard" or "hearing," was chosen to represent the tribe of Reuben. The mention of his father, Zaccur, follows the biblical tradition of identifying individuals through their lineage, emphasizing the importance of family heritage. Shammua's role as a spy was crucial, as he was tasked with assessing the Promised Land, a mission that would have significant implications for the future of Israel.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Shammua
He was the son of Zaccur and a representative from the tribe of Reuben. Shammua was one of the twelve spies sent by Moses to explore the land of Canaan.

2. Zaccur
The father of Shammua, belonging to the tribe of Reuben. His name is mentioned to establish the lineage and tribal affiliation of Shammua.

3. Tribe of Reuben
This was one of the twelve tribes of Israel, descended from Reuben, the firstborn son of Jacob. The tribe played a significant role in the history of Israel.

4. Canaan
The land that the Israelites were to explore and eventually conquer. It was described as a land flowing with milk and honey, representing God's promise to His people.

5. Moses
The leader of the Israelites who sent the twelve spies, including Shammua, to scout the land of Canaan as instructed by God.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Representation
Each tribe had a representative, highlighting the importance of communal involvement and responsibility in God's plans. Consider how you represent your community or family in spiritual matters.

Faith vs. Fear
The mission of the spies was a test of faith. Reflect on how fear can hinder us from seeing God's promises and how faith can empower us to overcome obstacles.

Legacy and Influence
Shammua's role reminds us that our actions and decisions can have lasting impacts on our community. Consider the legacy you are building through your faith and actions.

Obedience to God's Commands
The sending of the spies was in obedience to God's command. Reflect on the importance of following God's instructions, even when the path seems uncertain.

The Role of Leadership
Moses' leadership in sending the spies shows the importance of wise and godly leadership. Consider how you can support and pray for your leaders in their decision-making processes.

What Does Numbers 13:4 Mean
and these were their names:

• The verse opens by stressing that God records and reveals specific names. Scripture repeatedly shows the Lord calling people by name (Isaiah 43:1; Luke 10:20). He is precise and personal; the list of spies is not random but divinely appointed.

• Naming each representative underscores accountability. Just as Numbers 1:17–19 lists leaders for the census, so Numbers 13 lists leaders for a mission of faith. God’s Word is historically exact, reminding us that real men faced real choices that would shape Israel’s future (Romans 15:4; 1 Corinthians 10:11).

From the tribe of Reuben,

• Reuben was Jacob’s firstborn (Genesis 49:3), yet his tribe often lived with the tension of squandered privilege (Genesis 49:4). Selecting a Reubenite highlights God’s continuing grace toward a tribe with a mixed legacy.

• Reuben’s camp was on Israel’s south side (Numbers 2:10), near the direction the spies would initially travel. The tribe’s involvement shows that every segment of the nation shared responsibility for trusting the LORD’s promise (Deuteronomy 1:21).

• Cross references: Numbers 1:20–21 (Reuben’s census count), Joshua 22:1–4 (Reuben’s later part in settling east of the Jordan).

Shammua son of Zaccur;

• Shammua represents Reuben among the twelve spies (Numbers 13:3). His very inclusion signifies leadership recognition within his tribe.

• Although Scripture does not recount personal details of Shammua’s later life, the broader narrative reveals that only Caleb and Joshua brought back a faithful report (Numbers 14:6–9). By implication, Shammua aligned with the fearful majority. This cautions believers about the danger of unbelief even among recognized leaders (Hebrews 3:12; 1 Corinthians 10:5).

• The mention of his father, Zaccur, roots Shammua in a family line. God sees lineage and legacy, and choices made on this mission would shape both (Exodus 20:5–6; Deuteronomy 30:19–20).

summary

Numbers 13:4 is more than a footnote; it shows that God deliberately appoints identifiable people for specific tasks. The verse highlights divine precision, shared tribal responsibility, and the sobering truth that leadership titles do not guarantee faithful hearts. Remembered by name, Shammua challenges us to respond in faith when God calls us to trust His promises.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - These were their names. None of these names occur elsewhere, except those of Caleb and Joshua. The order of the tribes is the same as in ch. 1, except that Zebulun is separated from the other sons of Leah, and placed after Benjamin, while the two sons of Joseph are separated from one another. In verse 11 "the tribe of Joseph" is explained to be "the tribe of Manasseh;" elsewhere it is either common to both, or confined to Ephraim (see Revelation 7:8, and cf. Ezekiel 37:16). No spy was sent for the tribe of Levi, because it was now understood to have no territorial claims upon the land of promise, and to stand altogether by itself in relation to the national hopes and duties.
