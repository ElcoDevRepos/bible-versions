If you come with us
This invitation is extended by Moses to Hobab, his brother-in-law, as the Israelites prepare to leave Mount Sinai. The context is the journey to the Promised Land, a significant event in Israel's history. The invitation reflects the communal nature of the Israelite journey, emphasizing the importance of unity and shared purpose. It also highlights the value of Hobab's knowledge of the wilderness, which would be beneficial for the Israelites. This phrase can be seen as a call to join God's people, paralleling the invitation to follow Christ in the New Testament (Matthew 4:19).

we will share with you
The promise of sharing indicates a sense of community and mutual benefit. In the ancient Near Eastern culture, sharing resources and blessings was a common practice among tribes and families. This reflects the biblical principle of generosity and hospitality, which is echoed in the New Testament (Acts 2:44-45). The Israelites' willingness to share with Hobab underscores the inclusive nature of God's blessings.

whatever good things the LORD gives us.
This phrase acknowledges that all blessings come from the LORD, emphasizing divine providence and grace. The "good things" refer to the material and spiritual blessings that God promised to the Israelites, including the land flowing with milk and honey (Exodus 3:8). This promise is rooted in the Abrahamic covenant, where God pledged to bless Abraham's descendants (Genesis 12:2-3). Theologically, it points to the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises in Christ, who is the source of all spiritual blessings (Ephesians 1:3). The phrase also serves as a reminder of the Israelites' dependence on God's provision throughout their journey.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites, chosen by God to lead His people out of Egypt and through the wilderness toward the Promised Land.

2. Hobab
Moses' brother-in-law, the son of Reuel (also known as Jethro), who was invited by Moses to join the Israelites on their journey.

3. The Israelites
The chosen people of God, who were on their journey from Mount Sinai to the Promised Land.

4. The Wilderness
The setting of the Israelites' journey, representing both physical and spiritual challenges.

5. The LORD
The covenant God of Israel, who provides guidance, protection, and blessings to His people.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Community
God designed His people to journey together, supporting and encouraging one another. Just as Moses invited Hobab to join the Israelites, we are called to invite others into our faith community.

Sharing God's Blessings
The promise to share "whatever good things the LORD gives us" reflects a heart of generosity and stewardship. As believers, we are called to share our blessings with others, recognizing that all good things come from God.

Trust in God's Provision
The invitation to Hobab is rooted in trust that God will provide for His people. We are reminded to trust in God's provision and to extend that trust to others, inviting them to experience God's goodness.

Leadership and Partnership
Moses' invitation to Hobab highlights the value of partnership in leadership. Effective leadership often involves collaboration and the inclusion of diverse perspectives and skills.

What Does Numbers 10:32 Mean
If you come

Moses’ words to Hobab are a genuine, voluntary invitation. No coercion—just an open door.

• Exodus 18:9–10 shows Hobab’s family already rejoicing “for all the goodness that the LORD had done for Israel.”

• Jesus echoes this spirit in Matthew 11:28, “Come to Me, all you who are weary…”.

• Revelation 22:17 ends Scripture on the same note: “Let the one who is thirsty come.”

God honors a willing heart, and Israel’s leader models that expectancy here.

with us

The phrase stresses community. Blessing is discovered and enjoyed together.

• Ruth echoes it in Ruth 1:16, “Where you go I will go.”

• Hebrews 10:24-25 urges believers not to forsake “our meeting together.”

• Acts 2:44 records that “all the believers were together.”

The journey of faith is designed to be shared, not solo.

we will share with you

Moses pledges tangible generosity, linking presence with participation.

• 1 Samuel 30:24 sets the principle: “The share of the one who stays… shall be the same as the share of the one who goes.”

• Acts 4:32 shows the early church living this out: “No one claimed that any of his possessions was his own.”

• 2 Corinthians 8:14 speaks of “equality” so that “your abundance may supply their need.”

God’s people imitate His giving heart by opening their hands to others.

whatever good things

The promise is broad and undefined—because God’s goodness is limitless.

• Psalm 34:8 invites, “Taste and see that the LORD is good.”

• Psalm 84:11 assures, “No good thing does He withhold from those who walk uprightly.”

• James 1:17 reminds, “Every good and perfect gift is from above.”

Moses cannot list every blessing; he simply pledges all of them.

the LORD gives us

The source of every benefit is explicit: the LORD. Israel’s leader refuses self-credit.

• Deuteronomy 8:18 warns, “Remember the LORD your God, for it is He who gives you power to gain wealth.”

• Psalm 103:2 praises, “Forget not all His benefits.”

• Ephesians 1:3 celebrates, “He has blessed us in Christ with every spiritual blessing.”

Recognizing the Giver keeps gratitude flowing and humility intact.

summary

Numbers 10:32 captures covenant life in one sentence: a willing outsider is invited to join God’s people, journey with them, and receive every blessing the Lord pours out. The verse champions voluntary response, shared pilgrimage, open-handed generosity, confidence in God’s abundant goodness, and unwavering acknowledgment that all blessings come from Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 32. - If thou go with us. From Judges 1:16 we learn that the sons of Hobab joined themselves to the sons of Judah, and dwelt amongst them on the southern border of the land. Here is an "undesigned coincidence," albeit a slight one. Judah led the way on the march from Sinai to Canaan, and Hobab's duties as guide and scout would bring him more into contact with that tribe than with any other.
