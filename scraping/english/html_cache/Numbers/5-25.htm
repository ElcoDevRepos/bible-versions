The priest shall take from her hand the grain offering for jealousy
This phrase refers to the ritual described in Numbers 5, where a husband suspects his wife of infidelity. The grain offering, made of barley, is unique because it lacks oil and frankincense, symbolizing the gravity and solemnity of the situation. Barley, often considered a lesser grain, reflects the humbling nature of the offering. The priest's role as mediator underscores the importance of priestly intercession in resolving disputes and maintaining community purity. This ritual highlights the seriousness of marital fidelity in Israelite society and the need for divine intervention in matters of jealousy.

wave it before the LORD
Waving the offering before the LORD signifies presenting it to God, acknowledging His sovereignty and seeking His judgment. This act of waving is a form of consecration, setting the offering apart for divine purposes. It symbolizes the transfer of the matter from human hands to divine jurisdiction. The waving ritual is seen in other offerings, such as peace offerings, indicating a broader practice of dedicating offerings to God. This act underscores the belief that God is the ultimate judge and arbiter of truth.

and bring it to the altar
Bringing the offering to the altar signifies its dedication to God and the seriousness of the vow or judgment being sought. The altar, central to Israelite worship, represents God's presence and the place where sacrifices and offerings are made. This action connects the ritual to the broader sacrificial system, where offerings are made to atone for sin or seek divine favor. The altar's role in this ritual emphasizes the need for divine justice and the community's reliance on God's guidance in resolving disputes.

What Does Numbers 5:25 Mean
The priest shall take from her hand

– This opening action underlines that the woman personally surrenders the offering; the priest does not supply it for her (cf. Leviticus 2:1–2).

– By receiving it “from her hand,” the priest publicly affirms both her accountability and his own duty as mediator (Exodus 28:1; Hebrews 5:1).

– The step protects due process: only after the husband has brought his charge (Numbers 5:14–15) and the woman has appeared is the ritual allowed to proceed.

the grain offering for jealousy

– Unlike regular grain offerings that accompany thanksgiving (Leviticus 7:12–13), this one carries no oil or frankincense (Numbers 5:15), highlighting the somber nature of marital suspicion.

– It is called “for jealousy” because it addresses a breach of covenant love—echoing how the LORD describes Himself as “a jealous God” when covenant loyalty is threatened (Exodus 34:14).

– In practical terms the offering:

• Represents the woman’s life placed before God’s scrutiny.

• Allows the matter to be settled without rash violence (Proverbs 14:29).

wave it before the LORD

– A wave offering is lifted horizontally or moved back and forth, symbolically handing it over to God and then receiving His verdict (Exodus 29:24–25; Leviticus 8:27).

– This gesture declares that ultimate judgment belongs to the LORD, not to human suspicion (Deuteronomy 32:35).

– By involving the congregation gathered at the sanctuary (Numbers 5:16), it reminds Israel that purity in marriage affects the whole community (Malachi 2:15).

and bring it to the altar

– The altar is where sin and uncleanness are dealt with by sacrifice (Leviticus 17:11).

– Placing the offering there invites divine examination; if guilt exists, God will expose it through the subsequent “bitter water” ordeal (Numbers 5:27–28).

– The act foreshadows the need for a final, perfect sacrifice to bear humanity’s hidden sins—a role ultimately fulfilled at the cross (1 Peter 2:24).

summary

Numbers 5:25 portrays a careful, God-ordained process that protects marriage, upholds justice, and keeps the community holy. Each movement—taking the offering, designating it for jealousy, waving it before the LORD, and placing it on the altar—declares that hidden matters of the heart are open before God and must be resolved His way.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - Offer it upon the altar. According to the law of the minchah (Leviticus 2), only an handful was burnt as a "memorial" (Hebrew, azkarah), the rest being "presented," and then laid at the side of the altar to be subsequently eaten by the priests. All this was done before the actual ordeal by drinking the water, in order that the woman might in the most solemn and complete way possible be brought face to face with the holiness of God. She stood before him as one of his own, yet as one suspected and abashed, courting the worst if guilty, claiming complete acquittal if innocent.
