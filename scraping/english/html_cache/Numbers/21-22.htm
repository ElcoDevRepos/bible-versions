Let us pass through your land.
This request was made by the Israelites to the king of Edom as they journeyed toward the Promised Land. The Israelites sought permission to travel through Edomite territory peacefully. This reflects the broader biblical theme of seeking peace and cooperation with neighboring nations. Historically, Edom was descended from Esau, Jacob's brother, which adds a familial dimension to the request. The Israelites' appeal highlights the importance of diplomacy and respect for national boundaries.

We will not turn aside into any field or vineyard,
The Israelites assured the Edomites that they would not disrupt their agriculture. Fields and vineyards were vital for sustenance and economic stability in ancient times. This promise indicates a respect for property and resources, aligning with the biblical principle of loving one's neighbor and not coveting or taking what belongs to others. It also reflects the Israelites' reliance on God's provision rather than taking from others.

or drink water from any well.
Water was a precious resource in the arid regions of the Near East. By promising not to drink from the wells, the Israelites demonstrated their intention to avoid imposing on the Edomites' resources. This assurance underscores the importance of self-sufficiency and respect for others' needs. It also reflects the Israelites' trust in God's provision, as He had previously provided water miraculously during their desert wanderings.

We will stay on the King’s Highway
The King's Highway was an ancient trade route that ran from Egypt through the Sinai Peninsula to the northern regions of the Near East. It was a well-established path used for commerce and travel. By committing to stay on this road, the Israelites indicated their intention to pass through quickly and without deviation, minimizing their impact on the land. This route's historical significance highlights the interconnectedness of ancient civilizations and the importance of established pathways for communication and trade.

until we have passed through your territory.
The Israelites' request to pass through Edom was temporary, emphasizing their desire for a peaceful and transient passage. This reflects the broader biblical narrative of the Israelites' journey to the Promised Land, a journey marked by faith and reliance on God's guidance. The request also underscores the transient nature of their wilderness wanderings, pointing to the ultimate fulfillment of God's promise to give them a land of their own.

Teaching Points
Respect for Others' Property
The Israelites' request demonstrates a respect for the sovereignty and property of others, even when they have the power to take by force.

Peaceful Intentions
The emphasis on not turning aside into fields or vineyards and not drinking from wells shows a commitment to peace and integrity.

Trust in God's Plan
Despite the refusal, the Israelites' journey continues, reminding us to trust in God's overarching plan even when faced with obstacles.

Handling Rejection
The Israelites' experience teaches us how to handle rejection with grace and to seek God's guidance in the face of closed doors.

God's Sovereignty
The eventual outcome, where God delivers the Amorites into Israel's hands, underscores His control over nations and events.

What Does Numbers 21:22 Mean
Let us pass through your land

Israel is nearing the Promised Land and must cross Amorite territory. Their opening words reveal:

- Courtesy: a humble request, not a demand (cf. Numbers 20:17).

- Transparency: stating intentions up front keeps motives clear (cf. Romans 12:18).

- Trust in God’s route: this path is part of His promise (Genesis 15:18-21).

By seeking peaceful passage, Israel models the principle that believers should pursue peace first, even when confident of God-given rights.

We will not turn aside into any field or vineyard

The pledge shows respect for private property and labor:

- No trespassing into cultivated areas (Deuteronomy 23:25-26).

- No taking what others have planted; Israel will rely on manna and God’s provision (Exodus 16:35).

- Integrity in action: promises match behavior (Matthew 5:37).

God’s people are called to honor boundaries and avoid even the appearance of exploitation.

or drink water from any well

Water is precious in the desert. Israel promises to leave local resources untouched:

- Acknowledging scarcity in another’s land (Proverbs 25:17).

- Testifying that the Lord supplies their needs—He has already brought water from a rock twice (Exodus 17:6; Numbers 20:11).

- Eliminating excuses for conflict; hospitality cannot be coerced (Psalm 37:21).

The stance underscores self-restraint and reliance on divine provision.

We will stay on the King’s Highway

This ancient trade route ran north-south east of the Jordan:

- A public, established road (Deuteronomy 2:27).

- Predictable movement avoids suspicion; no wandering in villages (1 Thessalonians 4:11-12).

- Commitment to lawful order; Israel honors civil structures while under God’s higher authority (Romans 13:1-2).

Sticking to the main road pictures a straight, accountable walk of faith.

until we have passed through your territory.

Israel emphasizes the temporary nature of the request:

- No claim on Amorite land; covenant boundaries lie elsewhere (Deuteronomy 2:31).

- Assurance of quick, orderly transit (Hebrews 12:1).

- Reminder that God’s people are pilgrims heading toward promised inheritance (1 Peter 2:11).

They seek only what is necessary to fulfill God’s plan, nothing more.

summary

Numbers 21:22 presents a model of how God’s people interact with the world while journeying toward His promises. Israel seeks peaceful, respectful passage; pledges not to exploit resources; adheres to recognized pathways; and underscores the temporary nature of their presence. The verse teaches courtesy, integrity, self-restraint, lawful conduct, and a pilgrim mindset—values still vital for believers navigating a world that is not their final home.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Let me pass through thy land. Cf. Numbers 20:17. Israel was not commanded to spare the Amorites, indeed he was under orders to smite them (Deuteronomy 2:24), but that did not prevent his approaching them in the first instance with words of peace. If Sihon had hearkened, no doubt Israel would have passed directly on to Jordan, and he would at least have been spared for the present.
