Now the son of Pallu
Pallu was one of the sons of Reuben, the firstborn of Jacob. Reuben's descendants are significant in the tribal structure of Israel, though Reuben himself lost his birthright due to his transgression with Bilhah (Genesis 35:22). The tribe of Reuben settled east of the Jordan River, in a region known for its pastoral lands. This geographical location often placed them in a vulnerable position, both politically and spiritually, as they were somewhat isolated from the other tribes. The mention of Pallu here connects to the broader narrative of the Reubenites and their role in Israel's history.

was Eliab
Eliab, the son of Pallu, is noted in the genealogical records of the Israelites. His name means "God is Father," which reflects the theocentric naming conventions of the time, emphasizing the Israelites' relationship with God. Eliab's lineage is significant as it connects to the rebellion of Korah, Dathan, and Abiram (Numbers 16), where Dathan and Abiram, sons of Eliab, played a crucial role. This rebellion against Moses and Aaron highlights themes of authority, divine appointment, and judgment. The story serves as a warning against pride and disobedience, illustrating the consequences of challenging God's chosen leaders. Eliab's mention here underscores the importance of family lineage in understanding the unfolding narrative of Israel's journey and the lessons derived from their experiences.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Pallu
Pallu is one of the sons of Reuben, the firstborn of Jacob. He is part of the tribe of Reuben, which is one of the twelve tribes of Israel. His lineage is significant in the census of the Israelites.

2. Eliab
Eliab is the son of Pallu. He is mentioned in the context of the census taken in the wilderness, which is a critical event in the history of Israel as they prepare to enter the Promised Land.

3. The Census
This event is part of the second census of the Israelites, as recorded in the Book of Numbers. It was conducted to count the new generation of Israelites who would enter the Promised Land after the previous generation had perished in the wilderness.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Lineage and Heritage
Understanding our spiritual heritage is crucial. Just as the Israelites were counted and their lineage recorded, Christians are part of a spiritual lineage through faith in Christ.

God's Faithfulness Across Generations
The census reflects God's faithfulness in preserving His people. Despite the failures of the previous generation, God continues His plan through their descendants.

Preparation for God's Promises
The census was a preparation for entering the Promised Land. Similarly, believers are called to prepare their hearts and lives for the fulfillment of God's promises.

Accountability and Responsibility
Each individual counted in the census had a role in the community. Christians are reminded of their responsibility within the body of Christ.

What Does Numbers 26:8 Mean
Now

“Now the son of Pallu was Eliab.” (Numbers 26:8)

• The little word “Now” signals a continuation of the narrative begun in Numbers 26:1–4, where the LORD commands Moses and Eleazar to take a second census of Israel after the judgment in the wilderness.

• It reminds us that God’s dealings with His people are orderly and historical (cf. Exodus 12:40–42; 1 Kings 6:1). Each detail fits into a larger chronology that underscores His faithfulness to covenant promises (Genesis 15:13–14; Galatians 3:17).

• Because Scripture is accurate and literal, we can read this “Now” as anchoring Eliab’s family in a real moment during Israel’s journey east of the Jordan (Numbers 26:63).

the son of Pallu

• Pallu is listed earlier in the chapter as one of the five sons of Reuben, Israel’s firstborn (Numbers 26:5; Genesis 46:9).

• By tracing lineage through Pallu, the text emphasizes individual accountability within a tribe. Reuben forfeited certain firstborn privileges (Genesis 35:22; 49:3–4), yet God still tracks his descendants with precision.

• This reinforces a theme seen elsewhere: God cares about both the corporate body and every branch on the family tree (Isaiah 43:1; Luke 12:7).

• It also ties directly to the census purpose—determining inheritance rights when Israel would enter the land (Numbers 26:52–55).

was Eliab

• Eliab is identified not merely for record-keeping but because his sons, Dathan and Abiram, became notorious for joining Korah’s rebellion (Numbers 16:1, 27, 32; Deuteronomy 11:6).

• The placement here acts as both a historical footnote and a moral signal: generations remember how fathers influence children (Exodus 20:5–6; Psalm 106:16–18).

• Key takeaways:

– God’s Word documents both faithfulness and failure, testifying to His justice and mercy (Romans 15:4).

– Family reputation can be redeemed or marred; each generation must choose obedience (Joshua 24:15; 2 Corinthians 5:10).

– Despite earlier rebellion, Reubenite families still receive allotments in Canaan (Joshua 13:15–23), illustrating the balance of discipline and promise.

summary

Numbers 26:8 may appear as a simple genealogical note, yet it anchors Eliab within the unfolding census, affirms God’s meticulous record of His people, and foreshadows the impact of family choices on Israel’s journey. Even in a single sentence, Scripture reminds us that history, heritage, and holiness are woven together under God’s faithful oversight.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - And the sons of Pallu. This particular genealogy is added because of the special interest which attached to the fate of certain members of the family. The plural "sons" is to be explained here not from the fact (which has nothing to do with it) that several grandsons are afterwards mentioned, but from the fact that וּבְנֵי ("and the sons") was the conventional heading of a family list, and was written doom by the transcriber before he noticed that only one name followed.
