Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But the whole congregation threatened to stone Joshua and Caleb.
This phrase highlights the intense rebellion and fear among the Israelites. Joshua and Caleb, two of the twelve spies sent to explore Canaan, brought back a positive report and encouraged the people to trust in God's promise. However, the congregation, influenced by the negative reports of the other ten spies, reacted with hostility. Stoning was a common form of execution for blasphemy or rebellion, indicating the severity of the people's anger. This reflects a recurring theme of Israel's lack of faith and trust in God, despite witnessing His miracles. The people's reaction can be seen as a rejection of God's chosen leaders and His plan, paralleling other instances of rebellion in the wilderness (Exodus 17:4, 1 Samuel 30:6).

Then the glory of the LORD appeared to all the Israelites at the Tent of Meeting.
The appearance of the glory of the LORD signifies divine intervention at a critical moment. The Tent of Meeting, also known as the Tabernacle, was the central place of worship and where God's presence dwelled among the Israelites. This manifestation of God's glory served as a powerful reminder of His sovereignty and holiness. It often appeared in moments of crisis or decision, underscoring God's direct involvement in the affairs of His people (Exodus 40:34-35, Leviticus 9:23-24). The glory of the LORD appearing here acts as a deterrent to the people's rebellion and a confirmation of Joshua and Caleb's faithfulness. It also foreshadows the ultimate revelation of God's glory in Jesus Christ, who is described as the radiance of God's glory (Hebrews 1:3).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joshua and Caleb
Two of the twelve spies sent to explore the Promised Land. They were the only ones who trusted in God's promise and encouraged the Israelites to take possession of the land.

2. The Congregation of Israel
The collective body of the Israelites who, in fear and rebellion, threatened to stone Joshua and Caleb for their faithfulness and positive report.

3. The Glory of the LORD
A manifestation of God's presence, often depicted as a radiant light or cloud, which appeared at the Tent of Meeting to intervene in the situation.

4. The Tent of Meeting
A sacred place where God communicated with Moses and the Israelites, symbolizing God's presence among His people.

5. The Threat of Stoning
An expression of the congregation's rebellion and lack of faith, showing their willingness to resort to violence against God's chosen leaders.
Teaching Points
Faith in God's Promises
Joshua and Caleb exemplify unwavering faith in God's promises despite overwhelming opposition. Believers are called to trust in God's word even when circumstances seem daunting.

The Danger of Rebellion
The Israelites' threat to stone Joshua and Caleb highlights the destructive nature of rebellion against God. It serves as a warning to guard against unbelief and disobedience.

God's Protective Presence
The appearance of the glory of the LORD demonstrates God's protective presence over His faithful servants. Believers can find comfort in knowing that God is with them in times of trial.

Courage to Stand Alone
Joshua and Caleb's courage to stand alone in their convictions encourages believers to remain steadfast in their faith, even when it means standing against the majority.

The Role of Leadership
The incident underscores the importance of godly leadership and the responsibility of leaders to guide others in faith and obedience to God.

What Does Numbers 14:10 Mean
The people’s violent rejection

“ ‘But the whole congregation threatened to stone Joshua and Caleb.’ ” (Numbers 14:10a)

• Every family head and tribal leader in Israel has just heard the faith–filled report of Joshua and Caleb (Numbers 14:6-9), yet instead of responding with repentance they choose mob violence.

• Stoning was the community’s standard execution method for supposed blasphemers (Leviticus 24:16). By planning to hurl stones, the people brand Joshua and Caleb as enemies of God rather than defenders of His promise.

• This scene exposes the depth of Israel’s unbelief. Only days earlier they had vowed to obey the Lord (Exodus 24:7), but now they reject His appointed witnesses. Hebrews 3:15 recalls this moment to warn later believers: “Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts.”

• The moment also echoes earlier grumblings—at Rephidim they were ready to stone Moses (Exodus 17:4). The pattern proves that unchecked fear grows into open rebellion.

• Joshua and Caleb’s courage foreshadows New-Testament disciples who are persecuted for truth (Acts 7:52; 14:19). Faith may stand in a lonely minority, yet it still speaks out.

Divine intervention at the Tent of Meeting

“ ‘Then the glory of the LORD appeared to all the Israelites at the Tent of Meeting.’ ” (Numbers 14:10b)

• Right when the stones are about to fly, the shining presence of God breaks in. The same visible glory that filled the tabernacle in Exodus 40:34 now confronts the rebels face-to-face.

• The Tent of Meeting stands at the center of the camp (Numbers 2:17), making God’s intervention unmistakable to every eye. He is not distant; He steps between the mob and His servants.

• Earlier showings of this glory announced provision (Exodus 16:10) or acceptance (Leviticus 9:23). Here it signals judgment tempered by mercy—God stops the mob yet will discipline the nation (Numbers 14:26-35).

• Moses immediately enters dialogue with the Lord (Numbers 14:11-19), illustrating how God’s glory invites intercession. Psalm 99:6-8 later recalls Moses standing in the breach.

• For believers today, Christ reveals the same glory in human flesh (John 1:14) and promises never to abandon those who stand for Him (Matthew 28:20).

summary

Israel’s intent to stone Joshua and Caleb exposes the deadly consequence of unbelief: when fear eclipses faith, God’s messengers become the target. Yet the Lord visibly intervenes, defending His faithful servants and halting the rebellion. Numbers 14:10 therefore reminds us that God both sees and acts; He honors courageous faith and confronts hardened hearts, calling every generation to trust His promises without compromise.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) But all the congregation bade stone them with stones.--All the congregation here, as elsewhere, seems to denote the princes or chief men of the congregation; otherwise it is difficult to understand to whom the order was addressed to stone Joshua and Caleb. Stoning appears to have been a mode of death commonly adopted in Egypt (Exodus 8:26). Under the Jewish law stoning was the ordinary, and, as some think, the only prescribed mode of death, and was inflicted as the punishment for crimes such as adultery (Deuteronomy 22:22; Deuteronomy 22:24), idolatry (Deuteronomy 13:10), witchcraft (Leviticus 20:27), sabbath-breaking (Numbers 15:35), and blasphemy (Leviticus 24:16). Moses had apprehended the same outbreak of popular violence on a former occasion (Exodus 17:4), and, at a later period, David was threatened in a similar manner (1Samuel 30:6). . . .
