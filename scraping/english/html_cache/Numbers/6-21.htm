This is the law of the Nazirite
The Nazirite vow is a special commitment made by individuals who choose to dedicate themselves to God for a period of time. This vow is detailed in Numbers 6 and involves abstaining from wine, not cutting one's hair, and avoiding contact with the dead. The term "Nazirite" comes from the Hebrew word "nazir," meaning "consecrated" or "separated." This vow signifies a deeper level of holiness and dedication, similar to the priestly duties, but it is accessible to any Israelite, male or female.

who vows his offering to the LORD for his separation
The offering is a crucial part of the Nazirite vow, symbolizing the individual's commitment and separation unto the Lord. This separation is not just physical but spiritual, setting the individual apart for God's purposes. The offerings include a male lamb, a ewe lamb, a ram, unleavened bread, and drink offerings, reflecting the comprehensive nature of the vow. This act of separation is a precursor to the New Testament concept of being set apart for Christ.

in addition to whatever else he can afford
This phrase indicates that while there are prescribed offerings, the Nazirite can give additional offerings based on personal ability. This flexibility shows God's understanding of individual circumstances and emphasizes the heart's intent over the material value of the offering. It reflects the biblical principle that God values the willingness and sacrifice of the heart more than the quantity of the gift, as seen in the widow's offering in Mark 12:41-44.

he must fulfill whatever vow he makes
The fulfillment of vows is a serious matter in biblical teaching, underscoring the importance of integrity and faithfulness to one's word. Ecclesiastes 5:4-5 warns against making vows lightly, emphasizing that it is better not to vow than to make a vow and not fulfill it. This principle is echoed in Jesus' teaching in Matthew 5:33-37, where He advises letting one's "yes" be "yes" and "no" be "no."

according to the law of his separation
The law of separation for the Nazirite is a divine ordinance that must be adhered to strictly. This law includes specific guidelines and rituals that must be followed to maintain the vow's sanctity. The concept of separation is a recurring theme in Scripture, symbolizing holiness and dedication to God. It foreshadows the call for believers to be separate from the world, as seen in 2 Corinthians 6:17, and points to the ultimate separation and consecration found in Jesus Christ, who perfectly fulfilled the law and became the ultimate sacrifice for sin.

Teaching Points
Commitment to God
The Nazirite vow is a profound expression of dedication and separation to God. Believers today can reflect on their own commitments and how they set themselves apart for God's purposes.

Holiness and Separation
The vow underscores the call to holiness and separation from worldly influences. Christians are encouraged to live distinct lives that reflect their identity in Christ.

Faithfulness in Vows
The emphasis on fulfilling vows teaches the importance of integrity and faithfulness in our promises to God and others.

Sacrificial Living
The Nazirite vow involved personal sacrifice, reminding believers of the call to live sacrificially for the sake of the Gospel.

Generosity in Worship
The phrase "in addition to whatever else he can afford" suggests that offerings to God should be generous and from the heart, not merely obligatory.

What Does Numbers 6:21 Mean
This is the law of the Nazirite

- The verse opens by anchoring everything that follows in God-given legislation, not human tradition (cf. Numbers 6:1-2; Judges 13:5).

- “Law” underscores permanence and authority. Israel could not edit or dilute these commands.

who vows his offering to the LORD

- The Nazirite’s commitment is personal and voluntary, yet once declared it becomes a sacred obligation to “the LORD” (cf. Psalm 76:11; Ecclesiastes 5:4-5).

- Offerings include the animals and grain detailed in Numbers 6:13-20, highlighting that devotion to God costs something measurable.

for his separation

- “Separation” describes being set apart from ordinary life for a period of intensified holiness (cf. Leviticus 20:26; 2 Corinthians 6:17).

- Key markers of that separation—abstaining from grape products, avoiding corpses, not cutting hair—prefigure the believer’s call to distinct living (Romans 12:1-2).

in addition to whatever else he can afford

- God levels the playing field: rich or poor, each brings what he can (cf. Leviticus 5:7; Luke 21:1-4).

- Beyond the basic requirements, extra gifts were welcome, revealing hearts that delight in giving rather than minimal compliance.

he must fulfill whatever vow he makes

- Integrity before God is non-negotiable (cf. Deuteronomy 23:21-23; Matthew 5:33-37).

- The text stresses follow-through: words spoken at the sanctuary echo in heaven, so half-heartedness is unthinkable.

according to the law of his separation

- The Nazirite cannot redefine the terms mid-vow; he must conclude exactly as commanded (cf. Numbers 15:40; John 14:15).

- Obedience safeguards genuine worship from drifting into self-styled spirituality.

summary

- Numbers 6:21 caps the Nazirite instructions by tying personal devotion to clear, God-given standards.

- Vows are voluntary yet binding, sacrificial yet accessible to every income level, and always regulated by divine order rather than human whim.

- The verse calls believers to wholehearted, honest, and obedient dedication, confident that such consecration pleases the Lord who first set us apart.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - This is the law of the Nazarite who hath vowed, and of his offering. "And of" are not in the text. We should probably read, "This is the law of the Nazirite who hath vowed his offering unto the Lord in accordance with his consecration," i.e., these are the offerings which, as a Nazirite, he is bound to make. Beside that his hand shall get. Literally, "grasp." If he can afford or can procure anything more as a free-will offering, he may well do so. In later days it became customary for richer people to defray for their poorer brethren the cost of their sacrifices (Josephus, Ant., 19:6, 1; and cf. Acts 21:24).
