At this time tomorrow
This phrase indicates the precise timing of God's intervention, emphasizing His sovereignty and control over events. It highlights the divine orchestration in the selection of Saul as king, showing that God's plans are executed with precision.

I will send you a man from the land of Benjamin
The tribe of Benjamin was the smallest of the tribes of Israel, as noted in Judges 21:6. This choice underscores God's pattern of using the least likely individuals to fulfill His purposes, similar to His selection of David, the youngest son of Jesse, and later, Jesus, born in humble circumstances.

and you are to anoint him ruler over My people Israel
Anointing was a significant act in ancient Israel, symbolizing the setting apart and empowerment by God for a specific role. This act foreshadows the anointing of Jesus as the Messiah, the ultimate King and Savior. The term "ruler" indicates a leadership role, but it also implies responsibility and accountability to God.

he will save them from the hand of the Philistines
The Philistines were a constant threat to Israel during this period, representing the external pressures and challenges faced by God's people. Saul's role as a deliverer is a type of Christ, who delivers His people from sin and spiritual oppression. This deliverance is both physical and spiritual, pointing to the ultimate salvation through Jesus.

For I have looked upon My people
This phrase reflects God's compassion and concern for His people, reminiscent of His response to the Israelites' cries in Egypt (Exodus 3:7). It underscores the personal relationship between God and Israel, highlighting His role as a caring and attentive deity.

because their cry has come to Me
The cry of the Israelites signifies their distress and need for divine intervention. This mirrors the cries of humanity for redemption, which God answers through the sending of His Son, Jesus Christ. It also emphasizes the power of prayer and God's responsiveness to the pleas of His people.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Leadership
God is the ultimate authority in appointing leaders. He chooses Saul, demonstrating His control over the affairs of nations and His people.

Divine Response to Human Distress
God hears the cries of His people and responds with compassion and action. This encourages believers to bring their concerns to God, trusting in His attentive care.

The Role of Anointing
Anointing signifies God's selection and empowerment for a specific task. Believers are reminded of the importance of seeking God's anointing in their own lives for the roles they are called to fulfill.

Deliverance from Oppression
Just as God raised Saul to deliver Israel from the Philistines, He raises leaders and provides means for deliverance in our lives today. This points to the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ.

Listening to God's Instructions
Samuel's obedience to God's instructions is a model for believers to listen and act upon God's guidance in their lives.

What Does 1 Samuel 9:16 Mean
At this time tomorrow

God sets an exact timetable: “At this time tomorrow.” His sovereignty includes the details of when events unfold (Genesis 18:14; Exodus 9:5). For Samuel, it means he can prepare to act immediately, trusting the Lord’s precise orchestration (Jeremiah 1:12). When the Lord fixes a moment, that moment is immovable.

I will send you a man from the land of Benjamin

The Lord—not coincidence—directs Saul’s steps toward Samuel (Proverbs 16:9; 1 Samuel 10:20-21). Benjamin, the smallest tribe since Judges 20:47, highlights how God often raises up the unlikely (1 Corinthians 1:27). The “send” underscores divine initiative; Samuel is to recognize God’s choice, not make his own.

You are to anoint him ruler over My people Israel

Anointing with oil sets Saul apart publicly (1 Samuel 10:1; Psalm 89:20). “Ruler” shows that even Israel’s first king reigns under God’s authority (Deuteronomy 17:14-20). The people remain “My people,” a reminder that leadership is stewardship, not ownership (1 Peter 5:2-4).

He will save them from the hand of the Philistines

Israel’s current oppression (1 Samuel 7:13; 9:16) will be answered through Saul’s military leadership (1 Samuel 14:47-48). Yet ultimate victory always belongs to the Lord (Psalm 44:3). Saul’s mandate previews the pattern later perfected in David (2 Samuel 8:1) and ultimately in Christ, who delivers from a greater enemy (Luke 1:71-75).

For I have looked upon My people

God’s gaze is compassionate and covenantal (Exodus 2:25; Psalm 34:15). He watches over Israel despite their earlier demand for a king (1 Samuel 8:19-22). His watchfulness reassures: no distress escapes His notice (2 Chronicles 16:9).

Because their cry has come to Me

Like the cries in Egypt (Exodus 3:7) and under Midian (Judges 6:6-8), Israel’s prayers reach God’s throne. He answers, not because they deserve it, but because He is faithful to His covenant love (Nehemiah 9:27; Psalm 18:6). Prayer moves the hand that governs history.

summary

1 Samuel 9:16 reveals a God who hears, sees, and acts with precise timing. He raises up an unlikely Benjamite, Saul, to shepherd and deliver His people, demonstrating both mercy toward Israel’s cries and control over every detail of their future.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) The Philistines.--This statement evidently points to the fact--of which, a little later, we have such ample evidence--that at this juncture the Philistines were again harassing the Israelite territory with their destructive raids. The power of the Philistines was broken, but by no means destroyed, in the great battle of Mizpeh. We know that all through King Saul's reign, and in the early days of King David, these invasions were repeated with varying success. The statement of 1Samuel 7:13 must be understood not as representing that the victory of Mizpeh once and for all destroyed the Philistine power, but that from that day the power of these determined enemies of Israel began to decline. The words of 1Samuel 7:13 must be taken as including the ultimate result of the great Hebrew victory. It is clear that the annoyance of these Philistine raids and incursions were the immediate cause of the prayer for a king. The desire for this form of government, no doubt, for a very long while had existed among the people, but this pressing need for a younger and more warlike leader than their old prophet. judge prompted the request to Samuel.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - That he may save my people out of the hand of the Philistines. Though Samuel had lightened the yoke of the Philistines by his victory at Mizpah, yet he had by no means altogether broken their power. It is so constantly the habit of the historical books of the Bible to include the distant and ultimate results of an act in their account of it, that we must not conclude that what is said in 1 Samuel 7:13-15 was the immediate consequence of Samuel's victory. Especially, when it said that "the hand of Jehovah was against the Philistines all the days of Samuel," it is plain that Soul's successful wars are included in the writer's summary of events, inasmuch as Samuel's life was prolonged until nearly the close of that monarch's reign. The words further show that Soul's office was essentially military, though this is too much emphasised in the A.V., which renders by captain a word which really means prince, chief. Saul, as a Benjamite, belonged to the bravest and most warlike tribe of Israel, and one whose country was the seat of perpetual combat with the Philistines. Their cry is come unto me. Plainly, therefore, Israel was again suffering from Philistine domination.
