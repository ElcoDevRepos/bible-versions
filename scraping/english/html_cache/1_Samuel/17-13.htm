The three older sons of Jesse had followed Saul into battle:
Jesse's three eldest sons joining Saul highlights the family's involvement in Israel's military efforts against the Philistines. This reflects the cultural expectation for able-bodied men to serve in the army. Saul, as the first king of Israel, was leading the nation in battle, and the participation of Jesse's sons indicates their alignment with the king's cause. This also sets the stage for David's later involvement, as he initially remains at home, underscoring his youth and the responsibilities of his older brothers.

The firstborn was Eliab:
Eliab, as the firstborn, held a position of prominence and responsibility within the family. In ancient Israelite culture, the firstborn son often received a double portion of the inheritance and was expected to lead the family after the father's death. Eliab's presence in the battle suggests his role as a leader among his siblings. However, his later interactions with David (1 Samuel 17:28) reveal a tension that contrasts with his expected role, highlighting the theme of God's choice versus human expectations.

The second was Abinadab:
Abinadab, the second son, follows Eliab in the line of succession. His mention here, along with his brothers, emphasizes the family's full commitment to the national cause. The order of their names reflects the traditional patriarchal structure, where each son has a defined role based on birth order. Abinadab's presence in the narrative serves to underscore the normalcy of the family's involvement in the battle, setting the stage for the extraordinary actions of David.

And the third was Shammah:
Shammah, the third son, completes the trio of Jesse's sons who are actively participating in the battle. His inclusion in the list reinforces the idea of a complete and unified family effort in support of Saul's kingship. The mention of these three sons, without further elaboration on their actions in the battle, contrasts with the detailed account of David's later heroics, highlighting the unexpected nature of God's choice in using the youngest and seemingly least significant son to achieve His purposes.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesse
The father of David and his brothers, a resident of Bethlehem. Jesse is significant as the patriarch of the family from which David, the future king of Israel, comes.

2. Eliab
The firstborn son of Jesse. He is mentioned here as one of the three sons who followed Saul into battle. Eliab is later seen in the account when he reacts to David's presence at the battlefield.

3. Abinadab
The second son of Jesse. Like his brothers, he is part of Saul's army, indicating his role as a warrior.

4. Shammah
The third son of Jesse. He also follows Saul into battle, showing the involvement of Jesse's family in the national conflict.

5. Saul
The first king of Israel, leading the Israelites in battle against the Philistines. His leadership is a central theme in the account of 1 Samuel.
Teaching Points
God's Choice vs. Human Expectations
The selection of David, the youngest, over his older brothers, who were more traditionally suited for battle, underscores the biblical theme that God often chooses the unlikely to fulfill His purposes.

Family Dynamics and God's Plan
The interactions between David and his brothers remind us that family relationships can be complex, yet God can work through them to accomplish His will.

Courage and Faithfulness
While Eliab, Abinadab, and Shammah followed Saul into battle, it was David's faith and courage that led to victory over Goliath. This teaches us the importance of faithfulness and courage in the face of challenges.

Leadership and Humility
David's rise from shepherd to king illustrates the importance of humility and reliance on God for leadership, contrasting with Saul's faltering kingship.

What Does 1 Samuel 17:13 Mean
The three older sons of Jesse had followed Saul into battle:

- Scripture paints the scene of Israel’s conflict with the Philistines: “Now the Philistines gathered their forces for war” (1 Samuel 17:1). The king was expected to lead and conscript warriors (see 1 Samuel 8:20; 14:52).

- By noting that Jesse’s three eldest “followed Saul,” the verse underlines their military eligibility. Numbers 1:3 required those twenty and older to serve; David, being the youngest (1 Samuel 17:14), is not yet of that age.

- Their enlistment shows the family’s loyalty to Israel’s king, aligning with Saul’s ongoing practice of drawing the valiant into his ranks (again, 1 Samuel 14:52).

- This detail also sets the dramatic contrast that follows: while the seasoned brothers join the army, God will use the overlooked youngest to gain the victory (compare 1 Samuel 16:7 and 17:45–50).

The firstborn was Eliab,

- Eliab’s name resurfaces from the anointing scene: “When they arrived, Samuel saw Eliab and thought, ‘Surely the LORD’s anointed stands here’” (1 Samuel 16:6). His height and appearance impressed even the prophet, yet God rejected him, teaching that divine choice rests on the heart, not outward stature.

- Firstborn status carried special honor (Deuteronomy 21:17). Eliab likely held family authority and natural leadership qualities, making his presence in Saul’s ranks expected.

- Later, Eliab’s reaction to David—“Why have you come down here? … I know your pride and the wickedness of your heart” (1 Samuel 17:28)—reveals jealousy and a misjudgment that contrasts sharply with God’s earlier assessment of David’s heart (1 Samuel 16:7).

the second was Abinadab,

- Abinadab also appeared during Samuel’s visit: “Then Jesse called Abinadab and made him pass before Samuel. But Samuel said, ‘The LORD has not chosen this one either’” (1 Samuel 16:8). His rejection, like Eliab’s, reinforces that divine election bypassed natural seniority.

- Abinadab’s name later re-emerges when the ark returns from Philistia and remains in his house—though that is a different Abinadab from Kiriath-jearim (1 Samuel 7:1), the shared name underlines its common use within Israel.

- His placement in the verse signals the orderly listing of Jesse’s sons (1 Chronicles 2:13), validating the historical accuracy of the narrative.

and the third was Shammah.

- Shammah (also called Shimeah, 1 Chronicles 2:13) completes the trio first presented in 1 Samuel 16:9, where Samuel again says, “The LORD has not chosen this one.” God consistently bypasses the visible strength of Jesse’s older three sons.

- While Scripture records no direct words or deeds of Shammah in this chapter, his mention highlights the completeness of Jesse’s representation in Saul’s army—three sons, enough to reflect family honor yet still powerless to defeat Goliath.

- The silence about Shammah’s exploits contrasts with the loud faith of David, emphasizing that divine victory does not depend on a warrior’s résumé (Psalm 33:16-17).

summary

1 Samuel 17:13 grounds the upcoming confrontation with Goliath in everyday realities: Jesse’s eldest sons do what any patriotic Israelites would—join Saul’s army. The verse affirms the historical precision of Scripture, lists the brothers in birth order, and quietly sets up the surprise of God’s choice. The strongest and most qualified according to human standards stand in the king’s ranks, yet none of them will secure the victory. God will work through the shepherd-boy left at home, proving once more that “the battle belongs to the LORD” (1 Samuel 17:47).
