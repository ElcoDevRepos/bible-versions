Now please, may my lord the king hear the words of his servant:
David addresses Saul with respect, acknowledging Saul's position as king. This reflects the cultural importance of hierarchy and respect for authority in ancient Israel. David's approach is humble, recognizing Saul's God-given role despite Saul's pursuit of him.

If the LORD has stirred you up against me, then may He accept an offering:
David suggests that if God is the cause of Saul's anger, a sacrificial offering might appease Him. This reflects the Old Testament practice of making offerings to atone for sin or seek favor from God, as seen in Leviticus. It also shows David's understanding of divine sovereignty, acknowledging that God can influence human actions.

But if men have done it, may they be cursed in the presence of the LORD!:
David contrasts divine influence with human manipulation, implying that if others have incited Saul, they should be judged by God. This reflects the biblical theme of justice and accountability, as seen in Proverbs 11:21, where the wicked will not go unpunished.

For today they have driven me away from sharing in the inheritance of the LORD:
David laments being cut off from the land of Israel, which is seen as God's inheritance for His people. This highlights the significance of the Promised Land in Israelite identity and faith, as established in Genesis 12:7. Being exiled from it was akin to being separated from God's presence and blessings.

saying, ‘Go, serve other gods.’:
David equates being driven from Israel with being forced to serve other gods, as the land was central to worshiping Yahweh. This underscores the cultural and religious importance of the land, where the true worship of God was to be conducted, as emphasized in Deuteronomy 12:5. It also foreshadows the exile experiences of Israel, where separation from the land led to challenges in maintaining faithfulness to God.

Teaching Points
Trust in God's Sovereignty
David acknowledges that if the LORD is behind Saul's pursuit, then it is part of God's plan. We should trust God's sovereignty in our own trials.

Seek Reconciliation
David offers a solution of making an offering if God is the cause, showing the importance of seeking reconciliation with God and others.

Discernment of Motives
David distinguishes between divine and human motives behind actions. We should seek discernment to understand the true causes of conflicts in our lives.

Value of Spiritual Inheritance
David laments being driven from his spiritual inheritance. We should cherish and protect our spiritual heritage and relationship with God.

Curse of False Accusation
David calls for a curse on those who falsely accuse. We must be cautious not to bear false witness or unjustly accuse others.

What Does 1 Samuel 26:19 Mean
Now please, may my lord the king hear the words of his servant:

David speaks with humility and honor toward Saul, recognizing the God-given authority of Israel’s anointed king even while fleeing for his life (1 Samuel 24:8–10; Romans 13:1-2).

• “My lord the king” underscores submission, not flattery.

• “His servant” reminds Saul that David still considers himself loyal, echoing earlier appeals (1 Samuel 20:8).

David models respect for leadership despite injustice, foreshadowing Christ who “when reviled, did not revile in return” (1 Peter 2:23).

If the LORD has stirred you up against me, then may He accept an offering.

David allows that God could be using Saul to discipline him (Hebrews 12:5-6). If so, sacrifice is the path to restoration (Leviticus 5:15; 2 Samuel 24:25).

• He trusts God’s sovereignty more than his own innocence.

• An “offering” points to substitutionary atonement—a foreshadowing of the ultimate sacrifice (Hebrews 10:10).

• David invites Saul to seek God’s verdict rather than act on rumor.

But if men have done it, may they be cursed in the presence of the LORD!

If human slander, not divine displeasure, fuels Saul, those agitators deserve covenant curses (Deuteronomy 19:16-19; Proverbs 6:16-19).

• False witnesses fracture community; David appeals for God’s public judgment (Psalm 109:2-3).

• The phrase “in the presence of the LORD” stresses that God sees hidden motives (Hebrews 4:13).

This call for curse is not vengeance but a plea for righteous order.

For today they have driven me away from sharing in the inheritance of the LORD,

Being hunted has exiled David from the promised land and from worship at the tabernacle (Deuteronomy 12:5).

• “Inheritance” points to God’s covenant gift (Numbers 34:29; Psalm 16:6).

• Distance from Israel meant distance from the corporate life of faith—an Old-Testament foretaste of the sorrow of exile (Psalm 137:1-4).

David feels spiritual loss, not merely geographic displacement.

saying, ‘Go, serve other gods.’

By pushing David into Philistine territory (1 Samuel 27:1), Saul’s counselors inadvertently tempt him toward idolatry (Deuteronomy 4:27-28).

• Israel’s land, worship, and God are intertwined; exile equates to pressure toward apostasy.

• David’s lament mirrors the later exiles who longed for Zion (Psalm 42:1-4).

• His heart remains steadfast: “As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15).

summary

1 Samuel 26:19 reveals David’s reverent, God-centered reasoning amid persecution. He honors Saul, submits to God’s possible discipline, denounces human slander, mourns separation from covenant blessings, and refuses to compromise worship. The verse teaches believers to trust God’s sovereignty, respect authority, evaluate accusations carefully, cherish fellowship with God’s people, and guard against any pressure to forsake the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) Let him accept an offering.--The words here are difficult ones in a theological point of view. If, however, we are content to interpret them with Bishop Wordsworth according to the Arabic Version of the Chaldee Targum, the difficulty vanishes: "If the Lord hath stirred thee up against me for any fault of mine, let me know mine offence, and I am ready to make an offering for it to the Lord, that I may be forgiven."--Wordsworth. But by far the greater number of scholars and expositors understand the words of David in what seems to be their plain literal sense, viz.: "If Jehovah has incited you to do this evil thing, let Him smell an offering." The word for offering in the Hebrew is minchah, the meat offering, which signifies "sanctification of life and devotion to the Lord." In other words, "If you think or feel that God stirs you up to take this course against me--the innocent one--pray to God that He may take the temptation--if it be a temptation--from thee." This conception that the movement comes from God runs through the Old Testament. It is apparently expressed in such passages as "the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart," and in such sayings as we find here in this Book of Samuel of an evil spirit from the Lord haunting Saul. "Some have explained the conception by referring it to the intensity with which the Israelites had grasped the idea of the omnipresence of the Deity, and of His being the one power by whose energy all things exist and all acts are done; alike evil and good come from God, for He alone is the source of all . . . but it does not follow that everything to which His providence seems to lead is therefore right for man to do; on the contrary, all leadings of Providence are to be judged by God's immutable law."--Dean Payne Smith. These seeming leadings must be tested by prayer offered by an earnest heart: that is the meaning of the offering (minchah) here. The conception--strange as it may seem at first--is a true one, as in the case of Pharaoh, and also--though with some important modifications--of Saul. The Holy Spirit had pleaded long, and had pleaded in vain. It is possible, we know, for us to weary, or, as St. Paul puts it, quench that Spirit of God pleading within us; then at length, wearied or quenched, it wings its flight away from the wicked soul. This spreading its wings in flight may be said to be God's work. The sad and invariable result is, the deserted heart becomes hardened, as in the case of Pharaoh; the empty shrine becomes the swept and garnished home for the evil spirit, as in the case of Saul.

But if they be the children of men.--But David goes on to say, "If the cruel, unjust thoughts are the result of the envy and hatred of men who are my enemies, may God punish them as they deserve; for see what they have done for me: they have by their calumnies--whispered in your ears--driven me into exile; they have violently bidden me to go and serve other and strange gods." He means that, far away from the only country where Jehovah is loved and honoured, away from the influence of Jehovah's prophets and beloved priests, he and his would be tempted to serve other gods, and to share in the foul and impious practice of the heathen nations.
