Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Saul replied, “I have sinned.
This admission by Saul is significant as it reflects a moment of self-awareness and acknowledgment of wrongdoing. In the biblical narrative, Saul's reign is marked by disobedience to God, as seen in earlier chapters (1 Samuel 15:24). His confession here echoes the broader biblical theme of repentance, though Saul's repentance often lacks the depth and sincerity seen in figures like David (Psalm 51).

Come back, David my son.
Saul's use of "my son" indicates a personal and familial relationship, highlighting the tragedy of their conflict. David was once a favored member of Saul's court and his son-in-law (1 Samuel 18:27). This plea for David's return suggests a desire for reconciliation, though it is complicated by Saul's previous attempts on David's life (1 Samuel 19:1).

I will never harm you again,
Saul's promise not to harm David is significant given his history of pursuing David out of jealousy and fear (1 Samuel 18:8-9). This statement can be seen as an attempt to restore trust, though it is undermined by Saul's inconsistent behavior throughout the narrative.

because today you considered my life precious.
David's decision to spare Saul's life, despite having the opportunity to kill him, demonstrates his respect for God's anointed king (1 Samuel 26:9-11). This act of mercy reflects David's character and foreshadows the teachings of Jesus on loving one's enemies (Matthew 5:44).

I have played the fool and have committed a grave error!”
Saul's admission of folly and error is a poignant moment of self-reflection. The term "fool" in biblical literature often denotes someone who acts contrary to God's wisdom (Proverbs 1:7). Saul's acknowledgment of his mistakes highlights the tragic nature of his kingship, which is marked by a failure to fully submit to God's will. This contrasts with David, who, despite his own sins, is described as a man after God's own heart (1 Samuel 13:14).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Saul
The first king of Israel, anointed by the prophet Samuel. Saul's reign was marked by disobedience to God, leading to his eventual rejection as king.

2. David
The future king of Israel, anointed by Samuel while Saul was still reigning. David is known for his faithfulness to God and his refusal to harm Saul, despite being pursued by him.

3. The Wilderness of Ziph
A region in Judah where David was hiding from Saul. It is the setting for this encounter between Saul and David.

4. The Ziphites
Inhabitants of the Wilderness of Ziph who betrayed David's location to Saul, leading to this confrontation.

5. The Spear and Water Jug
Symbols of Saul's vulnerability and David's mercy. David took these items from Saul's camp to prove he could have killed Saul but chose not to.
Teaching Points
Repentance and Acknowledgment of Sin
Saul's admission, "I have sinned," highlights the importance of recognizing and confessing our wrongdoings before God and others.

The Value of Life and Mercy
David's sparing of Saul's life demonstrates the value of mercy and the sanctity of life, even towards those who seek to harm us.

Foolishness of Sin
Saul's statement, "I have acted like a fool," serves as a reminder of the foolishness and consequences of sin when we act outside of God's will.

The Power of Forgiveness
David's willingness to forgive Saul points to the transformative power of forgiveness in healing relationships and reflecting God's grace.

Trust in God's Justice
David's restraint shows his trust in God's justice rather than taking matters into his own hands, encouraging us to rely on God's timing and judgment.

What Does 1 Samuel 26:21 Mean
Then Saul replied

Saul’s words come immediately after David has spared his life a second time.

• The king at last responds verbally to the righteous appeal of his anointed rival (1 Samuel 26:17–20).

• Saul’s reply recognizes that he stands before a man who has shown him extraordinary mercy, just as earlier in the cave at En-gedi (1 Samuel 24:16–19).

• Proverbs 15:1 reminds us that “a gentle answer turns away wrath”; David’s gentle approach opens Saul’s heart to speak.

I have sinned.

• This confession echoes Saul’s earlier admission after disobeying God regarding the Amalekites (1 Samuel 15:24).

• Genuine repentance is always measured by lasting change (Luke 3:8); Saul’s track record shows that his words do not translate into enduring transformation.

• Contrast with David’s later confession to Nathan (2 Samuel 12:13), which brings real restoration. Saul’s words are sincere in the moment yet shallow in permanence.

Come back, David my son.

• Saul again calls David “my son,” a term of affection heard before (1 Samuel 24:16).

• The invitation sounds welcoming, yet previous behavior (1 Samuel 19:10) cautions us against trusting sentimental language without corresponding action (Proverbs 26:24–26).

• David will wisely keep his distance (1 Samuel 26:22), illustrating Jesus’ counsel to be “shrewd as serpents and innocent as doves” (Matthew 10:16).

I will never harm you again,

• Saul vows to abandon his pursuit, paralleling his earlier oath in the cave (1 Samuel 24:21-22) which he soon violated.

• Ecclesiastes 5:5 warns that it is better not to vow than to vow and not fulfill. Saul’s broken promises underscore the danger of impulsive pledges without Spirit-empowered resolve.

• The text shows that self-controlled restraint endures longer when founded on fear of the Lord rather than momentary emotion (Galatians 5:22-23).

because today you considered my life precious.

• Saul recognizes David’s act of mercy—refusing to strike the Lord’s anointed while the spear and water jug lay within reach (1 Samuel 26:11-12).

• By valuing Saul’s life, David practices the principle later articulated by Jesus: “Love your enemies” (Matthew 5:44).

• Romans 12:20 describes how showing kindness to an adversary “heaps burning coals on his head,” softening hearts and leaving judgment to God (1 Samuel 26:23).

I have played the fool and have committed a grave error!

• Saul’s self-indictment summarizes years of rebellion—rejecting God’s clear commands (1 Samuel 13:13; 15:23).

• The phrase captures the biblical portrait of folly: knowing truth yet acting against it (Proverbs 14:16).

• Numbers 12:11 records Aaron using similar language when repenting for speaking against Moses, highlighting that grave error demands honest acknowledgment.

• True wisdom begins when one admits folly and submits to God (Proverbs 9:10), but Saul’s confession lacks the enduring surrender that brings lasting change.

summary

Saul’s outburst in 1 Samuel 26:21 is a moment of clarity birthed by David’s mercy. He confesses sin, invites reconciliation, and promises safety, all while recognizing the preciousness of the life David spared. Yet history shows his words are fleeting. The passage reminds us that mercy can soften even hardened hearts, confession must be matched by repentance, and true wisdom lies in obeying God rather than merely admitting wrong.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) I have played the fool.--There seems something more in these words of Saul than sorrow for the past. He seems to blame himself here, as the Dean of Canterbury well suggests, for putting himself again in David's power through overweening confidence in his own strength. He reproaches himself with the unguarded state of his camp, but he pledges himself to do no harm to David for the future. He even begs that he will return to his court. But in these words, and also in his blessing of David (1Samuel 26:25), there is a ring of falseness; and this was evidently the impression made on the outlaw, for he not only silently declined the royal overtures, but almost immediately removed from the dominions of Saul altogether, feeling that for him and his there was no longer any hope of security in the land of Israel so long as his foe, King Saul, lived.

Here the two whom Samuel had anointed as kings--the king who has forfeited his crown, and the king of the golden future--parted for ever. They never looked on each other's faces again; not even when the great warrior Saul by dead was his former friend able to take a farewell look at the face he once loved so well. The kindest services his faithful subjects of Jabesh Gilead could show to their king's dishonoured remains, for which they had risked their lives, was at once, with all solemnity and mourning, to burn the disfigured body, and to draw a veil of flame over the mutilated corpse of Saul.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - I have sinned. Saul's answer here is very different from that in 1 Samuel 24:17-21, where the main idea was wonder that David should with such magnanimity spare the life of an enemy so manifestly delivered into his hand. Here a sense of vexation seems uppermost, and of annoyance, not merely because his purpose was frustrated, but because his own military arrangements had been so unsoldierlike. I have played the fool. His first enterprise had ended in placing his life in David's power, and it was folly indeed a second time to repeat the attempt. But though the words of Saul convey the idea rather of vexation with himself than of sorrow for his maliciousness, yet in one point there is a sign of better things. He bids David return, evidently with reference to the grief expressed with such genuine feeling by David at being driven away from Jehovah's land. It was of course impossible, as Saul had given David's wife to another, and David had himself married two other women, but at least it expressed a right and kindly feeling.
