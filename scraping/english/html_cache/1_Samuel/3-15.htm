Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Samuel lay down until the morning;
Samuel, a young boy serving under Eli the priest, had just experienced a divine encounter where God called him directly. This phrase indicates Samuel's return to his resting place after receiving a profound message from God. The act of lying down suggests a return to normalcy and obedience, as he waits for the appropriate time to act. In the context of ancient Israel, night was often a time for divine revelation, as seen in other biblical accounts such as Jacob's dream (Genesis 28:10-17). Samuel's ability to return to sleep after such an encounter demonstrates his trust and peace in God's presence.

then he opened the doors of the house of the LORD.
Opening the doors of the house of the LORD was likely part of Samuel's duties as a servant in the tabernacle. This act signifies the beginning of a new day and the continuation of worship and service in the house of God. The tabernacle, located in Shiloh at this time, was the central place of worship for the Israelites. Samuel's role in opening the doors symbolizes his growing responsibility and foreshadows his future leadership. This act also connects to the idea of Jesus as the door (John 10:9), through whom believers enter into the presence of God.

He was afraid to tell Eli the vision,
Samuel's fear in sharing the vision with Eli reflects the weight and seriousness of the message he received. The vision was a prophecy of judgment against Eli's household due to the sins of his sons, Hophni and Phinehas, and Eli's failure to restrain them (1 Samuel 3:11-14). In the cultural context, delivering a message of judgment to a superior, especially one like Eli who was both a father figure and a priest, would have been daunting. This fear highlights Samuel's humility and respect for Eli, as well as the challenging nature of prophetic ministry. It also prefigures the role of prophets in Israel, who often delivered difficult messages from God, much like Jesus, who spoke truth even when it was unpopular (John 8:40).

Teaching Points
Hearing God's Voice
Samuel's experience teaches us the importance of being attentive to God's voice, even when it brings challenging messages.

Courage in Delivering God's Message
Samuel's fear highlights the difficulty of delivering hard truths, yet it is essential to prioritize God's commands over personal fears.

Faithfulness in Service
Despite his youth, Samuel's dedication to his duties in the temple sets an example of faithfulness in serving God.

The Weight of Spiritual Leadership
Eli's situation serves as a warning about the responsibilities and consequences of spiritual leadership.

God's Sovereignty and Judgment
The prophecy against Eli's house reminds us of God's sovereignty and the certainty of His judgment against sin.

What Does 1 Samuel 3:15 Mean
Samuel lay down until the morning

“Samuel lay down until the morning” (1 Samuel 3:15)

• After hearing the LORD’s voice, Samuel returns to bed, showing immediate obedience to Eli’s instruction in verse 9.

• His ability to rest underscores a childlike trust in God, similar to Psalm 4:8, “In peace I will lie down and sleep, for You alone, O LORD, make me dwell in safety.”

• The night of waiting highlights God’s timing; He often seals a word in the quiet (Job 33:14–16).

• Samuel’s stillness contrasts with Jacob’s anxious awakening at Bethel (Genesis 28:16–17), teaching that revelation can coexist with calm assurance when the heart is surrendered.

then he opened the doors of the house of the LORD

“Then he opened the doors of the house of the LORD” (1 Samuel 3:15)

• At daybreak Samuel performs his routine duty. The tabernacle at Shiloh needed its doors unbarred for morning worship, a task assigned to Levites (1 Chronicles 9:27).

• Faithfulness in ordinary service follows extraordinary encounter; Moses tended Jethro’s flock after the burning bush (Exodus 3:1), and Peter mended nets after seeing the risen Christ (John 21:3–7).

• Opening the doors symbolizes inviting Israel to meet God—echoing Psalm 100:4, “Enter His gates with thanksgiving.”

• Though still a boy, Samuel steps into priestly responsibility, foreshadowing King Josiah’s youthful reforms (2 Chronicles 34:1–3). Routine obedience prepares him to steward greater revelation (Luke 16:10).

He was afraid to tell Eli the vision

“He was afraid to tell Eli the vision” (1 Samuel 3:15)

• The message pronounced judgment on Eli’s household (1 Samuel 3:11–14); naturally Samuel hesitates out of respect.

• Fear of delivering hard truth is common: Jeremiah protested, “I do not know how to speak; I am only a boy” (Jeremiah 1:6), and Ananias balked at visiting Saul (Acts 9:13–17).

• Yet God often entrusts difficult words to humble messengers so that the glory is clearly His (2 Corinthians 4:7).

• Samuel’s reluctance reveals tenderness, not rebellion; Proverbs 27:6 notes that “faithful are the wounds of a friend.” By verse 18, he overcomes fear and speaks plainly, modeling courage balanced with honor (Ephesians 4:15).

summary

1 Samuel 3:15 captures the blend of submission, service, and sober responsibility that marks genuine calling. Samuel rests in God’s word, rises to ordinary duty, and wrestles with the weight of revelation—demonstrating that true ministry holds together quiet trust, daily faithfulness, and loving boldness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) And opened the doors.--This is another notice which indicates that the sanctuary of Shiloh was enclosed in a house or temple. We have no record of the building of the first house of the Lord, but from the references contained in the record of Samuel's childhood it is clear that the sacred Tabernacle had been for some time enclosed by, and perhaps covered in with, permanent buildings.

Feared.--"Here was Samuel's first experience of the prophet's cross: the having unwelcome truth to divulge to those he loved, honoured, and feared. Jeremiah felt this cross to be an exceedingly heavy one" (Jeremiah 15:10; Jeremiah 17:15-18; Jeremiah 20:7-18).--Speaker's Commentary.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - Samuel... opened the doors. In Exodus 26:36; Exodus 36:37, the word used, though translated door, really means an opening, protected by a hanging curtain. The word used here means double or folding doors of wood, and we must therefore conclude that solid buildings had grown up round the tabernacle (see on 1 Samuel 1:9), and a wall for its defence in case of invasion, or the assault of predatory tribes. The confiding the keys of these enclosures to Samuel shows that he was no longer a mere child, or he would have been incapable of holding a position of such high trust (on the key as an emblem of authority see Isaiah 22:22). Vision, as noticed above on ver. 10, means something seen by a person awake and in full possession of his senses.
