Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Peter remembered the word that Jesus had spoken:
This phrase highlights the moment of realization for Peter. Jesus had previously predicted Peter's denial during the Last Supper (Matthew 26:34). This moment of remembrance signifies the fulfillment of Jesus' prophecy and underscores His omniscience. It also reflects the human tendency to forget spiritual warnings in moments of fear or pressure. Peter's memory of Jesus' words serves as a catalyst for his repentance, illustrating the power of Christ's words to convict and lead to transformation.

“Before the rooster crows, you will deny Me three times.”
This prophecy by Jesus is significant for several reasons. The rooster crowing was a common time marker in the ancient world, indicating early morning. The specificity of the prophecy underscores Jesus' divine knowledge and the certainty of His words. The number three often symbolizes completeness in the Bible, suggesting the totality of Peter's denial. This event fulfills Jesus' prediction and serves as a humbling reminder of human frailty and the need for divine grace. It also parallels the threefold restoration of Peter in John 21:15-17, where Jesus reaffirms Peter's role despite his earlier failure.

And he went outside and wept bitterly.
Peter's response of weeping bitterly indicates deep remorse and repentance. This emotional reaction is a turning point for Peter, marking the beginning of his restoration. The act of going outside may symbolize a separation from the scene of his failure and a step towards reconciliation with God. Bitter weeping reflects genuine sorrow for sin, aligning with the biblical principle that godly sorrow leads to repentance (2 Corinthians 7:10). Peter's tears foreshadow his eventual restoration and leadership in the early church, demonstrating that failure is not final when met with true repentance and God's forgiveness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Peter
One of Jesus' closest disciples, known for his impulsive nature and strong declarations of loyalty to Jesus. In this passage, he experiences a moment of profound failure and repentance.

2. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, whose prophecy about Peter's denial comes to pass in this verse. His foreknowledge and understanding of human weakness are highlighted.

3. The Rooster
Symbolic in this context as the marker of Peter's denial, fulfilling Jesus' prophecy and serving as a wake-up call for Peter's realization of his actions.

4. The Courtyard
The setting where Peter's denial takes place, a place of trial and fear, contrasting with the earlier setting of the Last Supper where Peter vowed loyalty.

5. The Denial
The event where Peter denies knowing Jesus three times, showcasing human frailty and the struggle between fear and faith.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Human Weakness
Even the most devoted followers of Christ can falter. Recognize the need for humility and dependence on God's strength.

The Importance of Repentance
Peter's bitter weeping signifies genuine remorse. True repentance involves acknowledging our sins and turning back to God.

The Power of Jesus' Foreknowledge and Grace
Jesus knew Peter would deny Him, yet He still loved him and had a plan for his restoration. Trust in Jesus' grace and His ability to redeem our failures.

The Role of Prophecy in Strengthening Faith
Jesus' prophecy about Peter's denial was fulfilled, reinforcing the reliability of His words and the importance of Scripture in guiding our lives.

The Call to Persevere in Faith
Despite failures, believers are called to persevere, knowing that Jesus intercedes for us and offers restoration and purpose.

What Does Matthew 26:75 Mean
Then Peter remembered the word that Jesus had spoken

Jesus’ earlier prediction suddenly explodes in Peter’s mind (Matthew 26:34; John 13:38).

• The moment is providential—God orchestrates the rooster’s crow as an audible reminder.

• Memory becomes conviction: Scripture declares, “The word of the LORD endures forever” (1 Peter 1:25), and here Peter experiences that permanence firsthand.

• Fulfilled prophecy validates Jesus’ divine authority, echoing earlier fulfillments such as the colt in Matthew 21:4–5 and the silver pieces in Matthew 27:9–10.

“Before the rooster crows, you will deny Me three times.”

• The specificity of “three times” and “before the rooster crows” shows Christ’s omniscience (John 2:24–25).

• Denial contrasts sharply with Peter’s prior pledge, “Even if I must die with You, I will never deny You” (Matthew 26:35), illustrating the weakness of human resolve apart from grace.

• Jesus had already warned Peter about Satan’s sift­ing (Luke 22:31–32), yet also promised intercession, hinting at restoration.

And he went outside

• Leaving the high priest’s courtyard (Matthew 26:58) separates Peter from the swirl of accusation and compromise.

• Stepping away often precedes turning back to God; Lot had to leave Sodom (Genesis 19:15–17), and the prodigal son “came to himself” in a far country (Luke 15:17–18).

• Physical distance creates space for spiritual reflection—an example for believers battling temptation.

and wept bitterly

• Bitter tears signal genuine, godly sorrow that “produces repentance leading to salvation without regret” (2 Corinthians 7:10).

• This is more than remorse; it is the broken and contrite heart God will not despise (Psalm 51:17).

• The depth of Peter’s grief prepares him for future restoration on the shore of Galilee (John 21:15–17), demonstrating that failure is not final when met with repentance and Christ’s forgiveness.

summary

Matthew 26:75 captures the collision of prophecy, human frailty, and divine grace. Peter’s memory of Jesus’ precise words convicts him; his departure signals a break with sin’s setting; his bitter weeping reveals authentic repentance. The verse teaches that Jesus’ foreknowledge is flawless, our self-confidence is fragile, and true restoration begins with heartfelt sorrow over sin—paving the way for the Savior’s restoring love.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(75) Peter remembered the word of Jesus.--St. Luke records (Luke 22:61) that it was at this moment, probably as He was passing from the council chamber, mocked and buffeted by the officers, that "the Lord turned and looked upon Peter." That glance, full, we must believe, of tenderest pity and deepest sadness, as of one who was moved not by anger but by sorrow, recalled him to his better self, and the flood-gates of penitence were opened. From that hour we lose sight of him till the morning of the Resurrection. We may infer from his then appearing in company with John (John 20:3), that he turned in his contrition to the friend and companion of his early years, who had probably witnessed his denials, and was not repulsed. The fact that the record of his fall appears in every Gospel, may be noted as indicating that, in after years, he did not shrink from letting men know of his guilt, but sought rather that men might find in him (as St. Paul afterwards in his experience, 1Timothy 1:12-16) a proof of the mercy and tender pity of his Lord.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 75. - Peter remembered the word of Jesus. Simultaneously with the crowing of the cock, the Lord turned round, and from the chamber facing the court looked upon Peter (Luke 22:61), singled him out from all the crowd, showed that amid all his own sufferings and sorrows be had not forgotten his weak apostle. What that look did for Peter we learn by succeeding events; it is for the homilist to expatiate thereon. Christ had prayed for him, and the effect of that prayer was now felt. He went out. From the portico where the denial had taken place; he rushed from that evil company into the night, a broken-hearted man, that no human eye might witness his anguish, that alone with his conscience and God he might wrestle out repentance. Wept bitterly. Tradition asserts that all his life long Peter hereafter never could hear a cock crow without failing on his knees and weeping.
