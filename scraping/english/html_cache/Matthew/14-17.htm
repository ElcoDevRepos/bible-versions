Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
We have here only five loaves of bread and two fish,
This phrase highlights the disciples' initial response to Jesus' instruction to feed the multitude. The mention of "five loaves of bread and two fish" underscores the limited resources available, setting the stage for the miraculous feeding of the 5,000. This event is one of the few miracles recorded in all four Gospels, emphasizing its significance. The loaves were likely small barley loaves, common food for the poor, as noted in John 6:9. The fish were probably small, salted fish, typical of the region around the Sea of Galilee, where fishing was a primary occupation. This setting reflects the everyday life and sustenance of the people in first-century Palestine.

they answered.
The disciples' response indicates their practical concern and limited understanding of Jesus' power at this moment. Their answer reflects a human perspective focused on scarcity and insufficiency. This moment serves as a teaching opportunity for Jesus to demonstrate His divine provision and foreshadows the spiritual nourishment He offers. The disciples' interaction with Jesus here can be compared to other instances where they struggle to grasp His teachings and miracles, such as in Mark 8:14-21, where Jesus reminds them of the feeding miracles to illustrate His ability to provide.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is about to perform the miracle of feeding the five thousand. His compassion and divine power are key themes.

2. The Disciples
They are the ones who bring the concern of limited resources to Jesus. Their interaction with Jesus highlights their initial lack of understanding of His power.

3. The Crowd
A large group of people who have followed Jesus, eager to hear His teachings and witness His miracles. They are the recipients of the miracle.

4. The Wilderness
The setting of this event, symbolizing a place of need and dependence on God, reminiscent of the Israelites' experience in the desert.

5. Five Loaves and Two Fish
The meager resources available, which Jesus uses to demonstrate His ability to provide abundantly.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God
Just as the disciples brought their limited resources to Jesus, we are called to bring our needs and insufficiencies to God, trusting in His provision.

God's Abundance
God's ability to multiply the loaves and fish reminds us that He can take our small offerings and use them for His glory and purposes.

Faith in Action
The disciples' role in distributing the food illustrates the importance of participating in God's work, even when we don't fully understand His plans.

Compassion for Others
Jesus' concern for the physical needs of the crowd challenges us to care for the holistic well-being of those around us.

What Does Matthew 14:17 Mean
We have here

• The disciples take inventory of their immediate resources, honestly reporting what is present in that very moment—no more, no less (cf. John 6:8–9 where Andrew identifies the same small lunch).

• Their statement echoes the wilderness experience where Israel repeatedly assessed “what we have here” and found it wanting, only to see the LORD supply (Exodus 16:2-4; Numbers 11:4-9).

• It shows a first step of faith: acknowledging reality before placing it in Jesus’ hands (Psalm 62:8 “Pour out your hearts before Him; God is our refuge,”).

only five loaves of bread

• The word “only” highlights insufficiency: human resources will never meet divine assignments on their own (cf. 2 Kings 4:42-44, a man brings twenty loaves for a hundred men and Elisha multiplies them).

• Bread points back to God’s pattern of sustenance—manna in the desert, Elijah sustained by a widow’s last flour (1 Kings 17:12-16), and points forward to Jesus as “the bread of life” (John 6:35).

• Practical takeaway: scarcity in our hands becomes sufficiency in His; faith begins where human arithmetic ends.

and two fish

• Fish are everyday Galilean fare—ordinary, inexpensive, common. Jesus delights to use the commonplace to display the uncommon (1 Corinthians 1:27-29).

• Earlier He called fishermen to become “fishers of men” (Mark 1:17). Here He uses actual fish to foreshadow that future catch: what starts small under Christ’s command multiplies to bless multitudes.

• This pairing of bread and fish recurs after the resurrection when Jesus serves breakfast (John 21:9-13), reminding disciples that He remains the Provider.

they answered

• The response reveals both obedience and limitation: the disciples speak truthfully but cannot yet envision the miracle (compare Numbers 11:13-15 where Moses feels overwhelmed).

• Jesus often invites His followers to verbalize need so He can reveal sufficiency (Mark 8:5 “‘How many loaves do you have?’ ‘Seven,’ they replied,”).

• Their answer sets the stage for divine action: confession of inadequacy precedes the display of God’s power (2 Corinthians 12:9).

summary

Matthew 14:17 records an honest inventory that underlines human insufficiency—“We have here only five loaves of bread and two fish.” The disciples’ meager resources, openly acknowledged, become the raw material for a miracle once placed in Jesus’ hands. Throughout Scripture God turns scarcity into supply, ordinary items into extraordinary instruments, and candid admissions of need into moments of supernatural provision. Our calling is likewise to bring what we have—no matter how small—to the Savior who multiplies it for His glory and the good of many.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - And they say unto him, We have here (ῶδε) but five loaves (Matthew 4:3, note), and two fishes (Matthew 7:9, note). St. Matthew omits the question, "Shall we go and buy?" etc., which comes in Mark and Luke, and essentially in John (ver. 5).
