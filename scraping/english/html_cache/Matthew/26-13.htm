Truly I tell you
This phrase is a solemn declaration by Jesus, emphasizing the certainty and importance of what He is about to say. It is a common introduction in Jesus' teachings, underscoring His authority and the truthfulness of His words. This phrase is often used to introduce significant teachings or prophecies, as seen in other parts of the Gospels.

wherever this gospel is preached in all the world
Jesus is referring to the global spread of the Gospel, indicating the universal nature of His message. This anticipates the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20) where Jesus commands His disciples to make disciples of all nations. The phrase "in all the world" highlights the inclusivity of the Gospel, transcending cultural and geographical boundaries, and foreshadows the missionary work of the early church as recorded in the Book of Acts.

what she has done
This refers to the act of the woman who anointed Jesus with expensive perfume, an act of devotion and worship. In the context of Matthew 26, this woman is identified as Mary of Bethany in John 12:3. Her action is a demonstration of sacrificial love and recognition of Jesus' impending death and burial. It contrasts with the betrayal of Judas, highlighting the theme of true discipleship versus false allegiance.

will also be told in memory of her
Jesus promises that the woman's act of devotion will be remembered wherever the Gospel is preached. This statement underscores the value Jesus places on acts of genuine love and worship. It serves as an encouragement to believers that their acts of faithfulness, though seemingly small, have eternal significance. This promise has been fulfilled as her story continues to be told throughout the world, illustrating the lasting impact of sincere devotion to Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is speaking to His disciples about the actions of a woman who anointed Him with expensive perfume.

2. The Woman
Although not named in this specific verse, the woman is identified in the surrounding context as Mary of Bethany, who anointed Jesus with costly perfume as an act of devotion and preparation for His burial.

3. Bethany
A village near Jerusalem where this event took place, often associated with Jesus' friends Mary, Martha, and Lazarus.

4. The Disciples
Present during this event, they initially criticized the woman's actions, but Jesus corrected them, highlighting the significance of her deed.

5. The Gospel
The good news of Jesus Christ, which is to be preached throughout the world, and within which this woman's act of devotion is to be remembered.
Teaching Points
The Value of Devotion
The woman's act of anointing Jesus with expensive perfume demonstrates the value of wholehearted devotion to Christ. Her willingness to give something precious reflects the call for believers to offer their best to God.

Eternal Significance of Our Actions
Jesus' promise that the woman's deed will be remembered wherever the gospel is preached highlights that acts of faith and love have eternal significance. Believers are encouraged to live in a way that honors God and impacts others.

Understanding True Worship
The woman's act was misunderstood by the disciples, yet Jesus affirmed it as true worship. This teaches us to discern and value genuine expressions of worship, even when they defy conventional expectations.

Preparation for Christ's Sacrifice
The anointing prefigures Jesus' burial, reminding believers of the importance of preparing their hearts for the reality of Christ's sacrifice and resurrection.

Legacy of Faith
The enduring memory of the woman's act challenges believers to consider the legacy of their faith and actions. What we do for Christ can leave a lasting impact beyond our lifetime.

What Does Matthew 26:13 Mean
Truly I tell you

Jesus opens with, “Truly I tell you,” underscoring absolute authority. Every “Truly” (Matthew 24:34; John 3:3) is a divine signature that what follows is utterly reliable. He wants the disciples—and us—to hear these words as settled fact, not hopeful possibility.

Wherever this gospel is preached

The anointing at Bethany is not a private footnote; it is woven into the fabric of the gospel message (Mark 14:9). The same gospel that must be proclaimed “to all nations” (Matthew 24:14; Acts 1:8) carries her story along with the death, burial, and resurrection it foreshadows.

In all the world

Jesus looks far beyond Jerusalem.

• His words anticipate the global reach later described in Romans 10:18 and Colossians 1:6.

• The promise affirms His sovereign plan to take the good news past every border, proving His lordship over geography, culture, and time.

What she has done

Mary’s simple, extravagant act—pouring costly perfume on Jesus’ head and feet (Matthew 26:12; John 12:3)—is heaven’s definition of significant service.

• It prepares Him for burial before the cross is even visible to others.

• It declares His worth, much like the heavens declare God’s glory (Psalm 19:1).

Will also be told

God refuses to let such devotion fade. Just as memorial stones reminded Israel of God’s acts (Joshua 4:7), Mary’s deed will accompany gospel proclamation, inspiring future believers to wholehearted worship (2 Corinthians 9:13).

In memory of her

The Lord personally guarantees her remembrance. Scripture says, “The memory of the righteous is a blessing” (Proverbs 10:7), and “God is not unjust; He will not forget your work” (Hebrews 6:10). Her legacy is secured by Christ Himself, the One who remembers every cup of cold water given in His name (Matthew 10:42).

summary

Jesus turns a single act of costly love into a timeless testimony. By His authoritative word, Mary’s devotion travels with the gospel to every corner of the earth, reminding believers that genuine worship never goes unnoticed and always impacts eternity.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Wheresoever this gospel shall be preached.--The prediction tended, of course assuming the extension of the gospel, to bring about its own fulfilment, but the prevision at such a moment of that universal extension may well take its place among the proofs of a foreknowledge not less than divine. Others saw victory only, and that immediate; He saw condemnation and shame and death, yet not these only, but through them a victory and dominion over the souls of men beyond their wildest dreams.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Wheresoever this gospel shall be preached. This weighty promise and prediction is introduced by the emphasizing formula, Verily I say unto you. The gospel is the story of the incarnation of Jesus - his life, teaching, death, resurrection, which implies written documents as well as oral exposition. Our Lord had already (Matthew 24:14) intimated that the gospel of the kingdom should be published throughout the world; he here affirms that Mary's deed shall be enshrined therein for all time. There shall also this, that this woman hath done (λαληθήσεται καὶ ο{ ἐποίησεν αὕτη, that also which this woman did) be told for a memorial of her. The history which records the grudging remonstrance of the disciples contains this remarkable approval of Mary's act, associating her forever with the Passion of the Lord. We may here quote the eloquent comment of Chrysostom, who, however, unreasonably identifies Mary with the sinner who previously anointed Jesus. "Who then proclaimed if, and caused it to be spread abroad? It was the power of him who is speaking these words. And while of countless kings and generals the noble exploits, even of those whose memorials remain, have sunk into silence; and having overthrown cities, and encompassed them with walls, and set up trophies, and enslaved many nations, they are not known so much as by hearsay, nor by name, though they have both set up statues, and established laws; yet that a woman who was a harlot poured out oil in the house of some leper, in the presence of ten men, - this all men celebrate throughout the world; and so great a time has passed, and yet the memory of that which was done hath not faded away, but alike Persians and Indians, Scythians and Thracians, and Sarmatians, and the race or the Moors, and they that inhabit the British Islands, spread abroad that which was done secretly in a house by a woman" ('Ham. 80. in Matthew').
