Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
News about Him spread all over Syria
The region of Syria during the time of Jesus was a Roman province that included a vast area north of Galilee. The spread of news about Jesus indicates the rapid dissemination of His fame beyond Jewish territories, highlighting the early reach of His ministry. This foreshadows the later mission to the Gentiles and fulfills the prophecy of Isaiah 9:1-2, which speaks of a great light shining in Galilee of the Gentiles. The mention of Syria underscores the geographical and cultural diversity of those who were drawn to Jesus.

and people brought to Him all who were ill with various diseases
The act of bringing the sick to Jesus demonstrates the faith and desperation of the people seeking healing. In the cultural context, illness was often seen as a result of sin or spiritual affliction, and the ability to heal was a sign of divine authority. This phrase emphasizes Jesus' role as the Great Physician, fulfilling the messianic expectations of one who would bear our infirmities, as prophesied in Isaiah 53:4.

those suffering acute pain
Acute pain could refer to both physical and emotional suffering. In a time without modern medicine, chronic pain was a significant burden. Jesus' healing of such conditions illustrates His compassion and power over human suffering, aligning with His mission to bring relief and restoration to a broken world.

the demon-possessed
Demon possession was a recognized condition in the ancient world, often associated with mental and physical afflictions. Jesus' authority over demons is a testament to His divine power and the inbreaking of God's kingdom, as He liberates individuals from spiritual bondage. This act fulfills the prophecy of Genesis 3:15, where the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head, symbolizing victory over evil.

those having seizures
Seizures, often misunderstood in ancient times, were sometimes attributed to demonic influence. Jesus' healing of those with seizures demonstrates His authority over both natural and supernatural realms. This act of healing reflects His compassion and the holistic nature of His ministry, addressing both physical and spiritual needs.

and the paralyzed
Paralysis was a debilitating condition that left individuals dependent on others. Jesus' healing of the paralyzed not only restored physical mobility but also symbolized spiritual liberation and forgiveness of sins, as seen in the healing of the paralytic in Matthew 9:1-8. This act points to Jesus' authority to forgive sins and His role as the one who makes all things new.

and He healed them
The comprehensive nature of Jesus' healing ministry is highlighted here. His ability to heal all manner of diseases and afflictions confirms His identity as the Messiah and the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies concerning the coming of a healer and redeemer. This phrase encapsulates the hope and restoration that Jesus brings, serving as a foretaste of the ultimate healing and restoration in the kingdom of God.

Teaching Points
The Compassion of Christ
Jesus' willingness to heal all who came to Him demonstrates His deep compassion for human suffering. We are called to emulate this compassion in our interactions with others.

The Authority of Jesus
Jesus' ability to heal and cast out demons underscores His divine authority. As believers, we can trust in His power over all aspects of life, including our struggles and challenges.

Faith and Action
The people brought the sick to Jesus, showing their faith in His ability to heal. We are encouraged to bring our needs and the needs of others to Jesus in prayer, trusting in His power and willingness to intervene.

The Spread of the Gospel
The news of Jesus' works spread throughout Syria, illustrating the natural spread of the Gospel through testimony. We are called to share the good news of Jesus' power and love with others.

Holistic Healing
Jesus' ministry addressed both physical and spiritual needs, reminding us of the importance of caring for the whole person in our ministry efforts.

What Does Matthew 4:24 Mean
News about Him spread all over Syria

“News about Him spread all over Syria”.

- Word-of-mouth traveled fast; no social media, just eyewitness testimony (John 1:40-42).

- Jesus’ ministry was never hidden (Luke 4:14-15).

- The reach into “Syria” shows Gentile territory already feeling the impact, foreshadowing the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19).

and people brought to Him all who were ill with various diseases

- Faith acts: friends carry the hurting (Mark 1:32-34).

- “Various diseases” proves no limit to His power (Psalm 103:2-3).

- Healing fulfills Isaiah’s forecast of Messiah bearing our sicknesses (Isaiah 53:4; Matthew 8:16-17).

those suffering acute pain

- Jesus cares about immediate, intense suffering—as much as chronic (Matthew 20:29-34).

- He invites the weary and burdened to Himself (Matthew 11:28-30).

- Physical pain often opens hearts to spiritual truth (John 5:14).

the demon-possessed

- Scripture treats demons as real personalities (Mark 1:23-26).

- Christ’s authority silences and expels them (Luke 4:36).

- Deliverance is a sign the kingdom of God has come near (Matthew 12:28).

those having seizures

- Also rendered “lunatics” (Matthew 17:15), describing convulsions.

- Shows Jesus’ dominion over neurological disorders (Mark 9:25-27).

- Highlights His compassion for conditions misunderstood or feared by society.

and the paralyzed

- Paralysis pictures helplessness; friends lowered one through a roof (Mark 2:1-12).

- Healing demonstrates forgiveness authority: “Which is easier…?”

- Fulfillment of Isaiah 35:6, “Then the lame will leap like a deer.”

and He healed them

- No exceptions, no failures (Matthew 12:15).

- Power validates His identity: “The blind receive sight… the lame walk…” (Matthew 11:4-5).

- Previews the complete restoration promised in the coming kingdom (Revelation 21:4).

- Confirms Acts 10:38—Jesus “went about doing good and healing all who were oppressed by the devil.”

summary

Matthew 4:24 showcases Jesus’ fame, the breadth of human misery, and the sweeping authority of the Messiah. News spreads, faith responds, every category of affliction—physical, mental, spiritual—meets the Savior. He heals all, proving His compassion, His kingly power, and the nearness of God’s kingdom.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) Throughout all Syria.--The word is probably used popularly, rather than with the definite significance of the Roman province with which St. Luke uses it in Luke 2:2. Our Lord's ministry, with the one exception of the journey to the coasts of Tyre and Sidon (Matthew 15:21), was confined to what is commonly known as Palestine. Traces of the wider fame are, however, found in the mention of hearers from Idumaea, and Tyre, and Sidon among the crowds that followed Him (Mark 3:8); in the faith of the Syro-Ph?nician woman in His power to heal (Mark 7:26); perhaps in the existence of disciples at Damascus so soon after the Ascension (Acts 9:2); perhaps, also, in St. Peter's appeal to the friends of Cornelius at Caesarea, as knowing already the broad facts of our Lord's ministry and miraculous working (Acts 10:37). . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - And his fame; Revised Version, and the report of him (ἡ ἀκοὴ αὐτοῦ). Our use of the word "fame" implies reputation and honour, which are not included under ἀκοή. Went throughout all (ver. 23) Syria; Revised Version, went forth into; ἀπῆλθεν εἰς. The expression not merely means that the report spread far and wide, but that it went beyond the expected limits of the Holy Land into the whole of Syria, i.e., probably, the Roman province with which Palestine was in some degree (Schurer, 1:2:46) incorporated. All sick people that were taken with divers diseases; Revised Version, grammatically, all that were sick, holden with, etc. Possibly, "all that were sick" is the genus of which the following expressions represent species; but Matthew 8:16 and Mark 1:32-34 suggest that the words all to diseases refer to bodily diseases only. The arrangement would then be

(1) bodily diseases,

(a) ordinary (ποικίλαις νόσοις),

(b) violent and painful cases (βασάνοις); . . .
