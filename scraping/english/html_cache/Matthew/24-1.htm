Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
As Jesus left the temple and was walking away
This phrase marks a significant moment in the Gospel narrative, as Jesus departs from the temple in Jerusalem, symbolizing a transition from the old covenant to the new. The temple, a central place of worship and sacrifice for the Jewish people, represents the religious system that Jesus often critiqued for its legalism and hypocrisy. His departure can be seen as a prophetic act, foreshadowing the eventual destruction of the temple in AD 70 by the Romans, as well as the establishment of a new covenant through His death and resurrection. This act also signifies Jesus' role as the true temple, where God's presence dwells among His people.

His disciples came up to Him
The disciples, who are often portrayed as learners and followers of Jesus, approach Him with questions and observations. This reflects their ongoing journey of understanding and their desire to comprehend the significance of Jesus' actions and teachings. The disciples' approach indicates their respect and recognition of Jesus as their teacher and leader, seeking His insight into the events unfolding around them.

to point out its buildings
The disciples' focus on the temple buildings highlights their awe and admiration for the grandeur and beauty of the temple complex. Herod the Great had expanded and renovated the temple, making it an architectural marvel of the ancient world. This admiration also underscores the disciples' attachment to the physical and traditional aspects of their faith. However, Jesus' subsequent teachings in this chapter will challenge them to look beyond the physical structures to the spiritual realities of God's kingdom. This moment sets the stage for Jesus to deliver the Olivet Discourse, where He speaks about the end times and the coming of the Son of Man, urging His followers to be vigilant and prepared.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
Central figure in the New Testament, the Son of God, who is leaving the temple in this passage.

2. The Disciples
Followers of Jesus who are with Him and draw His attention to the temple buildings.

3. The Temple
The Jewish temple in Jerusalem, a significant religious and cultural symbol for the Jewish people.

4. Jerusalem
The city where the temple is located, a central place in Jewish history and prophecy.

5. The Temple Buildings
The structures of the temple complex, noted for their grandeur and significance.
Teaching Points
The Transience of Earthly Structures
The disciples' admiration of the temple's buildings highlights human tendency to focus on physical grandeur. Jesus' later teachings remind us that earthly structures are temporary.

Spiritual Significance Over Physical Appearance
While the temple was magnificent, Jesus emphasizes the importance of spiritual truths over physical appearances. Our focus should be on eternal, spiritual matters rather than temporary, material ones.

Prophetic Fulfillment
Jesus' later prophecy about the temple's destruction (fulfilled in A.D. 70) underscores the reliability of His words and the importance of understanding biblical prophecy.

Awareness of God's Presence
The temple was a symbol of God's presence among His people. Today, believers are called to be aware of God's presence in their lives, as they are the temple of the Holy Spirit.

What Does Matthew 24:1 Mean
As Jesus left the temple

– Jesus is physically departing the very place where God’s presence was meant to dwell (1 Kings 8:10-11; Matthew 23:38).

– His exit follows a series of woes pronounced on the religious leaders, underscoring that divine judgment is imminent (Matthew 23:13-36).

– The action mirrors Ezekiel’s vision of God’s glory leaving the first temple (Ezekiel 10:18-19), hinting that something similar is unfolding here.

and was walking away

– The simple movement away signals a decisive break; Jesus is turning from the old covenant system soon to be fulfilled and replaced (Hebrews 8:13; John 4:21-23).

– This walk sets the stage for the Olivet Discourse, where He will explain future events in response to His departure.

His disciples came up to Him

– The disciples sense something weighty has just happened and want clarification, showing their ongoing need for teaching (Mark 13:1; Luke 21:5).

– Their approach illustrates intimacy: they can draw near to Christ even when confused, a pattern for every believer (Hebrews 4:16).

to point out its buildings

– They admire the grandeur of Herod’s temple complex—massive stones, gold plating, and impressive courts (Josephus, Antiquities 15.11, but see Mark 13:1 for the biblical description).

– The disciples, like many first-century Jews, link the temple’s splendor to God’s favor; Jesus will soon correct that assumption (Luke 19:41-44).

– By focusing on architecture, they reveal a natural tendency to value the visible and immediate, while Jesus is about to speak of eternal realities (2 Corinthians 4:18).

summary

Matthew 24:1 captures a pivotal moment: Jesus steps out of the temple, symbolically ending an era, while His disciples, still captivated by earthly glory, seek His perspective. The verse invites us to follow Christ outside the structures we might trust, listen to His prophetic word, and value what He values—the unseen kingdom that will never be shaken (Hebrews 12:27-28).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XXIV.

(1) And Jesus went out.--Better, following the best MSS., Jesus departed from the Temple, and was going on His way, when His disciples. St. Mark and St. Luke report the touching incident of the widow's mite as connected with our Lord's departure.

His disciples came to him.--We may well think of their action as following on the words they had just heard. Was that house, with all its goodly buildings and great stones, its golden and its "beautiful" gates (Acts 3:2)--through which they had probably passed--its porticos, its marble cupolas, the structural and ornamental offerings which had accumulated during the forty-six years that had passed since Herod had begun his work of improvement (John 2:20), to be left "desolate"? Would not the sight of its glories lead Him to recall those words of evil omen? This seems a far more natural explanation than that which sees in what they were doing only the natural wonder of Galilean peasants at the splendour of the Holy City. They had seen it too often, we may add, to feel much wonder.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-51. - PROPHECY OF THE DESTRUCTION OF JERUSALEM, AND OF THE TIMES OF THE END. (Mark 13:1-37; Luke 21:5-36.) There is no reason to think, with Olshauson, that St. Matthew or his editor has considerably amplified the original discourse of our Lord by introducing details and expressions from other quarters. The discourse, as we now have it (ch. 24. and 25.), forms a distinct whole, divided into certain portions closely related to each other and it would have been unnatural in St. Matthew, and opposed to his simple and veracious style, to have put words into our Lord's mouth at this moment, which were not actually uttered by him on this solemn occasion. Verses 1-3. - Occasion of the discourse. (Mark 13:1-4; Luke 21:5-7.) Verse 1. - From the temple; Revised Version, went out from the temple, and was going on his way (ἐπορεύετο). So the best manuscripts and versions. It was while he was proceeding on the route to Bethany that the disciples interrupted him with their remarks about the temple. He had now taken his final leave of the hallowed courts; the prophecy of the desolation of the house was beginning to be fulfilled (see on Matthew 23:38). His disciples came to him. They were disquieted by Christ's words recorded at the end of the last chapter, which spoke of a terrible retribution about to fall, of the desolation of the temple, of Christ's own departure for a time. St. Mark (Mark 13:3) tells us that Peter, James, John, and Andrew asked him privately when these things should be, and what signs should forewarn of their approach, as in ver. 3. St. Matthew records here that his disciples came to him for to show (ἐπιδεῖξαι, to display) him the buildings of the temple (ἱεροῦ, the whole sacred enclosure). They had gathered from his words that destruction awaited this edifice, but as they gazed upon it they could scarcely bring themselves to believe in its coming overthrow. So as they gained some commanding point of view, they drew Christ's attention to its beauty, magnificence, and unequalled solidity, desiring him to explain further the mode and time of the catastrophe. It was popularly said, "He who never saw the temple of Herod has never seen a fine building."
