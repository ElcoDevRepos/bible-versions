Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Go rather
This directive emphasizes a specific mission focus. Jesus instructs His disciples to prioritize their efforts, indicating a deliberate and strategic approach to ministry. This reflects the principle of divine order and purpose in God's plan. The use of "rather" suggests a contrast to a broader mission, highlighting the importance of following divine guidance in ministry work.

to the lost sheep
The term "lost sheep" is a metaphor for those who have strayed from the covenant relationship with God. In the Old Testament, Israel is often depicted as God's flock, with God as the shepherd (Psalm 23, Ezekiel 34). The imagery of sheep suggests vulnerability and the need for guidance and protection. This phrase underscores the compassion and urgency in reaching those who are spiritually wayward.

of Israel
This phrase identifies the initial target audience of Jesus' ministry. Israel, as God's chosen people, had a unique covenant relationship with Him. The focus on Israel aligns with the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies concerning the Messiah's coming to His people (Isaiah 53, Jeremiah 31:31-34). It also reflects Jesus' role as the promised King and Shepherd of Israel, fulfilling the Abrahamic and Davidic covenants. This mission to Israel sets the stage for the later expansion of the gospel to the Gentiles, as seen in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker of this verse, Jesus is instructing His disciples on their mission. He is the central figure in the New Testament, the Son of God, and the Savior of humanity.

2. The Disciples
The immediate audience of Jesus' instruction. They are being sent out to preach and perform miracles as part of their mission.

3. The Lost Sheep of the House of Israel
This phrase refers to the Jewish people who have strayed from their covenant relationship with God. It emphasizes Jesus' initial focus on the Jewish nation.

4. Israel
The nation chosen by God in the Old Testament, representing God's covenant people. Jesus' ministry initially focuses on Israel before extending to the Gentiles.

5. The Mission
The event of sending the disciples to preach the Kingdom of Heaven, heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse lepers, and cast out demons.
Teaching Points
Focus on the Covenant People
Jesus' initial mission was to the Jews, highlighting the importance of reaching those who have a foundational understanding of God but have strayed.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The focus on Israel demonstrates God's faithfulness to His covenant promises, reminding us of His unchanging nature.

Prioritizing the Lost
Like Jesus, we should prioritize reaching those who are spiritually lost, especially those within our own communities or families.

Understanding God's Timing
Jesus' mission strategy shows the importance of understanding and following God's timing and plan in ministry.

Expanding the Mission
While the initial focus was on Israel, the mission eventually expanded to all nations, teaching us to be open to God's broader plans.

What Does Matthew 10:6 Mean
Go

Jesus’ first word is a call to action. “Go” (Matthew 10:6) moves the disciples from classroom to mission field.

• Matthew 28:19 repeats the same verb: “Therefore go and make disciples of all nations.”

• Mark 16:15 echoes it: “Go into all the world and preach the gospel to every creature.”

The Lord never leaves His followers idle; obedience means movement toward people who need Him.

rather

The adverb narrows the assignment. The disciples might have assumed a wide-open itinerary, but Jesus redirects their steps.

• Acts 1:8 shows the pattern: Jerusalem first, then Judea, Samaria, and the ends of the earth.

• Romans 1:16 underscores priority: the gospel is “first to the Jew, then to the Greek.”

“Rather” teaches that while the message is universal, God sometimes assigns specific targets for strategic reasons.

to the lost sheep

Jesus paints a tender picture: people wandering, vulnerable, needing a shepherd.

• Isaiah 53:6 reminds us, “We all like sheep have gone astray.”

• Ezekiel 34:11-16 promises God Himself will search for His scattered sheep.

• Luke 15:4-7 tells of the shepherd leaving ninety-nine to rescue one lost sheep.

The phrase signals compassion, not condemnation. The mission is rescue, not merely recruitment.

of Israel

The focus is the covenant people, descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

• Genesis 12:1-3 establishes Israel as the channel of blessing to all nations.

• Jeremiah 31:31 forecasts a new covenant “with the house of Israel.”

• Matthew 15:24 records Jesus saying, “I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel.”

Israel’s Messiah must first be offered to Israel; only then will the blessing overflow to the Gentiles (Romans 9:4-5).

summary

Matthew 10:6 directs the disciples to an immediate, focused mission: actively obey (“Go”), embrace a God-given priority (“rather”), pursue the spiritually needy (“lost sheep”), and honor God’s covenant order (“of Israel”). By starting with Israel, Jesus affirms the Father’s faithful promises and sets the stage for the gospel’s eventual reach to every tribe and tongue.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - But go. On your daily journeying (πορέεσθε, present). Rather. With conscious preference. To the lost sheep of the house of Israel. Thus also he describes his own mission (Matthew 15:24). The words recall Jeremiah 50:6 (27:6, LXX.), "My people hath been lost sheep." Observe that our Lord implies a special relation of Israel to God (for the house has its owner) which was lacking in the case of all other nations. Yet, their proper teachers having proved faithless, they were now as shepherdless as these (Matthew 9:36). Lost. Notice here the basis of the parable related in Luke 15:4-7; cf. Matthew 18:12, 13 (ver. 11 of the Received Text is a gloss), where the term "wandering" is not so strong (Bengel).
