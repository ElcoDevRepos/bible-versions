Then John’s disciples came
John the Baptist had a group of dedicated followers who respected him as a prophet and teacher. This phrase indicates their loyalty and devotion, as they took the initiative to care for his remains. In the cultural context of the time, disciples often took responsibility for their teacher's affairs, especially in death, reflecting a deep bond and respect.

and took his body
The act of taking John's body shows the disciples' courage and commitment, as approaching the body of someone executed by Herod could have been dangerous. This reflects the Jewish custom of burying the dead promptly, as seen in Deuteronomy 21:23, which emphasizes the importance of burial on the same day.

and buried it.
Burial was a significant act of honor and respect in Jewish culture. The disciples' actions fulfilled the Jewish law and customs regarding the treatment of the dead. This also parallels the later burial of Jesus, where His followers ensured He was buried according to Jewish customs, as seen in John 19:38-42.

And they went and informed Jesus.
The disciples' decision to inform Jesus highlights their recognition of His authority and their need for guidance in a time of grief. This act of informing Jesus also signifies the transition of focus from John the Baptist to Jesus, as John's role was to prepare the way for Christ, as prophesied in Isaiah 40:3 and fulfilled in Matthew 3:3.

Persons / Places / Events
1. John the Baptist
A prophet and forerunner of Jesus, known for his call to repentance and baptizing Jesus. He was imprisoned and executed by Herod Antipas.

2. John's Disciples
Followers of John the Baptist who remained loyal to him even after his death. They took responsibility for his burial and informed Jesus of the events.

3. Herod Antipas
The ruler of Galilee who ordered the execution of John the Baptist. His actions were influenced by Herodias and her daughter.

4. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who was informed of John's death by John's disciples.

5. Burial of John
An act of respect and devotion by John's disciples, signifying the end of John's earthly ministry and the transition to Jesus' ministry.
Teaching Points
Loyalty and Devotion
John's disciples demonstrated loyalty by caring for his body and informing Jesus. We are called to show loyalty to our leaders and mentors in faith, even in difficult times.

Respect for the Deceased
The act of burial is a sign of respect and honor. As Christians, we should honor those who have gone before us in faith, recognizing their contributions to God's kingdom.

Transition of Ministry
John's death marked a transition to Jesus' ministry. We should be open to God's timing and the transitions He brings in our lives and ministries.

Informing Jesus
The disciples' act of informing Jesus highlights the importance of bringing our concerns and burdens to Christ, who is our ultimate source of comfort and guidance.

Courage in Adversity
Despite the risk, John's disciples acted courageously. We are encouraged to act with courage and faithfulness, even when facing adversity or loss.

What Does Matthew 14:12 Mean
Then John’s disciples came

“Then John’s disciples came…” (Matthew 14:12).

• Loyalty on display: these men have already stayed with John through his imprisonment (Matthew 11:2; Luke 7:18) and now refuse to abandon him in death.

• Connection to Jesus: some of these same disciples had earlier sought Jesus’ guidance on fasting (Matthew 9:14) and had heard John identify Jesus as “the Lamb of God” (John 1:35-36). Their movement toward Jesus is a natural continuation of their mentor’s witness.

• A lesson: faithful friends step in even when the cost is high. Herod’s court was hostile (Mark 6:20-28), yet John’s followers courageously approach the fortress to claim the body.

took his body

“…and took his body…” (Matthew 14:12).

• The grisly reality: Mark 6:29 records that the head had been presented to Herodias; only the mutilated torso remained. Scripture treats this as historical fact, underscoring the price John paid for righteousness (cf. Matthew 11:11).

• Respect for God’s servant: retrieving a martyr’s remains reflects a conviction that bodies matter to God (1 Corinthians 6:19-20).

• Echoes of earlier honorings: Abraham secured Sarah’s body for burial (Genesis 23:19); devout men later buried Stephen after his stoning (Acts 8:2).

and buried it.

“…and buried it.” (Matthew 14:12).

• Swift burial was a Jewish duty (Deuteronomy 21:23). John’s disciples fulfill the Law and honor their prophet.

• Hope embedded in sorrow: burial anticipates resurrection (Job 19:25-27; Daniel 12:2). Placing John in the earth is not the end of his story.

• Practical compassion: the disciples’ actions spare John’s body from further desecration, mirroring the kindness shown to Jesus by Joseph of Arimathea (Matthew 27:57-60).

And they went and informed Jesus.

“…And they went and informed Jesus.” (Matthew 14:12).

• Turning to the right Person: grief propels them to the One John had pointed them toward all along (John 3:28-30).

• Jesus’ response: “When Jesus heard this, He withdrew… ” (Matthew 14:13). His retreat shows compassion for the mourners and sets up the feeding of the five thousand, demonstrating that even in loss His ministry of life continues.

• Application points:

– Bring every heartache to Christ (Psalm 62:8; 1 Peter 5:7).

– Expect His compassionate leadership; He never minimizes suffering (Hebrews 4:15-16).

– Note the chain of witness: John prepares the way; his disciples testify to Jesus; Jesus strengthens them for new service (Mark 6:30-32).

summary

Matthew 14:12 presents a sober, literal account of John the Baptist’s followers: courageous in claiming his body, faithful in giving him a proper burial, and wise in carrying their sorrow straight to Jesus. The verse models honor for God’s servants, hope in bodily resurrection, and the instinct to seek Christ in every trial—principles that remain vital for believers today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) His disciples came.--Among those who thus transferred their allegiance to their true Lord were, we must believe, the two whom John had sent to Him from his prison. From this time they probably ceased in Judaea to be a distinct community, though, as the instances of Apollos (Acts 18:25) and the disciples at Ephesus (Acts 19:3) show, they still maintained a separate existence in the more distant regions to which the influence of the Baptist had indirectly penetrated.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - And his disciples came. "And when his disciples heard thereof, they came" (Mark). Perhaps they were not permitted to be so much with him as at an earlier period in his imprisonment (Matthew 11:2). But if the murder was in the evening, as would appear probable from the circumstances of it, they would naturally not be in the castle at the time. And took up the body; the corpse (Revised Version, τὸ πτῶμα). And buried it; him, (Revised Version, αὐτόν). It is right in Mark, but St. Matthew has preserved the more popular form of expression. And (Revised Version adds they) went and told Jesus. Matthew only. In Mark (Mark 6:30; cf. also Luke 9:10) this expression dearly belongs to the next paragraph, and is predicated of the twelve apostles on their return from their mission (Mark 6:7-12; our Matthew 10:5). It looks as though some confusion had arisen in the source before St. Matthew used it. As the words stand here they show the kindly feelings which both John and his disciples felt towards our Lord
