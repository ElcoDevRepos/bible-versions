The Lord said to my Lord
This phrase is a direct quotation from Psalm 110:1, a psalm attributed to David. In the context of Matthew 22:44, Jesus uses this verse to challenge the Pharisees' understanding of the Messiah's identity. The first "Lord" refers to Yahweh, the covenant name of God in the Old Testament. The second "Lord" refers to the Messiah, indicating a divine status. This highlights the dual nature of Christ as both David's descendant and his Lord, emphasizing His divinity and pre-existence.

Sit at My right hand
Sitting at the right hand is a position of honor and authority. In ancient Near Eastern cultures, the right hand of a king was a place reserved for the most trusted and powerful individuals. This phrase signifies the Messiah's exaltation and authority, affirming His role as King and Judge. In the New Testament, this imagery is used to describe Jesus' ascension and His current reign in heaven (Acts 2:33, Hebrews 1:3).

until I put Your enemies under Your feet
This phrase reflects the ancient practice of victors placing their feet on the necks of their defeated foes, symbolizing total conquest and subjugation. It is a prophetic declaration of the ultimate victory of the Messiah over all His enemies, including sin, death, and Satan. This imagery is echoed in 1 Corinthians 15:25-27, where Paul speaks of Christ reigning until all His enemies are defeated, culminating in the final victory at His second coming.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Lord (Yahweh)
Refers to God the Father, the supreme authority in the universe.

2. My Lord (Adonai)
Refers to Jesus Christ, the Messiah, whom David acknowledges as his Lord.

3. David
The author of the Psalm being quoted (Psalm 110:1), a king of Israel and a prophet.

4. Pharisees
Religious leaders of the time who questioned Jesus about His identity and authority.

5. Enemies
Symbolic of all forces opposed to Christ, both spiritual and earthly.
Teaching Points
Christ's Authority
Jesus is not just a descendant of David but is exalted above him, affirming His divine authority and identity as the Messiah.

Fulfillment of Prophecy
This verse demonstrates the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy, showing the continuity and reliability of Scripture.

Victory Over Enemies
Believers can have confidence in Christ's ultimate victory over all opposition, both spiritual and physical.

Position of Honor
Sitting at the right hand signifies a position of power and honor, reminding us of Christ's exalted status and our call to honor Him in our lives.

Invitation to Rest in Christ's Victory
As Christ is seated, believers are invited to rest in His completed work and ongoing reign, trusting in His sovereign plan.

What Does Matthew 22:44 Mean
The Lord said to my Lord

• David records a divine conversation (Psalm 110:1), revealing two distinct Persons: the covenant God (“The Lord”) and David’s sovereign (“my Lord”).

• Jesus cites this verse in Matthew 22:41-46 to show that the promised Messiah is greater than David—both David’s Son and David’s Lord.

• Acts 2:34-36 confirms that the risen Jesus fulfills this role; Peter proclaims that God has made Him “both Lord and Christ.”

• Hebrews 1:13 applies the same verse to Jesus, underscoring His supremacy over angels and all creation.

• The phrase establishes Christ’s divine identity and eternal preexistence while affirming that Scripture speaks with perfect unity and accuracy.

Sit at My right hand

• The right hand of God is the place of highest honor and absolute authority (Mark 16:19; Ephesians 1:20-22).

• After His resurrection, Jesus ascended and “sat down at the right hand of God” (Hebrews 1:3), signifying that His atoning work is finished and perfectly accepted.

• Romans 8:34 points to this seated position as the believer’s assurance: the One who intercedes for us rules from the throne of heaven.

• This enthronement is not symbolic only; it affirms Christ’s present reign over the universe while He gathers His people and advances His kingdom.

• For us, it means we approach a reigning, victorious Savior who welcomes us into His grace (Colossians 3:1).

until I put Your enemies under Your feet

• The Father promises a decisive, ongoing subjugation of every force opposed to Christ (1 Corinthians 15:24-28).

• Hebrews 10:12-13 pictures Jesus waiting confidently as each enemy is made His footstool—already defeated at the cross, yet progressively manifested in history.

• These enemies include Satan (Revelation 20:10), sin’s dominion (Romans 6:14), death itself (1 Corinthians 15:26), and every rebellious nation or power (Psalm 2:8-12).

• Revelation 19:11-16 portrays the final stage, when the King of kings returns to consummate the victory promised in Matthew 22:44.

• Believers share in this triumph, standing firm in faith while God works all things under Christ’s sovereign feet.

summary

Matthew 22:44 reveals the Father’s public enthronement of His Son, assuring us that Jesus is both divine Lord and conquering Messiah. He now reigns at God’s right hand, His redemptive work complete, while the Father actively subdues every enemy. This unshakeable promise anchors our hope, fuels our worship, and calls us to live confidently under the authority of the risen, reigning King.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 44. - The Lord said unto my Lord (Psalm 110:1). The quotation is from the Septuagint. But neither this nor our English Version is an adequate rendering of the original, where the word translated "Lord" is not the same in both parts of the clause, More accurately, the solemn beginning of the psalm is thus given: "Utterance [or, 'oracle'] of Jehovah to my Lord (Adonai)." The psalmist acknowledges the recipient of the utterance as his sovereign Lord; this could be no earthly potentate, for on earth he had no such superior; Jewish tradition always applied the term unto the Messiah, or the Word. The prediction repeats the promise made by Nathan to David (2 Samuel 7:12), which had no fulfilment in his natural progeny, and could be regarded as looking forward only to the Messiah. Sit thou on my right hand. Thus Messiah is exalted to the highest dignity in heaven. Sitting at God's right hand does not necessarily imply complete Divine majesty (as Hengstenberg remarks), for the sons of Zebedee had asked for such a position in Messiah's earthly kingdom (Matthew 20:21); but it denotes supreme honour, association in government, authority second only to that of Monarch. This is said of Christ in his human nature. He is "equal to the Father, as touching his Godhead; inferior to the Father, as touching his manhood." In his Divine nature he could receive nothing; in his human nature all "power was given unto him in heaven and earth" (Matthew 28:18). Till I make (ἕως α}ν θῷ) thine enemies thy footstool; ὑποπόδιον τῶν ποδῶν σου. This is the Septuagint reading. Many manuscripts here give ὑποκάτω τῶν ποδῶν σου Τιλλ Ι πυτ τηινε ενεμιεσ υνδερνεατη τηψ φεετ. Some few have both ὑποπόδιον and ὑποκάτω. Vulgate, Donec ponam inimicos tuos scabellum pedum tuorum. The complete subjection of all adversaries is denoted (comp. 1 Corinthians 15:25-27; Hebrews 1:13); and they are subjected not merely for punishment and destruction, but, it may be, for salvation and glory. The relative particle "till" must not be pressed, as if Christ's session was to cease when his victory was completed. We have before had occasion to observe that the phrase, ἕως οῦ, or ἕως α}ν, asserts nothing of the future beyond the event specified. As St. Jerome says of such negative phrases, "Ita negant praeteritum ut non ponant futurum" (comp. Matthew 1:25; Matthew 5:26; Matthew 18:34). Of Christ's kingdom there is no end.
