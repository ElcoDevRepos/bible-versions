Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The servant who had received the five talents
This phrase introduces the servant entrusted with the largest amount of money, indicating a high level of trust from the master. In biblical times, a talent was a significant sum, often equated to about 20 years of a laborer's wages. This servant represents those given substantial gifts or responsibilities by God. The number five can symbolize grace and responsibility in biblical numerology.

went at once
The immediacy of the servant's actions highlights his diligence and eagerness to fulfill his master's expectations. This reflects the biblical principle of prompt obedience, as seen in Abraham's immediate response to God's commands (Genesis 22:3). It underscores the importance of not delaying in using one's God-given gifts.

and put them to work
This phrase indicates the servant's proactive approach to stewardship. In the cultural context of the time, investing money was a common practice, often involving trade or lending. The servant's actions align with the biblical call to be fruitful and multiply (Genesis 1:28), extending beyond physical offspring to include the multiplication of resources and talents.

and gained five more
The doubling of the talents signifies successful stewardship and the blessing of God on diligent efforts. This outcome reflects the biblical principle that faithful service leads to increase, as seen in the parable of the sower (Matthew 13:23). It also prefigures the rewards of faithful service in the kingdom of heaven, where spiritual investments yield eternal dividends.

What Does Matthew 25:16 Mean
The servant

“The servant” reminds us that in the parable each person is under the authority of the Master, representing the Lord (Matthew 25:14).

• Servanthood underscores accountability, echoed in Romans 14:12—“So then, each of us will give an account of himself to God.”

• It also points to privilege: the Master entrusts real resources, just as 1 Timothy 1:12 celebrates being considered faithful and appointed to service.

who had received the five talents

The five-talent servant was given the largest amount, yet it was still a trust, not an ownership.

• James 1:17 reminds us that “every good and perfect gift is from above,” placing our abilities, opportunities, and resources firmly in God’s hands.

• Luke 12:48 adds the principle: “From everyone who has been given much, much will be required.” The verse hints that larger gifts carry larger expectations.

went at once

Immediate action shows faith and devotion.

• Psalm 119:60 models this urgency: “I hurried and did not delay to obey Your commands.”

• John 9:4 captures the same urgency: “We must do the works of Him who sent Me while it is day.” Delay can be a form of disobedience; prompt obedience reveals trust.

and put them to work

The servant engaged, invested, and risked in order to honor the Master.

• 1 Corinthians 15:58 urges believers to be “always abounding in the work of the Lord,” knowing it is never in vain.

• Galatians 6:10 exhorts us to “do good to everyone,” showing that faith expresses itself through active service.

Practical implications:

– Use spiritual gifts within the church (1 Peter 4:10).

– Steward finances generously (2 Corinthians 9:6-8).

– Apply time and abilities to kingdom purposes (Ephesians 5:15-16).

and gained five more

Faithful effort, blessed by God, produces increase.

• Proverbs 11:24-25 illustrates the paradox of generous increase: “A generous soul will prosper.”

• John 15:5 shows the ultimate source of fruitfulness: “If you remain in Me and I in you, you will bear much fruit.”

The return doubles the original trust, emphasizing that God delights to multiply what is surrendered to Him.

summary

Matthew 25:16 portrays a diligent servant who, recognizing that his substantial trust comes from the Master, immediately acts in faithful obedience, strategically invests what was entrusted, and experiences abundant fruitfulness. The verse calls believers to embrace their role as stewards, respond promptly to God-given opportunities, labor energetically for His glory, and trust Him to bring a multiplied harvest.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Traded with the same.--Literally, wrought, or, was busy. The fact that the capital was doubled implies that the trading was both active and prosperous.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Went. The one who had received the five talents, the mark of the greatest trust, lost no time, but betook himself to business with zeal and energy. Traded with the same (εἰργάσατο ἐν αὐτοῖς, made gain with them). The verb is applied to husbandry or any work by which profit is obtained. A special method of increasing the allotted sum is mentioned in ver. 27; but here the term is general, and implies only that the slave used the money in some business which would prove to his master's advantage. In other words, he exercised his faculties and powers in his master's service and with a view to his master's interests. Made [them] other five talents. The addition "them" is unnecessary. He doubled his principal - "made" being equivalent to "gained." In the parable of the "pounds" we find the same sum increased in different proportions; here we have different sums multiplied in the same proportion.
