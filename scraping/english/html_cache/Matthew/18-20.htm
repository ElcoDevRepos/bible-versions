For where two or three gather together in My name,
This phrase emphasizes the importance of community and fellowship among believers. In the Jewish tradition, a minimum of ten men, known as a minyan, was required for certain religious activities. Jesus, however, highlights that even a small gathering of believers is significant. The phrase "in My name" indicates that the gathering is centered around Jesus and His teachings, reflecting a unity of purpose and faith. This aligns with the early Christian practice of meeting in homes for worship and prayer, as seen in Acts 2:46. The emphasis on gathering "in My name" underscores the authority and presence of Jesus in the assembly, distinguishing it from secular or non-Christian gatherings.

there am I with them.”
This promise of Jesus' presence is a profound assurance to believers. It reflects the omnipresence of Christ, a divine attribute, and His commitment to be with His followers. This echoes the promise in Matthew 28:20, where Jesus assures His disciples of His presence "to the very end of the age." Theologically, this presence is understood as spiritual and real, facilitated by the Holy Spirit, who indwells believers. This assurance would have been particularly comforting to the early church, which often faced persecution and isolation. It also connects to the Old Testament, where God's presence among His people was a sign of His favor and covenant, as seen in Exodus 33:14. The promise of Jesus' presence reinforces the idea that the church is not merely a human institution but a spiritual body united by Christ Himself.

What Does Matthew 18:20 Mean
For where

- The sentence opens with “For,” linking back to Matthew 18:15-19, where Jesus teaches about church discipline and united prayer.

- He is giving a reason, not a suggestion. In the same way that “the word of the Lord is upright” (Psalm 33:4), His statement that follows carries unquestionable authority and certainty.

two or three

- Jesus deliberately sets the bar low: even “two or three.”

• This mirrors Ecclesiastes 4:9-12, which praises the strength of a small cord of companions.

• After His resurrection, the risen Lord met a pair of disciples on the road to Emmaus (Luke 24:13-32), proving He values even the smallest gathering.

- There is no quota for divine attention; whether a stadium or a living room, Christ notices.

gather together

- The verb implies intentional, unified assembly, not accidental proximity. Hebrews 10:25 urges believers not to neglect meeting together, underscoring the same priority.

- Acts 2:42 shows the early church devoted to fellowship, a practical picture of this phrase lived out.

- Unity is essential; Psalm 133:1 celebrates how good and pleasant it is when God’s people dwell together in unity.

in My name

- Gathering “in My name” means assembling under His authority, seeking His will, and honoring His character. Colossians 3:17 instructs, “Whatever you do … do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus,” capturing the same heartbeat.

- It is more than merely mentioning His name; it is yielding to His lordship, just as John 14:13-14 ties asking “in My name” to aligning with His purposes.

there am I

- Jesus speaks in the present tense—“am”—signifying continuous, living presence.

- This fulfills His promise, “Surely I am with you always” (Matthew 28:20).

- Revelation 1:13 depicts Him “among the lampstands,” a picture of His ongoing presence in the churches.

with them

- “With” is personal and relational, echoing the covenant assurance of Exodus 33:14, “My presence will go with you.”

- His nearness comforts (Psalm 34:18), guides (John 10:27), and empowers (Acts 4:31).

- The promise is corporate yet intimate: each believer can say with Paul, “The Lord stood by me and strengthened me” (2 Timothy 4:17).

summary

Matthew 18:20 is Jesus’ guarantee that whenever even a handful of believers intentionally unite under His authority, He is literally and actively present among them. His nearness does not depend on numbers, buildings, or titles but on hearts gathered for Him. That living presence turns ordinary meetings into sacred moments, assures us of divine backing in our decisions and prayers, and reminds every small group of saints that they never meet alone.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) Where two or three . . .--The true meaning of the words is well embodied in the well-known patristic axiom, Ubi tres, ibi Ecclesia ("Where three are there is a church"). The strength of the Christian society was not to be measured by a numerical standard, but by its fulfilment of the true conditions of its life. The presence of Christ was as true and mighty, His communion with His Church as real, when His followers were but as a remnant, as when they were gathered in the great congregation. He would be with the "two or three" (there is, perhaps, a special reference to the self-same words in Matthew 18:16), to plead for them as the great High Priest, to impart Himself to them, to ratify their decisions.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - The promise is applied to the public prayer of the congregation, as we see in what is called "the prayer of St. Chrysostom" in the English Prayer book. Are gathered together. For the purpose of worship. It is a simpler form of the word used in Hebrews 10:25, "Not forsaking the assembling of ourselves together." In my Name (εἰς τὸ ἐμὸν ὄνομα); literally, into my Name; i.e. with love to me, yearning for union with me, and acting for my glory. This would imply decent and orderly meeting for the highest ends. There am I in the midst of them. Christ promises a real, actual presence, though invisible, as true as when he appeared to his disciples after his resurrection, as true as when the Shechinah shone in tabernacle or temple. The rabbis had a saying that if two sat at table and conversed about the Law of God, the Shechinah rested upon them. The promise in the text, of course, implies Christ's omnipresence and omniscience. This is his blessing on united, congregational prayer.
