Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then I looked
This phrase indicates a transition in John's vision, suggesting a new scene or aspect of the heavenly revelation. The act of looking signifies John's active participation in the vision, emphasizing the importance of what he is about to witness. This is a common motif in apocalyptic literature, where the seer is guided through a series of visions.

and I heard the voices of many angels
Hearing the voices of angels highlights the auditory aspect of the vision, emphasizing the grandeur and majesty of the heavenly realm. Angels are often depicted as messengers and servants of God throughout Scripture, such as in Daniel 7:10 and Hebrews 1:14, where they serve and worship God. Their voices contribute to the worship and proclamation of God's glory.

encircling the throne
The throne is central to the vision, symbolizing God's sovereign rule and authority. The encircling of the throne by angels suggests a scene of worship and reverence, similar to Isaiah 6:1-3, where seraphim surround God's throne. This imagery underscores the holiness and majesty of God, with the throne as the focal point of divine governance.

and the living creatures and the elders
The living creatures and elders are significant figures in the heavenly court. The living creatures, described in Revelation 4:6-8, are often associated with cherubim or seraphim, representing aspects of God's creation and attributes. The elders, numbering twenty-four, likely symbolize the twelve tribes of Israel and the twelve apostles, representing the fullness of God's people. Together, they participate in the worship of God, highlighting the unity of creation and redemption.

And their number was myriads of myriads and thousands of thousands
This phrase conveys an innumerable multitude, emphasizing the vastness and magnitude of the heavenly host. The use of "myriads" and "thousands" is a common biblical expression to denote an uncountable number, as seen in Daniel 7:10. This multitude reflects the overwhelming glory and power of God, with countless beings engaged in worship. It also points to the fulfillment of God's promises, where multitudes from every nation and tongue will worship Him, as seen in Revelation 7:9.

Teaching Points
The Majesty of God’s Throne
The vision of countless angels and elders around the throne emphasizes God's supreme authority and the reverence He commands.

The Universality of Worship
The presence of myriads of angels and elders signifies that worship is a universal response to God's holiness and sovereignty.

Angels are depicted as active participants in worship, reminding us of the spiritual reality and the importance of worship in our lives.

What Does Revelation 5:11 Mean
Then I looked

“Then I looked” (Revelation 5:11) signals a fresh, Spirit-guided turn in John’s heavenly tour. Each new gaze reveals more of God’s glory (Revelation 4:1) and invites us to lift our own eyes above earthly pressures (Colossians 3:1-2). Like Ezekiel’s shifting visions (Ezekiel 1:1), John’s attentive watching models an expectant faith that trusts God to unveil truth in His time.

…and I heard the voices of many angels

John’s ears now join his eyes. Angels—God’s ministering spirits (Hebrews 1:14)—raise their voices, emphasizing that worship in heaven is both seen and heard (Luke 2:13-14). Their unified cry echoes Daniel 7:10, where a vast angelic assembly stands before the Ancient of Days, underscoring God’s unchanging throne room across Scripture.

…encircling the throne

Everything gathers around the throne because God Himself is the worship center (Revelation 4:2-3). Angels form a perfect circle, reflecting order, harmony, and the limitless reach of divine sovereignty (Isaiah 6:1-3). The throne’s placement at the scene’s heart reminds believers that every created being finds purpose in orbiting the Lord, not vice versa (Psalm 97:1-2).

…and the living creatures and the elders

The four living creatures (Revelation 4:6-8) and the twenty-four elders (Revelation 4:4, 10) stand shoulder to shoulder with the angelic host. These two groups picture all creation—angelic and redeemed humanity—joining voices. Their togetherness anticipates the church’s destiny to reign with Christ (Revelation 5:9-10) and shows that no division mars heavenly worship (Ephesians 2:19).

…their number was myriads of myriads and thousands of thousands

John piles up terms to convey a crowd beyond calculation—innumerable, inexhaustible praise (Psalm 68:17; Hebrews 12:22). A literal reading affirms that heaven teems with untold multitudes of loyal angels. For the believer, this countless host testifies that God is never outnumbered, His kingdom never threatened (2 Kings 6:16-17). If such armies worship the Lamb, we can face any earthly opposition with confidence (Romans 8:31).

summary

Revelation 5:11 pulls back the curtain on a universe alive with worship. John looks, listens, and learns that:

• Heaven’s focus is God’s throne.

• Angels by the countless millions exist to magnify the Lamb.

• Redeemed humanity joins that chorus.

• Together they underscore God’s absolute, unchallengeable rule.

Assured of this reality, believers can live boldly, knowing we already belong to the winning side whose praise will never end.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) And I beheld . . .--More literally, And I saw, and I heard a voice of many angels around the throne, and the living beings, and the elders; and the number of them was myriads of myriads, and thousands of thousands. The chorus of the redeemed is followed by a chorus of angels; for "that which is the highest act of love, towards whatever persons it was manifested, from whatever calamities it saved them, must be the highest manifestation of the divine character and will; therefore must be the cause of delight to all creatures, fallen or unfallen. If the Revelation is true, there can be no breach in the sympathies of any part of God's voluntary and intelligent universe." It is needless to observe that the numbers are not to be taken literally; they are simply employed to express the countless throng of that "innumerable company of angels" (Hebrews 12:22) which raised the song--

"Loud as from numbers without number, sweet

As from blest voices, uttering joy."

--Paradise Lost, iii. 346, 347.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - And I beheld marks a new feature of the vision, viz. the introduction of the angelic host as taking part in the adoration of the Lamb (see on Revelation 4:1). And heard the voice of many angels; a voice. The angels who have "desired to look into" the mystery of the redemption of the world (1 Peter 1:12) have now had declared to them "by the Church the manifold wisdom of God, according to the eternal purpose which he purposed in Christ Jesus our Lord" (Ephesians 3:10, 11); and are thus enabled to join in the song of the redeemed. Round about the throne and the beasts and the elders. The innumerable company of angels encircle the throne and the beasts and the elders. Thus the throne is in the vision seen as occupying the centre, the four living creatures are placed round it in different directions; the elders form the next circle, and the angels enclose the whole. The Lamb is in the midst before the throne (see on Revelation 4:6). "Thus," says Bisping, "the redeemed creation stands nearer to the throne of God than even the angels (see Hebrews 2:5)." And the number of them was ten thousand times ten thousand, and thousands of thousands. The readings vary here, though the sense of the passage is not affected. After πρεσβυτέρων, "elders,

(1) the Authorized and Revised Versions, following א, A, B, P, etc., render as above;

(2) 1, Erasmus, Stephens edit. 1550 (though the last probably per errorem), omit "and the number of them was ten thousand times ten thousand;"

(3) Vulgate, both manuscripts and Clementine edition, simply omit "ten thousand times ten thousand;" . . .
