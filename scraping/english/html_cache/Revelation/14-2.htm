And I heard a sound from heaven
This phrase indicates a divine revelation or message being communicated to John, the author of Revelation. The sound from heaven suggests a heavenly origin, emphasizing the authority and purity of the message. In biblical context, hearing from heaven often signifies God's direct communication, as seen in instances like the baptism of Jesus (Matthew 3:17).

like the roar of many waters
The imagery of many waters is often used in Scripture to describe the voice of God, as seen in Ezekiel 43:2. This metaphor conveys power, majesty, and overwhelming presence. The sound of many waters can also symbolize the multitude of voices or the collective praise of the redeemed, reflecting the vastness and unity of God's people.

and the loud rumbling of thunder
Thunder is frequently associated with God's presence and judgment throughout the Bible, such as in Exodus 19:16 during the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai. It signifies awe, power, and the seriousness of divine pronouncements. This imagery underscores the authority and might of the message being revealed.

And the sound I heard was like harpists strumming their harps
The mention of harps suggests a harmonious and worshipful element to the sound. Harps are often associated with praise and worship in the Bible, as seen in the Psalms and in the worship practices of the temple. This phrase indicates that the message from heaven, while powerful and authoritative, is also beautiful and filled with divine harmony, reflecting the worship of God by the heavenly host.

Teaching Points
The Majesty of God's Voice
The description of God's voice as the roar of many waters and loud thunder highlights His supreme authority and power. Believers are reminded of the awe and reverence due to God.

The Harmony of Worship
The sound of harpists playing their harps signifies the beauty and unity of worship in heaven. This encourages believers to strive for harmony and sincerity in their worship on earth.

The Assurance of God's Presence
The heavenly sound assures believers of God's constant presence and His active role in the unfolding of His divine plan. This provides comfort and hope amidst earthly challenges.

The Call to Listen
Just as John heard the sound from heaven, believers are called to be attentive to God's voice in their lives, through Scripture, prayer, and the Holy Spirit.

What Does Revelation 14:2 Mean
A sound from heaven

John reports, “And I heard a sound from heaven…” (Revelation 14:2).

• The origin is “from heaven,” underscoring divine authority; the scene unfolds under God’s direct initiative, not human invention (Revelation 4:1; Acts 2:2).

• Heavenly sounds in Scripture often precede revelation or action—e.g., the Father’s voice at Jesus’ baptism (Matthew 3:17) and at the temple (John 12:28-29).

• In context, the Lamb stands on Mount Zion with 144,000 sealed believers (Revelation 14:1); the sound signals worship and victory that flow downward from God’s throne.

Roar of many waters

The sound is “like the roar of many waters.”

• “Many waters” suggests overwhelming power—unstoppable, majestic, and cleansing (Psalm 93:4).

• Ezekiel 43:2 records a similar moment: “His voice sounded like the roar of many waters.” The same imagery describes Jesus in Revelation 1:15.

• The comparison magnifies God’s voice; what John hears is vast, penetrating every corner of creation.

Loud rumbling of thunder

John adds the element of “the loud rumbling of thunder.”

• Thunder often signals God’s judicial presence (Exodus 19:16; Revelation 11:19).

• Psalm 29:3-4 affirms, “The God of glory thunders… the LORD thunders over the mighty waters.”

• The thunder, paired with roaring waters, blends awe and warning: heaven’s proclamation announces both comfort for the redeemed and impending judgment for the unrepentant.

Harpists strumming their harps

Surprisingly, the same sound “was like harpists strumming their harps.”

• The roar and thunder resolve into music—heaven’s worship is powerful yet harmonious. Revelation 5:8 and 15:2 picture elders and victorious saints holding harps “given them by God.”

• Harps accompany praise (Psalm 33:2-3). What seems fearful becomes beautiful when heard through redeemed ears.

• The 144,000 will “sing a new song before the throne” (Revelation 14:3), so the heavenly sound likely introduces their anthem—a testimony that divine judgment and joyous praise coexist without contradiction.

summary

Revelation 14:2 paints an auditory tapestry: a heavenly sound that is massive like ocean waves, authoritative like rolling thunder, yet melodious like skilled harpists. For believers, it signals God’s sovereign voice, the certainty of His coming judgment, and the sweet assurance of victorious worship.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) And I heard a voice . . .--Translate, And I heard a voice out of the heaven. The saints stand with their Lord, the Lamb, on Mount Zion, and just as of old a voice came from heaven bearing witness to Christ, so round the abode of the saints heavenly voices are heard, full of majesty, terribleness, and sweetness, as though the sounds of sea and thunder blended with the music of heavenly harps. We call to mind the magnificent 29th Psalm; there the saints, secure in Zion, hear all around them the voice of God in the thunder and in the sea, while in His safe sanctuary the saints can sing of His honour.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - And I heard a voice from heaven, as the voice of many waters, and as the voice of a great thunder. Evidently the song of the heavenly inhabitants, as described also in Revelation 7:9-11, where we are told they "cried with a loud voice." The greatness of the voice is evidence of the vastness of the number. "Heaven," from which the sounds come, includes the "Mount Zion" of ver. 1, on which the Lamb and his followers stand. And I heard the voice of harpers harping with their harps. The Revised Version is better, and the voice which I heard [was] as [the voice] of harpers harping with their harps. This reading is supported by א, A, B, C, and other good authorities. As the voice; that is, in regard to its pleasantness; reminding the hearer of the temple worship. (On the word "harp," see on Revelation 5:8.)
