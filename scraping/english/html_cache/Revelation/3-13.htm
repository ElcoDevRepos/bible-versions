He who has an ear
This phrase is a call to attention, urging the listener to be spiritually perceptive. It echoes the words of Jesus in the Gospels, particularly in Matthew 11:15 and Mark 4:9, where He often used similar language to emphasize the importance of understanding His teachings. The "ear" symbolizes the ability to comprehend spiritual truths, suggesting that not everyone will be receptive to the message.

let him hear
The imperative "let him hear" underscores the responsibility of the listener to actively engage with the message. In the biblical context, hearing implies obedience and action, not just passive listening. This call to hear is a recurring theme in the letters to the seven churches in Revelation, emphasizing the need for discernment and response to divine instruction.

what the Spirit says
The "Spirit" refers to the Holy Spirit, who communicates God's will and truth to believers. In the context of Revelation, the Spirit is the source of prophetic revelation, guiding the churches in understanding and applying the messages given to them. This highlights the role of the Holy Spirit as a teacher and comforter, as seen in John 14:26.

to the churches
The term "churches" refers to the seven churches in Asia Minor addressed in Revelation chapters 2 and 3. These churches were real congregations in cities like Ephesus, Smyrna, and Laodicea, each facing unique challenges and receiving specific messages. The plural form indicates that the message is not just for one church but for all, suggesting a universal application for the entire body of Christ. The historical and cultural context of these churches provides insight into the specific issues they faced, such as persecution, false teaching, and complacency.

Teaching Points
Spiritual Discernment
The phrase "He who has an ear, let him hear" calls for active listening and spiritual discernment. Believers are encouraged to be attentive to the Holy Spirit's guidance.

Universal Message
Although addressed to specific churches, the message is relevant to all believers. It underscores the timeless nature of God's Word and its applicability to the church today.

Responsibility to Respond
Hearing implies a responsibility to respond. Believers are called to not only hear but also act upon the Spirit's message.

Role of the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit plays a crucial role in communicating God's truth. Believers should seek the Spirit's guidance in understanding and applying Scripture.

Warning Against Complacency
The repeated call to hear what the Spirit says serves as a warning against spiritual complacency and encourages vigilance in faith.

What Does Revelation 3:13 Mean
He who has an ear

Revelation 3:13 opens with a simple image: an ear. Everyone with physical hearing—and by extension anyone capable of understanding—falls under this call. Jesus used the same wording during His earthly ministry: “He who has ears to hear, let him hear” (Matthew 11:15). The phrase stresses:

• Inclusiveness—no believer is exempt (Isaiah 55:3).

• Personal responsibility—each individual must decide to listen (1 Samuel 3:10).

• Urgency—the Lord is speaking now, not later (Hebrews 3:7-8).

Let him hear

Hearing in Scripture always carries the expectation of obedience. The command is not passive; it calls for response.

• “So faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of Christ” (Romans 10:17).

• “But be doers of the word, and not hearers only” (James 1:22).

• When Israel’s greatest confession begins with “Hear, O Israel” (Deuteronomy 6:4), the point is action—loving God with heart, soul, and strength (v. 5).

In Revelation 3, Jesus seeks the same: attentive, obedient disciples who move from information to transformation.

What the Spirit says

The speaker is the Holy Spirit, the divine Author and Interpreter of Scripture.

• Jesus promised, “When the Spirit of truth comes, He will guide you into all truth” (John 16:13).

• “The Spirit explicitly says that in later times some will abandon the faith” (1 Timothy 4:1).

• Prophecy “never had its origin in the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit” (2 Peter 1:21).

Because the Spirit is God, His words carry inerrant authority. Dismissing them is not a light matter; it is refusing God Himself (Acts 28:25-27).

To the churches

The message is addressed to “the churches,” plural. Every congregation—ancient and modern—must take note.

• The same refrain appears after each of the seven letters (Revelation 2:7, 11, 17, 29; 3:6, 13, 22), underscoring a collective obligation.

• “To the church of God in Corinth… together with all who everywhere call on the name of our Lord Jesus Christ” (1 Corinthians 1:2) shows the pattern: truth spoken to one church is meant for all believers.

• Acts 9:31 portrays “the churches throughout Judea, Galilee, and Samaria” walking “in the fear of the Lord and in the comfort of the Holy Spirit,” mirroring the expectation set in Revelation—local bodies united under one divine voice.

summary

Revelation 3:13 is a concise, four-part call: if you possess the capacity to listen, then listen—and obey—because the Holy Spirit is speaking, and His words apply to every church. The verse moves us from mere awareness to active obedience, reminding believers that God still addresses His people collectively and personally, and we are accountable to respond in faith and action.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the Churches (see on Revelation 2:7). Of the condition of the Church in Philadelphia we know nothing from Holy Writ, except what is contained in the passage before us. But its comparative immunity from trouble and destruction, and its continued existence to the present day (see on vers. 7-13, "Philadelphia"), render it probable that the message of the apostle was not without some effect. Thus Gibbon writes: "In the loss of Ephesus the Christians deplored the fall of the first angel, the extinction of the first candlestick, of the Revelation; the desolation is complete; and the Temple of Diana or the Church of Mary will equally elude the search of the curious traveller. The circus and three stately theatres of Laodicea are now peopled with wolves and foxes; Sardis is reduced to a miserable village; the God of Mahomet, without a rival or a Son, is invoked in the mosques of Thyatira and Pergamos, and the populousness of Smyrna is supported by the foreign trade of the Franks and Armenians. Philadelphia alone has been saved by prophecy or courage" ('Decline and Fall,' c). 64.
