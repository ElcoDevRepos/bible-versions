Those I love
This phrase emphasizes the relationship between God and His people, highlighting divine love as the foundation for His actions. In the biblical context, love is not merely an emotion but a commitment to the well-being of others. This echoes Proverbs 3:12, where a father disciplines the son he delights in, illustrating God's paternal care. The love mentioned here is agape, a selfless, sacrificial love that seeks the best for others, reflecting God's nature.

I rebuke and discipline
Rebuke and discipline are tools used by God to correct and guide His followers. The Greek word for "rebuke" implies a strong disapproval meant to bring about conviction and change. Discipline, akin to training, is intended for growth and maturity, as seen in Hebrews 12:6-11, where discipline is portrayed as a sign of legitimate sonship and a means to share in God's holiness. This process is not punitive but restorative, aiming to align believers with God's will.

Therefore be earnest
The call to be earnest suggests a sincere and zealous response to God's correction. It implies a sense of urgency and seriousness in addressing one's spiritual state. This aligns with the biblical call to wholehearted devotion, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:5, where believers are commanded to love God with all their heart, soul, and strength. Earnestness here is a call to genuine repentance and transformation.

and repent
Repentance is a central theme in Scripture, involving a change of mind and direction. It is not merely feeling sorry but turning away from sin and towards God. This call to repent is consistent with the messages of the prophets and John the Baptist, who urged people to prepare for the Kingdom of God. In the New Testament, repentance is a prerequisite for forgiveness and salvation, as seen in Acts 2:38. It signifies a return to God and a renewal of the covenant relationship.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker in this verse, addressing the church in Laodicea. He is portrayed as the one who loves, rebukes, and disciplines.

2. Laodicea
A city in Asia Minor, known for its wealth and self-sufficiency, but spiritually lukewarm, as described in the preceding verses.

3. The Church in Laodicea
The recipients of this message, representing believers who have become complacent in their faith.
Teaching Points
Understanding Divine Love
God's love is not passive; it actively seeks our growth and correction. His rebuke and discipline are expressions of His deep love for us.

The Call to Repentance
Repentance is a necessary response to God's discipline. It involves a sincere turning away from sin and a renewed commitment to follow Christ.

Earnestness in Faith
The Greek word for "earnest" (z?leue) implies a zealous and fervent attitude. Believers are encouraged to be passionate and sincere in their faith journey.

The Role of Discipline
Discipline is not punitive but corrective. It is meant to guide us back to the right path and strengthen our relationship with God.

Self-Examination
Regular self-examination helps us remain aware of areas where we need to repent and grow, aligning our lives more closely with God's will.

What Does Revelation 3:19 Mean
Those I love

Jesus prefaces His warning with affection: “Those I love…” (Revelation 3:19).

• His correction flows out of genuine covenant love, the same love declared in John 3:16 and demonstrated at the cross (Romans 5:8).

• This affirmation assures believers that they are not being cast off but drawn closer, echoing Isaiah 43:1, “I have called you by name; you are Mine.”

I rebuke and discipline

“…I rebuke and discipline.” Loving correction is two-fold:

• Rebuke—calling out sin or complacency (Proverbs 3:11-12; Hebrews 12:6).

• Discipline—training that shapes character, sometimes through hardship (Hebrews 12:7-11; John 15:2).

Both aspects aim to restore spiritual health, never to simply punish. Psalm 94:12 reminds us, “Blessed is the man You discipline, O LORD.”

Therefore be earnest

“Therefore be earnest…” The proper response to Christ’s discipline is zeal.

• Earnestness replaces Laodicean lukewarmness (Revelation 3:16).

• Scripture calls believers to “Never be lacking in zeal” (Romans 12:11) and to be “zealous for good deeds” (Titus 2:14).

• Genuine fervor springs from gratitude for grace, propelling wholehearted devotion (Mark 12:30).

And repent

“…and repent.” Turning is the final step.

• Repentance is a decisive change of mind leading to changed behavior (Acts 3:19; Luke 13:3).

• It is ongoing for believers, not a one-time event (1 John 1:9; Revelation 2:5).

• True repentance restores fellowship, opening the door for Christ’s intimate presence (Revelation 3:20).

summary

Revelation 3:19 assures believers of Christ’s steadfast love. Because He loves, He rebukes and disciplines, urging us toward renewed zeal and genuine repentance. His correction is not condemnation but invitation—an open door to deeper fellowship, vibrant faith, and a life that reflects His holy love.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) I rebuke and chasten.--The first word is that used in the work of the Holy Spirit (John 16:8), and signifies to bring conviction; it is not empty censure. The second word signifies to educate by means of correction. The pronoun is emphatic, "I," and calls attention to the fidelity of Christ's love in comparison with the weak partiality seen in human love. (Comp. Hebrews 12:6.)

Be zealous.--Or, be in a constant zealous state; and now, once for all, repent.

(2ø) Behold, I stand at the door, and knock.--It is difficult not to see an allusion in this image to Song of Solomon 5:2-6. Perhaps, also, the memory of the first night spent by St. John with his Master and Friend (John 1:39) may have been strong in his mind. Indeed, the life of Christ on earth teems with illustrations which may well have suggested the image (Luke 10:38; Luke 19:5-6; Luke 22:11-13; Luke 24:29-30). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - As many as I love, I rebuke and chasten: be zealous therefore, and repent. As many as. Not one whom God loves escapes chastening; if he be not chastened, he is not a son (Hebrews 12:8), for "all have sinned, and come short." "I love" is φιλῶ, I love dearly; not merely ἀγαπῶ. I rebuke (ἐλέγχω), to reprove, so as to convict of sin and turn to repentance; the work of the Holy Ghost, who should "convict the world of sin" (John 16:8). This verse is a solace and encouragement for the Laodi-ceans. They were required to make the sacrifices demanded of them, not so much that they might be punished for their transgressions, but to prove themselves of the number of God's elect. The stern reproof administered was a pruning, which was an evidence of God's loving care for them; the final sentence, "Cut it down," had not yet gone forth. But though thus intended for encouragement rather than condemnation, yet it could not but contain implied reproach, however tender. No one can be exhorted to change his path and to seek that which is holy without being reminded that he is unholy and has wandered from the right way. Those in Laodicea who took this message to heart must needs think of their unchastened life - the life full of prosperity and self-satisfied security, into which so little zeal had been infused, in which so little need for repentance bad been felt. The Church, indeed, needed some of that chastening, that persecution, and hardship, which should arouse her from the perilous slumber of ease into which she had fallen, and call forth some zeal and self-sacrifice, the frequent and natural result of opposition.
