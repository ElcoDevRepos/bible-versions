Who among you will pay attention to this?
This phrase is a call to the people of Israel to heed the message being delivered. In the context of Isaiah, the prophet often speaks to a nation that is spiritually deaf and blind (Isaiah 42:18-20). The rhetorical question emphasizes the rarity of true listeners among the people. Historically, Israel had a pattern of ignoring prophetic warnings, leading to consequences such as exile. This call to attention is a common prophetic device used to awaken the people to their spiritual condition and the need for repentance. It echoes the call of other prophets, such as Jeremiah (Jeremiah 6:10) and Ezekiel (Ezekiel 12:2), who also faced a resistant audience.

Who will listen and obey hereafter?
This phrase underscores the importance of not just hearing but also acting upon God's word. The Hebrew concept of "listening" often implies obedience, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:4-5, where the Shema calls Israel to hear and love God with all their heart. The future-oriented "hereafter" suggests a turning point or a new beginning, urging the people to change their ways moving forward. This call to obedience is a theme throughout Scripture, seen in the teachings of Jesus, who emphasized the importance of being doers of the word (Matthew 7:24-27). The prophetic message here is not just for the immediate audience but extends to future generations, highlighting the timeless nature of God's call to His people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book bearing his name. He prophesied to the Kingdom of Judah and is known for his messages of both judgment and hope.

2. Israel
The primary audience of Isaiah's prophecies. At this time, Israel was often disobedient and faced consequences for their actions, yet God continually called them back to Himself.

3. God
The speaker through the prophet Isaiah, calling His people to listen and obey His words.

4. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which was often the focus of Isaiah's prophecies, especially concerning their need for repentance and return to God.

5. Prophecy
The context of Isaiah 42 is a prophetic message concerning the coming of God's servant, who will bring justice and be a light to the nations.
Teaching Points
The Call to Listen
God continually calls His people to listen to His voice. In a world full of distractions, we must prioritize hearing God's word above all else.

Obedience as a Response
Listening to God is not passive; it requires an active response of obedience. True listening in the biblical sense involves a commitment to act on what is heard.

The Importance of Reflection
Reflect on past experiences and learn from them. Isaiah's call is not just to hear but to consider and change based on what God has revealed.

The Role of Prophecy
Prophecy serves as both a warning and a guide. Understanding its purpose helps us align our lives with God's will.

Community Accountability
The call to listen and obey is not just individual but communal. Encourage one another in the faith to heed God's word together.

What Does Isaiah 42:23 Mean
Who among you will pay attention to this?

Isaiah has just rehearsed how God’s covenant people became “plunder with none to rescue” (Isaiah 42:22). Now the prophet pauses and throws a challenge to the hearers.

• “Pay attention” is an invitation to wake up spiritually, like Moses’ call, “Give ear, O heavens, and I will speak” (Deuteronomy 32:1).

• It implies personal responsibility; no one can heed for someone else (Ezekiel 18:20).

• The phrase pierces complacency: “My people do not consider” (Isaiah 1:3). God asks them to face the painful facts of their sin and His just discipline.

• Jesus echoes the same urgency: “He who has ears to hear, let him hear” (Mark 4:23), showing the continuity of God’s call across both Testaments.

• Paying attention means more than noticing; it means treasuring God’s word (Proverbs 4:20–22) and letting it reshape the heart (Psalm 119:9–11).

Who will listen and obey hereafter?

The first question targets the present; the second projects into the future. God seeks lasting change, not momentary interest.

• “Listen” points to humble reception of revelation (Jeremiah 26:3).

• “Obey” (lit. “hear for the afterward”) links hearing with action—James would later write, “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only” (James 1:22).

• The “hereafter” highlights that choices today shape tomorrow. Israel’s exile proved the cost of stubbornness (Isaiah 42:24–25), yet God still offers a fresh start (Isaiah 55:6–7).

• The same pattern appears in Hebrews: “Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts” (Hebrews 3:15), and in Revelation’s letters, “Whoever has an ear, let him hear what the Spirit says to the churches” (Revelation 2:7).

• Obedience is never abstract. It flows into concrete faithfulness—justice (Micah 6:8), trust in the Servant-Messiah (Isaiah 42:1), and hope in the promised redemption (Jeremiah 29:11–14).

summary

Isaiah 42:23 is God’s heartfelt appeal in the middle of judgment: Wake up, listen deeply, and commit to lifelong obedience. He wants His people to look honestly at their sin, receive His word with humble hearts, and walk forward in trusting, responsive faith.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - Who among you will give ear? Surely there are some among you, less hardened than the rest, who will take advantage of my warning, and repent at this, the eleventh hour. God's arm was straitened; the people could not be delivered out of captivity unless they ceased in large numbers to be "blind" and "deaf" - unless they listened to the prophet's words, and profited by them.
