Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Behold, the former things have happened
This phrase emphasizes God's sovereignty and faithfulness in fulfilling past prophecies. The "former things" refer to events and prophecies that have already come to pass, demonstrating God's control over history. This can be connected to the fulfillment of earlier prophecies in Isaiah, such as the Assyrian invasion and the deliverance of Jerusalem. It underscores the reliability of God's word, as seen in Deuteronomy 18:21-22, where a true prophet's words are confirmed by their fulfillment.

and now I declare new things
Here, God is introducing new revelations and future events. This declaration signifies a shift from past events to future promises, highlighting God's ongoing involvement in the world. The "new things" can be seen as the coming of the Messiah and the establishment of a new covenant, as prophesied in Jeremiah 31:31-34. This also points to the unfolding of God's redemptive plan, which includes the inclusion of the Gentiles, as seen in Isaiah 49:6.

Before they spring forth I proclaim them to you.
This phrase emphasizes God's omniscience and the prophetic nature of His declarations. By proclaiming events before they occur, God demonstrates His foreknowledge and the certainty of His plans. This is a hallmark of biblical prophecy, distinguishing the God of Israel from idols, as seen in Isaiah 41:21-23. The phrase also foreshadows the coming of Jesus Christ, who fulfills numerous Old Testament prophecies, such as those found in Isaiah 53, affirming the trustworthiness of God's word and His ultimate plan for salvation.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book that bears his name. He prophesied during the reigns of several kings of Judah and is known for his messages of both judgment and hope.

2. God (Yahweh)
The speaker in this verse, God is declaring His sovereignty and ability to foretell and bring about future events. This highlights His omniscience and omnipotence.

3. Israel
The primary audience of Isaiah's prophecies, Israel is the nation chosen by God to be His people. They are often the recipients of both His promises and His warnings.

4. Former Things
Refers to past events or prophecies that have already been fulfilled, serving as a testament to God's faithfulness and power.

5. New Things
These are the future events or prophecies that God is now revealing, demonstrating His ongoing involvement in the world and His plan for redemption.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Faithfulness
God is in control of history and fulfills His promises. We can trust Him with our future because He has proven faithful in the past.

The Assurance of Prophecy
Prophecies serve as a reminder of God's omniscience. They assure us that God knows the future and is actively working out His plan.

Embrace the New
As God declares new things, we should be open to the changes He brings into our lives, trusting that they are part of His divine plan.

Living in Expectation
We are called to live with an expectation of God's continued work in our lives and the world, looking forward to the fulfillment of His promises.

Transformation through Christ
Just as God declares new things, we are made new in Christ. This transformation should be evident in our daily lives as we reflect His character.

What Does Isaiah 42:9 Mean
Behold

“Behold” is God’s way of saying, “Look closely—pay attention!” He wants listeners to pause and take His words seriously.

- Throughout Scripture, the Lord often begins pivotal declarations this way (Isaiah 7:14; John 1:29).

- It underscores that what follows is certain and demands a response (Isaiah 40:9; Revelation 21:5).

the former things have happened

God reminds His people that every prophecy He previously announced has unfolded exactly as spoken.

- Israel saw earlier promises fulfilled—the deliverance from Egypt (Exodus 12:40-42), the conquest of Canaan (Joshua 21:45), and the return from exile foretold in Isaiah 44:28.

- By pointing to completed works, the Lord builds faith for what He will do next (Isaiah 41:22-23).

- This track record confirms His sovereignty over history (Psalm 33:11; Proverbs 19:21).

and now I declare new things

Having proven Himself, God unveils fresh promises.

- In immediate context, He is announcing the coming Servant who will bring justice to the nations (Isaiah 42:1-4).

- Ultimately these “new things” climax in Christ’s first advent (Luke 4:17-21) and anticipate His future reign (Revelation 11:15).

- God’s revelation is progressive—He keeps opening deeper layers of His redemptive plan (Hebrews 1:1-2).

Before they spring forth I proclaim them to you

The Lord speaks beforehand so His people recognize His hand when events emerge.

- Prophecy serves as a divine signature, distinguishing the true God from idols (Isaiah 46:9-10).

- His foretelling offers comfort: nothing catches Him off guard (Psalm 139:16; Matthew 24:6).

- It also calls for readiness and obedience, since what He announces will surely appear (2 Peter 3:11-13).

summary

Isaiah 42:9 is the Lord’s invitation to trust Him. He points to past fulfillments to prove His faithfulness, then reveals fresh promises centered in His Servant, Jesus. By announcing the future in advance, God assures His people that He rules history and invites them to live expectantly, confident that every word He declares will come to pass.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 9-17. - ANNOUNCEMENT OF THE COMING DELIVERANCE OF ISRAEL FROM BABYLON, AND CALL ON THE NATIONS FOR A SONG OF PRAISE AND JUBILATION. Jehovah is still the speaker. He begins by promising a new revelation (ver. 9). Then, before the revelation is made, he calls upon the nations - especially those in the vicinity of Palestine - to rejoice at what is about to happen (vers. 10-12). After this he proceeds to make the announcement promised in ver. 9 - an announcement that he is about to deliver his people (ver. 16) and to execute vengeance on their enemies (vers. 13-15 and 17). Verse 9. - Behold, the former things are come to pass; i.e. former prophecies have been fulfilled. Israel has been led lute captivity, and in her captivity has suffered grievous things. The reference is, perhaps, especially to such prophecies as Isaiah 39:5-7. And new things do I declare (comp. Isaiah 43:19). The voluntary restoration of a captive people to their own land by the power to which they were subject, and which could compel their services, was emphatically a "new thing" in the world's history. How unwilling the sovereign power was ordinarily to lose such services may be seen by the narrative in Exodus (Exodus 5-14.), and again by the account which Herodotus gives (1:73, 74) of the ground of quarrel between Alyattes and Cyaxares. Before they spring forth; or, shoot forth. The metaphor is one taken from the vegetable world (comp. Isaiah 43:19; Isaiah 45:8).
