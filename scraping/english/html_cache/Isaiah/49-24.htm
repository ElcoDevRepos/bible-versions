Can the plunder be snatched from the mighty
This phrase raises a rhetorical question that highlights the seeming impossibility of rescuing what has been taken by a powerful force. In the historical context, the "mighty" could refer to empires like Babylon, which had taken Israel into captivity. The imagery of plunder suggests a situation where the strong have taken what they want by force, leaving the weak in a state of loss and despair. Biblically, this reflects the human condition under sin, where Satan is often seen as the "mighty" one who holds people captive. This phrase sets the stage for God's power to intervene, as seen in other scriptures like Matthew 12:29, where Jesus speaks of binding the strong man to plunder his house, symbolizing His victory over Satan.

or the captives of a tyrant be delivered?
The term "captives" refers to those who have been taken prisoner, often in the context of war or oppression. The "tyrant" is a figure of absolute power and cruelty, representing rulers like Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon. Historically, captives had little hope of release unless a stronger power intervened. This phrase underscores the hopelessness of the captives' situation without divine intervention. In a broader theological sense, it points to humanity's bondage to sin and the need for a savior. This is fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who delivers believers from the tyranny of sin and death, as echoed in passages like Colossians 1:13, where Paul speaks of being rescued from the dominion of darkness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book that bears his name. He prophesied to the Kingdom of Judah and spoke of both judgment and redemption.

2. The Mighty
This term refers to powerful rulers or nations that have taken captives or plundered others. In the context of Isaiah, it often alludes to oppressive empires like Babylon or Assyria.

3. The Captives
These are the people who have been taken by force, often representing the Israelites who were exiled or oppressed by foreign powers.

4. The Tyrant
A ruler who exercises power in a harsh, cruel manner. In Isaiah's context, this could refer to any oppressive leader or nation that holds others in captivity.

5. The Lord's Promise
The overarching event in this passage is God's promise of deliverance and redemption for His people, despite the seeming impossibility of their situation.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Earthly Powers
No matter how powerful earthly rulers or nations may seem, God is sovereign and can deliver His people from any situation.

The Impossibility of Human Deliverance
Humanly speaking, it may seem impossible to rescue captives from a mighty tyrant, but with God, all things are possible.

The Promise of Redemption
God's promises of redemption and deliverance are sure, and He will fulfill them in His perfect timing.

Faith in God's Deliverance
Believers are called to trust in God's ability to deliver them from spiritual and physical bondage.

Christ as the Ultimate Deliverer
Jesus Christ fulfills the promise of deliverance, setting us free from the tyranny of sin and death.

What Does Isaiah 49:24 Mean
Can the plunder be snatched from the mighty?

Isaiah’s first question pictures Israel as “plunder” locked away by a power too strong for ordinary rescue. From a human standpoint the answer is an obvious “no.” Yet the Lord’s track record says otherwise. He broke Egypt’s grip on His people (Exodus 6:6-7), did the same to Midian (Judges 7:7), and promised again, “Their Redeemer is strong; He will vigorously plead their case” (Jeremiah 50:34). Jesus echoed the image when He spoke of binding “the strong man” so that his goods could be carried off (Matthew 12:29). So the verse pushes us to admit the limits of our strength while recognizing that God is never limited. He alone can walk right into the “strong man’s house” and reclaim what belongs to Him.

Or the captives of a tyrant be delivered?

The second question brings the plight of individual people into focus—men, women, and children forced to serve a harsh ruler. Babylon was that tyrant in Isaiah’s day, just as Pharaoh had been centuries earlier. The Lord’s answer comes in the very next verse: “Yes, captives will be taken from warriors, and plunder retrieved from the fierce” (Isaiah 49:25). Jesus applied the same truth spiritually when He announced, “He has sent Me to proclaim freedom to the captives” (Luke 4:18), then proved it by liberating those bound by sin and Satan (Luke 11:21-22; Colossians 1:13). God’s deliverance is literal, personal, and total—whether He is bringing exiles home to Jerusalem or rescuing sinners into the kingdom of His Son.

summary

Isaiah 49:24 sets up two seemingly unanswerable questions to underscore one awesome reality: what humans cannot wrest from the grip of overwhelming power, God can and will. He has done it in Israel’s history, He does it through Christ today, and He will consummate it when every captive that belongs to Him stands free forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) Shall the prey be taken . . .?--The question is asked by Zion in her little faith. The next phrase, "lawful captive," literally "captive of righteousness," may mean, (1) as in the Authorised version a captive whom the conqueror had a right to take, or (2) one who was righteous and yet had been given into captivity. Neither meaning is quite satisfactory. A conjectural emendation gives the captives of the terrible one, which fits in with the parallelism of the next verse.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - Shall the prey be taken, etc.? The incredulous among the exiles thought it well-nigh impossible that Babylon should be forced to disgorge her prey - the captives whose labours were so valuable to her. Babylon was mighty. By the laws of war she had a rightful claim to her captives. How was she to be induced or compelled to give them up?
