Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the jealousy of Ephraim will depart
This phrase refers to the historical division and rivalry between the northern kingdom of Israel, often represented by Ephraim, and the southern kingdom of Judah. The jealousy signifies the longstanding enmity and competition for dominance. The prophecy indicates a future reconciliation and unity among God's people, suggesting a time of peace and restoration. This can be seen as a foreshadowing of the Messianic age, where divisions among God's people are healed.

and the adversaries of Judah will be cut off
This part of the verse speaks to the removal of external threats and internal strife against Judah. Historically, Judah faced numerous adversaries, both from surrounding nations and internal factions. The promise of these adversaries being "cut off" suggests divine intervention and protection, ensuring the security and peace of Judah. This can also be interpreted as a future time when God's kingdom is established, and all opposition is removed.

Ephraim will no longer envy Judah
The envy of Ephraim towards Judah highlights the historical tension and desire for supremacy between the two kingdoms. This prophecy envisions a time when such envy is eradicated, symbolizing a harmonious relationship. This can be connected to the broader biblical theme of unity among God's people, as seen in passages like Psalm 133:1, which speaks of the goodness of brethren dwelling together in unity.

nor will Judah harass Ephraim
This phrase indicates the cessation of hostilities initiated by Judah against Ephraim. Historically, there were instances where Judah sought to assert dominance over the northern tribes. The prophecy envisions a future where such harassment ceases, pointing to a time of mutual respect and cooperation. This reflects the ideal of peace and unity within the covenant community, aligning with the prophetic vision of a united Israel under the reign of the Messiah.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ephraim
Represents the northern kingdom of Israel, often used to denote the ten tribes that split from Judah after Solomon's reign. Ephraim was the dominant tribe in the northern kingdom.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom, consisting of the tribes of Judah and Benjamin, with Jerusalem as its capital. It remained loyal to the Davidic line of kings.

3. Jealousy and Envy
These emotions are highlighted as causes of division and strife between Ephraim and Judah. The prophecy speaks of a future reconciliation.

4. Adversaries
Refers to those who oppose or are hostile towards Judah. The prophecy indicates that these adversaries will be removed.

5. Prophetic Vision of Unity
This verse is part of a larger prophetic vision of peace and unity under the reign of the Messiah, as described in Isaiah 11.
Teaching Points
Unity in Christ
Just as Ephraim and Judah are prophesied to be reconciled, believers are called to unity in Christ, setting aside divisions and working together for the Kingdom of God.

Overcoming Jealousy and Envy
These emotions can cause division within the body of Christ. We are encouraged to celebrate each other's successes and gifts, recognizing that we are all part of one body.

God's Sovereign Plan
The prophecy shows that God has a plan for reconciliation and peace. Trusting in His sovereignty can bring hope and assurance in times of division.

The Role of the Messiah
Isaiah 11 speaks of the Messiah's reign, which brings peace and justice. As followers of Christ, we are to reflect His peace and justice in our interactions.

Reconciliation as a Witness
The unity and reconciliation of God's people serve as a powerful witness to the world of God's love and transformative power.

What Does Isaiah 11:13 Mean
Then the jealousy of Ephraim will depart

The split after Solomon left the northern kingdom (often called Ephraim, Isaiah 7:9) resentful of the southern kingdom led by Judah. Here God promises that envy will vanish when the Messianic King (Isaiah 11:1, 10) reigns.

• Division gives way to unity just as foretold in Ezekiel 37:15-17, 19-22, where two sticks become one.

• Past rivalry (Isaiah 9:21; Hosea 13:1) is replaced by shared blessing under the restored Davidic throne (Jeremiah 23:5-6).

and the adversaries of Judah will be cut off

Hostile powers—both foreign enemies and rebellious kin—will be removed. “Cut off” points to decisive judgment (Zechariah 12:9).

• The Lord has done this before: Assyria fell after threatening Jerusalem (Isaiah 37:36-38).

• Ultimate fulfillment comes when every foe is subdued under Christ (Psalm 2:9; Revelation 19:15).

• Judah will dwell securely, echoing promises in Jeremiah 30:16-17 and Zechariah 2:4-5.

Ephraim will no longer envy Judah

The Holy Spirit changes hearts, ending the north’s craving for Judah’s prominence.

• God reverses centuries of bitterness (2 Chronicles 10:16-19) by writing His law within (Jeremiah 31:33).

• In the kingdom age all tribes rejoice together at Zion (Isaiah 2:2-4; Micah 4:1-3).

• Mutual jealousy has no place among those clothed with salvation (Isaiah 61:10).

nor will Judah harass Ephraim

Judah’s earlier attempts to dominate or retaliate cease (2 Chronicles 28:6-15).

• Peace replaces hostility, pictured beautifully in the same chapter by wolf and lamb lying down (Isaiah 11:6).

• God’s people become “one nation” under “one Shepherd” (Ezekiel 37:24).

• The fruit of righteousness is peace (Isaiah 32:17), showing the inward change produces outward harmony.

summary

Isaiah 11:13 looks ahead to the Messiah’s reign, when ancient rivalries between Ephraim and Judah disappear. Jealousy ends, enemies are removed, and both houses stand united under the righteous Branch of David. God’s covenant faithfulness secures lasting peace, proving that when Christ rules, division gives way to harmony and security for all His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) The envy also of Ephraim shall depart . . .--The prophet's vision of the future would not have been complete if national unity had not been included in it. He looked back on the history of the past, and saw almost from the first the deep line of cleavage between north and south, Israel and Judah. Century by century the chasm had grown deeper and wider; sub-sections of antagonism had increased its bitterness (Isaiah 9:21); but in the times of the Christ the sense of unity should be stronger than the old hostilities. The prophet's hope connects itself with Hezekiah's efforts after a restored unity (2Chronicles 30:1-12). The "envy" of Ephraim "is, as the parallelism shows, that of which Ephraim was the object. By a subtle turn of thought, however, the latter half of the verse represents Ephraim as not feeling envy or ill-will against Judah, i.e., he is neither object nor subject, and Judah, free from its own adversaries, is no longer an adversary to Ephraim.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - The envy also of Ephraim shall depart. In the kingdom of the Prince of Peace there shall no longer be quarrels or jealousies among the members. Old feuds shall be put aside; the northern and southern tribes shall agree together, and there shall be peace and harmony throughout the entire Church. Adversaries of Judah. If any such remain among the Ephraimites, Divine vengeance shall "cut them off," that there be no open disturbance of the harmony.
