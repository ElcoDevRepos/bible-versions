Woe to those who are heroes in drinking wine
The term "woe" is a pronouncement of judgment and lament, often used by prophets to signal divine displeasure. In the context of Isaiah, it serves as a warning to the people of Judah who have strayed from God's commandments. The phrase "heroes in drinking wine" uses irony, as the term "heroes" typically denotes valor and strength, yet here it is applied to those who excel in indulgence and excess. This reflects a cultural context where social gatherings and feasting were common, but the excessive consumption of wine was seen as a moral failing. The Bible frequently warns against drunkenness (Proverbs 20:1, Ephesians 5:18), highlighting the loss of self-control and the potential for moral decay.

and champions in mixing strong drink
The word "champions" further emphasizes the irony, as it suggests mastery and skill, yet it is applied to those who take pride in their ability to mix and consume strong drink. This phrase indicates not just consumption but also the preparation and celebration of intoxicating beverages, which were prevalent in ancient Near Eastern cultures. The mixing of strong drink could refer to the practice of diluting wine with spices or other substances to enhance its potency. This behavior is condemned as it leads to moral and spiritual decline, distracting individuals from their responsibilities and relationship with God. The Bible consistently associates sobriety with wisdom and righteousness (1 Peter 5:8, Titus 2:2), contrasting it with the folly of drunkenness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book that bears his name. He prophesied to the Kingdom of Judah during a time of moral and spiritual decline.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which Isaiah addressed in his prophecies. The people of Judah were often warned about their sinful behaviors and the consequences thereof.

3. Wine and Beer
These are symbols of indulgence and excess in the context of this verse. They represent the moral decay and lack of self-control among the people.

4. Woe
A term used frequently by prophets to pronounce judgment or express lament over the sinful actions of the people.

5. Heroes and Champions
In this context, these terms are used ironically to describe those who pride themselves on their ability to consume large amounts of alcohol, highlighting their misplaced values.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Excess
This verse warns against the dangers of excessive drinking and the false sense of heroism it can create. It calls for self-control and moderation.

Misplaced Values
The use of "heroes" and "champions" ironically points to the misplaced values of society. True heroism is found in righteousness and godly living, not in indulgence.

Spiritual Sobriety
Just as physical sobriety is important, spiritual sobriety is crucial. We must remain alert and self-controlled, avoiding anything that dulls our spiritual senses.

Judgment and Accountability
The "woe" pronounced here serves as a reminder of the judgment that comes from living a life contrary to God's commands. We are accountable for our actions.

Influence and Responsibility
Our actions influence others. We must be mindful of how our behavior, especially in areas like alcohol consumption, affects those around us.

What Does Isaiah 5:22 Mean
Woe to those

• “Woe” signals God’s solemn warning of impending judgment, not mere disappointment (cf. Isaiah 5:20; 10:1).

• The warning is directed at people who have grown comfortable in sin, thinking there will be no consequences (cf. Amos 6:1; Romans 2:4-5).

• By beginning with “Woe,” the Lord sets a sober tone: His holiness cannot overlook deliberate rebellion (cf. Hebrews 10:30-31).

who are heroes in drinking wine

• “Heroes” is usually reserved for warriors of courage (cf. 2 Samuel 23:8-17), but here the term is used sarcastically. Their only battlefield is the banquet table.

• God mocks their misplaced pride: instead of mastering sin, they are mastered by it (cf. Proverbs 20:1; John 8:34).

• Wine itself is a good gift when received with gratitude and self-control (cf. Psalm 104:14-15; 1 Timothy 5:23), yet abuse turns blessing into bondage (cf. Proverbs 23:29-32).

• Drunken “heroes” forget the fear of the Lord, ignore His law, and lose moral clarity—setting the stage for the wider corruption cataloged in Isaiah 5 (cf. verses 23-24; Ephesians 5:18).

and champions in mixing strong drink

• “Champions” again highlights ironic applause: they excel at what offends God (cf. Philippians 3:19).

• “Mixing strong drink” suggests lavish, carefully crafted cocktails—a picture of calculated indulgence rather than an occasional lapse (cf. Isaiah 28:7-8).

• Their skill in pouring spirits is matched by skill in perverting justice (see the next verse, Isaiah 5:23), showing how personal vice spills into social evil.

• Scripture consistently pairs drunkenness with moral dullness and leadership failure (cf. Proverbs 31:4-5; Hosea 4:11; 1 Thessalonians 5:6-8). When rulers or influencers glorify substance abuse, entire communities suffer (cf. Habakkuk 2:15).

summary

Isaiah 5:22 pronounces God’s “Woe” on those who brag about their drinking prowess. The verse exposes a tragic inversion: men hailed as “heroes” and “champions” are, in fact, enslaved weaklings. Their celebration of intoxication numbs conscience, distorts judgment, and helps unravel society. The Lord’s warning stands: revelry that ignores His holiness invites His judgment, while those who walk in sobriety and reverence find true honor and lasting joy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) Woe unto them that are mighty to drink. . . . strong drink.--The words in part reproduce the "woe" of Isaiah 5:11-12, but with the distinctive feature that there the revellers were simply of the careless self-indulgent type, while here they are identified with the unjust and corrupt rulers. They were heroes and valiant men only in and for their cups. To such men it seemed a light matter to acquit the guilty and condemn the guiltless. The prophet dwells on the familiar truth, Judex damnatur cum nocens absolvitur. The Targum, it may be noticed, has "the mammon of falsehood" (comp. Luke xvi, 9), for the "reward" of the Hebrew.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Woe unto them that are mighty to drink wine. The sixth woe seems at first sight a repetition of the second. But there is this difference, that the drinkers in the present verse do not succumb to their liquor, or remain at the banquet all day, but proceed to the business of their lives, attend courts and judge causes, but with brain obfuscated and moral vision bedimmed, so that they are easily induced to pervert justice on receipt of a bribe. The sixth woe may be considered to be pronounced rather upon their corruption than upon their drinking, and so to be really quite distinct from the second (comp. Proverbs 31:4, 5).
