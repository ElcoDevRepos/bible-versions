Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
a spirit of justice to him who sits in judgment
This phrase highlights the divine empowerment given to leaders who are tasked with making judicial decisions. In the context of ancient Israel, judges and leaders were expected to uphold God's law and administer justice fairly. The "spirit of justice" implies a divine influence or guidance, reminiscent of the wisdom granted to Solomon (1 Kings 3:28). This aligns with the broader biblical theme that true justice originates from God (Psalm 89:14). The phrase also foreshadows the Messianic prophecy of Jesus Christ, who embodies perfect justice (Isaiah 11:2-4).

and a strength to those who repel the onslaught at the gate
In ancient cities, gates were strategic points of defense and commerce. The "onslaught at the gate" refers to military attacks or sieges, common in the turbulent history of Israel. The promise of "strength" suggests divine support for those defending the city, akin to the protection God provided to Jerusalem during the Assyrian siege (2 Kings 19:32-36). This strength can be seen as both physical and spiritual, empowering defenders to withstand external threats. Theologically, it points to God's role as a protector and deliverer, a theme echoed in the New Testament where believers are called to stand firm against spiritual adversaries (Ephesians 6:10-17).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book bearing his name. He prophesied to the Kingdom of Judah during a time of moral and spiritual decline.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which was often under threat from surrounding nations. Isaiah's prophecies were directed towards the leaders and people of Judah.

3. Judges
Those who sit in judgment, likely referring to leaders or rulers in Judah who are responsible for maintaining justice and order.

4. Gates
In ancient cities, gates were strategic points of defense and commerce. They were often the first line of defense against enemy attacks.

5. Enemies
The surrounding nations that threatened Judah, representing both physical and spiritual adversaries.
Teaching Points
The Role of Justice in Leadership
Leaders are called to embody a spirit of justice, ensuring fairness and righteousness in their judgments. This is a divine empowerment that comes from God.

Strength in Times of Adversity
God provides strength to those who defend against attacks, whether physical or spiritual. Believers are encouraged to rely on God's strength in their battles.

Spiritual Readiness
Just as the gates were crucial for a city's defense, believers must be vigilant and prepared to defend their faith against spiritual attacks.

Dependence on the Holy Spirit
The "spirit of justice" implies reliance on the Holy Spirit for guidance and strength in fulfilling our roles, whether in leadership or daily life.

Community Defense
The imagery of repelling onslaughts at the gate suggests a communal effort. Believers are called to support and defend one another in faith.

What Does Isaiah 28:6 Mean
a spirit of justice

Isaiah declares that the LORD will be “a spirit of justice.” Justice is not merely an abstract principle; it is God Himself actively breathing fairness, wisdom, and moral clarity into human affairs. Scripture shows this pattern repeatedly: “He has told you, O man, what is good—and what does the LORD require of you but to act justly” (Micah 6:8). When the Spirit of the LORD came upon leaders like Othniel (Judges 3:10) or Jehoshaphat’s judges (2 Chronicles 19:6-7), impartial decisions followed. The same Spirit who empowered Solomon to discern rightly (1 Kings 3:28) is promised here to steady Israel’s courts once more.

to him who sits in judgment

The phrase pinpoints those occupying the bench or city gate, places where legal matters were settled (Ruth 4:1; Proverbs 31:23). God pledges to supply every righteous judge with discernment that aligns with His own character. Psalm 72:1-2 pictures this: “Grant the king Your justice, O God…may he judge Your people with righteousness.” In Isaiah’s context, corrupt leaders had erred (Isaiah 28:7), yet the Lord offers real, literal intervention—He Himself becoming their resource for upright verdicts.

a strength

The second half of verse 6 shifts from courts to combat: the LORD is “a strength.” The word evokes steady endurance and victorious power. David sang, “The LORD is my strength and my shield” (Psalm 28:7); Isaiah echoes that assurance. The same divine might that split the Red Sea (Exodus 15:2) stands ready to fortify His people when danger presses in.

to those who repel the onslaught at the gate

City gates were the first target in an invasion (2 Samuel 18:24). Defenders needed courage, skill, and stamina. Here God pledges to be the literal backbone of every soldier on the wall. Nehemiah encouraged his builders in similar language: “Do not be afraid…remember the Lord, who is great and awesome, and fight” (Nehemiah 4:14). Ultimately, Christ embodies this promise—He is the conquering King who “judges and wages war” (Revelation 19:11-16), ensuring that no enemy prevails against His people (Matthew 16:18).

summary

Isaiah 28:6 assures God’s people that He personally steps in when leadership and safety hang in the balance. He gifts judges with His own sense of right and wrong, and He girds warriors with supernatural might. Whether decisions in the courtroom or battles at the city gate, the Lord Himself is both wisdom and power for all who trust Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) And for a spirit of judgment . . .--The words remind us of the list of spiritual gifts in Isaiah 11:2. The injustice of corrupt judges was the crying evil of both Samaria and Jerusalem, and their place was to be taken by those who should be just and faithful. And brave warriors, able to drive back the enemy to the gate of the city from which they had issued forth (2Samuel 11:23)--or, perhaps, to defeat them at the gate of that which they attacked--should be the companions of the upright judges.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - For a spirit of judgment. How far Judah had departed from the spirit of just judgment was made apparent in the very opening chapter of Isaiah's prophecy (vers. 15-27). To him that sitteth in judgment; rather, that sitteth on the judgment-seat (Cheyne). For strength to them that turn the battle to the gate; i.e. "to those who repulse an enemy, and drive him back to his own city's gate" (comp. 2 Kings 18:8, "He smote the Philistines, even unto Gaza ").
