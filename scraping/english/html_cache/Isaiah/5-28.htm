Their arrows are sharpened
This phrase indicates readiness and precision in battle. In ancient warfare, sharpened arrows were essential for effective combat, symbolizing preparedness and the ability to strike with accuracy. The imagery of sharpened arrows can be connected to other biblical passages that speak of God's judgment or the readiness of His people, such as Psalm 7:13, where God prepares His instruments of judgment. The sharpening of arrows also reflects the meticulous preparation of the invading forces, emphasizing the imminent threat they pose.

and all their bows are strung
The strung bows signify that the army is fully prepared for immediate action. In the ancient Near East, bows were a primary weapon of war, and having them strung meant the soldiers were ready to engage at a moment's notice. This readiness can be seen as a metaphor for the swift and decisive nature of God's judgment. The imagery of strung bows is also found in other scriptures, such as Jeremiah 50:14, where it describes the readiness of Babylon's enemies.

The hooves of their horses are like flint
Flint is a hard, durable stone, and comparing the horses' hooves to flint suggests strength and resilience. Horses were a symbol of military power and speed in ancient times, and this description emphasizes the unstoppable force of the approaching army. The comparison to flint also implies that the army's advance is relentless and unyielding. This imagery can be linked to other biblical passages that describe the power and might of invading forces, such as in Joel 2:4, where the appearance of horses is likened to the sound of chariots.

their chariot wheels are like a whirlwind
The whirlwind imagery conveys speed, chaos, and overwhelming power. Chariots were a formidable component of ancient military forces, and their rapid movement could cause panic and destruction. The comparison to a whirlwind suggests not only the speed but also the destructive capability of the army. This imagery is reminiscent of other biblical descriptions of divine judgment, such as in Nahum 1:3, where the Lord's way is in the whirlwind and the storm. The whirlwind also symbolizes the swift execution of God's purposes, reflecting His sovereign control over the events of history.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book. He prophesied during the reigns of several kings of Judah and is known for his messages of both judgment and hope.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which Isaiah primarily addressed. The people of Judah were often warned by Isaiah about impending judgment due to their disobedience to God.

3. Assyrian Army
While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the context of Isaiah 5 includes warnings about foreign invaders, often interpreted as the Assyrian army, known for their military prowess and threat to Judah.

4. Chariots and Horses
Symbols of military strength and speed in ancient warfare, representing the formidable nature of the approaching threat.

5. God's Judgment
The overarching theme of Isaiah 5, where God uses foreign nations as instruments of His judgment against His people for their unfaithfulness.
Teaching Points
The Certainty of God's Judgment
God's warnings through His prophets are not idle threats. The vivid imagery of sharp arrows and swift chariots underscores the certainty and seriousness of His judgment.

The Instruments of God's Judgment
God can use any means, including foreign nations, to accomplish His purposes. This reminds us of His sovereignty over all nations and events.

The Call to Repentance
The warnings of impending judgment serve as a call to repentance. Just as Judah was warned, we too are called to examine our lives and turn back to God.

The Power of Imagery in Scripture
The Bible uses powerful imagery to convey spiritual truths. Reflecting on these images can deepen our understanding of God's messages.

Trust in God's Sovereignty
Even in the face of judgment, believers can trust in God's ultimate plan and His ability to bring about His purposes for good.

What Does Isaiah 5:28 Mean
Their arrows are sharpened

“ Their arrows are sharpened ” (Isaiah 5:28) pictures a force meticulously prepared for battle.

• Sharpened arrows communicate lethal precision; nothing about this invasion is haphazard (cf. Psalm 7:13, “He prepares His deadly weapons; He ordains His arrows with fire”).

• The emphasis on readiness underscores that God’s judgment does not arrive half-formed; He brings it through a well-equipped army (cf. Jeremiah 50:14).

• Because the surrounding verses speak of God “raising a banner for distant nations” (Isaiah 5:26), the sharpened arrows show the certainty of His announced discipline on Judah’s complacency.

and all their bows are strung

Once bows are strung, there is no delay between intent and action.

• The invaders keep weapons constantly tensioned, illustrating relentless resolve (cf. Psalm 11:2, “For behold, the wicked bend their bows; they set their arrow on the string”).

• Judah’s leaders had presumed they could manage external threats, yet God reveals attackers already poised to release judgment (cf. Amos 2:15).

• The image confronts any notion that divine warnings are empty; a strung bow reminds that God’s word is both promise and performance.

The hooves of their horses are like flint

Flint evokes hardness and spark-creating impact.

• Horses pounding ground with flint-like hooves signal unstoppable momentum (cf. Jeremiah 5:15-17, where the enemy’s horses are “swifter than leopards”).

• This strength is God-granted: He controls even the temperament of warhorses (Job 39:19-25).

• Judah had trusted in alliances and cavalry of its own (Isaiah 31:1), yet God now turns another nation’s cavalry against them, exposing misplaced confidence.

their chariot wheels are like a whirlwind

The whirlwind image blends speed, noise, and disorientation.

• Chariots racing with cyclone force mean the land will feel overwhelmed (cf. Nahum 2:3-4, which describes chariots flashing like fire).

• Whirlwinds in Scripture often mark God’s direct intervention (Isaiah 66:15). Here, the enemy’s machinery becomes the very whirlwind of divine presence in judgment.

• For the faithful remnant, the whirlwind also hints that God can later reverse calamity with equal swiftness (Habakkuk 3:8-13).

summary

Isaiah 5:28 paints a vivid, literal snapshot of an invading army—arrows honed, bows strung, horses unstoppable, chariots roaring. Each detail underlines the certainty and thoroughness of God’s impending judgment on a complacent people. What appears as the might of foreign soldiers is, in truth, the orchestrated hand of the Lord, proving His warnings sure and His sovereignty absolute, while calling hearts back to reverent trust.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - Whose arrows are sharp, and all their bows bent. The special weapon of the Assyrian soldiers is the bow. From the king in his chariot to the light-armed recruit just pressed into the service, all fight mainly with this weapon, more particularly in the earlier times (see 'Ancient Monarchies,' vol. 1. pp. 414, 423, 424-437, etc.). Swords and spears are also known, but comparatively little used. Their horses' hoofs... like flint. Hard, strong, and solid, as was most necessary when shoeing was unknown. Their wheels like a whirlwind. Sennacherib (Isaiah 37:24) is represented as boasting of the "multitude of his chariots;" and both the sculptures and the inscriptions of Assyria show that the chariot throe was numerous, and was regarded as more important than any other. The king always went to battle in a chariot. For the comparison of the rush of chariot-wheels to a whirlwind, see below, Isaiah 66:15; and comp. Jeremiah 4:13).
