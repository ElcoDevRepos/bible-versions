And Hezekiah prayed
Hezekiah, the king of Judah, is depicted as a devout leader who turns to God in times of crisis. This act of prayer signifies his reliance on divine intervention rather than solely on military or political solutions. Historically, Hezekiah's reign was marked by religious reforms and a return to the worship of Yahweh, as seen in 2 Kings 18:3-6. His prayer life is a model of faith and dependence on God, reflecting the biblical principle found in Philippians 4:6, which encourages believers to present their requests to God through prayer.

to the LORD:
The use of "LORD" in all capital letters indicates the Hebrew name Yahweh, the covenant name of God, emphasizing a personal and relational aspect of God with His people. This invocation of Yahweh highlights the covenant relationship between God and Israel, reminding readers of God's promises and faithfulness. Theologically, it underscores the importance of approaching God with reverence and recognition of His sovereignty, as seen in the broader narrative of the Old Testament where God is consistently portrayed as the protector and deliverer of Israel.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Hezekiah
The King of Judah during a time of great crisis. He is known for his faithfulness to God and his efforts to reform the religious practices of Judah.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, to whom Hezekiah directs his prayer. Yahweh is the central figure in the deliverance of Judah from its enemies.

3. Jerusalem
The capital city of Judah, under siege by the Assyrian army led by King Sennacherib. It is the focal point of Hezekiah's prayer for deliverance.

4. Assyrian Threat
The Assyrian Empire, under King Sennacherib, poses a significant threat to Judah, having already conquered many surrounding nations.

5. Isaiah the Prophet
A major prophet in Judah who provides counsel and assurance to Hezekiah during the Assyrian crisis.
Teaching Points
The Power of Prayer
Hezekiah's immediate response to crisis is to pray, demonstrating the importance of turning to God in times of trouble.

Faith in God's Sovereignty
Hezekiah's prayer reflects his belief in God's ultimate control over nations and events, encouraging believers to trust in God's sovereignty.

Seeking Godly Counsel
Hezekiah's relationship with Isaiah shows the value of seeking guidance from godly leaders and prophets.

God's Faithfulness
The account underscores God's faithfulness to His covenant people, reminding believers of His promises and protection.

Courage in Leadership
Hezekiah's actions provide a model of courageous leadership rooted in faith, inspiring leaders to rely on God in challenging times.

What Does Isaiah 37:15 Mean
And

• The conjunction ties this verse directly to what just happened: Sennacherib’s threatening letter (Isaiah 37:14; cf. 2 Kings 19:14).

• Instead of panic, the narrative immediately pivots—showing that every crisis for God’s people should be followed by earnest communion with Him (Psalm 46:1).

Hezekiah

• A king with a proven record of trusting the Lord (2 Kings 18:5), he models leadership that seeks divine, not merely political, solutions.

• His name’s appearance here reminds us that even the most powerful earthly figures remain dependent on heaven’s throne (2 Chronicles 32:1–8).

prayed

• Action, not mere intention: Hezekiah takes his burden straight to God (Philippians 4:6).

• Prayer is presented as the primary weapon against overwhelming odds—more decisive than armies or alliances (James 5:16; Acts 4:24).

• The narrative underscores persistence: earlier he had sought Isaiah’s intercession (Isaiah 37:4); now he personally petitions the Lord, revealing a growing, personal faith.

to the LORD:

• The object of faith is specific—the covenant God who revealed Himself to Moses (Exodus 3:15).

• By addressing “the LORD,” Hezekiah appeals to the One who rules nations (Psalm 22:28) and keeps promises to His people (Deuteronomy 7:9).

• The colon hints that what follows is a recorded prayer (Isaiah 37:16–20), setting a pattern: acknowledge God’s greatness, recount the enemy’s blasphemy, and ask for deliverance “so that all kingdoms of the earth may know that You alone, LORD, are God” (v. 20; cf. Jeremiah 29:12).

summary

Isaiah 37:15 captures a decisive moment: in the face of impossible odds, Judah’s king turns immediately to the covenant-keeping God. The verse teaches that crises should propel God’s people into prayer, that leaders must model dependence on the Lord, and that victory begins not on the battlefield but in the throne room of heaven.
