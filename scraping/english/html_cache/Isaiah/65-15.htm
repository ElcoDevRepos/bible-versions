You will leave behind your name as a curse for My chosen ones
This phrase indicates a divine judgment where the name of the wicked becomes synonymous with a curse. In biblical times, names held significant meaning and were often reflective of one's character or destiny. The idea of a name becoming a curse suggests a complete reversal of fortune and reputation. This is reminiscent of other biblical figures whose names became bywords for evil, such as Judas. The "chosen ones" refers to the faithful remnant of Israel, God's elect, who are set apart for His purposes. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where He promises to protect and vindicate them.

and the Lord GOD will slay you
This part of the verse underscores the certainty of divine retribution. The use of "Lord GOD" emphasizes the sovereignty and authority of God in executing judgment. Historically, this can be seen as a warning to those in Israel who were unfaithful, aligning with the broader prophetic theme of judgment against idolatry and injustice. The slaying is both literal and metaphorical, indicating both physical destruction and spiritual separation from God. This echoes the fate of the unrepentant throughout the prophetic books, where God's justice is portrayed as inevitable and righteous.

but to His servants He will give another name
The promise of "another name" signifies a new identity and status bestowed by God. In biblical tradition, receiving a new name often marks a transformation or a new mission, as seen with Abram becoming Abraham or Jacob becoming Israel. This new name represents a fresh start and a renewed relationship with God. For the faithful remnant, it symbolizes their vindication and the blessings of being God's servants. This concept is further developed in the New Testament, where believers are given a new identity in Christ, reflecting the transformative power of salvation and the hope of eternal life.

What Does Isaiah 65:15 Mean
You will leave behind your name as a curse for My chosen ones

• Isaiah is contrasting the fate of the unfaithful with the future of the faithful. Those who persist in rebellion (cf. Isaiah 65:2-7) will see their very name become a warning label for the righteous remnant—much like Deuteronomy 28:37 and Jeremiah 29:22 describe names turned into bywords of judgment.

• This curse is not merely verbal; it signals disgrace, exclusion, and shame (Psalm 69:28; Matthew 23:35-36). The remnant will look back and use the rebels’ memory as a proverb of what happens when people harden their hearts (Luke 13:34-35).

and the Lord GOD will slay you

• The judgment hinted at in the previous clause now becomes explicit: God Himself executes the sentence. Isaiah earlier declared, “The LORD will bring judgment on all flesh” (Isaiah 66:16). Here the rebels face that verdict.

• Historical tastes of this came through invasions and exile, yet the wording pulls us toward final judgment as well (2 Thessalonians 1:7-9; Revelation 19:15). Those who reject God’s covenant grace meet His covenant justice (Romans 1:18).

• This sober reminder underlines the certainty and severity of divine holiness; sins unrepented lead to death (Ezekiel 18:4; Romans 6:23).

but to His servants He will give another name

• In sharp contrast, the servants—those who trust and obey—receive a fresh identity. Scripture ties “another name” to restoration, honor, and intimacy with God (Isaiah 62:2; Revelation 2:17; 3:12).

• This new name signals:

– Adoption into God’s family (John 1:12; Galatians 4:7)

– Participation in the promises to Abraham (Galatians 3:29)

– Transformation of character and destiny (2 Corinthians 5:17)

• Just as Abram became Abraham and Saul became Paul, faithful believers exchange their old reputation for a Spirit-given one that reflects God’s grace (Isaiah 56:5).

summary

Isaiah 65:15 contrasts two destinies: rebels leave behind a cursed name and meet God’s judgment, while obedient servants receive a new, honored name that embodies covenant blessing. The verse underscores God’s unwavering justice against sin and His lavish mercy toward those who follow Him, urging every reader to forsake rebellion and embrace the identity He offers through faith.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) Ye shall leave your name for a curse . . .--The phrase has parallels in Numbers 5:21; Zechariah 8:13; Jeremiah 29:22, the thought in each case being that the person named is under so heavy a penalty from the wrath of Jehovah that he becomes a representative instance of what that wrath can accomplish, and because the old name, say of Jacob or of Judah, has been thus identified with evil. He will call His chosen ones, the true Israel, as by another name, which shall be for blessing, and not for cursing. (Comp. Isaiah 62:2, Revelation 2:17; Revelation 3:12.) . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - Ye shall leave your name for a curse (comp. Jeremiah 29:22). In their formulas of imprecation the Jews were in the habit of saying, "The Lord make thee like" this or that person, or this or that class of persons. The name of the exiles should be used in this manner. Unto my chosen (see the comment on ver. 9). The Lord God shall slay thee (see the comment on ver. 12). Some, however, take the words as part of the formula of imprecation. And call his servants by another name (compare what is said of "new name" in Isaiah 62:2).
