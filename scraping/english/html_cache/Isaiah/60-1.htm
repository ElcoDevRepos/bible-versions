Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Arise, shine
This phrase is a call to action, urging the people of God to awaken from spiritual slumber and reflect the light of God's presence. The imperative "arise" suggests a transition from a state of inactivity or darkness to one of activity and illumination. In the context of Isaiah, this is a prophetic call to the nation of Israel, encouraging them to step into their role as a light to the nations. The command to "shine" implies that the light is not inherent but is a reflection of God's glory. This echoes the creation narrative where God commands light to shine out of darkness (Genesis 1:3). It also prefigures the New Testament call for believers to be the light of the world (Matthew 5:14-16).

for your light has come
The "light" here symbolizes salvation and the presence of God. This phrase indicates the arrival of a new era marked by divine intervention and blessing. In the historical context, this can be seen as a promise of restoration for Israel after the Babylonian exile. Theologically, it points to the coming of the Messiah, who is often associated with light (John 1:4-9). The fulfillment of this prophecy is seen in the advent of Jesus Christ, who declares Himself as the Light of the World (John 8:12). The light represents truth, guidance, and the revelation of God's will.

and the glory of the LORD rises upon you
The "glory of the LORD" refers to the manifest presence of God, often associated with His holiness and power. In the Old Testament, God's glory is depicted as a radiant light, such as the Shekinah glory that filled the tabernacle and the temple (Exodus 40:34-35, 1 Kings 8:10-11). The phrase "rises upon you" suggests a dawn or new beginning, indicating that God's favor and presence are now visibly resting upon His people. This is a promise of divine favor and blessing, signifying that God's covenant relationship with Israel is being renewed. In a broader sense, it foreshadows the ultimate revelation of God's glory in the person of Jesus Christ and the establishment of His kingdom. This rising glory is also a call to reflect God's character and mission to the world, aligning with the prophetic vision of a restored and glorified Zion.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is traditionally credited with writing the Book of Isaiah. His prophecies often focus on the themes of judgment and redemption.

2. Jerusalem
The city is often the focal point of Isaiah's prophecies, symbolizing the people of Israel and their spiritual state. In Isaiah 60, Jerusalem is depicted as a city that will rise and shine with God's glory.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, whose glory is described as rising upon His people, signifying His presence and favor.

4. The Light
Symbolic of God's presence, truth, and salvation. In the context of Isaiah 60, it represents the coming of God's glory and the fulfillment of His promises.

5. The Nations
While not explicitly mentioned in verse 1, the surrounding context of Isaiah 60 speaks of nations coming to the light of God's people, indicating a future time of global recognition of God's glory.
Teaching Points
The Call to Action
"Arise, shine" is an imperative, calling believers to actively reflect God's light in their lives. It is a call to spiritual awakening and readiness.

The Source of Light
The light is not self-generated; it comes from the LORD. Believers are reminded that their ability to shine comes from God's presence and glory in their lives.

The Glory of the LORD
God's glory is transformative. When it rises upon His people, it changes them and their circumstances, bringing hope and restoration.

Impact on the World
As God's people shine with His light, they become a beacon to the nations, drawing others to the truth and salvation found in God.

Living in Expectation
Isaiah 60:1 encourages believers to live with the expectation of God's promises being fulfilled, both in their personal lives and in the world.

What Does Isaiah 60:1 Mean
Arise

“Arise” is the Lord’s summons to stand up—physically, spiritually, and morally.

• For Israel in Isaiah’s day, it was a call to rise from the rubble of exile and expect restoration (Isaiah 52:1-2).

• For every believer, it echoes the gospel’s wake-up call: “Wake up, O sleeper, rise from the dead, and Christ will shine on you” (Ephesians 5:14).

• The verb insists on decisive action; we leave passivity behind and step into God’s purposes (Romans 13:11-12).

Shine

Once up, we are told to shine—reflect, not manufacture, light.

• Our radiance derives solely from the Lord; we are “like the moon” reflecting the sun.

• Jesus taught, “You are the light of the world… let your light shine before men” (Matthew 5:14-16), linking Isaiah 60 directly to kingdom witness.

• A life that “shines” features clear obedience, compassionate works, and verbal testimony (Philippians 2:15-16).

Your Light Has Come

Why can we shine? Because “your light has come.”

• Isaiah foresaw the Messiah: “The people walking in darkness have seen a great light” (Isaiah 9:2).

• Jesus declared, “I am the Light of the world” (John 8:12). His incarnation, cross, and resurrection bring the dawn of salvation (John 1:4-9; 2 Corinthians 4:6).

• The verb tense—has come—underscores certainty; God’s redemptive light is already present, not merely future.

The Glory of the LORD Rises Upon You

The verse crescendos with God’s own glory settling on His people.

• Glory (kavod) is God’s manifested presence; it once filled the tabernacle (Exodus 40:34-35) and will fill the New Jerusalem, where “the glory of God gives it light” (Revelation 21:23).

• Isaiah pictures a reversal of Ezekiel 10, where glory departed; now “the glory of the LORD rises upon you” (cf. Ezekiel 43:2-5).

• The phrase anticipates Israel’s future restoration (Isaiah 60:19-22) and the church’s present experience of the Spirit’s indwelling (2 Corinthians 3:18).

• Because God’s glory rests on His people, darkness around them only highlights heaven’s brightness (Isaiah 60:2).

Summary

Isaiah 60:1 calls God’s people to wake up, stand up, and light up the world. We arise from sin and despair, we shine with the reflected brilliance of Christ, we live in the reality that our Light—Jesus—has already come, and we rejoice that God’s own glory now and forever rises over us.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
LX.

(1) Arise, shine . . .--The description of the redeemed Zion--i.e., the new Jerusalem--seen in the prophet's vision as under the forms of the old. She has been prostrate, as in the darkness of Sheol (as in Isaiah 51:23; Isaiah 57:9). The word comes that bids her rise to a new life, radiant with the glory of the Lord. In Ephesians 5:14 we have, perhaps, an echo, though not a quotation, of the prophet's words.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - Arise, shine. The subject of the address does not distinctly appear until ver. 14, where it is found to be "the city of the Lord, the Zion of the Holy One of Israel." Zion has long been prostrate in the dust from the prophet's standpoint, and covered with thick darkness. Now she is bidden to "arise" and "shine forth as the day." For thy light is come. Zion cannot shine with her own light, for she has no light of her own, having preferred to "walk in darkness" (Isaiah 59:9). But she may reflect the radiance which streams from the Person of Jehovah, whose glory is risen upon her. "In thy light shall we see light" (Psalm 36:9).
