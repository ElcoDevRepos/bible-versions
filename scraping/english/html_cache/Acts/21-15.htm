Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
After these days
This phrase indicates a transition period following previous events. In the context of Acts, Paul had been in Caesarea, staying with Philip the evangelist. The "days" refer to the time spent in fellowship and preparation for the journey to Jerusalem. This reflects the early Christian practice of community and hospitality, as seen in Acts 2:42-47.

we packed up
The act of packing up suggests preparation and intention. It implies a deliberate decision to move forward despite potential challenges. This reflects the missionary zeal and commitment of Paul and his companions. The use of "we" indicates the presence of Luke, the author of Acts, as part of the traveling group, emphasizing the firsthand account of the events.

and went on to Jerusalem
Jerusalem holds significant importance as the center of Jewish worship and the early Christian church. Paul's journey to Jerusalem is pivotal, as it fulfills his desire to bring aid to the believers there, as mentioned in Romans 15:25-27. This journey also foreshadows the trials and tribulations Paul will face, similar to Jesus' own journey to Jerusalem. The determination to go to Jerusalem despite warnings (Acts 21:10-14) highlights Paul's commitment to his mission and the spread of the Gospel.

Persons / Places / Events

What Does Acts 21:15 Mean
After these days

“After these days” (Acts 21:15) points back to Paul’s extended stay in Caesarea with Philip the evangelist and the prophetic warnings from Agabus (Acts 21:8–14).

• Luke signals a deliberate pause that allowed the believers to pray, reflect, and steel themselves for what lay ahead, just as Jesus often withdrew before decisive moments (Mark 1:35; Luke 6:12).

• The phrase reminds us that God orders time and events; each “day” is part of His sovereign timeline (Psalm 31:15).

we packed up

To “pack up” implies careful, intentional preparation.

• Paul and his companions gathered offerings for the Jerusalem church (Romans 15:25–26) and readied themselves for danger already foretold (Acts 20:22–23).

• Their actions echo Israel’s readiness to move when the cloud lifted (Numbers 9:17), teaching us to prepare promptly when God signals the next step.

and went on

Luke’s wording conveys steady, determined movement.

• Despite repeated pleas to stay, Paul pressed forward in obedience, following Christ’s pattern of resolute advance toward the cross (Luke 9:51).

• Faith often means moving ahead while trusting God with unknown outcomes (Hebrews 11:8).

to Jerusalem

Jerusalem is more than a destination; it is symbolic of God’s redemptive plan.

• For Paul, bringing Gentile offerings there fulfilled the unity of the body (Ephesians 2:14–16) and demonstrated the gospel’s power to break ethnic barriers (Acts 24:17).

• The city where Jesus was crucified (John 19:17–18) and rose again (Luke 24:46) now becomes the backdrop for Paul’s own suffering and testimony (Acts 21:27–36).

• Like Paul, believers are called to carry their witness into the heart of opposition, confident that God turns hardship into gospel advance (Philippians 1:12–14).

summary

Acts 21:15 captures a decisive, obedient transition: after prayerful waiting, Paul and his team deliberately prepared, set out, and journeyed to the spiritually pivotal city of Jerusalem. Their example encourages us to honor God’s timing, prepare faithfully, move forward confidently, and embrace whatever place He sends us—even when the road leads through difficulty—for the glory of Christ and the unity of His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) After those days we took up our carriages . . .--Better, we took up our baggage. The English word now used always of the vehicle that carries, was in common use at the time of the Authorised version, for the things carried--the luggage or impedimenta of a traveller. So, in 1Samuel 17:22, David leaves his carriage (or, as in the margin, the vessels from upon him) in the hand of the "keeper of the carriage." So, in Udal's translation of Erasmus's Paraphrase of the New Testament (Luke 5:14), the bearers of the paralytic are said to have "taken their 'heavie carriage' to the house-roof." (Comp. also Judges 18:21; Isaiah 10:28; Isaiah 46:1.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - These for those, A.V.; baggage for carriages, A.V. We took up, etc. Απισκευασάμενοι, is the reading of the R.T., as of Mill, Bengel, Griesbach, Lachmann, Tischendorf, Meyer, Alford, etc. It occurs only here in the New Testament, but is common in classical Greek, in the sense of "fitting out for a journey," "lading a ship" or "beasts of burden" with baggage, "collecting baggage," and the like. The ἀποσκευάζεσθαι of the A.V. means" to unload," "to get rid of baggage," and thence generally "to remove," which gives no good sense here.
