Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
After they had prayed
Prayer is a central theme in the early church, reflecting dependence on God for guidance and strength. The apostles and early believers often gathered for prayer, as seen in Acts 1:14 and Acts 2:42. This communal prayer underscores the unity and faith of the early Christians, who sought divine intervention in the face of persecution. The act of praying together also reflects Jewish traditions of communal prayer, which were integral to worship and community life.

their meeting place was shaken
The physical shaking of the meeting place is reminiscent of God's presence in the Old Testament, such as at Mount Sinai (Exodus 19:18). It signifies divine approval and the power of God manifesting in response to prayer. This event parallels the shaking of the earth at Jesus' crucifixion (Matthew 27:51) and resurrection (Matthew 28:2), symbolizing God's active involvement in the unfolding of salvation history.

and they were all filled with the Holy Spirit
The filling of the Holy Spirit is a recurring event in Acts, first occurring at Pentecost (Acts 2:4). This filling empowers believers for ministry and bold proclamation of the gospel. It fulfills Jesus' promise in Acts 1:8 that the disciples would receive power when the Holy Spirit comes upon them. The Holy Spirit's role is central in the book of Acts, guiding, empowering, and sustaining the early church.

and spoke the word of God boldly
Boldness in speaking the word of God is a hallmark of the apostles' ministry, as seen in Acts 4:13 and Acts 9:27-28. This boldness is a direct result of the Holy Spirit's empowerment, enabling the apostles to preach despite threats and opposition. The phrase emphasizes the transformative power of the Holy Spirit, turning ordinary men into fearless witnesses of Christ. This bold proclamation fulfills the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20) and is a testament to the truth and urgency of the gospel message.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Apostles and Early Believers
The primary individuals involved in this passage are the apostles and the early Christian community. They were gathered together in prayer, seeking God's guidance and strength.

2. The Meeting Place
The specific location is not named, but it is a place where the believers gathered to pray. The shaking of this place signifies God's powerful presence.

3. The Holy Spirit
The third person of the Trinity, who fills the believers, empowering them to speak God's word with boldness.

4. Prayer
The act of communicating with God, which precedes the filling of the Holy Spirit and the bold proclamation of the word.

5. Bold Proclamation
The result of being filled with the Holy Spirit, enabling the believers to speak the word of God without fear.
Teaching Points
The Power of Unified Prayer
When believers come together in prayer, God responds in powerful ways. The unity and earnestness of the early church's prayer led to a tangible manifestation of God's presence.

The Role of the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit is essential for boldness in witnessing. Just as the early believers were filled and empowered, we too must rely on the Spirit for courage and effectiveness in sharing the gospel.

Boldness in Proclamation
Speaking the word of God boldly is a direct result of being filled with the Holy Spirit. We should seek this boldness in our own lives, trusting that God will provide the words and courage needed.

God's Presence in Our Gatherings
The shaking of the meeting place symbolizes God's active presence. We should expect and seek God's presence in our gatherings, knowing that He is with us and working through us.

Continual Filling of the Spirit
The filling of the Holy Spirit is not a one-time event but an ongoing experience. We should continually seek to be filled with the Spirit for daily living and ministry.

What Does Acts 4:31 Mean
After they had prayed

The verse opens by anchoring everything that follows in corporate, persistent prayer. Earlier the believers “devoted themselves to prayer” (Acts 2:42), and again they gather with one voice after Peter and John report the Sanhedrin’s threats (Acts 4:23-24). Prayer here is:

• Unified—“with one accord” (Acts 1:14).

• Scripture-saturated—they cite Psalm 2 in their request (Acts 4:25-26).

• Mission-focused—they ask not for comfort but for boldness (Acts 4:29).

God consistently responds to such prayer: compare Elijah calling down fire in 1 Kings 18:36-38 and the church’s later intercession for Peter in Acts 12:5-11.

their meeting place was shaken

The literal physical shaking signals God’s immediate, tangible response. Similar manifestations mark decisive divine encounters: Mount Sinai “trembled violently” when the Lord descended (Exodus 19:18), Isaiah saw the temple thresholds shake (Isaiah 6:4), and an earthquake freed Paul and Silas while they prayed and sang hymns (Acts 16:25-26). Here, the trembling room underscores that the Sovereign Lord they just addressed (Acts 4:24) is present and active, authenticating their mission.

and they were all filled with the Holy Spirit

This is a fresh filling, not a first coming; many present had already received the Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2:4). Scripture shows repeated fillings for empowered service (Acts 9:17; 13:9), echoing the call “be filled with the Spirit” (Ephesians 5:18). God supplies exactly what they requested in Acts 4:29: divine enablement to continue His work. The filling produces immediate, observable fruit, just as in Luke 1:41-42 when Elizabeth erupted in Spirit-inspired praise.

and spoke the word of God boldly

Boldness (parrēsia) is fearless, Spirit-given freedom to declare truth. Earlier the council “recognized that they had been with Jesus” because of this very boldness (Acts 4:13). Now, despite fresh threats, they proclaim without hesitation—fulfilling Acts 1:8, “you will receive power… and you will be My witnesses.” The pattern runs through Acts: Paul and Barnabas “spoke boldly for the Lord” (Acts 14:3), and Paul in Rome kept “preaching the kingdom of God with all boldness” (Acts 28:31). Bold speech is the natural overflow of Spirit-filled hearts (2 Timothy 1:7; Ephesians 6:19-20).

summary

Acts 4:31 portrays a seamless sequence: unified prayer, divine confirmation, Spirit empowerment, and fearless proclamation. God answers a faith-filled petition by shaking the room, filling every believer afresh, and propelling them to speak His word with courage. The verse assures us that when God’s people pray according to His will and rely on His Spirit, He supplies real power to advance the gospel, no matter the opposition.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) The place was shaken. . . .--The impression on the senses was so far a renewal of the wonder of the Day of Pentecost, but in this instance without the sign of the tongues of fire, which were the symbols of a gift imparted once for all, and, perhaps also, without the special marvel of the utterance of the tongues. The disciples felt the power of the Spirit, the evidence of sense confirming that of inward, spiritual consciousness, and it came in the form for which they had made a special supplication, the power to speak with boldness the word which they were commissioned to speak.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - Wherein they were gathered for when they were assembled, A.V. When they had prayed. When they had finished the preceding prayer. The place was shaken, perhaps by a mighty wind, as in Acts 2:2. The word σαλεύεσθαι is properly used of ships or of the sea agitated and tossed by the wind; so Matthew 11:7, "A reed shaken by the wind." But it is also applied to the rocking caused by an earthquake (Acts 16:26), which maybe the kind of shaking here meant. In this fresh outpouring or the Spirit, whereby they were enabled to speak the word of God with boldness, they had a direct and immediate answer to their prayer (see Isaiah 65:24).
