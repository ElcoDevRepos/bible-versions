Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
In those days
This phrase refers to the period following Jesus' ascension and before the day of Pentecost. It was a time of waiting and prayer for the disciples, as instructed by Jesus in Acts 1:4. This period was crucial for the early church as they prepared for the coming of the Holy Spirit.

Peter stood up
Peter, often seen as the leader among the apostles, takes initiative here. His leadership role is consistent with Jesus' words in Matthew 16:18, where He refers to Peter as the rock on which He will build His church. Peter's actions here set the stage for his prominent role in the early church.

among the brothers
The term "brothers" indicates the close-knit community of believers, united by their faith in Christ. This group included the apostles and other followers of Jesus, emphasizing the familial bond among early Christians, as seen in passages like Romans 12:10.

(a gathering of about a hundred and twenty)
This number signifies the small but significant size of the early Christian community in Jerusalem. The gathering likely included the apostles, Jesus' family, and other disciples. The number 120 is also significant in Jewish tradition, as it was the minimum number required to form a community with its own council.

and said
Peter's speech marks the beginning of the apostolic leadership in the church. His words are not only a call to action but also fulfill the need for guidance and decision-making within the early church. This moment foreshadows Peter's future sermons and leadership, as seen in Acts 2:14-41.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Peter
One of Jesus' twelve apostles, Peter often took a leadership role among the disciples. In this passage, he stands up to address the early Christian community.

2. The Brothers
Refers to the early followers of Jesus, including the apostles and other disciples. The term "brothers" (Greek: adelphoi) is often used to denote both male and female believers in the early church.

3. The Gathering
Approximately 120 people were present, indicating the size of the early Christian community in Jerusalem at this time.

4. Jerusalem
The city where the early church was gathering, and where significant events in the early Christian movement took place.

5. The Days Following Jesus' Ascension
This event occurs shortly after Jesus' ascension into heaven, during a time of waiting for the promised Holy Spirit.
Teaching Points
Leadership in the Early Church
Peter's role in addressing the assembly shows the importance of leadership in guiding and organizing the early church community.

Unity and Fellowship
The gathering of 120 believers demonstrates the unity and fellowship among the early Christians, emphasizing the importance of community in the faith.

Preparation for the Holy Spirit
This period of waiting and prayer highlights the necessity of spiritual preparation and anticipation for God's promises.

The Role of Scripture in Decision-Making
Peter's later use of Scripture to guide the selection of a new apostle (Acts 1:20) underscores the importance of Scripture in guiding church decisions.

What Does Acts 1:15 Mean
In those days

• Luke pinpoints the short window between the Lord’s ascension and the outpouring of the Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 1:3-5, 2:1).

• The phrase roots the scene in real history, not legend; Jesus “presented Himself alive” for forty days, so these events fall inside that literal timeline.

• It shows obedient waiting. Jesus had said, “Stay in the city until you are clothed with power from on high” (Luke 24:49). The disciples took that word seriously.

• Application: every work of God has its divinely appointed season (Ecclesiastes 3:1); patience is faith in action.

Peter stood up

• Peter’s physical act signals Spirit-given leadership already stirring in him, fulfilling Christ’s charge: “Feed My sheep” (John 21:15-17).

• He rises before Pentecost, demonstrating that the ascended Christ is still guiding His flock through chosen servants (cf. Matthew 16:18-19).

• This standing foreshadows Acts 2:14 where Peter again rises to proclaim the gospel—boldness replacing former fear (Luke 22:57).

• Application: God uses restored people; past failure does not disqualify future usefulness when grace has intervened.

among the brothers (a gathering of about a hundred and twenty)

• The number is literal, attesting to eyewitness precision. Luke notes “about a hundred and twenty” to underline the small yet sufficient nucleus from which the worldwide church would grow (Matthew 13:31-32).

• “Brothers” embraces men and women united in Christ; v.14 already listed women and Mary His mother. Their unity fulfills Psalm 133:1 and anticipates Acts 4:32.

• They met in a larger upper-room setting (Acts 1:13). Scripture often highlights faithful remnants—Noah’s eight (Genesis 7:13), Gideon’s three hundred (Judges 7:7).

• Application: God measures success by faithfulness, not crowd size; obedient minorities can shake nations (1 Corinthians 1:27-29).

and said

• Peter’s words will lead to selecting a twelfth apostle, grounding every decision in Scripture: “Brothers, the Scripture had to be fulfilled…” (Acts 1:16).

• The moment models congregational life shaped by the Word (2 Timothy 3:16-17) and prayerful consensus (Acts 1:24-25).

• It also shows initiative; faith does not paralyze but propels action in line with revealed truth (James 2:17).

• Application: gatherings that listen to the Lord through His written Word receive clear direction (Psalm 119:105).

summary

Acts 1:15 captures a pivotal pause between promise and power. In those literal days of waiting, Peter—restored and appointed—stands to lead a united, expectant band of 120 believers. Their story reminds us that God works through humble numbers, raises forgiven leaders, and guides His people by Scripture while they await the next move of His Spirit.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) The number of names together were about an hundred and twenty.--The number probably included the Seventy of Luke 10:1, perhaps also Joseph of Arimathaea and Nicodemus, and some of the "five hundred" who had seen their risen Lord in Galilee or elsewhere (1Corinthians 15:6). The use of "names" may be merely as a synonym for "persons," but It suggests the idea of there having been a list from which St. Luke extracted those that seemed most conspicuous.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - These for those, A.V.; brethren for disciples, A.V. and T.R.; and there was a multitude of persons gathered together for the number of names together were, A.V.; a for an, A.V. Peter justifies his primacy by taking the lead in the first onward movement of the Church. Names is a common Hebraism for "persons" (see Revelation 3:4; Numbers 1:2). Gathered together; i.e. to one place and at one time (see the same phrase, Acts 2:1, 44). Wordsworth quotes Ignat., 'Ad Magnes' 7, and Clem. Romans 1:4, where the same phrase, ἐπὶ τὸ αὐτὸ, indicative of Church unity occurs.
