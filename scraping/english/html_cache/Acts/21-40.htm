Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Having received permission
Paul, having been granted permission by the Roman commander, demonstrates the legal and orderly process of Roman governance. This reflects the Roman respect for due process, even for those accused of causing disturbances. It also highlights Paul's Roman citizenship, which afforded him certain rights and protections.

Paul stood on the steps
The steps likely refer to those leading up to the Antonia Fortress, a military barracks in Jerusalem. This location is significant as it was a place of authority and control, symbolizing the power dynamics at play between the Roman authorities and the Jewish populace. Paul's position on the steps also provided a vantage point from which he could address the large crowd.

and motioned to the crowd
Paul's gesture to the crowd indicates his desire to communicate and engage with them. This act of motioning is a common rhetorical technique used to gain attention and establish a connection with an audience. It shows Paul's intent to speak directly and personally to those gathered.

A great hush came over the crowd
The sudden silence of the crowd suggests a moment of anticipation and respect, possibly due to Paul's reputation and the authority with which he carried himself. This silence also indicates the crowd's willingness to listen, at least initially, to what Paul had to say.

and he addressed them in Hebrew
Paul's choice to speak in Hebrew (or Aramaic, the common language of the Jews at the time) is significant as it establishes a cultural and religious connection with his audience. It demonstrates his identity as a Jew and his respect for Jewish traditions. This choice of language would have resonated deeply with the Jewish crowd, as it was the language of their scriptures and worship.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul
A key figure in the early Christian church, formerly known as Saul of Tarsus. He was a Pharisee and a Roman citizen who converted to Christianity after an encounter with Jesus on the road to Damascus. In this passage, Paul is about to address a hostile crowd in Jerusalem.

2. The Crowd
The people gathered in Jerusalem, many of whom were Jews who had become agitated due to misunderstandings about Paul's teachings and actions.

3. The Steps
Likely the steps of the Antonia Fortress, a military barracks in Jerusalem where Roman soldiers were stationed. This location provided Paul a platform to address the crowd.

4. The Hebrew Language
Paul chose to speak in Hebrew (or Aramaic, the common language of the Jews at the time) to connect with the Jewish audience, showing respect and understanding of their culture and heritage.

5. Jerusalem
The city where this event takes place, significant as the center of Jewish worship and the location of the Temple.
Teaching Points
Cultural Sensitivity in Evangelism
Paul’s choice to speak in Hebrew demonstrates the importance of understanding and respecting the cultural and linguistic context of those we are trying to reach with the Gospel.

Courage in the Face of Opposition
Despite the hostility of the crowd, Paul stands firm in his mission. This teaches us to be bold and steadfast in sharing our faith, even when facing adversity.

The Power of Personal Testimony
Paul uses his personal account to connect with his audience. Our testimonies can be powerful tools in witnessing to others about the transformative power of Christ.

Seeking Peaceful Engagement
By motioning for silence and addressing the crowd calmly, Paul exemplifies the importance of seeking peaceful and respectful dialogue, even in tense situations.

God’s Sovereignty in Our Circumstances
Paul’s ability to speak to the crowd, despite the chaos, shows how God can provide opportunities for His message to be shared, even in challenging circumstances.

What Does Acts 21:40 Mean
Having received permission

- “Having received permission” shows Paul’s respect for the chain of command.

• Earlier, he had courteously asked the Roman commander for a hearing (Acts 21:37–39).

• Paul consistently honors governing authorities while keeping his ultimate allegiance to Christ (Acts 24:10; Romans 13:1).

• The Lord often opens doors for witness through orderly submission (1 Peter 2:13–17).

Paul stood on the steps

- Those steps led up to the Antonia Fortress adjoining the temple (Acts 21:35).

• From that elevated spot Paul could be seen and heard by thousands, much like Peter on Solomon’s Colonnade in Acts 3:11.

• His physical stance pictures spiritual courage; the Spirit had already told him he would testify in Jerusalem (Acts 20:22–24).

and motioned to the crowd

- A simple hand motion quieted a volatile mob (compare Acts 12:17; Acts 13:16; Acts 19:33).

• Paul acts with calm authority, trusting God to turn chaos into opportunity (Proverbs 16:7).

• The gesture underscores that the gospel message deserves attentive hearing.

A great hush came over the crowd

- God answers Paul’s faith with immediate silence, echoing moments like Jesus calming the storm (Mark 4:39) and the crowd’s hush in the synagogue (Luke 4:20).

• The Lord can still hearts so His Word is heard (Psalm 46:10).

• The sudden quiet anticipates the serious testimony to follow (Acts 22:2).

and he addressed them in Hebrew

- Paul chooses the language of the people’s heart, showing pastoral sensitivity (Acts 22:1–2).

• Speaking in Hebrew (or Aramaic, the common dialect) affirms his shared heritage (Philippians 3:5).

• This choice builds a bridge, just as he later uses Greek before Gentiles (Acts 26:1–2).

• It fulfills Christ’s call to be “all things to all people” to win some (1 Corinthians 9:20–22).

summary

Acts 21:40 captures a Spirit-orchestrated moment: Paul, respectful of authority yet bold for Christ, takes a strategic position, gestures for order, watches God still an enraged crowd, and speaks in their own tongue. Every detail highlights God’s sovereignty and Paul’s readiness, reminding believers that the Lord provides both the platform and the power for faithful witness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(40) Paul stood on the stairs.--The position was one which raised him above the people, and the characteristic gesture commanded instant attention. And he spoke, not as they expected in the Greek, which belonged to one who fraternised with Gentiles, but in the Hebrew or Aramaic, which he had studied at the feet of Gamaliel. It was a strange scene for that Feast of Pentecost. The face and form of the speaker may have been seen from time to time by some during his passing visits to Jerusalem, but there must have been many who had not heard him take any part in public action since the day when, twenty-five years before, he had kept the garments of those who were stoning Stephen. And now he was there, accused of the self-same crimes, making his defence before a crowd as wild and frenzied as that of which he had then been the leader.
