Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
They called out
In the context of Acts 10, "they" refers to the men sent by Cornelius, a Roman centurion. This action of calling out indicates a sense of urgency and respect, as they were seeking someone of importance. In the ancient world, calling out was a common way to announce one's presence, especially when approaching a household. This reflects the cultural norms of communication and hospitality in the first-century Mediterranean world.

to ask if Simon called Peter
Simon Peter, one of Jesus' closest disciples, is often referred to by both his given name, Simon, and the name Jesus gave him, Peter, which means "rock" (Matthew 16:18). This dual naming highlights his transformation and role in the early church. The use of both names here emphasizes his identity and authority as an apostle. Peter's presence in Joppa, a significant port city, underscores the spread of the Gospel beyond Jerusalem, aligning with Jesus' command in Acts 1:8 to be witnesses "to the ends of the earth."

was staying there
Peter was staying at the house of Simon the tanner in Joppa, as mentioned earlier in Acts 9:43. Tanning was considered an unclean profession by Jewish standards due to the handling of dead animals, which shows Peter's growing openness to associating with those outside traditional Jewish purity laws. This setting foreshadows the upcoming revelation of the inclusion of Gentiles into the Christian faith, a pivotal moment in the early church's history. The geographical location of Joppa, a coastal city, also symbolizes the outreach of the Gospel to the Gentile world, as it was a gateway for trade and cultural exchange.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Simon Peter
One of Jesus' apostles, known for his leadership in the early church. He is staying at Simon the Tanner's house in Joppa.

2. Cornelius' Messengers
Sent by Cornelius, a Roman centurion, to find Peter. Cornelius is a God-fearing Gentile who receives a vision from God instructing him to send for Peter.

3. Simon the Tanner's House
The location in Joppa where Peter is staying. Tanners were often considered unclean due to their work with dead animals, highlighting Peter's openness to breaking Jewish purity laws.

4. Joppa
A port city in ancient Israel, significant as a place of transition and divine encounters, as seen in the account of Jonah.

5. Cornelius
A centurion in the Roman army, described as devout and God-fearing. His vision and subsequent actions set the stage for the inclusion of Gentiles in the early Christian church.
Teaching Points
Divine Guidance
God orchestrates events to fulfill His purposes. Cornelius' vision and the messengers' arrival demonstrate God's active role in guiding His people.

Breaking Barriers
Peter's willingness to stay with a tanner and engage with Gentiles shows the breaking of traditional Jewish barriers, emphasizing the inclusivity of the Gospel.

Obedience to God's Call
Both Cornelius and Peter respond obediently to God's instructions, highlighting the importance of listening to and acting upon divine guidance.

Unity in Diversity
The early church's expansion to include Gentiles underscores the unity and diversity within the body of Christ, a principle that remains relevant today.

Hospitality and Openness
Peter's stay at Simon the Tanner's house and his openness to Cornelius' messengers reflect the Christian call to hospitality and openness to God's work in unexpected ways.

What Does Acts 10:18 Mean
They called out

“Having called out, they asked...” (Acts 10:18)

• Cornelius’ messengers arrive at Joppa and raise their voices outside Simon the tanner’s house, signaling urgency and respect (Acts 10:17).

• Publicly calling from the street honored household boundaries common in that culture, a gentle approach that mirrors other respectful appeals (Luke 18:38; John 1:38).

• Their actions show how God orchestrates human steps to fulfill His plans, just as He earlier directed Philip toward the Ethiopian (Acts 8:29–30).

to ask

• The purpose of their call is inquiry, not intrusion—illustrating that divine appointments often begin with simple questions (Matthew 7:7; James 1:5).

• Asking affirms dependency on God’s revealed guidance: an angel sent them (Acts 10:3–6), but they still need confirmation at each stage (Proverbs 3:5–6).

• God welcomes earnest seekers; this request prepares Peter for the vision’s outworking in the next verses (Acts 10:19–20).

if Simon, called Peter

• Using both names connects the apostle’s old identity with his God-given mission (John 1:42).

• Repetition of the full designation (Acts 10:5, 32) underscores divine precision; the messengers cannot mistake another Simon in the same house (Acts 10:6, 17).

• God singles people out for specific tasks—here, Peter will unlock the gospel to Gentiles, fulfilling Jesus’ earlier words about the “keys of the kingdom” (Matthew 16:19; Acts 15:7).

was staying there

• The question confirms location: Peter is lodging “with Simon the tanner” (Acts 9:43), already pushing Jewish purity boundaries by staying in a tanner’s home (Leviticus 11:39–40).

• God places His servant where the next step of revelation becomes unavoidable; being “there” positions Peter for the Spirit’s directive (Acts 10:19–23).

• Divine timing meets human hospitality, echoing other moments when the Lord arranged critical stays—Jesus at Zacchaeus’ house (Luke 19:5) or Paul with Lydia (Acts 16:15).

summary

Acts 10:18 captures a divinely arranged intersection: Cornelius’ men respectfully call from the street, seeking the precise man God has prepared. Their inquiry illustrates obedient faith, while Peter’s presence in an unconventional setting signals God’s widening embrace of the nations. The verse, though brief, sets the stage for the outpouring of salvation to Gentiles and reminds us that God’s sovereign details—names, addresses, and the humble act of asking—advance His redemptive plan.
