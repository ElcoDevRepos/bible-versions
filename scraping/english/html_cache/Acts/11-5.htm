Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I was in the city of Joppa praying
Joppa, known today as Jaffa, is an ancient port city on the Mediterranean coast. It is historically significant as the place where Jonah attempted to flee from God's command (Jonah 1:3). In the New Testament, Joppa is where Peter raised Tabitha (Dorcas) from the dead (Acts 9:36-42). The act of praying indicates Peter's devotion and openness to divine guidance, a common practice among early Christians seeking God's will.

and in a trance I saw a vision
The trance state suggests a supernatural experience, often used by God to communicate important messages (Acts 10:10). Visions are a recurring method of divine revelation in Scripture, seen in the experiences of prophets like Daniel (Daniel 7:1) and apostles like Paul (Acts 16:9). This indicates the importance of the message Peter is about to receive.

of something like a large sheet being let down from heaven by its four corners
The sheet symbolizes the universality of God's message, with the four corners possibly representing the four corners of the earth, indicating the global scope of the Gospel. The descent from heaven signifies divine origin and authority. This imagery challenges Jewish dietary laws and foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles into the faith, aligning with prophecies of a universal kingdom (Isaiah 49:6).

and it came right down to me
The personal nature of the vision emphasizes Peter's role in the unfolding plan of God. It signifies a direct and personal revelation, underscoring Peter's leadership in the early church and his pivotal role in the acceptance of Gentiles. This moment is a turning point, leading to the baptism of Cornelius and his household (Acts 10:44-48), fulfilling Jesus' command to make disciples of all nations (Matthew 28:19).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Peter
The apostle who is recounting his vision to the believers in Jerusalem. He is a central figure in the early church and a key leader among the apostles.

2. Joppa
A coastal city in ancient Israel, known today as Jaffa, part of modern-day Tel Aviv. It is significant as the place where Peter received his vision.

3. Vision of the Sheet
A divine revelation given to Peter, symbolizing the inclusion of Gentiles into the Christian faith. The sheet contained various animals, representing the breaking down of Jewish dietary laws and the acceptance of all people.

4. Heaven
The origin of the vision, indicating divine authority and the will of God being revealed to Peter.

5. Trance
A state of spiritual awareness or ecstasy in which Peter received the vision, highlighting the supernatural nature of the revelation.
Teaching Points
Divine Revelation
God communicates His will through visions and spiritual experiences. We should be open to receiving guidance from God in various forms.

Breaking Barriers
The vision signifies the breaking down of cultural and religious barriers. As Christians, we are called to embrace diversity and unity in the body of Christ.

Prayer and Openness
Peter was in prayer when he received the vision. Regular prayer cultivates a heart that is open to God's leading and new understandings.

Obedience to God’s Will
Peter’s willingness to act on the vision demonstrates the importance of obedience to God’s revelations, even when they challenge our preconceived notions.

Inclusivity in the Gospel
The vision underscores the universal nature of the Gospel. We are called to share the message of Christ with all people, regardless of their background.

What Does Acts 11:5 Mean
I was in the city of Joppa praying

Peter sets the scene in the coastal town where Jonah once fled (Jonah 1:3). Joppa had long been a gateway for God’s plans to reach the nations. While staying there, “Peter went up on the roof to pray” (Acts 10:9). Prayer positions the believer to receive divine guidance (Matthew 6:6; Philippians 4:6). This opening clause reminds us that God often speaks when His servants intentionally seek Him.

and in a trance

Acts records several moments when God suspends normal awareness to deliver revelation—Paul in the temple (Acts 22:17) or Abram’s deep sleep (Genesis 15:12). The word underscores that Peter’s experience was supernatural, not imagination. God can still interrupt routine to clarify His will (Numbers 12:6).

I saw a vision of something like a large sheet

“Vision” (Acts 10:11) signals symbolic communication with literal implications. The “large sheet” suggests completeness—ample enough to hold “all kinds of four-footed animals” (Acts 10:12). Visions in Scripture often convey God’s redemptive program—Ezekiel 1:1; Revelation 21:2. Here, the sheet anticipates the church’s worldwide mission.

being let down from heaven by its four corners

The origin “from heaven” confirms divine initiative (James 1:17). Four corners call to mind the four winds (Revelation 7:1) and the gathering of exiles “from the four corners of the earth” (Isaiah 11:12). The imagery points to God’s intent to reach every nation; no corner is excluded. What Peter deemed unclean God was declaring accepted through Christ (Acts 10:15; Ephesians 2:13-16).

and it came right down to me

God’s message was personal and direct. The sheet “came right down” so Peter could not miss it (Acts 10:14-16). Revelation becomes responsibility: Peter soon applies it by welcoming the Gentile messengers and preaching to Cornelius (Acts 10:34-48). As Acts 1:8 promised, the witness moves from Jerusalem outward, and Peter is God’s chosen instrument for this pivotal step.

summary

Acts 11:5 recounts how God broke through Peter’s prayer time in Joppa with a vivid, heaven-sent vision. Each detail—the setting of prayer, the trance, the immense sheet, its heavenly descent, and its nearness—underscores that the inclusion of Gentiles was God’s sovereign plan, not human innovation. The verse teaches that when believers earnestly seek the Lord, He can clearly reveal His purposes, expanding our horizons to match His heart for the whole world.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) It came even to me.--The variations in the narrative are few and of little importance. There is, perhaps, a touch of the vividness of personal recollection in the description of the sheet as coming "even to me," as compared with its being let down "to the earth" in Acts 10:11.
