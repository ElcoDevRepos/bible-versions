Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Agrippa said to Paul,
King Agrippa II, the last of the Herodian dynasty, was well-versed in Jewish customs and controversies, making him a significant figure in this narrative. His presence highlights the political and religious tensions of the time. Agrippa's lineage connects him to Herod the Great, who sought to kill Jesus as a child, and Herod Antipas, who played a role in the trial of Jesus. This connection underscores the ongoing conflict between the early Christian movement and the ruling authorities.

“You have permission to speak for yourself.”
This phrase indicates a formal setting, likely a Roman judicial proceeding, where Paul is given the opportunity to present his case. The Roman legal system allowed the accused to defend themselves, reflecting the procedural fairness of Roman law. This moment is significant as it provides Paul a platform to share his testimony and the gospel message with both Jewish and Gentile audiences, fulfilling his mission as an apostle to the Gentiles.

Then Paul stretched out his hand
The gesture of stretching out one's hand was a common rhetorical practice in ancient oratory, used to capture attention and signal the beginning of a formal speech. This act demonstrates Paul's education and familiarity with Greco-Roman rhetorical techniques, which he uses to effectively communicate his message. It also symbolizes his readiness and confidence in presenting his defense and the gospel.

and began his defense:
Paul's defense is not merely a legal argument but a testimony of his conversion and mission. This defense serves as a fulfillment of Jesus' prophecy that Paul would testify before kings (Acts 9:15). It also parallels the trials of Jesus, who stood before rulers and authorities, highlighting Paul's role as a type of Christ in suffering and witness. Through his defense, Paul seeks to persuade his audience of the truth of the resurrection and the fulfillment of the Scriptures in Jesus Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Agrippa
King Herod Agrippa II, the ruler of the region, who was well-versed in Jewish customs and controversies. He was the great-grandson of Herod the Great.

2. Paul
The Apostle Paul, formerly known as Saul of Tarsus, who was a Pharisee and a zealous persecutor of Christians before his conversion. He is now defending his faith and mission before Agrippa.

3. Defense
This event is part of Paul's legal defense against accusations made by Jewish leaders. It is a significant moment where Paul shares his testimony and the gospel message.

4. Caesarea
The location where this hearing takes place. It was a major Roman city and the administrative center of the province of Judea.

5. Roman Legal System
The context of this event is a legal proceeding under Roman law, highlighting the intersection of Roman authority and Jewish religious issues.
Teaching Points
Courage in Witnessing
Paul demonstrates courage in sharing his faith, even in the face of powerful authorities. Believers are encouraged to be bold in their witness for Christ.

Preparedness to Defend the Faith
Like Paul, Christians should be prepared to articulate their faith and the reasons for their hope in Christ, as instructed in 1 Peter 3:15.

Respectful Engagement
Paul shows respect to Agrippa by acknowledging his authority and knowledge. Believers should engage others respectfully, even when they disagree.

God's Sovereignty in Trials
Paul's situation reminds us that God can use difficult circumstances for His purposes, including the spread of the gospel.

The Power of Personal Testimony
Paul's defense includes his personal testimony, illustrating the power of sharing one's personal encounter with Christ as a tool for evangelism.

What Does Acts 26:1 Mean
Agrippa said to Paul

• Festus introduces Paul to King Agrippa II (Acts 25:23–27), but it is Agrippa who now speaks directly, signaling that authority has shifted to the king.

• Scripture has already shown that kings will hear the gospel through Paul (Acts 9:15; 23:11). This moment fulfills that promise in literal detail.

• Paul’s respectful silence until spoken to reflects Romans 13:1—recognition of God-ordained government—while readying him to testify just as Jesus foretold (Luke 21:12–13).

You have permission to speak for yourself

• The king’s invitation legally opens the floor, yet spiritually it opens a door for the gospel (1 Corinthians 16:9).

• Paul is not called to self-promotion but to Christ-exaltation. He will answer “for himself,” yet every word will point to the risen Lord (Acts 26:22–23).

• Similar permissions in Acts 24:10 and Acts 25:16 show God repeatedly providing lawful avenues for witness, reminding us of 1 Peter 3:15—“always be ready to give a defense.”

Then Paul stretched out his hand

• A common orator’s gesture (Acts 12:17; 13:16) signals confidence and invites attention; Paul is poised, not panicked.

• The motion also mirrors God’s frequent use of an “outstretched hand” for deliverance (Exodus 6:6), hinting that Paul’s testimony will extend salvation to his hearers.

• His calm demeanor fulfills Jesus’ promise that the Spirit will supply words and composure before rulers (Matthew 10:18–20).

And began his defense

• “Defense” (apologia) echoes earlier speeches (Acts 22:1; Philippians 1:7). Paul will recount his conversion and commission, proving the prophets and Moses spoke of Christ (Acts 26:27).

• His strategy:

– Start with shared Jewish heritage (v. 4–8).

– Admit past persecution (v. 9–11).

– Describe the Damascus encounter (v. 12–18).

– Proclaim resurrection and repentance (v. 19–23).

• Through it all, he models 2 Timothy 4:2—“preach the word… in season and out of season.”

summary

Acts 26:1 records a divinely arranged courtroom scene. Agrippa’s address, the formal permission, Paul’s poised gesture, and his ensuing defense all illustrate God’s sovereign plan to place His servant before kings, fulfilling Scripture and advancing the gospel with clarity, courage, and literal precision.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XXVI.

(1) Then Paul stretched forth the hand.--The characteristic attitude reminds us of Acts 21:40. Here it acquires a fresh pictorial vividness from the fact that St. Paul now stood before the court as a prisoner, with one arm, probably the left, chained to the soldier who kept guard over him. (Comp. Acts 26:29.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - And for then, A.V.; his for the, A.V.; made his defense for answered for himself, A.V. Agrippa said. It was by the courtesy of Festus that Agrippa thus took the chief place. It was, perhaps, with the like courtesy that Agrippa said, impersonally, Thou art permitted, without specifying whether by himself or by Festus. Stretched forth his hand. The action of an orator, rendered in this case still more impressive by the chains which hung upon his arms. Luke here relates what he saw. Made his defense (ἀπελογεῖτο); Acts 25:8; Acts 24:10, note.
