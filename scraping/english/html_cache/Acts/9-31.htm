Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the church throughout Judea, Galilee, and Samaria experienced a time of peace.
This phrase indicates a period of respite for the early Christian church following the intense persecution led by Saul, who had recently converted to Christianity (Acts 9:1-30). Judea, Galilee, and Samaria were regions in ancient Israel, each with its own cultural and historical significance. Judea was the heartland of Jewish religious life, Galilee was known for its diverse population and was the region where Jesus conducted much of His ministry, and Samaria was a region with a mixed population of Jews and Samaritans, often at odds with each other. The peace experienced by the church may have been due to Saul's conversion, which removed a significant threat, and possibly due to political changes in the Roman Empire. This peace allowed the church to focus on growth and internal strengthening.

It grew in strength and numbers,
The growth in strength and numbers suggests both spiritual maturity and an increase in the number of believers. This growth can be attributed to the apostles' teaching, the work of the Holy Spirit, and the church's commitment to living out the teachings of Jesus. The early church's communal life, characterized by prayer, breaking of bread, and fellowship (Acts 2:42-47), would have contributed to this strengthening. The numerical growth also fulfills Jesus' command to make disciples of all nations (Matthew 28:19-20), showing the church's obedience to the Great Commission.

living in the fear of the Lord
The fear of the Lord refers to a deep reverence and respect for God, acknowledging His holiness and authority. This fear is not about being afraid but about honoring God and aligning one's life with His will. Proverbs 9:10 states that "the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom," indicating that this reverence leads to wise living. The early church's commitment to living in the fear of the Lord would have set them apart from the surrounding culture and drawn others to the faith.

and the encouragement of the Holy Spirit.
The Holy Spirit, promised by Jesus as the Comforter and Helper (John 14:16-17), played a crucial role in guiding and empowering the early church. The encouragement of the Holy Spirit would have provided believers with the strength to endure trials, the wisdom to navigate challenges, and the boldness to proclaim the gospel. This divine encouragement is a fulfillment of Jesus' promise that the Spirit would be with His followers, teaching them and reminding them of His words (John 14:26). The Holy Spirit's work in the church is a testament to God's ongoing presence and activity among His people.

What Does Acts 9:31 Mean
Then the church throughout Judea, Galilee, and Samaria

The single word “Then” links this moment to Saul’s dramatic conversion (Acts 9:1-30). With the main persecutor removed, Luke zooms out to show the gospel’s spread across the very regions Jesus named in Acts 1:8.

• One church, many locations—Luke speaks of “the church” in the singular, underscoring spiritual unity (Ephesians 4:4-6).

• Judea, Galilee, Samaria—places once divided by history now knit together in Christ, exactly as foretold (John 10:16).

• Earlier persecution (Acts 8:1) had scattered believers into these areas; now those scattered seeds are sprouting.

experienced a time of peace

After years of hostility, God grants a breathing space.

• Political calm—Saul’s absence and shifting Roman priorities relieve pressure on believers, illustrating Proverbs 16:7.

• Spiritual calm—Christ’s promise stands: “Peace I leave with you; My peace I give to you” (John 14:27).

• Purpose of peace—so the church can deepen, not doze. Paul later urges prayer “that we may live peaceful and quiet lives in all godliness” (1 Timothy 2:2).

and was strengthened

Peace is not an end but a platform for growth.

• Teachings of the apostles (Acts 2:42) reinforce doctrine.

• Mutual encouragement (Hebrews 10:24-25) builds resilience.

• Divine empowerment—“that He may strengthen you with power through His Spirit” (Ephesians 3:16).

living in the fear of the Lord

A healthy awe of God shapes every decision.

• Reverence motivates obedience (Proverbs 9:10).

• Fear guards against hypocrisy—after Ananias and Sapphira, “great fear came over the whole church” (Acts 5:11).

• Walking, not sprinting—the phrase pictures an ongoing lifestyle of holy caution (2 Corinthians 7:1).

and the encouragement of the Holy Spirit

The same Spirit who convicts also comforts.

• Jesus calls Him “another Advocate” (John 14:16).

• He fills believers with joy (Acts 13:52) and hope (Romans 15:13).

• His inner witness assures the church that it is on the right path even when external applause is absent.

it multiplied

Strength plus holiness plus Spirit-given comfort produces outward expansion.

• “The Lord added to their number daily” (Acts 2:47); growth is God’s work.

• The Word keeps spreading—“the number of disciples in Jerusalem multiplied greatly” (Acts 6:7).

• Every new disciple becomes another witness, so multiplication accelerates (Acts 12:24).

summary

Acts 9:31 captures a sweet season when the unified church, relieved from persecution, deepened in character and doctrine, walked in reverent obedience, enjoyed the Spirit’s consoling presence, and consequently grew numerically. Peace was not merely the absence of trouble; it was the God-given environment in which truth took root, hearts were fortified, and the gospel advanced with unstoppable momentum.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) Then had the churches rest.--The better MSS. have "the Church" in the singular. The tranquility described may have been due, partly to the absence of any leading men among the opponents of the new society; partly, perhaps, to public excitement being diverted to the insane attempt of Caligula to set up his statue in the Temple at Jerusalem--an attempt from which he was only dissuaded by the earnest entreaties of Herod Agrippa, whom he had raised to the dignity of King of Judaea, but who happened at the time to be at Rome, and of Petronius, the Pr?ses of Syria. The latter was influenced by great showers of rain falling from a clear sky, after a long drought, in answer to the prayers of Israel (Jos. Ant. xviii. 8, ? 6). Such prayers, made at a crisis in which believing and unbelieving Jews felt an equal interest, may, probably, have suggested St. James's allusion to the old historical parallel of Elijah (James 5:17).

Throughout all Judaea and Galilee and Samaria.--Brief as the notice is, it is every way significant. It is the first intimation since the opening of the apostolic history of the existence, not of disciples only, such as had gathered round our Lord during His personal ministry, but of organised religious communities, in the towns and villages of Galilee. We may think of such churches as formed in Capernaum and Tiberias, in Chorazin and the two Bethsaidas, perhaps even in Nazareth. The history is silent as to the agency by which these churches had been founded; but looking to the close relations between St. Luke and St. Philip, and to the probability that the latter made Caesarea his head-quarters for the work of an Evangelist, we may legitimately think of him as having worked there as he had worked in Samaria. It is not improbable, however, that here also, as in that region, he may have been followed, after he had done his work as an Evangelist, by the Apostles to whom it belonged to confirm and organise. (See Note on Acts 8:14.) The mention of Samaria in like manner indicates the extent and permanence of the result of Philip's work there, followed up as it had been by the preaching of Peter and John.

Were edified; and walking. . . .--The more accurate construction of the sentence gives, The Church . . . . had peace, being edified and walking in the fear of the Lord, and was multiplied by the counsel of the Holy Ghost. The passage is noticeable for the appearance of the word "edified," or "built up," in the sense in which St. Paul had used it (1Corinthians 8:1; 1Corinthians 14:4), as describing orderly and continuous growth, the superstructure raised wisely upon the right foundation,

Walking in the fear of the Lord.--The phrase, so common in the Old Testament, is comparatively rare in the New, being used only by St. Luke here, and in 2Corinthians 5:11, where it is wrongly translated "the terror of the Lord." What it describes, as interpreted by its Old Testament use (Job 28:28; Psalm 111:10; Proverbs 1:7, et al.), is the temper of reverential awe; the scrupulous obedience to the commandments of God, which had been described of old as "the beginning" of wisdom. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - So the Church... had peace, being edified for then had the Churches rest,... and were edified, A.V. and T.R.; was multiplied for were multiplied, A.V. and T.R. It is thought that the attention of the Jews to the progress of the faith of Jesus Christ was diverted at this time, and their active hostility stayed, by the still greater danger to the Jews' religion which arose from Caligula's intention of placing a statue to himself as a god in the holy of holies. Thus did God's gracious providence intervene to give rest to his harassed saints, and to build up his Church in numbers, in holiness, and in heavenly comfort. Especially Paul had another breathing-time, which may have been the more required if, as is thought, one at least of the five scourgings mentioned in 2 Corinthians 12:24 had been inflicted at Damascus, and one of the three shipwrecks alluded to in the same passage and been undergone in the dangerous coasting voyage from Caesarca to Scleucia.
