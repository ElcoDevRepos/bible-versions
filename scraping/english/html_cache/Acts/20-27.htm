For I did not shrink back
Paul is speaking to the Ephesian elders, emphasizing his boldness and commitment in ministry. The phrase "did not shrink back" suggests courage and perseverance, reflecting Paul's dedication despite opposition and hardship. This echoes his earlier experiences, such as in Acts 14:19-20, where he was stoned and left for dead but continued his mission. It also aligns with the exhortation in Hebrews 10:39, encouraging believers not to shrink back in faith.

from declaring to you
Paul's role as an apostle was to proclaim the message of the Gospel. The term "declaring" indicates a public and authoritative announcement, akin to a herald. This reflects the apostolic duty to communicate God's message clearly and without reservation, as seen in 1 Corinthians 9:16, where Paul states, "Woe to me if I do not preach the gospel!"

the whole will of God
This phrase underscores the comprehensive nature of Paul's teaching. He did not selectively present parts of God's message but delivered the full counsel, including both comforting and challenging truths. This aligns with the biblical mandate to teach "the whole counsel of God" (Acts 20:20), ensuring that believers are fully equipped. The "will of God" encompasses His redemptive plan through Jesus Christ, moral directives, and eschatological promises, as seen throughout Scripture, including Romans 12:2 and Ephesians 1:9-10.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The speaker of this verse, Paul is addressing the Ephesian elders. He is a central figure in the New Testament, known for his missionary journeys and epistles.

2. Ephesian Elders
Leaders of the church in Ephesus, whom Paul is addressing in this farewell speech. Ephesus was a major city in Asia Minor, known for its significant Christian community.

3. Miletus
The location where Paul delivers this speech. It is a coastal city near Ephesus, where Paul called the Ephesian elders to meet him.

4. The Whole Will of God
Refers to the complete message of God's plan and purpose, which Paul has faithfully communicated.

5. Paul's Missionary Journey
This event is part of Paul's third missionary journey, during which he revisits churches to strengthen and encourage them.
Teaching Points
Faithful Proclamation
As believers, we are called to faithfully proclaim the entirety of God's message, not just the parts that are comfortable or popular.

Courage in Ministry
Like Paul, we should not shrink back from sharing the Gospel, even when it is challenging or met with opposition.

Comprehensive Understanding
It is important to seek a comprehensive understanding of God's will through diligent study of Scripture and prayer.

Responsibility of Leaders
Church leaders have a responsibility to teach the full counsel of God, ensuring that their congregations are well-equipped in their faith.

Personal Accountability
Each believer should examine their own life to ensure they are living in accordance with the whole will of God, not selectively following His commands.

What Does Acts 20:27 Mean
For

- This opening word reaches back to the previous verse, where Paul says, “Therefore I testify to you this day that I am innocent of the blood of all men” (Acts 20:26).

- “For” shows causation: Paul’s innocence before God and people rests on what he is about to affirm. Compare Ezekiel 33:7-9, where the watchman avoids guilt by faithfully warning others.

- It reminds us that obedience in teaching is not optional—our accountability to God derives from His command, as seen again in James 3:1.

I did not shrink back

- Paul refused to dodge, dilute, or soften the message. Acts 20:20 echoes this: “I did not shrink back from proclaiming anything that was profitable.”

- Courage in ministry is a hallmark of fidelity. Consider 2 Timothy 4:2: “Preach the word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke, and encourage…”

- Shrinking back is contrasted with the boldness described in Romans 1:16, “I am not ashamed of the gospel.”

- Faithful shepherds must embrace every topic Scripture raises, even those that confront cultural norms (Galatians 1:10).

from declaring

- “Declaring” is open, public, and verbal. Paul’s ministry was transparent (2 Corinthians 4:2).

- Declaration involves teaching, warning, urging, and comforting (Colossians 1:28).

- The verb underscores that truth must be spoken—not merely implied or lived out silently (Romans 10:14-17).

to you

- Paul’s audience was personal: the Ephesian elders he had discipled. Ministry is relational, not detached.

- It shows specificity—truth addressed to real people with real needs (1 Thessalonians 2:8).

- Shepherds feed the flock God has entrusted to them, just as Jesus instructed Peter in John 21:15-17.

the whole will of God

- “Whole” underscores completeness: nothing omitted, nothing added. Psalm 119:160 affirms, “The entirety of Your word is truth.”

- “Will of God” encompasses the redemptive plan (Acts 2:23), sound doctrine (1 Timothy 4:16), moral commands (Ephesians 5:17-18), warnings of judgment (Acts 17:30-31), and promises of hope (Titus 2:11-14).

- Partial teaching breeds shallow disciples. Full counsel matures believers (Ephesians 4:11-15).

- Paul modeled this by proclaiming repentance and faith (Acts 20:21), teaching on the kingdom (Acts 20:25), and urging holiness (Acts 20:32).

- Ultimately, the whole will of God centers on Christ crucified and risen (1 Corinthians 15:3-4) and leads to obedience (Matthew 28:20).

summary

Paul connects his clear conscience to an unswerving commitment: he boldly, verbally, and relationally delivered every facet of God’s revealed plan to those under his care. By refusing to omit or soften any truth—from sin and repentance to grace and glory—he exemplifies the calling of every believer who handles God’s Word. Acts 20:27 invites us to the same faithful, full-orbed proclamation that honors Christ and nurtures His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) I have not shunned to declare unto you all the counsel of God.--The words point to a greater degree of receptivity for Divine truth than had been found elsewhere. So in the Epistle to the Ephesians, which, even on the assumption that it was an encyclical letter, was addressed to them principally, he speaks to them as able to understand his knowledge in the mystery of Christ (Ephesians 3:4), the universality of His redeeming work, the brotherhood of mankind in the common Fatherhood of God. In "I have not shunned" we have the same word and image as in the "kept back" of Acts 20:20.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - Shrank not from declaring for have not shunned to declare, A.V. (see ver. 20, note); the whole for all the, A.V. Counsel of God. His revealed will and purpose concerning man's salvation (Acts 2:23; Acts 4:28; Ephesians 1:11).
