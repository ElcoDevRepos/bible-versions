Then the Lord said to him
This phrase refers to God speaking directly to Moses. It highlights the personal nature of God's communication with His chosen leaders. In the context of Acts 7, Stephen is recounting the history of Israel to the Sanhedrin, emphasizing God's direct involvement in guiding His people. This divine encounter is a pivotal moment in Moses' life, marking the beginning of his mission to lead the Israelites out of Egypt. It parallels other instances in Scripture where God speaks directly to individuals, such as His conversations with Abraham (Genesis 12:1) and Samuel (1 Samuel 3:10).

‘Take off your sandals
Removing sandals is a sign of reverence and respect in the presence of the divine. In ancient Near Eastern culture, it was customary to remove shoes when entering a sacred space, acknowledging the holiness of the ground. This act signifies humility and submission before God. The removal of sandals can also symbolize the shedding of earthly ties and impurities, preparing Moses to receive God's instructions. This practice is echoed in Joshua 5:15, where Joshua is also instructed to remove his sandals in the presence of the Lord.

for the place where you are standing is holy ground.
The concept of "holy ground" indicates a location set apart by God's presence. Although the physical location is the desert near Mount Horeb (also known as Mount Sinai), it becomes holy because of God's manifestation there. This underscores the idea that holiness is not inherent in the place itself but is conferred by God's presence. The burning bush encounter (Exodus 3:5) is a theophany, a visible manifestation of God, which transforms the ordinary into the sacred. This theme of holiness is central to the biblical narrative, as seen in the construction of the Tabernacle and later the Temple, where God's presence dwells among His people. The holiness of God is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, calling believers to live in reverence and obedience.

Persons / Places / Events

The speaker in Acts 7, delivering a powerful sermon before the Sanhedrin, recounting Israel's history and God's faithfulness.

2. Moses
The central figure in this verse, who encounters God in the burning bush on Mount Horeb, a pivotal moment in his calling to lead the Israelites out of Egypt.

3. Mount Horeb
Also known as Mount Sinai, this is the location where Moses encounters God, and it is considered a holy place due to God's presence.

4. The Burning Bush
A miraculous event where God appears to Moses in a bush that is on fire but not consumed, symbolizing God's holy presence and calling.

5. The Lord (Yahweh)
The one speaking to Moses, emphasizing His holiness and the sanctity of the ground due to His presence.
Teaching Points
Reverence for God's Holiness
Recognize the holiness of God and approach Him with reverence and humility, acknowledging His presence in our lives.

God's Presence Transforms Ordinary Places
Understand that any place can become holy ground when God is present, encouraging us to seek His presence in our daily lives.

Responding to God's Call
Like Moses, be ready to respond to God's call, understanding that He equips those He calls, even in moments of doubt or inadequacy.

Symbolism of Removing Sandals
Removing sandals symbolizes the removal of worldly concerns and distractions, encouraging us to focus solely on God when in His presence.

God's Faithfulness in History
Reflect on how God has been faithful throughout history, as recounted by Stephen, and trust in His continued faithfulness in our lives.

What Does Acts 7:33 Mean
Then the Lord said to him

• Stephen recounts Moses’ encounter with God (Acts 7:30-34). The “Lord” is God Himself speaking from the burning bush, just as Exodus 3:4-6 relates.

• Scripture consistently shows God taking the initiative—Genesis 12:1 with Abram, Isaiah 6:8 with Isaiah—underscoring that revelation starts with Him, not human effort.

• The authority of the voice means Moses must listen and obey, echoing John 10:27: “My sheep listen to My voice; I know them, and they follow Me.”

Take off your sandals

• Removing sandals was a sign of respect and submission. Compare Joshua 5:15, where the commander of the Lord’s army gives the same command to Joshua.

• It also symbolizes laying aside worldly defilement (James 1:21) so nothing hinders fellowship with the Holy One.

• Practical takeaway: God still calls believers to tangible acts of reverence—humble posture (Ephesians 3:14), modesty of life (1 Peter 5:5-6).

For the place where you are standing

• God sanctifies ordinary ground when He manifests His presence. Jacob discovered this at Bethel: “Surely the LORD is in this place… This is the house of God” (Genesis 28:16-17).

• Wherever God chooses to reveal Himself—whether a desert, a church sanctuary, or a living room Bible study—becomes a meeting place between heaven and earth (Matthew 18:20).

Is holy ground

• “Holy” means set apart for God’s purposes (Leviticus 11:44). Holiness is not optional; Hebrews 12:14 urges believers to “pursue…holiness, without which no one will see the Lord.”

• God’s holiness demands reverent fear (Hebrews 12:28-29) balanced by warm access through Christ (Hebrews 4:16).

• Because Christians are indwelt by the Holy Spirit, our very bodies are now “a temple of the Holy Spirit” (1 Corinthians 6:19), calling for continual sanctification (1 Thessalonians 4:3-7).

summary

Acts 7:33 reminds us that God speaks authoritatively, expects humble response, transforms any place by His presence, and reveals His uncompromising holiness. Like Moses, we remove our “sandals” by shedding pride and impurity, approaching the Lord with reverent devotion and obedient hearts.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(33, 34) Then said the Lord to him . . . .--The words are almost a verbal reproduction of Exodus 3:5; Exodus 3:7-8. The citation was in part an implied answer to the charge of disregarding the sanctity of places in which man stands as in the presence of God, partly an implied protest against the narrowing thoughts which limited that sanctity to the Temple of Jerusalem.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 33. - And the Lord said unto him for then said the Lord to him, A.V.; loose the shoes for put off by shoes, A.V. Loose the shoes, etc. In Exodus 3:5 it is λύσαι... ἐκ τῶν ποδῶν σου. Iamblichus, quoted by Meyer, refers the Pythagorean precept, "Sacrifice and worship with thy shoes off," to an Egyptian custom. The custom of Orientals to take off their sandals on entering mosques or other sacred places, as existing to the present day, is noticed by many travelers (see also Joshua 5:15).
