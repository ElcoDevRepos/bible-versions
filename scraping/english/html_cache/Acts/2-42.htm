They devoted themselves to the apostles’ teaching
The early believers committed themselves wholeheartedly to the teachings of the apostles, who were the primary witnesses of Jesus Christ's life, death, and resurrection. This devotion indicates a continuous and steadfast adherence to the doctrine that the apostles imparted, which was rooted in the teachings of Jesus and the Hebrew Scriptures. The apostles' teaching formed the foundation of Christian doctrine and practice, emphasizing the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies in Christ. This dedication to learning reflects the Jewish tradition of studying the Torah, but now with the revelation of Christ as the fulfillment of the Law and the Prophets.

and to the fellowship
The term "fellowship" (Greek: koinonia) implies a deep, communal bond among believers, characterized by mutual support, sharing, and unity. This fellowship was not merely social but spiritual, reflecting the unity of the body of Christ. The early church's fellowship was a radical departure from the individualism of the surrounding Greco-Roman culture, emphasizing instead a community that shared resources and supported one another. This communal life is reminiscent of the communal aspects of Jewish life, such as the shared meals and festivals, but now centered around the shared faith in Christ.

to the breaking of bread
The breaking of bread likely refers to both the communal meals shared among believers and the observance of the Lord's Supper, or Communion. This practice was a tangible expression of their unity in Christ and a remembrance of His sacrifice. The breaking of bread is rooted in Jewish customs, such as the Passover meal, which Jesus reinterpreted at the Last Supper as a symbol of the new covenant in His blood. This act of breaking bread together was a proclamation of the Lord's death until He comes, as Paul later explains in 1 Corinthians 11:26.

and to prayer
Prayer was a central aspect of the early church's life, reflecting their dependence on God and their desire for His guidance and power. The early Christians continued the Jewish tradition of regular prayer, but with a new focus on the name of Jesus and the coming of the Holy Spirit. Prayer was both a personal and communal activity, encompassing praise, thanksgiving, intercession, and supplication. The emphasis on prayer highlights the church's reliance on the Holy Spirit, who empowered them for witness and service, fulfilling Jesus' promise in Acts 1:8.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Apostles
The original disciples of Jesus Christ who were chosen to spread His teachings. They played a crucial role in the early church by teaching and guiding new believers.

2. Early Believers
The first converts to Christianity who formed the early church community in Jerusalem. They were characterized by their devotion and communal lifestyle.

3. Jerusalem
The city where the early church was established and where the events of Acts 2 took place, including the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost.

4. Pentecost
A significant event in Acts 2 where the Holy Spirit descended upon the apostles, empowering them to preach the gospel in various languages.

5. Fellowship
The communal life and shared experiences of the early believers, emphasizing unity and mutual support.
Teaching Points
Devotion to Teaching
The early church prioritized learning from the apostles, emphasizing the importance of sound doctrine and biblical teaching in our spiritual growth.

Importance of Fellowship
Fellowship is more than socializing; it involves sharing life, supporting one another, and growing together in faith.

Breaking of Bread
This practice symbolizes unity and remembrance of Christ's sacrifice, encouraging us to regularly participate in communion with reverence and gratitude.

Commitment to Prayer
Prayer was central to the early church's life, reminding us to cultivate a consistent and earnest prayer life, both individually and corporately.

What Does Acts 2:42 Mean
They devoted themselves

The Spirit-filled believers didn’t dabble; they persisted. Acts 1:14 shows the same heartbeat: “With one accord they all continued in prayer and supplication…”. Devotion means:

• Steady, daily attention rather than sporadic spurts.

• A conscious choice to prioritize the things of God above personal comfort (Colossians 4:2).

• Perseverance that bears witness to genuine faith (Hebrews 3:14).

Their example invites us to schedule, guard, and celebrate our own steady rhythms of obedience.

to the apostles’ teaching

The church’s first commitment was sitting under the Word delivered by Christ’s chosen messengers (Acts 2:43). Jesus had already commissioned them to “teach…all that I have commanded you” (Matthew 28:20). For us, that teaching is preserved in the New Testament and affirmed by the Old (2 Timothy 3:16).

• We read and hear Scripture expecting God to speak plainly and authoritatively.

• We shape doctrine, worldview, and daily choices by what the apostles wrote, not by shifting culture (Jude 3).

• We test every new idea against the settled testimony of the written Word (Acts 17:11).

and to the fellowship

“Fellowship” (koinonia) is more than cookies after church; it is shared life in Christ. John writes, “Our fellowship is with the Father and with His Son Jesus Christ” (1 John 1:3), and therefore with one another.

• Believers meet regularly, encouraging and stirring one another “to love and good deeds” (Hebrews 10:24-25).

• Material needs are met within the family (Acts 4:32-35).

• Unity proves to the watching world that Jesus is Lord (John 17:23).

Intentional hospitality, honest accountability, and practical service all flow from this bond.

to the breaking of bread

Verse 46 clarifies that the church “continued to meet daily…breaking bread from house to house”. The phrase includes ordinary meals and the Lord’s Supper. Jesus set the pattern: “This is My body given for you; do this in remembrance of Me” (Luke 22:19). Paul echoes it in 1 Corinthians 11:24-26.

• Regular communion anchors the gospel at the center—Christ’s body broken, His blood shed, His return anticipated.

• Shared tables erase social divisions and embody our equality in grace.

• Every meal can become an act of worship when offered with gratitude (1 Timothy 4:3-5).

and to prayer

Corporate and personal prayer powered everything else. After prayer, “the place where they were assembled was shaken, and they were all filled with the Holy Spirit” (Acts 4:31). Paul urges the same posture: “Be anxious for nothing, but in everything, by prayer and petition…present your requests to God” (Philippians 4:6).

• Prayer keeps the church God-dependent rather than program-dependent.

• It invites divine guidance, boldness, and miraculous intervention (James 5:16-18).

• Union with the Father knits believers together in one heart and mind.

summary

Acts 2:42 sketches the daily priorities of a Spirit-empowered church: persistent devotion, Scripture-anchored learning, Christ-centered community, gospel-saturated meals, and united prayer. These rhythms are not relics but God’s timeless blueprint for healthy, vibrant disciples who make Jesus unmistakably visible in every generation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(42) And they continued steadfastly.--The one Greek word is expressed by the English verb and adverb. As applied to persons, the New Testament use of the word is characteristic of St. Luke (Acts 2:46; Acts 6:4; Acts 8:13; Acts 10:7), and peculiar to him and St. Paul (Romans 12:12; Romans 13:6; Colossians 4:2). . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 42. - Teaching for doctrine, A.V.; in the breaking for and in breaking, A.V. and T.R.; the prayers for in prayer, A.V. And fellowship; better, as in the margin, in fellowship; not meaning the apostles' fellowship, but the fellowship of the Church - that common life of close brotherhood in which all that they did was done in common, and all that they possessed was possessed in common, so that there seemed to be but one heart and one mind amongst them all. Breaking of bread; in the Holy Eucharist (see Matthew 26:26; Mark 14:22; Luke 22:19; Luke 24:30; 1 Corinthians 11:24; 1 Corinthians 10:16; Acts 20:7). The prayers; the common prayers of the Church.
