Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and told him,
This phrase refers to God speaking to Abraham. It highlights the direct communication between God and the patriarchs, emphasizing the personal relationship God had with them. This divine instruction is foundational in the narrative of God's covenant with Abraham, which is a central theme in the Bible.

‘Leave your country
Abraham was originally from Ur of the Chaldeans, a prosperous city in ancient Mesopotamia. This command required Abraham to leave behind his familiar surroundings, which signifies a call to faith and obedience. It reflects the theme of separation from worldly ties to pursue God's promises, a recurring motif in Scripture.

and your kindred
This instruction to leave his family underscores the radical nature of Abraham's call. It required prioritizing God's command over familial bonds, illustrating the cost of discipleship. This mirrors Jesus' teaching in the New Testament about the necessity of placing God above family ties (Matthew 10:37).

and go to the land I will show you.’
The land refers to Canaan, which God promised to Abraham and his descendants. This promise is central to the Abrahamic Covenant, which is foundational for understanding the biblical narrative of Israel. The land is not specified initially, requiring Abraham to trust in God's guidance. This act of faith is later commended in Hebrews 11:8, where Abraham's obedience is highlighted as an example of faith in action.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Stephen
The speaker in Acts 7, Stephen is one of the first deacons of the early church and the first Christian martyr. He is addressing the Sanhedrin, recounting the history of Israel to make a point about their resistance to God's messengers.

2. Abraham
The patriarch to whom God originally spoke these words. Abraham's journey of faith began with this call to leave his homeland and trust in God's promise.

3. Mesopotamia
The region where Abraham was living when God called him. It represents the starting point of Abraham's journey of faith.

4. Canaan
The land God promised to show Abraham, symbolizing the fulfillment of God's promises and the future inheritance of Abraham's descendants.

5. Sanhedrin
The Jewish council before whom Stephen is speaking. They are the religious leaders of Israel, and Stephen is challenging their understanding of God's work and promises.
Teaching Points
Obedience to God's Call
Abraham's response to God's call is a model of obedience. We are called to trust and obey God's direction, even when the path is unclear.

Faith in God's Promises
Like Abraham, believers are called to have faith in God's promises, trusting that He will fulfill them in His timing and way.

Leaving Comfort Zones
God's call often requires us to leave our comfort zones, just as Abraham left his homeland. This can mean stepping out in faith in various aspects of our lives.

God's Sovereignty and Guidance
God's call to Abraham demonstrates His sovereignty and guidance. We can trust that God knows the best path for our lives and will lead us accordingly.

Witnessing Through Faith
Stephen uses Abraham's account to witness to the Sanhedrin. Our faith and obedience can be a powerful testimony to others about God's faithfulness.

What Does Acts 7:3 Mean
Leave your country

“Leave your country…” (Acts 7:3)

• God’s first word to Abram (Genesis 12:1) confronts security and familiarity.

• He calls us, like Abram, to step out of cultural comfort. “Come out from among them and be separate” (2 Corinthians 6:17).

• Hebrews 11:8 notes Abram “obeyed and went, even though he did not know where he was going,” underscoring that faith begins with a decisive break from the old life.

• Jesus echoes the same cost in Luke 14:33: “Any of you who does not give up everything he has cannot be My disciple.”

And your kindred

“…and your kindred…” (Acts 7:3)

• Family ties, treasured as they are, can bind us to unbelief. Jesus places allegiance to Him above relatives (Matthew 10:37).

• Abram took Lot, yet later separation became necessary (Genesis 13:8-11), illustrating that partial obedience still brings conflict.

• Luke 14:26 stresses the priority: love for Christ must dwarf every other attachment.

Go to the land I will show you

“…and go to the land I will show you.” (Acts 7:3)

• The promise is open-ended. God gives direction step by step, not an entire roadmap (Psalm 119:105).

• Joshua 24:2-3 recounts how God “led him throughout Canaan,” confirming the journey was guided at every stage.

• Faith rests on God’s character, not on visible certainty. Abram “was looking forward to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God” (Hebrews 11:10).

• Christ’s promise, “I go to prepare a place for you” (John 14:2-3), mirrors this pattern: leave, trust, and inherit.

summary

Acts 7:3 recalls God’s call to Abram as a timeless pattern: break from worldly security, surrender even family loyalties, and follow God into the unseen future. True faith obeys immediately, trusts completely, and receives abundantly.
