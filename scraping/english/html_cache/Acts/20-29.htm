Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I know that after my departure
Paul is addressing the Ephesian elders, aware that his time with them is coming to an end. This phrase reflects Paul's prophetic insight and pastoral concern. His departure signifies a transition, highlighting the vulnerability of the church without his direct leadership. This echoes Jesus' warnings to His disciples about future challenges (John 16:4-7).

savage wolves will come in among you
The imagery of "savage wolves" is a metaphor for false teachers or leaders who would infiltrate the church. Wolves are predators, symbolizing danger and destruction. This warning is consistent with Jesus' caution about false prophets in Matthew 7:15. Historically, the early church faced numerous heresies and false teachings, such as Gnosticism, which threatened doctrinal purity.

and will not spare the flock
The "flock" represents the church, a common biblical metaphor for God's people (Psalm 23, John 10:11-16). The phrase underscores the threat's severity, as these wolves intend to cause harm without mercy. This reflects the spiritual warfare the early church faced and the need for vigilance and strong leadership to protect the community of believers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The speaker of this verse, Paul is addressing the elders of the church in Ephesus. He is on his way to Jerusalem and is giving a farewell speech, warning them of future challenges.

2. Ephesus
A significant city in the Roman province of Asia, known for its large Christian community and the Temple of Artemis. Paul spent considerable time here, establishing and strengthening the church.

3. Ephesian Elders
The leaders of the church in Ephesus, responsible for shepherding the congregation. Paul is speaking directly to them, emphasizing their role in protecting the church.

4. Savage Wolves
A metaphor used by Paul to describe false teachers or leaders who would infiltrate the church, causing harm and leading believers astray.

5. The Flock
Represents the Christian community or congregation, whom the elders are tasked with protecting and nurturing.
Teaching Points
Vigilance in Leadership
Church leaders must be vigilant and discerning, always on guard against false teachings that can infiltrate the church.

The Importance of Sound Doctrine
Believers should be grounded in sound doctrine to recognize and resist false teachings.

Community Responsibility
While leaders have a primary role, all members of the church community share the responsibility to uphold truth and protect one another.

Spiritual Discernment
Cultivating spiritual discernment through prayer and study of the Scriptures is essential for identifying and countering false teachings.

Pastors and elders are called to be shepherds who protect and nurture the flock, emulating the Good Shepherd, Jesus Christ.

What Does Acts 20:29 Mean
I know

Paul speaks with certainty. This is not speculation but Spirit-given insight, echoing Jesus’ forewarnings (John 16:13). His confidence reminds us:

• God equips leaders to discern coming threats (Amos 3:7).

• Believers should not be surprised when warnings prove true (2 Timothy 3:1-5).

Cross reference: 2 Peter 3:17—“Therefore, beloved, since you already know these things, be on your guard…”

After my departure

Paul’s looming absence would remove a trusted shepherd. The verse teaches:

• No human leader is indispensable, yet their presence restrains error (Philippians 2:12).

• Transition times invite vulnerability (Deuteronomy 31:14-18).

Cross reference: 2 Timothy 4:6-8 shows Paul aware of finishing his race and leaving others to stand firm.

Savage wolves

The imagery is deliberate: wolves devour helpless sheep. Scripture consistently uses this picture for false teachers:

• “Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep’s clothing but inwardly are ravenous wolves” (Matthew 7:15).

• “There will also be false teachers among you” (2 Peter 2:1).

Key truths:

– False teachers are ferocious, not merely misguided.

– Their intent is harm, not honest disagreement.

Cross reference: Jude 4 describes ungodly people “who pervert the grace of our God.”

Will come in among you

Threats arise inside the fellowship, not just outside. Note:

• Proximity breeds influence; danger is greatest when trust is presumed (Galatians 2:4).

• Vigilance belongs to every local church (1 John 2:18-19).

Cross reference: 1 Timothy 4:1 warns, “Some will depart from the faith, paying attention to deceitful spirits.”

Will not spare the flock

The objective is total destruction of faith and unity. Practical implications:

• Sound doctrine protects life (Titus 1:9).

• Shepherds must confront error promptly (Acts 20:31).

• Every believer carries responsibility to test teaching (1 John 4:1).

Cross reference: Ezekiel 34:5-6 pictures scattered sheep because shepherds failed to guard them.

summary

Acts 20:29 is a solemn alert: once Paul leaves, aggressive false teachers will infiltrate the Ephesian church, aiming to ravage believers. Scripture urges constant watchfulness, faithful teaching, and courageous leadership so that the flock remains safe under the true Shepherd, Jesus Christ.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(29) After my departing shall grievous wolves enter in among you.--The figurative language followed naturally on the idea of the flock and of the shepherds who keep watch over it. It lies in the nature of the case that the wolf stands primarily for the open enemies of the flock, the persecutors of all ages. (Comp. John 10:12.) The wolves, however, might come in sheep's clothing (Matthew 7:15), and so the false prophets, the usurpers of authority, and leaders of parties within the Church, are also included in the term. Here this latter class is distinctly pointed out in the following verse. We find traces of the fulfilment of the prediction in the "turning away" of 2Timothy 1:15; the "fiery trial" of 1Peter 1:7; 1Peter 4:12; the suffering "as a Christian" of 1Peter 4:16. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - I know for, for I know this, A.V. and T.R.; grievous wolves shall for shall grievous wolves, A.V. After my departure (ἄφιξιν, not ἀνάλυσιν, as 2 Timothy 4:6). The word, which is only found here in the New Testament, usually means "arrival" in classical Greek, but it also means, as here, "departure." It is not to be taken in the sense of "departure from this life," but refers to that separation, which he thought was forever, which was about to take place. Grievous wolves; still keeping up the metaphor of the flock. The wolves denote the false teachers, principally Judaizers. See 2 Timothy 3:1-12, and 13, "But evil men and seducers shall wax worse and worse, deceiving, and being deceived." These came from Judaea.
