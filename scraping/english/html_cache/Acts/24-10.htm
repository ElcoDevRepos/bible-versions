Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
When the governor motioned for Paul to speak
This phrase sets the scene in a Roman judicial context, where Paul is standing trial before the governor, Felix. The governor's motion indicates a formal proceeding, typical of Roman legal practices. Felix, the governor of Judea, was known for his harsh rule and corruption, as noted by the historian Tacitus. This setting underscores the tension and gravity of the situation for Paul, who is defending himself against accusations from Jewish leaders.

he began his response:
Paul's response is a critical moment in his defense. It reflects his readiness and confidence to speak, despite the serious charges against him. This moment is reminiscent of Jesus' own trials, where He also stood before Roman authorities. Paul's ability to articulate his defense is a testament to his education and rhetorical skills, which he often used to spread the Gospel.

Knowing that you have been a judge over this nation for many years
Paul acknowledges Felix's long tenure as a judge, which could be seen as a strategic move to appeal to Felix's sense of authority and experience. Felix's rule over Judea began around AD 52, and he was familiar with Jewish customs and disputes. This acknowledgment serves to establish common ground and respect, a common rhetorical strategy in ancient speeches.

I gladly make my defense.
Paul expresses his willingness to defend himself, indicating his confidence in the truth of his message and his innocence. This phrase reflects Paul's unwavering faith and his commitment to the Gospel, even in the face of persecution. His defense is not just for his own sake but for the advancement of the Christian message. This echoes 1 Peter 3:15, where believers are encouraged to always be ready to give a defense for their hope.

What Does Acts 24:10 Mean
When the governor motioned for Paul to speak

Felix, the Roman governor, signals that Paul may reply to the Sanhedrin’s accusations.

• In Acts 24:1–9 the high priest and elders present their case; now Paul is granted the same courtesy.

• Scripture consistently shows God opening doors at the right moment (Acts 23:11; Luke 21:12–15).

• The moment underscores Romans 13:1—earthly authority is ultimately under God, who directs events for His purposes.

He began his response

Paul does not hesitate or stall; he steps forward in faith.

• Acts 26:1 shows the same pattern when Agrippa invites Paul to speak—readiness is a hallmark of a spirit-filled witness (1 Peter 3:15).

• Paul’s composure fulfills Jesus’ promise that the Spirit will give words in trials (Luke 12:11-12).

• By taking initiative, Paul models Proverbs 28:1—“the righteous are as bold as a lion.”

“Knowing that you have been a judge over this nation for many years”

Paul acknowledges Felix’s experience without empty flattery.

• Respect for authority aligns with Proverbs 24:21 and Paul’s own instruction in Romans 13:7.

• Felix had governed Judea since A.D. 52; Paul recognizes that longevity should bring familiarity with Jewish customs and law (Acts 24:22).

• This respectful preface contrasts with the exaggerated praise Tertullus gave earlier (Acts 24:2-4), showing integrity in Paul’s words (Ephesians 4:25).

“I gladly make my defense.”

Paul’s heart is free from fear or bitterness.

• “Gladly” reveals confidence in both his innocence and the gospel he proclaims (2 Timothy 1:8-12).

• His defense is not self-promotion but an opportunity to testify about Christ, as he soon will before Felix and Drusilla (Acts 24:24-25).

• Acts 25:8 echoes the same stance—truth removes dread, allowing Paul to stand boldly before any tribunal (Psalm 27:1).

summary

Acts 24:10 pictures Paul seizing a God-given moment: the governor signals, Paul responds promptly, he shows due respect, and he defends the truth with joy. The verse teaches believers to trust God’s timing, honor authority without compromise, and speak the gospel with fearless confidence whenever the Lord opens a door.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) Forasmuch as I know . . .--We note at once the difference between St. Paul's frank manliness and the servile flattery of the advocate. He is content to appeal to the experience of the "many years" (really about six, but this was more than the average duration of a procuratorship, and the words might, therefore, be used without exaggeration) during which he had held office. Such a man was not likely to attach too much weight to the statements of Tertullus and Ananias. Felix, after having ruled for a short time with a divided authority (see Note on Acts 24:2), had superseded Cumanus in A.D. 52 or 53.

I do the more cheerfully answer for myself.--The verb for "answer" is connected with our English "apology" in its older sense of "vindication" or "defence."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - And when the governor, etc., Paul answered for then Paul, after that the governor, etc., answered, A.V.; cheerfully for the more cheerfully, A.V. and T.R.; make my defense for answer for myself, A.V. Forasmuch as I know, etc. St. Paul, with inimitable skill, pitched upon the one favorable side of his judge's person, viz. his long experience in Jewish affairs, and made it the subject of his opening reference - a courteous and conciliatory reference, in striking contrast with the false, fulsome flattery of Tertullus. Of many years. If Paul was speaking in the year A.D. , and Felix had been governor only since A.D. , "many years" was rather an hyperbole. But Tacitus expressly states that Felix was joint procurator with Cumanus; and therefore he had been a judge to the Jewish nation long before the banishment of Cumanus. Tacitus's authority is infinitely superior to that of Josephus, and this passage strongly supports the statement of Tacitus ('Annal.,' 12:54). Make my defense (τὰ περὶ ἐμαυτοῦ ἀπολογοῦμαι). For the word ἀπολογοῦμαι, and for the situation of St. Paul, and for the gracious promise provided for such situation, see Luke 12:12; Luke 21:15; see too Acts 19:33; Acts 25:8; Acts 26. l, 2; and for the use of ἀπολογία, see Acts 22:1, note.
