Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
who found favor in the sight of God
This phrase refers to King David, who is described in the Bible as a man after God's own heart (1 Samuel 13:14, Acts 13:22). David's favor with God is evident in his anointing as king and his victories over Israel's enemies. His deep devotion and repentance, as seen in the Psalms, highlight his relationship with God. This favor is not based on David's perfection but on his faith and obedience.

and asked to provide a dwelling place
David expressed a desire to build a temple for God, as recorded in 2 Samuel 7:1-2. This request was motivated by his reverence for God and his desire to honor Him with a permanent place of worship. The concept of a dwelling place for God reflects the importance of the temple in Jewish worship, serving as a central location for sacrifices and festivals.

for the God of Jacob
The reference to "the God of Jacob" emphasizes the continuity of God's covenant with the patriarchs—Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. It underscores the faithfulness of God to His promises and His enduring relationship with the people of Israel. The temple was to be a symbol of God's presence among His chosen people, fulfilling the covenantal promises made to the forefathers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The central figure in this verse, David is described as having found favor in God's sight. He is known as a man after God's own heart and is significant for his desire to build a temple for God.

2. God
The Almighty, who David sought to honor by providing a dwelling place. God's favor upon David is a key theme in this passage.

3. The God of Jacob
This title emphasizes the covenant relationship God had with the patriarchs, including Jacob, and highlights the continuity of God's promises through generations.

4. The Temple
Although not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the "dwelling place" refers to the temple David desired to build, which was later constructed by his son Solomon.

5. Stephen's Speech
This verse is part of Stephen's defense before the Sanhedrin, where he recounts Israel's history to illustrate God's faithfulness and Israel's repeated disobedience.
Teaching Points
Favor with God
David's life exemplifies how seeking God's heart and will can lead to divine favor. Believers are encouraged to pursue a relationship with God that aligns with His purposes.

Desire to Honor God
David's intention to build a temple shows his desire to honor God with his resources and abilities. Christians today can reflect on how they can use their gifts to glorify God.

God's Sovereign Plan
While David had the desire to build the temple, it was Solomon who completed it. This teaches us that God's plans may unfold differently than we expect, but His purposes are always fulfilled.

Covenant Faithfulness
The reference to the "God of Jacob" reminds believers of God's faithfulness to His promises. Trusting in God's covenant promises can provide assurance and hope.

What Does Acts 7:46 Mean
Found favor in the sight of God

Acts 7:46 opens by recalling David, “who found favor in the sight of God.” That short clause pulls us back to the moment God chose David over Saul (1 Samuel 13:14; Acts 13:22).

• “Favor” underscores grace—God’s unearned goodness shown to one who trusted Him.

• David’s life displays that favor through covenant promises (2 Samuel 7:8-16) and preserved lineage leading to Christ (Luke 1:32-33).

• Stephen’s speech (Acts 7) has already highlighted God’s favor to Abraham (v. 8) and Joseph (v. 10), showing a consistent pattern of the Lord’s gracious election.

• For believers today, David’s experience reminds us that obedience and repentance matter, but standing accepted before God is ultimately a result of His gracious choice (Ephesians 2:8-9).

And asked

The next phrase says David “asked.” Scripture records the request: “Here I am, living in a house of cedar, while the ark of God remains in a tent” (2 Samuel 7:2; 1 Chronicles 17:1).

• David’s concern sprang from gratitude; God had elevated him from shepherd to king (Psalm 78:70-72).

• He recognized the disconnect between his own comfort and the humble tent that housed the ark, prompting him to seek a more fitting structure for the Lord’s presence.

• This initiative reveals a heart that longs to honor God first (Psalm 132:1-5).

To provide a dwelling place

David wanted “to provide a dwelling place.” While God later assigned the actual construction to Solomon (1 Kings 8:17-19), He affirmed David’s desire (2 Samuel 7:12-13).

• “Dwelling place” echoes Exodus 25:8: “Make a sanctuary for Me, so that I may dwell among them.”

• The tabernacle had always signified God’s nearness; the temple would give that truth a permanent, centralized symbol.

• Yet God reminded David, “Heaven is My throne, and the earth is My footstool” (Isaiah 66:1). The temple could never contain Him; it simply served as a visible testimony to His covenant presence.

• In the New Covenant Jesus fulfills this reality: “The Word became flesh and tabernacled among us” (John 1:14), and believers are now “a dwelling place for God in His Spirit” (Ephesians 2:21-22).

For the God of Jacob

The verse ends with a covenant title: “the God of Jacob.”

• Linking David’s request to “the God of Jacob” ties the monarchy to the patriarchal promises (Genesis 28:13).

• It underscores continuity: the same God who watched over Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob was now shepherding Israel through David (Psalm 23:1; 80:1).

• The name also carries a missionary note. When Solomon dedicates the temple, he prays that foreigners too would come and know “this house that I have built is called by Your Name” (1 Kings 8:41-43).

• Ultimately, Jesus—Son of David and Lord of Jacob—gathers both Jew and Gentile into one household of faith (Acts 15:16-17).

Summary

Acts 7:46 celebrates David as a man graced by God, whose heartfelt request sprang from a passion to honor the Lord with a permanent sanctuary. While the temple pointed Israel to God’s abiding presence, it also foreshadowed the greater dwelling place realized in Christ and in His people. God’s favor, our response of worship, the longing for His nearness, and the unbroken covenant line from Jacob to David to Jesus—all converge in this single sentence, reminding us that the Lord still delights to make His home among those who seek Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(46) Who found favour before God.--Again we trace, though still in the form of a narrative, an indirect answer to the accusation brought against Stephen. He was ready to acknowledge without reserve that the Temple was planned by the man after God's own heart, and built by the wisest of the sons of men. But the question still remained whether it was therefore the symbol of a final and perfect worship, whether it did not bear witness to its own incompleteness.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 46. - In the sight of for before, A.V. (ἐνώπιον); asked for desired, A.V.; habitation for tabernacle, A.V. (σκήνωμα). Habitation. In Deuteronomy 33:18 σκήνωμα stands in the LXX. for אִהֶל, and in 2 Peter 1:13, 5:14, for the human body as the tabernacle or temporary dwelling of the soul or spirit. And the idea of a temporary or movable dwelling seems to suit Stephen's argument better than that of a fixed one. The מִשְׁכָנות of Psalm 132:5 (to which perhaps, as well as 2 Samuel 7:1-6, Stephen refers) is equally applicable to a tent.
