Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
So take courage, men
This phrase is a call to bravery and reassurance. In the context of Acts 27, Paul is addressing the crew and passengers aboard a ship caught in a violent storm. The exhortation to "take courage" is a common biblical theme, seen in passages like Joshua 1:9, where God commands Joshua to be strong and courageous. This reflects a consistent biblical principle that faith in God provides strength and courage in the face of adversity. Historically, the Mediterranean Sea was known for its treacherous storms, making this encouragement particularly poignant.

for I believe God
Paul's declaration of faith is central to his message. His belief is not in the ship's ability to withstand the storm or in human efforts, but in God's promise. This reflects the broader biblical theme of faith in God's word, as seen in Hebrews 11:1, which defines faith as confidence in what we hope for and assurance about what we do not see. Paul's faith is reminiscent of Abraham's faith in God's promises (Romans 4:3), serving as a model for believers to trust in God's word despite circumstances.

that it will happen
This phrase underscores the certainty of God's promises. Paul's confidence that "it will happen" is rooted in the reliability of God's word. This echoes Isaiah 55:11, where God declares that His word will not return void but will accomplish what He desires. The assurance of God's promises is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, providing believers with hope and certainty in God's faithfulness.

just as He told me
Paul refers to a specific revelation from God, which he received through an angel (Acts 27:23-24). This divine communication assures Paul of the safety of all aboard the ship. The phrase highlights the importance of divine revelation and prophecy in the biblical narrative. It also points to the broader theme of God's sovereignty and His ability to communicate His will to His servants. This assurance is similar to the prophetic messages given to Old Testament prophets, affirming that God's plans will be fulfilled exactly as He declares.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul
The apostle who is on his way to Rome as a prisoner. He is the central figure in this passage, demonstrating faith and leadership during a perilous sea voyage.

2. The Ship's Crew and Passengers
Includes sailors, soldiers, and other prisoners. They are caught in a violent storm and are fearful for their lives.

3. The Storm
A severe storm, known as a "Northeaster," that threatens to destroy the ship and endanger all aboard.

4. God's Promise
Paul receives a divine message assuring him that all aboard will be saved, though the ship will be lost.

5. The Voyage to Rome
The journey itself is significant as it is part of Paul's mission to spread the Gospel and testify in Rome.
Teaching Points
Faith in God's Promises
Paul's unwavering belief in God's word serves as a model for trusting God's promises, even when circumstances seem dire.

Courage in Crisis
The call to "take courage" is a reminder that faith can provide strength and calm in the midst of life's storms.

Leadership through Faith
Paul's leadership is rooted in his faith, demonstrating that true leadership often involves guiding others to trust in God's plan.

God's Sovereignty and Protection
The assurance of safety for all aboard the ship underscores God's control over all situations and His ability to protect His people.

Witnessing through Trials
Paul's experience shows that trials can be opportunities to witness to others about God's faithfulness and power.

What Does Acts 27:25 Mean
So take courage, men

Acts 27 places Paul and 275 others in a violent storm that raged for two weeks (Acts 27:20). Panic was spreading, supplies were running low, and every earthly hope seemed gone. Into that chaos Paul says, “So take courage, men.”

• Courage is not self-generated bravado; it comes from a word from God. Joshua heard the same call while facing Canaanite giants—“Be strong and courageous, for the LORD your God is with you” (Joshua 1:9).

• Encouragement is contagious. When one believer stands on God’s promise, others find fresh strength, much like Jonathan’s faith stirred the trembling Israelites (1 Samuel 14:6-15).

• The Lord delights to give courage precisely when circumstances scream otherwise: “In the world you will have tribulation. But take courage; I have overcome the world” (John 16:33).

For I believe God

Paul anchors his exhortation in a settled conviction: “I believe God.”

• Faith rests on God’s character. “The word of the LORD is upright, and all His work is trustworthy” (Psalm 33:4). If the Almighty cannot lie (Numbers 23:19; Titus 1:2), His promise is as solid as His throne.

• Faith is personal. Paul does not merely believe in God; he believes God—taking Him at His word just as Abraham “was fully convinced that God was able to do what He had promised” (Romans 4:20-21).

• Faith is practical. In the pitch-black Mediterranean night, belief translates into action—cutting away lifeboats (Acts 27:30-32) and eating a meal in expectation of rescue (Acts 27:33-36). “We walk by faith, not by sight” (2 Corinthians 5:7).

That it will happen just as He told me

An angel had already assured Paul, “Do not be afraid… God has granted you all those who sail with you” (Acts 27:23-24). Paul treats that revelation as settled fact.

• God’s specific promises lead to specific expectations. Jesus said, “Let us go over to the other side of the lake” (Luke 8:22), and not even a sudden squall could nullify His word.

• Obedience flows from certainty. Because Paul trusts the promise, he can direct the crew without hesitation: stay with the ship, run aground where God appoints, and everyone will survive (Acts 27:31-32, 44).

• Fulfillment glorifies God alone. When all 276 souls stand safely on Malta’s shore (Acts 27:44), it is evident that “not one word has failed of all His good promise” (1 Kings 8:56).

summary

Acts 27:25 is a clarion call to courageous faith. In the darkest storm Paul looks beyond circumstances to the unbreakable word of God, rallies others to do the same, and watches the promise unfold exactly as spoken. When He says it, it will happen. Our task is simple: believe God, take courage, and act accordingly.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - Even so for even, A.V.; hath been spoken unto me for was told me, A.V. Be of good cheer; εὐθυμεῖτε, as ver. 22; elsewhere only James 5:13, but we have εὔθυμος ver. 36 and Acts 24:10; common in classical Greek and in medical language (see ver. 22, note). Note how the servant of God has the light of hope and trust in the darkest night of danger and suffering (Psalm 112:4, 7; Psalm 46:1-3). It shall be even so, etc. Compare for the lesson of faith in God's promise, Luke 1:45, "There shall be a performance of those things which were told her from the Lord." "Lord, increase our faith."
