Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All the circumcised believers who had accompanied Peter
This phrase refers to Jewish Christians who had been circumcised according to Jewish law, highlighting their Jewish heritage and adherence to the Mosaic Law. These believers accompanied Peter from Joppa to Caesarea, a journey that underscores the early church's mission to spread the gospel beyond Jerusalem. The presence of these Jewish Christians is significant as it sets the stage for the unfolding revelation that God's salvation extends beyond the Jewish people. This group serves as witnesses to the pivotal moment in the early church when the inclusion of Gentiles into the faith community is divinely affirmed.

were astounded
The astonishment of the Jewish believers indicates the unexpected nature of the event they witnessed. Despite the teachings of Jesus and the prophecies in the Hebrew Scriptures about the inclusion of the Gentiles (e.g., Isaiah 49:6), the reality of this inclusion was still surprising to them. This reaction highlights the deeply ingrained cultural and religious barriers that existed between Jews and Gentiles at the time. Their amazement underscores the radical nature of the gospel, which breaks down these barriers and fulfills the promise of a universal salvation.

that the gift of the Holy Spirit
The "gift of the Holy Spirit" refers to the outpouring of the Spirit, which is a sign of God's acceptance and empowerment. This event mirrors the Pentecost experience in Acts 2, where the Holy Spirit was first given to Jewish believers. The Holy Spirit's presence is a fulfillment of Jesus' promise in Acts 1:8 and serves as a divine endorsement of the Gentiles' inclusion in the church. Theologically, this gift signifies the new covenant, where the Spirit is given to all believers, regardless of ethnic background, as prophesied in Joel 2:28-29.

had been poured out
The imagery of the Spirit being "poured out" suggests abundance and generosity, echoing Old Testament prophecies such as Joel 2:28 and Isaiah 44:3. This language emphasizes the transformative and inclusive nature of God's action. The pouring out of the Spirit is a key theme in Acts, symbolizing the fulfillment of God's promises and the inauguration of the church's mission to all nations. It also reflects the baptism of the Holy Spirit, which empowers believers for witness and service.

even on the Gentiles
This phrase marks a significant turning point in the early church, as it confirms that Gentiles are fully included in God's plan of salvation. The inclusion of Gentiles fulfills the Abrahamic covenant, where God promised that all nations would be blessed through Abraham's descendants (Genesis 12:3). This event challenges the prevailing Jewish understanding of God's covenant and redefines the boundaries of the faith community. It also prefigures the later decisions of the Jerusalem Council in Acts 15, which formally recognized the Gentile mission. Theologically, this inclusion underscores the universality of the gospel and the breaking down of ethnic and cultural divisions in Christ (Ephesians 2:14-16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Peter
One of Jesus' apostles, a key leader in the early church, who received a vision from God to visit Cornelius, a Gentile.

2. Circumcised Believers
Jewish Christians who accompanied Peter to Cornelius' house. They were surprised by the outpouring of the Holy Spirit on the Gentiles.

3. Cornelius
A Roman centurion and a Gentile who feared God and was visited by an angel instructing him to send for Peter.

4. Gentiles
Non-Jewish people who were traditionally seen as outside the covenant community of Israel.

5. Holy Spirit
The third person of the Trinity, whose outpouring on the Gentiles signified God's acceptance of them into the faith community.
Teaching Points
The Universality of the Gospel
The outpouring of the Holy Spirit on the Gentiles demonstrates that the gospel is for all people, regardless of ethnic or cultural background.

Breaking Down Barriers
Just as the Jewish believers were surprised by God's work among the Gentiles, we must be open to God working in unexpected ways and among unexpected people.

The Role of the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit is the evidence of God's acceptance and inclusion, transcending human boundaries and prejudices.

Obedience to God's Leading
Peter's willingness to follow God's vision and visit Cornelius shows the importance of obedience to God's guidance, even when it challenges our preconceptions.

Unity in Diversity
The early church's experience with Gentile believers calls us to embrace diversity within the body of Christ, recognizing that all believers are equal recipients of God's grace.

What Does Acts 10:45 Mean
All

• The verse begins, “All … were astounded.” Nothing is left out; every observer shared the same reaction.

• Scripture consistently treats “all” as comprehensive (e.g., “All the people answered together, ‘We will do everything that the LORD has spoken.’ ” — Exodus 19:8).

• The unanimity highlights the unmistakable work of God; no one could deny what had happened (see Acts 4:16).

The circumcised believers

• These are Jewish followers of Jesus—men who trusted Christ yet still bore the covenant sign of circumcision.

• Their presence fulfills Jesus’ promise that His gospel would reach “Jerusalem, and in all Judea …” (Acts 1:8).

• Like the early church at large (Acts 2:41), they represent continuity with Israel while witnessing the expansion of grace to others.

Who had accompanied Peter

• Acts 10:23–29 shows Peter bringing six brothers from Joppa (Acts 11:12). Their role: eyewitnesses who could verify the event to the wider church.

• Traveling with Peter put them in the path of God’s larger plan—illustrating Proverbs 3:6, “In all your ways acknowledge Him, and He will make your paths straight”.

• Their companionship underscores biblical accountability (Ecclesiastes 4:9–10).

Were astounded

• “Astounded” signals overwhelming surprise. God’s actions exceeded their expectations, echoing Ephesians 3:20.

• Similar astonishment follows other Spirit outpourings: Acts 2:7 and Acts 19:6–7 record crowds amazed by the Spirit’s unmistakable manifestations.

• Their shock exposes lingering assumptions that God’s promises were primarily for Israel alone.

That the gift of the Holy Spirit had been poured out

• The “gift” is the Person of the Spirit Himself (Acts 2:38). His arrival confirms salvation (Ephesians 1:13).

• “Poured out” recalls God’s pledge in Joel 2:28, fulfilled at Pentecost and now extended here.

• The identical experience—speaking in tongues and praising God (Acts 10:46)—shows that Gentile believers received the same blessing as Jewish believers, no second-class status (Titus 3:5–6).

Even on the Gentiles

• “Even” marks the shocking, inclusive reach of grace. Romans 10:12 affirms, “There is no difference between Jew and Greek.”

• Peter will later declare, “God made no distinction between us and them” (Acts 15:9).

• This moment inaugurates the full enfolding of the nations, fulfilling Genesis 12:3, “All the families of the earth will be blessed through you.”

summary

Every single Jewish believer present witnessed the Holy Spirit bestowed on uncircumcised Gentiles exactly as He had been poured out on them. Their unanimous astonishment underscores how God shattered ethnic and religious barriers, verifying that salvation in Christ is for all who believe—Jew and Gentile alike—just as Scripture had promised.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(45) And they of the circumcision which believed . . .--St. Luke obviously dwells on this as a testimony, beyond suspicion, to the reality of the gift. Those who came with Peter were apparently not sharers at the time in the exultant joy which they were yet compelled to recognise as the Spirit's work. They listened with amazement as they heard the rapturous chant burst from the lips of the as yet unbaptised heathens. Here, accordingly, was one definite fulfilment of Peter's vision. Those who so spake had been, as it were, carried up into heaven, as the four-footed beasts and creeping things had been, and so a proof was given that no man might henceforth call them common or unclean. Peter himself had indeed learnt that lesson so fully (Acts 10:28) as not to need this special attestation, but for those who came with him this evidence was needed and was sufficient.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 45. - Amazed for astonished, A.V. They of the circumcision would doubtless be the brethren from Joppa who accompanied Peter (ver. 23). A more striking confirmation of Peter's vision cannot be conceived than this descent of the Holy Ghost upon the uncircumcised. How could they any longer be reckoned common or unclean whom God thus cleansed with his Holy Spirit?
