Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“Go!” said the Lord.
The command "Go!" is a direct and urgent instruction from the Lord, emphasizing the divine authority and immediacy of the mission. This reflects the nature of God's call, which often requires prompt obedience. The context here is the Lord speaking to Ananias in a vision, instructing him to visit Saul of Tarsus. This mirrors other biblical instances where God calls individuals to action, such as Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:10) and Jonah being sent to Nineveh (Jonah 1:2).

“This man is My chosen instrument
Saul, later known as Paul, is identified as God's "chosen instrument," indicating divine selection and purpose. This echoes the concept of election found throughout Scripture, where God chooses individuals for specific tasks, such as Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) and David (1 Samuel 16:12). The term "instrument" suggests a tool or vessel through which God will accomplish His work, highlighting the idea that God uses people to fulfill His divine plans.

to carry My name
The phrase "to carry My name" signifies the mission of proclaiming and representing the name of Jesus Christ. In biblical terms, a name encompasses character and authority. Saul's mission is to spread the gospel, bearing the name of Christ, which aligns with the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). This task involves both verbal proclamation and living a life that reflects Christ's teachings.

before the Gentiles and their kings,
This part of the verse highlights the scope of Saul's mission, which includes the Gentiles and their rulers. It marks a significant shift in the early Christian mission, as the gospel extends beyond the Jewish community to the Gentile world. This fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the inclusion of the Gentiles (Isaiah 49:6). Saul's future interactions with Gentile leaders, such as King Agrippa (Acts 26:1-28), demonstrate the fulfillment of this mission.

and before the people of Israel.
Despite the focus on the Gentiles, Saul's mission also includes the people of Israel. This dual focus reflects the early church's struggle and mission to reach both Jews and Gentiles, as seen in Paul's pattern of preaching first in synagogues (Acts 13:5, 14). It underscores the continuity of God's covenant with Israel and the fulfillment of His promises through Jesus Christ, who is the Messiah for both Jews and Gentiles.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Lord
Refers to Jesus Christ, who is speaking to Ananias in a vision. This highlights the divine authority and purpose behind the mission given to Saul (Paul).

2. Ananias
A disciple in Damascus who is initially hesitant to meet Saul due to Saul's reputation for persecuting Christians. Ananias is obedient to the Lord's command despite his fears.

3. Saul (Paul)
Initially a persecutor of Christians, Saul is chosen by God to become a key figure in spreading the Gospel. His transformation is central to the account of Acts.

4. Gentiles
Non-Jewish people. Saul's mission includes bringing the message of Christ to the Gentiles, indicating the universal scope of the Gospel.

5. Kings and the people of Israel
Saul is tasked with proclaiming the Gospel to both political leaders and the Jewish people, showing the breadth of his mission.
Teaching Points
Divine Selection and Purpose
God chooses individuals for specific purposes, often beyond human understanding. Like Saul, believers are called to embrace God's plan for their lives, trusting in His wisdom and timing.

Obedience Despite Fear
Ananias' obedience to God's command, despite his initial fear, serves as a model for believers. Trusting God and stepping out in faith can lead to transformative outcomes.

The Universal Scope of the Gospel
Saul's mission to the Gentiles and kings underscores the inclusivity of the Gospel. Believers are encouraged to share the message of Christ with all people, regardless of background or status.

Transformation and New Identity
Saul's conversion illustrates the power of God's grace to transform lives. Believers are reminded that their past does not define them; in Christ, they are new creations with a new purpose.

What Does Acts 9:15 Mean
Go!

When God tells Ananias, “Go!” (Acts 9:15), He issues a direct, non-negotiable command.

• Similar urgency appears when Jesus says “Go therefore and make disciples” in Matthew 28:19, underscoring that obedience precedes understanding.

• Jonah’s flight (Jonah 1:1-3) contrasts Ananias’s compliance, reminding us that resisting God’s clear call only delays His plan—not defeats it.

God often moves His servants out of comfort zones so His larger purposes advance.

Said the Lord

The speaker is the risen Christ, making this a divine mandate.

• Saul’s encounter with “Jesus whom you are persecuting” (Acts 9:5) establishes Jesus’ authority to redirect lives.

• Like Samuel hearing “Speak, for Your servant is listening” (1 Samuel 3:10), Ananias hears and obeys, showing that God still speaks personally and specifically to His people.

The authority behind the command guarantees its outcome.

This man is My chosen instrument

God singles out Saul—soon to be Paul—as His deliberate selection.

• God chose unlikely vessels before: Moses the reluctant (Exodus 3:11), Gideon the fearful (Judges 6:15), David the overlooked (1 Samuel 16:11-13).

• Ephesians 2:10 affirms believers are “created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand,” emphasizing purpose and forethought.

An “instrument” implies usefulness only when held by the Master; Paul’s readiness comes from surrender, not pedigree.

To carry My name before the Gentiles and their kings

The mission is global and bold.

• Isaiah 49:6 foretold a servant who would be “a light for the nations,” fulfilled in Acts 13:46-47 when Paul turns to the Gentiles.

• Paul stands before kings Agrippa and Festus (Acts 25–26) and ultimately Caesar’s court (Acts 27:24; Philippians 1:12-13), proving the prophecy literal.

• Romans 15:20 reveals Paul’s passion “to preach the gospel where Christ was not known,” showing single-minded focus on unreached peoples.

And before the people of Israel

Paul’s ministry never abandons his own nation.

• Every city visit begins in a synagogue (Acts 13:5, 14; 14:1; 17:1-2), fulfilling Romans 1:16—“first to the Jew.”

• His grief for Israel’s unbelief (Romans 9:1-3) illustrates that embracing a wider call never nullifies ongoing concern for one’s roots.

God’s plan integrates Jew and Gentile into one redeemed family (Ephesians 2:14-16).

summary

Acts 9:15 reveals God’s sovereign choice of Paul, the scope of the gospel, and the certainty that divine commands carry divine enablement. Ananias’s obedience, Paul’s commissioning, and the subsequent spread of the gospel remind us that when the Lord says “Go,” He provides direction, power, and purpose to reach every audience He names.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) He if a chosen vessel unto me.--Literally, a vessel of election. The term has nothing directly analogous to it in the Old Testament, but it is Hebrew in its form; the second noun being used as a genitive of the characteristic attribute, and so equivalent to an intensified adjective. So in Isaiah 22:7, we have in the LXX. "valleys of election" for the "choicest valleys" of the English version. The term "vessel" is used in the Old Testament of arms (Genesis 27:3), of garments (Deuteronomy 22:5), of household goods (Genesis 31:36-37). In the New Testament its range of meaning is yet wider, as in Matthew 12:29; Luke 8:16; John 19:29; Romans 9:22; 2Corinthians 4:7. Here our word "instrument" or "implement" comes, perhaps, nearest to its meaning. The persecutor had been chosen by the Lord as the "tool" with which He would work out His gracious will for him and for the Gentiles. In this sense it was used by classical writers of useful and trusty slaves, just as we speak of one man being the "tool" of another. Possibly, however, the words may be interpreted as containing the germ of the parable of the potter's vessel on which St. Paul dwells in Romans 9:21-23, and implied that the convert was not only chosen, but moulded, for his future work. The word "election," which occurs here for the first time in the New Testament, and is afterwards so prominent in the teaching of St. Paul (Romans 9:11; Romans 11:5; Romans 11:7-8; 1Thessalonians 1:4), affords yet another instance of the influence exercised on the Apostle by the thoughts and language of the instructor through whom alone he could have learnt what is here recorded. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - A chosen vessel (comp. Galatians 2:15; Romans 9:21, 22). To bear my name before the Gentiles (see Acts 22:21; Acts 26:17, 18; Romans 15:16; Galatians 2:7-9, etc.) and kings (Acts 25; Acts 26; 2 Timothy 4:16, 17, with reference to Nero), and the children of Israel. The Gentiles are named before the children of Israel, because St. Paul's special call was to be the apostle of the Gentiles. But we know that even St. Paul's practice was to preach Christ to the Jews first, in every city where there were Jews.
