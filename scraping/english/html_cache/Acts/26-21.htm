Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For this reason
This phrase refers to the preceding context where Paul is explaining his mission and calling to King Agrippa. Paul has been sharing his testimony and the vision he received from Jesus Christ. The "reason" is his preaching of the Gospel, which was seen as controversial and threatening to Jewish religious leaders.

the Jews
This term refers to the Jewish religious authorities and leaders who opposed Paul's message. Historically, Paul was a Pharisee and well-versed in Jewish law, which made his conversion and subsequent preaching particularly contentious. The opposition from the Jewish leaders is a recurring theme in Acts, highlighting the tension between early Christians and traditional Jewish beliefs.

seized me
Paul's arrest was a result of his preaching, which was perceived as blasphemous and a threat to the Jewish faith. This act of seizing reflects the legal and religious authority the Jewish leaders held in Jerusalem. It also demonstrates the lengths to which they would go to protect their traditions and power.

in the temple courts
The temple in Jerusalem was the center of Jewish worship and a symbol of their religious identity. The temple courts were areas where teaching and discussions often took place. Paul's presence and preaching in this sacred space would have been seen as provocative, especially given his message about Jesus as the Messiah.

and tried to kill me
This reflects the severity of the opposition Paul faced. The attempt on his life underscores the intense hostility towards the message of Jesus Christ and the perceived threat it posed to Jewish religious authority. This echoes other instances in Acts where early Christians faced persecution, such as Stephen's martyrdom in Acts 7. It also fulfills Jesus' prophecy that His followers would face persecution (John 15:20).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul (Saul of Tarsus)
The apostle who is giving his defense before King Agrippa. Formerly a persecutor of Christians, Paul became a fervent follower of Christ and a missionary to the Gentiles.

2. The Jews
Referring to the Jewish leaders and people who opposed Paul's message about Jesus being the Messiah. They saw his teachings as a threat to their religious traditions and authority.

3. The Temple Courts
The location in Jerusalem where Paul was seized. The temple was the center of Jewish worship and a place of significant religious and cultural importance.

4. King Agrippa
Herod Agrippa II, the ruler before whom Paul is making his defense. He was knowledgeable about Jewish customs and controversies.

5. Paul's Arrest
The event where Paul was seized by the Jews, leading to his series of trials and eventual appeal to Caesar.
Teaching Points
Perseverance in Faith
Paul’s experience teaches us the importance of standing firm in our faith, even when faced with opposition or persecution.

Understanding Opposition
Recognize that opposition can often arise from misunderstanding or fear of change, as seen in the Jewish leaders' reaction to Paul’s message.

God’s Sovereignty in Trials
Trust that God can use difficult circumstances for His purposes, as He used Paul’s arrest to spread the Gospel to new audiences, including Roman officials.

Courage in Witnessing
Be encouraged to share your faith boldly, knowing that God is with you, just as He was with Paul during his trials.

The Role of Testimony
Paul’s defense is a powerful reminder of the impact of personal testimony in witnessing to others about Christ.

What Does Acts 26:21 Mean
For this reason

Paul has just finished recounting how he obeyed the heavenly vision by preaching that both Jews and Gentiles “should repent and turn to God, performing deeds worthy of their repentance” (Acts 26:20). That gospel—centered on the risen Christ and offered freely to all—became the immediate cause of the hostility he faced. Similar tension surfaces when he earlier declared, “I will send you far away to the Gentiles” (Acts 22:21), provoking an uproar (Acts 22:22). Paul’s message of grace offended those who clung to ethnic and ceremonial distinctions, yet he steadfastly proclaimed it, convinced of its divine origin (Galatians 1:11-12; Romans 1:16).

the Jews seized me

Luke notes the ringleaders were representatives of the Jewish religious establishment who “stirred up the whole crowd” (Acts 21:27-30). Their seizure of Paul reflects a pattern: faithful witnesses often face violent opposition from their own people (John 15:20; 1 Thessalonians 2:14-16). Still, Paul never stopped loving his kinsmen (Romans 9:1-3); he simply refused to dilute the truth for the sake of peace (Galatians 1:10).

in the temple courts

Ironically, the arrest occurred in the very place designed for worship and sacrificial cleansing. Paul had entered the temple “to present offerings” (Acts 24:18) and demonstrate respect for the Law (Acts 21:26). His presence there exposes the false charge that he despised the temple (Acts 24:6). Instead, the real defilement came from those who misused that sacred space to attack God’s messenger—echoing Jesus’ own rejection within those courts (Mark 11:15-18).

and tried to kill me

Hatred escalated so quickly that an assassination plot soon followed (Acts 23:12-15). Yet God preserved Paul, just as He earlier delivered him from stonings, beatings, and shipwrecks (2 Corinthians 11:23-26). The Lord’s promise in Acts 23:11—“Take courage… you must testify in Rome”—shows that no human scheme can thwart His sovereign plan (Psalm 118:6; Acts 9:15-16).

summary

Acts 26:21 pinpoints the clash between gospel truth and hardened unbelief. Paul’s Christ-centered, grace-filled message provoked a violent backlash, yet every detail—from the temple setting to the failed murder attempt—demonstrates God’s faithful protection of His servant. The verse reminds us that obedience to the risen Lord may invite intense opposition, but His purpose and presence remain unshakable.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) For these causes . . .--Better, perhaps, on account of these things. With this brief touch, avoiding any elaborate vindication of his own character, St. Paul indicates the real cause of the hostility of the Jews. The one unpardonable sin, in their eyes, was that he taught the Gentiles that they might claim every gift and grace which had once been looked on as the privilege and prerogative of Israel. The historical precedence of the Jew remained (see Notes on Acts 13:46; Romans 3:1-2), but in all essential points they were placed on a footing of equality.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - This cause for these causes, A.V.; seized for caught, A.V.; essayed for went about, A.V. For this cause. Here again is a most telling statement. "I have spent my life in trying to persuade men to repent and turn to God, and for doing so the Jews seek to kill me. Can this be right? Will not you, O King Agrippa, protect me from such an unjust requital?" To kill me; διαχειρίσασθαι, here and in Acts 5:30 only in the New Testament; not in the LXX., but in Polybius, and in Hippocrates and Galen, of surgical operations.
