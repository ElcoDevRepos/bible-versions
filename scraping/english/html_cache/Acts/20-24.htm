Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But I consider my life of no value to me
Paul expresses a profound commitment to his mission, valuing it above his own life. This reflects the teachings of Jesus, who emphasized self-denial and taking up one's cross (Matthew 16:24-25). Paul's perspective aligns with the biblical theme of self-sacrifice for the sake of the Gospel, as seen in the lives of other apostles and early Christians who faced persecution and martyrdom.

if only I may finish my course
The metaphor of a race or course is common in Paul's writings (1 Corinthians 9:24-27; 2 Timothy 4:7). It suggests discipline, perseverance, and a clear goal. The imagery would resonate with the Greco-Roman audience familiar with athletic competitions. Paul’s focus is on completing the task set before him, emphasizing endurance and faithfulness.

and complete the ministry I have received from the Lord Jesus
Paul acknowledges his calling as divinely appointed, echoing his conversion experience on the road to Damascus (Acts 9:15-16). This divine commission underscores the authority and responsibility of his apostolic mission. The concept of receiving a ministry from the Lord highlights the idea of stewardship and accountability to God.

the ministry of testifying to the good news of God’s grace
The core of Paul's ministry is the proclamation of the Gospel, centered on the grace of God. This grace is a key theme in Paul's theology, emphasizing salvation as a gift from God, not earned by works (Ephesians 2:8-9). The message of grace contrasts with the legalism of the Judaizers and is foundational to the New Covenant established through Christ’s sacrifice. The term "testifying" implies a witness, suggesting both personal experience and authoritative proclamation.

1. Paul the Apostle
The speaker of this verse, Paul is addressing the Ephesian elders, expressing his dedication to the mission given to him by Jesus Christ.

2. Ephesian Elders
Leaders of the church in Ephesus, whom Paul is addressing in his farewell speech, emphasizing the importance of their leadership and vigilance.

3. Ephesus
A significant city in the early Christian church, known for its diverse population and as a center for early Christian evangelism.

4. The Ministry
Refers to the specific calling and mission that Paul received from Jesus Christ, which is to spread the gospel and testify to God's grace.

5. The Good News of God’s Grace
Central to Paul's message, this refers to the gospel of Jesus Christ, emphasizing salvation through grace.
Teaching Points
The Value of Life in Christ
Our lives gain true value when aligned with God's purpose. Like Paul, we should prioritize our divine calling over personal ambitions.

Completing the Course
The Christian journey is likened to a race. We are called to persevere and remain faithful to the end, focusing on the mission God has given us.

Testifying to God’s Grace
Sharing the gospel is a central aspect of our faith. We are called to be witnesses of God's grace in our lives, just as Paul was.

Sacrificial Living
Paul’s willingness to sacrifice his life for the gospel challenges us to consider what we are willing to sacrifice for our faith.

Dependence on God’s Strength
Completing our God-given mission requires reliance on His strength and grace, not our own abilities.

What Does Acts 20:24 Mean
But I consider my life

Paul opens with a sharp contrast to the self-preservation instinct. His outlook mirrors Jesus’ teaching that whoever loses his life for Christ will find it (Matthew 16:24-25). Like the psalmist who declares, “Your love is better than life” (Psalm 63:3), Paul weighs Christ’s mission against personal comfort and chooses the mission every time.

of no value to me

Here Paul places zero price tag on his own safety, ambitions, or reputation. He is not embracing nihilism; he is prioritizing eternity. Philippians 3:7-8 shows the same heart: “Whatever was gain to me I now consider loss for the sake of Christ.” When the gospel is supreme, the believer refuses to cling to lesser treasures (Hebrews 10:34).

if only I may finish my course

The image shifts to a race. Paul’s “course” is divinely marked out, echoing 2 Timothy 4:7, “I have finished the race.” The focus is steadfastness over speed—pressing on regardless of hurdles (Hebrews 12:1-2). Every believer has a God-assigned lane; fulfillment comes from running to the tape, not comparing tracks.

and complete the ministry I have received from the Lord Jesus

Paul did not volunteer for a hobby; he was entrusted with a sacred stewardship (1 Corinthians 9:16-17). The source—“the Lord Jesus”—guarantees both authority and accountability. Like the servants in Jesus’ parable of the talents (Matthew 25:14-23), Paul longs to be found faithful, not flashy.

the ministry of testifying to the good news of God’s grace

The content of Paul’s assignment is crystal clear: declare grace. This “good news” reaches Jew and Gentile alike (Acts 20:21; Romans 1:16-17). Grace highlights what God has done, not what we achieve (Ephesians 2:8-9). Thus, Paul’s self-forgetfulness is logical—grace leaves no room for boasting except in the cross (Galatians 6:14).

summary

Acts 20:24 showcases a life recalibrated by Christ’s commission. Personal preservation bows before gospel proclamation, finishing the God-given race matters more than prolonging it, and grace—lavish, unearned, unstoppable—remains the message worth any sacrifice.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) But none of these things move me . . .--Literally, But I take account of nothing, nor do I hold my life . . . We note the parallelism with Luther's famous declaration, when warned by his friends not to go to Worms, "I will go thither, though there should be devils on every house-top."

So that I might finish my course with joy.--The two last words are wanting in many of the best MSS., and were probably inserted as a rhetorical improvement. The passage is grander without them. What St. Paul desired was to finish his course--whether "with joy" or not mattered little. The dominance of the same ruling thought finds utterance once again in his last Epistle (2Timothy 4:7).

The ministry, which I have received of the Lord Jesus.--We have again to note the parallelism with St. Paul's language elsewhere (2Corinthians 4:1; 2Corinthians 5:18; 1Timothy 1:12); the words that follow are in apposition with the "ministry," and explain what it consisted in. To bear witness, especially as a living example of its power (1Timothy 1:12-16), of the good tidings that God was not a harsh Judge, but a gracious Father, willing all men to be saved (1Timothy 2:4), that was the truth to the proclamation of which his life was to be devoted. In this there was the central truth of the kingdom of God, of which the next verse speaks. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - I hold not my life of any account, as dear for none of these things move me, neither count I my life dear, A.V. and T.R.; may accomplish my course for might finish my course with joy, A.V. and T.R.; received for have received, A.V.; from for of, A.V. I hold not my life, etc. It is inconceivable that St. Paul should have uttered, or St. Luke have reported, such an unintelligible sentence as that of the R.T., when it was perfectly easy to express the meaning clearly. Neither does the mention of his life, in the first instance, tally with that of "bonds and afflictions." The T.R., which has considerable support, seems to be far preferable. The first clause, Οὐδενὸς λόγον ποιοῦμαι, means quite naturally," I take no account of anything;" I value nothing, neither liberty, nor ease, nor comfort. I am ready to suffer the loss of all things, and I do count them as dung (Philippians 3:7-9); and then he adds yet further, "Neither do I count my own life as precious, so as to accomplish my course," etc. This metaphor of running a race is a favorite one with St. Paul (1 Corinthians 9:24; Galatians 5:7; Philippians 3:13, 14; 2 Timothy 4:7). To testify the gospel of the grace of God. An invaluable epitome of the Christian ministry. The essential feature of the gospel is its declaration of God's free grace to a guilty world, forgiving sins, and imputing righteousness through faith in Jesus Christ. The distinctive work of the ministry is to declare that grace. So St. Paul describes his own ministry, and the record of his ministry in the Acts and in his Epistles exactly agrees with this description.
