Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For even while they were in their kingdom
This phrase refers to the period when the Israelites were established in the Promised Land, enjoying the sovereignty and stability of their own nation. Historically, this encompasses the time from the conquest of Canaan under Joshua to the Babylonian exile. Despite having their own kingdom, the Israelites often fell into idolatry and disobedience, as seen in the cycles of sin and repentance in the Book of Judges and the accounts of the kings in 1 and 2 Kings.

with the abundant goodness that You had given them
God's abundant goodness is evident in the blessings He bestowed upon Israel, including prosperity, peace, and His presence among them. This reflects the covenant promises made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, where God pledged to bless their descendants. The land flowing with milk and honey (Exodus 3:8) symbolizes this abundance. Despite these blessings, the Israelites frequently turned away from God, highlighting human ingratitude and the tendency to take divine gifts for granted.

and in the spacious and fertile land that You had set before them
The land of Canaan, described as spacious and fertile, was a tangible sign of God's promise to the patriarchs. Geographically, it was a land of diverse topography and climate, suitable for agriculture and livestock. Archaeological findings confirm the fertility of ancient Canaan, supporting biblical descriptions. This land was meant to be a place where Israel could thrive under God's law, yet they often failed to uphold their end of the covenant.

they would not serve You
This phrase underscores Israel's repeated failure to remain faithful to God. Despite the clear commandments given at Sinai and the renewal of the covenant under leaders like Joshua, the people frequently turned to other gods. This disobedience is a central theme in the prophetic books, where prophets like Isaiah and Jeremiah call Israel back to true worship. The refusal to serve God is seen as a rejection of His kingship and a breach of the covenant relationship.

or turn from their wicked ways
The call to turn from wickedness is a recurring theme in the Bible, emphasizing repentance and the need for a change of heart. The prophets consistently urged Israel to abandon idolatry and injustice, which were prevalent in the society. This phrase highlights the moral and spiritual decline that led to the eventual exile. It also foreshadows the New Testament call to repentance, where John the Baptist and Jesus preach the necessity of turning away from sin to enter the Kingdom of God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Nehemiah
A Jewish leader who played a crucial role in the rebuilding of Jerusalem's walls and the spiritual renewal of the people. He is the central figure in the book of Nehemiah.

2. Israelites
The people of God who are the focus of this passage. They are reflecting on their history and acknowledging their failure to serve God despite His blessings.

3. The Kingdom
Refers to the period when the Israelites were established in the Promised Land, enjoying God's blessings and prosperity.

4. The Land
The Promised Land, described as spacious and fertile, which God gave to the Israelites as an inheritance.

5. God's Abundant Goodness
The blessings and prosperity that God bestowed upon the Israelites, which they failed to appreciate and respond to with obedience.
Teaching Points
Gratitude for God's Blessings
Recognize and appreciate the blessings God has given us, understanding that they are meant to draw us closer to Him.

The Danger of Complacency
Prosperity can lead to spiritual complacency. We must remain vigilant in our devotion to God, regardless of our circumstances.

Repentance and Obedience
True repentance involves turning away from evil ways and serving God wholeheartedly. Reflect on areas in your life where you need to make changes.

Historical Reflection for Spiritual Growth
Reflecting on past failures can lead to spiritual growth and renewal. Use history as a tool for learning and transformation.

God's Faithfulness Despite Human Failure
God's abundant goodness remains constant, even when we fail. Trust in His faithfulness and strive to align your life with His will.

What Does Nehemiah 9:35 Mean
For even while they were in their kingdom

- Israel enjoyed the dignity of self-rule—kings on David’s throne, fortified cities, a standing army (1 Chronicles 29:25; 2 Kings 14:23-27).

- Independence should have driven them to thankful obedience, yet “Jeshurun grew fat and kicked” (Deuteronomy 32:15).

- The verse reminds us that outward success never guarantees inward faithfulness (2 Chronicles 36:11-16).

with the abundant goodness that You had given them

- God loaded the nation with material and spiritual benefits—peace under Solomon (1 Kings 4:20-25), overflowing harvests (Deuteronomy 8:7-10), and prophetic guidance (Amos 2:11).

- Psalm 103:2 says, “Bless the LORD, O my soul, and forget not all His benefits.” Israel forgot.

- When blessing is met with cold hearts, the Lord eventually removes what He gave (Hosea 2:8-9).

and in the spacious and fertile land that You had set before them

- The “land flowing with milk and honey” (Exodus 3:8) was proof of covenant love.

- God cleared out stronger nations, planting Israel “in a good and spacious land” (Ezekiel 20:6).

- Yet idolatry polluted that land (Jeremiah 2:7), showing that environment alone cannot secure holiness.

they would not serve You

- Service is the expected response to grace (Deuteronomy 10:12-13; 1 Samuel 12:24).

- Instead, they “served the Baals and the Ashtoreths” (Judges 2:13), exchanging the living God for lifeless idols.

- Persistent refusal to serve anticipates exile (Jeremiah 25:8-11).

or turn from their wicked ways

- Prophets kept calling, “Return now every one of you from his evil way” (Jeremiah 25:5), but the people hardened their necks (Zechariah 7:11-12).

- Repentance would have triggered mercy (2 Chronicles 7:14; Ezekiel 18:30-32), yet they clung to sin until judgment fell.

- Nehemiah’s prayer (chapter 9) confesses this pattern so the restored community will break it.

summary

Nehemiah 9:35 exposes the tragedy of a blessed people who, despite kingship, abundance, and a rich land, refused to serve or repent before God. Blessing magnifies responsibility; when privilege meets rebellion, judgment follows. The verse calls every generation to remember God’s goodness, serve Him wholeheartedly, and turn quickly from sin.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - They have not served thee in their kingdom. There is no need of altering the reading here. "In their kingdom" means, "while they had a kingdom of their own, and were not subjects, as now, to a foreign power." Thy great goodness. See above, ver. 25. The large and fat land. Compare Exodus 3:8. Although the limits of Palestine are narrow, yet the land which God flare to his people, extending as it did from the Euphrates to the river of Egypt (Genesis 15:18), might well he termed a "large" or "broad" land.
