Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Before them the earth quakes;
This phrase suggests a powerful, divine intervention that causes the earth to shake. Earthquakes in the Bible often symbolize God's presence or judgment (e.g., Exodus 19:18, Matthew 27:51). In the context of Joel, it signifies the coming of the Day of the Lord, a time of both judgment and restoration. The imagery of an earthquake underscores the seriousness and magnitude of the events described.

the heavens tremble.
The trembling of the heavens indicates cosmic disturbances, often associated with divine activity. This phrase can be linked to other biblical passages where the heavens are affected by God's actions (e.g., Isaiah 13:13, Haggai 2:6). It emphasizes the totality of God's power, affecting both the earth and the heavens, and serves as a warning of impending judgment.

The sun and moon grow dark,
The darkening of the sun and moon is a common biblical motif for divine judgment and the Day of the Lord (e.g., Isaiah 13:10, Amos 8:9). This imagery conveys a sense of foreboding and the disruption of natural order, symbolizing the removal of God's favor and the onset of His wrath. It also foreshadows eschatological events described in the New Testament (e.g., Matthew 24:29, Revelation 6:12).

and the stars lose their brightness.
The dimming of the stars further emphasizes the cosmic scale of the events. Stars losing their brightness can symbolize the fall of earthly powers or spiritual forces (e.g., Isaiah 34:4, Revelation 8:12). This imagery points to the comprehensive nature of God's judgment, affecting all creation. It also serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty over the universe, as He alone controls the celestial bodies.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joel
A prophet in the Old Testament, Joel is the author of the book that bears his name. He is known for his prophecies concerning the Day of the Lord and calls for repentance.

2. The Day of the Lord
A significant theme in Joel, referring to a time of divine judgment and deliverance. It is a day of both terror and hope, depending on one's relationship with God.

3. The Earth and Heavens
In this verse, they are described as quaking and trembling, symbolizing the cosmic scale of God's intervention.

4. The Sun, Moon, and Stars
These celestial bodies are depicted as losing their brightness, indicating a dramatic change in the natural order as a sign of divine activity.

5. The Locust Plague
Earlier in Joel, a locust plague is described, which serves as a precursor and metaphor for the coming judgment and the need for repentance.
Teaching Points
The Sovereignty of God
God's control over the natural world is absolute. The quaking earth and trembling heavens remind us of His power and authority over creation.

The Urgency of Repentance
The dramatic signs in the heavens serve as a call to repentance. Believers are urged to examine their lives and turn back to God before the Day of the Lord.

Hope Amidst Judgment
While the imagery is terrifying, it also points to the hope of restoration for those who are faithful. God's judgment is not only punitive but also purifying.

Eschatological Awareness
Christians are encouraged to live with an awareness of the end times, understanding that current events are part of God's larger redemptive plan.

Spiritual Preparedness
The signs in Joel 2:10 challenge believers to be spiritually prepared, living in a way that reflects readiness for Christ's return.

What Does Joel 2:10 Mean
Before them the earth quakes

Joel pictures an advancing army of judgment so fearsome that even creation reacts.

• Earthquakes in Scripture often accompany God’s direct intervention—Exodus 19:18 when the LORD descended on Sinai, and Matthew 27:51 when Jesus died.

• The shaking underscores that this is no ordinary invasion; it is divinely orchestrated, echoing Haggai 2:6, “I will once more shake the heavens and the earth.”

• By placing the quaking “before them,” Joel stresses that nature itself clears a path for the Lord’s hosts.

the heavens tremble

Heavenly bodies respond to the same divine presence that unsettles the ground.

• Isaiah 13:13 links cosmic trembling to “the wrath of the LORD of Hosts.”

• Hebrews 12:26 recalls God’s promise to shake “not only the earth but heaven as well,” pointing forward to final judgment.

• The phrase reminds readers that nothing—earthly or celestial—stands independent of God’s sovereignty.

The sun and moon grow dark

Light-bearers dim in the face of the Almighty’s judgment.

• Amos 8:9 foretells a day when God “will make the sun go down at noon.”

• At Christ’s crucifixion a similar darkness fell (Luke 23:44-45), signaling divine displeasure and fulfillment of prophetic patterns.

• Darkness also represents the removal of comfort and order; life grinds to a halt when the usual rhythms of day and night disappear.

and the stars lose their brightness

Even distant lights cannot withstand God’s day of reckoning.

• Isaiah 34:4 speaks of stars “dissolving” when the LORD’s sword is unleashed.

• Revelation 6:13 depicts stars falling “to the earth, like figs dropped from a fig tree,” tying Joel’s prophecy to end-time events.

• The image assures God’s people that the powers they deem immutable are still under His command; conversely, it warns the unrepentant that no refuge exists in creation.

summary

Joel 2:10 layers earthquake, cosmic trembling, darkened luminaries, and fading stars to present a single truth: when God rises to judge, every level of creation yields. The verse calls the rebellious to repentance and reassures the faithful that the Lord who shakes earth and heaven also keeps covenant with His people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) The earth shall quake before them.--Some commentators call this description "a specimen of the highly-wrought hyperbolical features of Hebrew poetry," but it is the presence and judgment, the voice of the Lord in the thunder, which causes this trepidation. The signs in the heavens will be manifested at the judgment day.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 10, 11. - These verses picture the dreadful consequences of the then present and temporary visitation of the locusts, and of the future and final judgment of which it was a type. The earth shall quake before them;

(1) the locusts. The heavens tremble. The alighting of the locusts on the earth would make it quake, and their flight through the heavens would make it tremulous. As applied to the visitation o! locusts, the language would be hyperbolical, unless we accept Jerome's explanation as follows: "It is not that the strength of the locusts is so great that they can move the heavens and shake the earth, but that to those who suffer from such calamities, from the amount of their own terror the heavens appear to shake and the earth to reel."

(2) Before him; i.e. Jehovah himself amid the storm; and all in accordance with fact. But a greater judgment than that of the locusts is typified by the language of the prophet. Kimchi observes on this (tenth)verse that "all the expressions are parabolical, or figurative, to set forth the greatness of a calamity; for this is the usage of Scripture, as, 'The sun shall be darkened in his going forth,' and the like." So also Abarbauel on this verse: "Which all is a parabolical expression of the calamities of the Jews." Aben Ezra understands it differently: "Men of the earthquake." Rashi: "The heavens quake and tremble because of the punishment that comes upon Israel." The second part of the verse, as also the verse following, appear to us to indicate this. The sun and the moon shall be dark, and the stars shall withdraw their shining: and the Lord shall utter his voice before his army. That a storm succeeded and put an end to the plague of locusts, and that the darkening of the sun and moon and stars signified the obscuration of the heavenly luminaries by the storm-clouds that overspread the heavens and darkened the face of day, would fall short of expressions of such solemn grandeur as are here employed by the prophet, Besides, our Lord applies language of the same import to the last judgment in the Gospels: "The sun shall be darkened, and the moon shall not give her light, and the stars shall fall from heaven, and the powers of the heavens shall be shaken: and then shall appear the sign of the Son of man in heaven." Thunder, no doubt, is the voice of the Lord, which he utters while marching at the head of his army to execute judgment and manifest his wrath against his enemies. For his camp is very great: for he is strong that executeth his word: for the day of the Lord is great and very terrible; and who can abide it? Three reasons are here assigned for the preceding sublime description of Jehovah coming to judgment at the head of his hosts. These are the following: the greatness of his army in number and might; the power with which his army executes his word of command; and the terrible character of the day of judgment when the vials of Divine wrath shall be poured forth.
