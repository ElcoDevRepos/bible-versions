Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All these buildings were constructed with costly stones
The use of costly stones signifies the grandeur and opulence of Solomon's construction projects, reflecting the wealth and resources available during his reign. This aligns with the biblical portrayal of Solomon's era as a time of prosperity and peace (1 Kings 4:25). The choice of expensive materials also underscores the importance of the temple and royal palace as central places of worship and governance.

cut to size and trimmed with saws inside and out
The precision in cutting and trimming the stones indicates advanced craftsmanship and technology for the time. This meticulous work reflects the care and dedication in building structures meant to honor God and serve as a lasting legacy. The use of saws suggests a level of sophistication in construction techniques, which may have been influenced by interactions with neighboring cultures known for their architectural advancements.

from the foundation to the eaves
This phrase emphasizes the thoroughness and completeness of the construction, ensuring that every part of the building, from the base to the top, was made with the same high-quality materials and attention to detail. It symbolizes the stability and durability of Solomon's kingdom, which was established on a strong foundation of wisdom and divine favor (1 Kings 3:12-14).

and from the outside to the great courtyard
The mention of the great courtyard highlights the expansive and inclusive nature of the temple complex, designed to accommodate large gatherings for worship and community events. This layout reflects the temple's role as a central place for the Israelites to come together in the presence of God. The courtyard's design may also foreshadow the New Testament concept of the church as a spiritual community open to all believers (Ephesians 2:19-22).

Teaching Points
The Value of Excellence in Work
The use of costly stones and precise craftsmanship in Solomon's buildings reflects the importance of excellence in our work for God. We should strive to offer our best in all we do, recognizing that our work is an act of worship.

The Significance of God's Dwelling Place
The temple's construction with high-quality materials signifies the reverence and honor due to God's dwelling place. In our lives, we should prioritize creating spaces—both physical and spiritual—that honor God's presence.

Stewardship of Resources
Solomon's use of resources for the temple and palace challenges us to consider how we steward the resources God has entrusted to us. Are we using them to glorify God and serve His purposes?

The Temporary Nature of Earthly Structures
While Solomon's buildings were magnificent, they were ultimately temporary. This reminds us to focus on eternal values and invest in what lasts beyond this life.

What Does 1 Kings 7:9 Mean
All these buildings were constructed with costly stones

The author has just described Solomon’s palace complex (1 Kings 7:1-8) and now summarizes the materials.

• “Costly stones” points to genuine monetary value and enduring quality (1 Kings 5:17; 2 Chron 3:6). God is worthy of the finest.

• These stones proclaim stability and permanence, echoing how the temple itself was “built to last” (1 Kings 6:7).

• They remind believers that we, too, are called “living stones” being built into a spiritual house (1 Peter 2:5), reflecting His glory through excellence.

Cut to size and trimmed with saws inside and out

Every block was shaped before placement, testifying to meticulous craftsmanship.

• Precision fulfilled the earlier practice of silent quarry work so the construction site stayed calm (1 Kings 6:7).

• Inside and outside shaping suggests a consistency God expects in His people—integrity both publicly and privately (Matthew 23:26).

• The skill was God-given, like Bezalel’s in Exodus 31:3, reminding us that excellence in vocation honors the Lord.

From the foundation to the eaves

Solomon’s builders ensured quality from bottom to top.

• A sound foundation is vital (Isaiah 28:16); in the New Testament Christ is that foundation (1 Corinthians 3:11).

• The eaves mark the roofline, completing the structure. From start to finish, no corner was treated casually—mirroring Philippians 1:6, where God promises to complete what He begins in us.

And from the outside to the great courtyard

Not just the inner chambers but the entire complex—including public areas—shared the same high standard.

• The “great courtyard” (2 Chron 4:9) was where people gathered; God’s glory was to be evident in communal spaces, not hidden.

• Ezekiel’s vision of the outer court (Ezekiel 40:17-19) later echoes this theme: holiness is meant to permeate every sphere.

• Our witness matters “outside” as much as within church walls (Colossians 4:5-6).

summary

1 Kings 7:9 highlights the lavish resources, painstaking skill, comprehensive scope, and public testimony involved in Solomon’s construction. Literally laid with precious, perfectly cut stones from the foundation upward and across the entire compound, the verse showcases God’s worthiness of our very best and His desire for integrity throughout every layer of life.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - All these [i.e.buildings palaces] were of costly [or precious; cf. 1 Kings 5:31 [1 Kings 5:17] and vers. 10, 11] stones, according to the measures of hewed stones [lit., of squaring or hewing, same word in 1 Kings 5:31 (Hebr.) [1 Kings 5:17], 1 Kings 6:36, and Isaiah 9:9, etc. All the stones in these several buildings were shaped to certain specified dimensions], sawed with saws [גָּרַר is obviously an onomatopoetic word, like our saw. Gesenius cites σαίρω, serro, etc. The Egyptians, whose saws were apparently all single handed, do not seem to have applied this instrument to stone, but part of a double-handed saw was found at Nimrud (Layard, p. 195, and Dict. Bib., art. "Saw"). That saws were in common use and were made of iron is implied in 2 Samuel 12:31], within and without [It is not quite clear whether the meaning is that the two surfaces exposed to view, one within and the other without, the building were shaped with saws, or that the inner and hidden surface of the stone was thus smoothed as well as the exposed parts], even from the foundation unto the coping [or corbels. It is generally agreed (Gesen., Keil, Bight) that the reference is to the "projecting stones on which the beams rest," though Thenius would understand battlements (Deuteronomy 22:8) to be intended. But for these a different word is always used, and the LXX γεῖσος signifies the projection of the roof, not an erection upon it], and so on the outside toward the great court [i.e., the pavement of the court was of sawed stones (see ver. 12).]
