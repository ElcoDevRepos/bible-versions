When all the people saw this
This phrase refers to the dramatic event on Mount Carmel where Elijah called down fire from heaven to consume the sacrifice, altar, and even the water in the trench. This miraculous event was a direct challenge to the prophets of Baal and a demonstration of the power of the God of Israel. The people witnessing this were Israelites who had been wavering between worshiping Yahweh and Baal. The context is a time of spiritual apostasy in Israel, under the rule of King Ahab and Queen Jezebel, who promoted Baal worship. The event is a pivotal moment in the narrative, showcasing God's supremacy over false gods.

they fell facedown
Falling facedown is a posture of worship, submission, and reverence. In the ancient Near Eastern culture, this act signified acknowledgment of a superior power or authority. It is a physical expression of humility and recognition of divine intervention. This response is consistent with other biblical instances where individuals encounter the divine presence, such as in Genesis 17:3 when Abram fell facedown before God. It signifies the people's acknowledgment of God's undeniable power and presence.

and said, “The LORD, He is God! The LORD, He is God!”
This declaration is a profound confession of faith and recognition of Yahweh's sovereignty. The repetition emphasizes the sincerity and intensity of their acknowledgment. The name "LORD" (Yahweh) is the covenant name of God, highlighting His unique relationship with Israel. This moment is a turning point, as the people publicly renounce Baal and affirm their allegiance to Yahweh. It echoes the Shema in Deuteronomy 6:4, "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one," reinforcing the monotheistic faith of Israel. This confession can also be seen as a type of Christ, as it foreshadows the ultimate recognition of Jesus as Lord, as seen in Philippians 2:11, where every tongue will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord.

Teaching Points
Acknowledging God's Sovereignty
The people's response to God's demonstration of power reminds us to recognize and submit to God's authority in our lives.

The Power of Prayer
Elijah's prayer was simple yet powerful, showing that sincere prayer aligned with God's will can lead to miraculous outcomes.

Confronting Idolatry
Just as Elijah confronted the false prophets, we are called to confront and remove any idols in our lives that take the place of God.

Public Declaration of Faith
The Israelites' public acknowledgment of the LORD as God challenges us to boldly declare our faith in God before others.

God's Faithfulness
God’s response to Elijah’s prayer demonstrates His faithfulness to His people and His willingness to reveal Himself to those who seek Him.

What Does 1 Kings 18:39 Mean
When all the people saw this

- “This” points back to the fire of the LORD consuming Elijah’s sacrifice (1 Kings 18:38). The drastic, visible miracle leaves no room for doubt—God has answered.

- Throughout Scripture, seeing God’s power leads to conviction (Exodus 14:31 when Israel saw the Red Sea miracle; Luke 5:8 after the miraculous catch of fish).

- The people have witnessed a clear contrast: Baal’s silence versus the LORD’s fiery response, echoing Psalm 115:4–8 where idols are shown powerless.

They fell facedown

- Falling facedown reflects urgent submission and holy fear. The gesture mirrors Joshua 5:14 before the commander of the LORD’s army and Revelation 7:11 before God’s throne.

- Their posture acknowledges absolute authority. Like 2 Chronicles 7:3 when fire fell at Solomon’s Temple dedication, worship and awe accompany God’s undeniable act.

- The bodily response highlights that genuine recognition of God moves believers beyond mental assent to heartfelt, humble worship (Romans 12:1).

And said

- Words flow naturally from seeing and surrendering. Belief cannot stay silent (Romans 10:10).

- Confession here forms a corporate testimony, similar to Nehemiah 8:6 where the people respond, “Amen, Amen!” after the reading of the Law.

- Speaking aloud unites the community in shared allegiance, contrasting with their earlier wavering between two opinions (1 Kings 18:21).

“The LORD, He is God! The LORD, He is God!”

- The double declaration emphasizes certainty: Yahweh alone holds the title of true God (Deuteronomy 4:35).

- Repetition underscores repentance. The people abandon Baal and return to covenant loyalty (Deuteronomy 6:4–5; Hosea 14:2).

- This cry anticipates later confessions—Thomas’ “My Lord and my God!” in John 20:28 and the universal acknowledgment in Philippians 2:11 that “every tongue will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord.”

- The moment foreshadows the ultimate triumph of God over every false deity and ideology (Isaiah 45:22–23).

summary

Witnessing God’s unmistakable power led Israel from hesitation to wholehearted worship. Seeing the fire, they bowed low, voiced allegiance, and affirmed that the LORD alone is God. Their response models the proper human reaction to divine revelation: recognize His work, humble ourselves, and confess His exclusive sovereignty.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(39) They fell on their faces.--Exactly as in Leviticus 9:24, at the inauguration of the sacrifices of the new Tabernacle by the fire from heaven, with the characteristic addition of the cry, "Jehovah; He, and He only, is God."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 39. - And when all the people saw it, they fell on their faces [As in Leviticus 9:24; 2 Chronicles 7:3; cf. Numbers 22:31; Joshua 5:14; Revelation 11:16. They recognized in the fire, that is to say, the token of the Divine Presence]: and they said, The Lord [Jehovah. The connexion of this verse with the three verses preceding is obscured by our translation], he is the God; the Lord, he is the God. [The echo of ver. 24. The Hebrew words are the same. Stanley remarks that it is as if (by a slight inversion) they turned "the name of the prophet himself into a war-cry, 'Eli-Jah-hu.'"]
