For all the promises of God
This phrase refers to the numerous promises found throughout the Old Testament and New Testament. These promises include covenants made with figures like Abraham, Moses, and David, as well as prophecies concerning the coming Messiah. The promises encompass blessings, redemption, and eternal life. They are foundational to the faith and hope of believers, demonstrating God's faithfulness and unchanging nature.

are “Yes” in Christ.
In Christ, all of God's promises find their fulfillment and affirmation. Jesus is the embodiment of God's faithfulness, as He fulfills the law and the prophets (Matthew 5:17). This phrase underscores the belief that Jesus is the central figure in God's redemptive plan, fulfilling prophecies such as those found in Isaiah 53 and Psalm 22. Christ's life, death, and resurrection confirm God's promises of salvation and reconciliation.

And so through Him,
This indicates that it is only through Jesus Christ that believers have access to the promises of God. Jesus serves as the mediator between God and humanity (1 Timothy 2:5), and it is through His sacrifice and resurrection that believers are able to partake in the divine promises. This highlights the necessity of faith in Christ for salvation and the reception of God's promises.

our “Amen” is spoken
"Amen" is a Hebrew word meaning "truly" or "so be it," used to express agreement and affirmation. In this context, it signifies the believer's response to God's promises, acknowledging their truth and reliability. The use of "Amen" reflects a deep-seated faith and trust in God's word, as seen in its frequent use in both Jewish and Christian liturgies.

to the glory of God.
The ultimate purpose of the fulfillment of God's promises in Christ is to bring glory to God. This reflects the biblical theme that all things are to be done for God's glory (1 Corinthians 10:31). The realization of God's promises through Christ leads to worship and praise, as believers recognize His sovereignty, grace, and love. This phrase emphasizes that the fulfillment of God's promises is not only for the benefit of humanity but also for the exaltation of God's name.

Teaching Points
The Certainty of God's Promises
God's promises are unwavering and find their fulfillment in Jesus Christ. Believers can trust in the reliability of God's word.

Christ as the Fulfillment
Jesus is the embodiment of all God's promises. Understanding His role helps believers grasp the depth of God's faithfulness.

The Role of 'Amen'
Saying "Amen" is an affirmation of faith and agreement with God's promises. It is a declaration of trust and submission to His will.

Living for God's Glory
Our response to God's promises should be to live in a way that glorifies Him, reflecting His faithfulness in our lives.

Encouragement in Trials
In times of difficulty, remembering that God's promises are 'Yes' in Christ provides hope and strength to persevere.

What Does 2 Corinthians 1:20 Mean
For all the promises of God

• Scripture presents God as a promise-maker who never fails (Joshua 21:45; 1 Kings 8:56).

• From the first gospel hint in Genesis 3:15 to the covenant with Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) and the promise of a new heart (Ezekiel 36:26-27), every commitment stands intact.

• Paul reminds the Corinthians that every single promise—without exception—still rests on God’s integrity, not human merit (Romans 3:3-4).

are “Yes” in Christ

• Jesus is the fulfillment and guarantee of every divine pledge (Luke 24:44; Romans 15:8).

• His sinless life, substitutionary death, and bodily resurrection confirm that God’s word cannot be broken (John 19:30; Acts 13:32-33).

• Because Christ embodies God’s faithfulness (Revelation 19:11), the answer to the question “Will God keep His word?” is always “Yes” when viewed through Him (Hebrews 10:23).

And so through Him

• Access to the promises flows only “through Him” (John 14:6; Acts 4:12).

• Union with Christ by faith grafts believers into the covenant blessings (Galatians 3:14, 29; Ephesians 1:3).

• Even our ability to approach the Father rests on Jesus’ mediating work (Ephesians 2:18; Hebrews 4:14-16).

our “Amen” is spoken

• “Amen” means “so be it,” an affirmation of trust (Nehemiah 8:6).

• Corporate worship echoes this affirmation—believers say “Amen” to preached truth and shared prayer (1 Corinthians 14:16; Revelation 7:12).

• By voicing “Amen,” we personally appropriate God’s promises, declaring confidence that what He has said is already settled (2 Timothy 2:13).

to the glory of God

• God’s faithfulness magnified in Christ turns every fulfilled promise into praise (Romans 11:36; 1 Peter 4:11).

• When believers respond with “Amen,” the spotlight remains on God, not on the recipients (Ephesians 1:14).

• Thus, daily reliance on His promises becomes a practical way to “do all to the glory of God” (1 Corinthians 10:31).

summary

Every promise God has ever uttered is fully affirmed in Jesus Christ. Because He is the flawless “Yes,” believers can confidently add their heartfelt “Amen,” knowing that their agreement flows through Christ’s mediation and redounds to God’s glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) All the promises of God . . .--Literally, as many as are the promises of God. Many of the better MSS. give a different reading: "In him is the Yea, wherefore also by him is the Amen to God for glory by our means." The thought in either case is the same. The promises of God have been fulfilled and ratified in Christ. He was, as it were, a living incarnate "Amen" to those promises. Comp. St. John's use of the word Amen as a name of Christ, the "faithful and true witness" (Revelation 3:14). The words "by us" are determined by the context as referring to the preacher rather than to the hearers of the Word.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - For all the promises of God in him are yea; rather, For so many as be the promises of God, in him is the yea. All the promises of God find in him their unchangeable fulfilment. He was "a minister to confirm the promises" alike to the Jews and the Gentiles (Romans 15:8, 9); and "the premise of the eternal inheritance" can only be fulfilled in him (Hebrews 9:15). And in him Amen. The true reading is," Wherefore by him also is the Amen to God, uttered by us to his glory" (א, A, B, C, F, G, etc.). In Christ is the "yea" of immutable promise and absolute fulfilment; the Church utters the "Amen" of perfect faith and grateful adoration. Here, as in 1 Corinthians 14:16, we have a proof of the ancientness of the custom by which the congregation utters the "Amen" at the end of praise and prayer. But as the "yea" is in Christ, so it is only through him that we can receive the grace to utter aright the "Amen" to the glory of God.
