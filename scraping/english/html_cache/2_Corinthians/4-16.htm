Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Therefore we do not lose heart.
This phrase serves as a conclusion to the preceding discussion about the trials and tribulations faced by Paul and his companions. Despite the hardships, Paul emphasizes perseverance and hope. The use of "therefore" connects this statement to the previous verses where Paul discusses the light of the gospel and the power of God sustaining them. This reflects a common biblical theme of enduring faith, as seen in Hebrews 12:3, where believers are encouraged not to grow weary or lose heart.

Though our outer self is wasting away,
Paul acknowledges the physical decline and suffering that come with life and ministry. This reflects the human condition post-Fall, as described in Genesis 3, where mortality and decay entered the world. The "outer self" refers to the physical body, which is subject to aging, illness, and persecution. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative of the temporary nature of earthly life, as seen in Ecclesiastes 12:1-7, which poetically describes the aging process.

yet our inner self is being renewed day by day.
In contrast to the physical decline, Paul speaks of a spiritual renewal that occurs continually. This renewal is a work of the Holy Spirit, as mentioned in Titus 3:5, where the Spirit regenerates and renews believers. The "inner self" refers to the spiritual aspect of a person, which grows stronger through faith and communion with God. This daily renewal is a process of sanctification, aligning with Romans 12:2, which calls believers to be transformed by the renewing of their minds. This phrase underscores the hope and strength found in a relationship with Christ, who is the source of spiritual life and renewal.

What Does 2 Corinthians 4:16 Mean
Therefore

Paul is drawing a line of logic from what he has just said about the resurrection power of Jesus working in frail human vessels (2 Corinthians 4:14-15). Because God “raised the Lord Jesus” and “will also raise us,” everything that happens to us serves a larger, eternal purpose. • This outlook mirrors Romans 8:28, where God works all things for good. • It echoes 1 Peter 1:3-5, where a “living hope” steadies believers in trials. The “therefore” invites us to view our circumstances through the unshakable certainty of future glory.

we do not lose heart

“So we do not lose heart”. Paul repeats this phrase from 2 Corinthians 4:1, bookending the section. Confidence in Christ keeps discouragement at bay. • Galatians 6:9 urges, “Let us not grow weary in well-doing.” • Hebrews 12:3 calls us to “consider Him who endured such hostility… so that you will not grow weary and lose heart.” Refusing to despair is not a grin-and-bear-it mindset; it is faith anchored in God’s character and promises.

Though our outer self is wasting away

The apostle openly acknowledges physical decline: injuries, sickness, aging, persecution (2 Corinthians 11:23-28). • Psalm 73:26: “My flesh and my heart may fail.” • Ecclesiastes 12:1-7 paints aging in vivid detail. • 2 Corinthians 5:1-4 likens the body to a temporary tent that will one day be replaced by an eternal house. Honesty about frailty is not unbelief; it keeps us realistic about life in a fallen world.

yet our inner self is being renewed day by day

While the body deteriorates, the spirit is continually refreshed by the Holy Spirit. • Ephesians 3:16 speaks of being “strengthened with power through His Spirit in your inner being.” • Colossians 3:10 describes believers as “being renewed in knowledge according to the image of their Creator.” • Romans 12:2 commands, “Be transformed by the renewing of your mind.” • Lamentations 3:22-23 reminds us, “His mercies are new every morning.” The renewal is “day by day,” indicating a steady, ongoing work; God supplies fresh grace for each 24-hour slice of life.

summary

Paul urges believers to view life through an eternal lens: because God will raise us with Jesus, discouragement need not dominate us. The body can weaken, but the spirit can flourish through daily infusions of divine mercy and power. Knowing this, we face trials realistically yet confidently, certain that present decay is giving way to everlasting glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) For which cause we faint not.--Ho returns, after a long digression, to the assertion with which 2 Corinthians 4 had opened, but in repeating the words he enters once again on the same line of thought, but under a different succession of imagery. The "outward man," the material framework of the body, is undergoing a gradual process of decay, but the "inward man," the higher spiritual life, is "day by day" passing through successive stages of renewal, gaining fresh energies. This verb also, and its derivative "renewal," are specially characteristic of St. Paul. (Comp. Romans 12:2; Colossians 3:10; Titus 3:5.) The verb in Ephesians 4:23, though not the same, is equivalent in meaning.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 16-18. - The Christian minister is upheld by hope. Verse 16. - Therefore. Knowing that our daily death is the pathway to eternal life (ver. 14). We faint not (see ver. 1). Though; rather, even if. Our outward man. Our life in its human and corporeal conditions. The inward man. Namely, our moral and spiritual being, that "new man which is renewed in knowledge after the image of him that created him" (Colossians 3:10). Is renewed; literally, is being renewed; i.e. by faith and hope. Day by day. The Greek phrase is not classical, but is a reminiscence of the Hebrew.
