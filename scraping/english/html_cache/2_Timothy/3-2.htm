Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For men will be lovers of themselves
This phrase highlights a self-centered attitude that contrasts with the biblical call to love God and others (Matthew 22:37-39). In the Greco-Roman world, self-love was often seen as a virtue, but Scripture warns against it as it leads to pride and a lack of humility (Philippians 2:3-4). This self-love is a sign of the last days, indicating a departure from godly values.

lovers of money
The love of money is a root of all kinds of evil (1 Timothy 6:10). In the ancient world, wealth was often equated with divine favor, yet Jesus taught that one cannot serve both God and money (Matthew 6:24). This phrase warns against materialism and greed, which can lead to corruption and moral decay.

boastful
Boasting is condemned throughout Scripture as it reflects a heart that seeks glory for itself rather than for God (Jeremiah 9:23-24). In the cultural context of the time, boasting was common among those seeking honor and status. However, true honor comes from humility and reliance on God (James 4:6).

arrogant
Arrogance is an inflated sense of one's own importance, often leading to a disregard for others. The Bible consistently warns against pride, which precedes destruction (Proverbs 16:18). Arrogance is the opposite of the humility exemplified by Christ (Philippians 2:5-8).

abusive
Abuse, whether verbal or physical, is a manifestation of a heart that lacks love and respect for others. In a society where power dynamics often led to oppression, the call to love one's neighbor (Leviticus 19:18) stands in stark contrast. Abuse is a violation of the command to treat others with kindness and dignity.

disobedient to their parents
Disobedience to parents reflects a breakdown in family structure, which is foundational to societal stability. The commandment to honor one's father and mother (Exodus 20:12) is the first with a promise, emphasizing its importance. In biblical times, family was central to community life, and disobedience was seen as a serious offense.

ungrateful
Ingratitude is a failure to recognize and appreciate the blessings and grace of God. The Israelites' journey in the wilderness is a prime example of ingratitude leading to rebellion (Numbers 14:2-4). Gratitude is a key aspect of worship and acknowledgment of God's provision (1 Thessalonians 5:18).

unholy
Holiness is a central theme in Scripture, calling believers to be set apart for God (1 Peter 1:15-16). Unholiness represents a life that is contrary to God's nature and commands. In the cultural context, holiness was often associated with ritual purity, but the New Testament emphasizes moral and spiritual purity.

Teaching Points
Self-Examination
Believers should regularly examine their lives for signs of self-centeredness and other sinful behaviors listed in this verse.

Cultural Awareness
Recognize the influence of cultural values that promote self-love and materialism, and actively resist conforming to them.

Parental Honor
Emphasize the biblical mandate to honor and obey parents, understanding it as a reflection of our relationship with God.

Gratitude and Holiness
Cultivate a heart of gratitude and pursue holiness, recognizing these as antidotes to the ungrateful and unholy attitudes described.

Community Accountability
Engage in Christian community for mutual encouragement and accountability in living out biblical values.

What Does 2 Timothy 3:2 Mean
Lovers of themselves

Paul opens the list with self-love because it undercuts the two great commands to love God and neighbor. Philippians 2:3 urges, “Do nothing out of selfish ambition or empty pride, but in humility consider others more important than yourselves”. When people place self on the throne, life becomes ordered around personal comfort, image, and agenda rather than around Christ (Mark 12:30-31).

• Symptoms: constant pursuit of personal praise, inability to serve without recognition, disdain for accountability

• Remedy: daily submission to Christ’s Lordship, deliberate acts of hidden service, meditation on the cross that re-centers the heart on sacrificial love

Lovers of money

Next comes the clutching of wealth. “For the love of money is the root of all kinds of evil” (1 Timothy 6:10). Money itself is neutral; affection for it is the snare. Matthew 6:24 warns that devotion to riches sets one at odds with God.

• Signs: career choices driven only by income, stinginess toward gospel work, envy of others’ prosperity

• Correction: cultivate generosity, embrace contentment (Hebrews 13:5), remember that true riches are eternal

Boastful

Boasting flows naturally from self-worship and money-worship. Jeremiah 9:23 counsels, “Let not the wise man boast in his wisdom… but let him who boasts boast in this, that he understands and knows Me”. 1 Corinthians 1:31 echoes the same call.

• Expressions: exaggerating achievements, name-dropping, seeking applause

• Gospel answer: boast in the Lord’s accomplishments, celebrate others, practice quiet faithfulness

Arrogant

Arrogance takes boasting a step deeper, reflecting an inflated view of self over God and others. Proverbs 16:5 states, “Everyone who is proud in heart is detestable to the LORD”. James 4:6 adds, “God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.”

• Traits: unwillingness to admit wrong, disdain for counsel, entitlement mentality

• Antidote: remember divine holiness, confess sin quickly, pursue humility through service

Abusive

Verbal or physical harshness reveals hearts untethered from God’s love. Ephesians 4:31 commands, “Get rid of all bitterness, rage and anger, outcry and slander, along with every form of malice”. Proverbs 12:18 notes that reckless words pierce like a sword.

• Manifestations: cutting sarcasm, intimidation, domestic violence, online hostility

• Healing path: repentance, Spirit-filled self-control (Galatians 5:22-23), valuing every person as an image-bearer

Disobedient to their parents

Family order reflects God’s design. “Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right” (Ephesians 6:1). Disregard for parents previews a wider rebellion against authority.

• Outcomes: fractured homes, loss of generational wisdom, social instability

• Restoration: honor parents with respect and care (Deuteronomy 5:16), parents model godliness to invite willing obedience

Ungrateful

Thanklessness blinds hearts to God’s daily mercies. Romans 1:21 notes that failure to glorify God or give thanks darkens understanding. 1 Thessalonians 5:18 directs, “Give thanks in every circumstance”.

• Indicators: chronic complaining, entitlement, inability to celebrate others’ blessings

• Practice: keep a gratitude journal, voice thanks aloud, recount answered prayers

Unholy

The list ends with a general absence of reverence. God calls His people to reflect His character: “Be holy, because I am holy” (1 Peter 1:16). Hebrews 12:14 urges pursuit of holiness without which no one will see the Lord.

• Features: casual attitude toward sin, blurred moral boundaries, disregard for worship

• Response: separation from sinful practices, renewal of mind through Scripture, fellowship with believers who spur holiness

summary

2 Timothy 3:2 sketches a culture spiraling away from God: self-focused, money-driven, proud, cruel, authority-rejecting, thankless, and irreverent. Each trait traces back to hearts disconnected from the living Christ. Believers stand apart by loving God above self, stewarding resources, walking humbly, speaking life, honoring family, giving thanks, and pursuing holiness through the power of the Holy Spirit.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) For men shall be lovers of their own selves.--Hofmann and others have attempted to portion out these vices into groups. But any such effort seems artificial. A certain connection seems to exist in some part; but when pressed to preserve the groups, a strained meaning has to be given to some of the terms. It seems, therefore, best simply to understand the catalogue as representing the various more prominent vices which appeared on the surface of Christian society, and threatened the very existence of the Church, even in those early times when Timothy ruled over the congregations of Christians at Ephesus. Hofmann, however, divides the catalogue contained in 2Timothy 3:2-4 into three groups, consisting of five, six, and seven terms, respectively.

Lovers of their own selves.--Selfishness well heads the dreary list. It is the true root of all sin.

Covetous.--More accurately rendered, lovers of money. This "love of money" has been happily termed "the daughter of selfishness."

Boasters.--Those who arrogate to themselves honour which does not fairly belong to them.

Proud.--These are they who contemptuously look down on others beneath them, either in social position or wealth, or perhaps in natural gifts. The Latin, ostentatio, represents the vice which affects the first of these classes--"the boasters;" and superbia, that which affects the second class--"the proud."

Blasphemers.--The two vices just mentioned refer to man's conduct to his brother man; this alludes to his behaviour towards his God. The pride with which he looks down on his fellows develops itself into insolence in thought, if not in word, towards his God: and this is termed blasphemy.

Disobedient to parents.--The blasphemer of the Father which is in heaven is only too likely to train up little ones who, in their turn, will display a disobedience and disrespect of their earthly parents. The home life of the man who chooses not to know God in his heart will too easily reflect his evil thoughts and senseless pride.

Unthankful.--Or, ungrateful. The children who begin life with disobedience to their parents, with rare exceptions, are ungrateful to all others who may show them kindness in their life journey.

Unholy.--Unholy through their want of inward purity. (See 1Timothy 1:9.) . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - Self for their own selves, A.V.; lovers of money for covetous, A.V.; boastful for boasters, A.V.; haughty for proud, A.V.; railers for blasphemers, A.V. Men (οἱἄνθρωποι); men in general, the bulk of men in the Church; for he is speaking, not of the world at large, but of professing Christians. Lovers of self (φίλαυτοι); only here in the New Testament, and not found in the LXX.; but used by Aristotle in a striking passage (quoted by Alford), where he distinguishes those who are φίλαυτοι in a good sense, and those who are justly blamed for being φίλαυτοι, i.e. selfish and greedy. The Christian character is exactly the opposite (see 1 Corinthians 10:24; 1 Corinthians 13:5). Lovers of money (φιλάργυροι); elsewhere in the New Testament only in Luke 16:14, though not uncommon in classical Greek; φιλαργυρία is found in 1 Timothy 6:10. Boastful (ἀλάζονες); as Romans 1:30, and in classical Greek. It the derivation of the word is ἄλη, wandering, we may compare the περιερχόμενοι of Acts 9:13, "vagabond Jews." Such vagabonds were usually boasters. Hence ἀλαζών came to mean "a boaster." Haughty, railers. Υπερηφανία and βλασφημία are coupled together in Mark 7:22; and ὑπερηφάνους and ἀλάζονας in Romans 1:30. In the New Testament βλάσφημος and βλασφημία are most commonly used of evil speaking against God and holy things; but not always (see Ephesians 4:31; Colossians 3:8; 1 Timothy 6:4). Here apparently it means generally "evil speakers." Unthankful (ἀχάριστοι); as Luke 6:35. Found occasionally in the LXX., and common in classical Greek. The ingratitude which they showed to their parents was a part of their general character. We ought to take special note of this passive sin - the not being thankful for good received from God and man. Unholy (ἀνόσιοι); as 1 Timothy 1:9 (where see note).
