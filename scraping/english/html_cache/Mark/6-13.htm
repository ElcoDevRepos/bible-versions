They also drove out many demons
This phrase highlights the authority given to the disciples by Jesus to cast out demons. In the context of the Gospels, demon possession was a significant issue, and Jesus frequently demonstrated His power over evil spirits. The disciples' ability to drive out demons is a testament to the authority and power of Jesus being extended to His followers. This act also fulfills the prophecy of the Messiah bringing liberation and freedom from spiritual bondage, as seen in Isaiah 61:1. The casting out of demons signifies the in-breaking of God's kingdom and the defeat of Satan's power.

and healed many of the sick
Healing the sick was a central aspect of Jesus' ministry, demonstrating His compassion and divine authority. The disciples' ability to heal in Jesus' name shows the continuation of His work and the validation of their mission. This healing ministry fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah, such as Isaiah 53:4-5, which speaks of the suffering servant bearing our sicknesses. The act of healing also serves as a sign of the coming kingdom of God, where there will be no more sickness or pain.

anointing them with oil
Anointing with oil has historical and cultural significance in Jewish tradition. Oil was used for medicinal purposes, as seen in the parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10:34), and for religious rituals, symbolizing the presence and blessing of God. In this context, the use of oil by the disciples may have served as a tangible expression of God's healing power and the Holy Spirit's presence. James 5:14-15 later instructs the church to anoint the sick with oil in the name of the Lord, indicating a continued practice of seeking divine healing through faith and prayer.

Persons / Places / Events

2. Demons
Spiritual beings opposed to God, often causing physical and spiritual harm to individuals. The disciples were given authority by Jesus to cast them out.

3. The Sick
Individuals suffering from various physical ailments. The disciples ministered to them through healing.

4. Anointing with Oil
A practice used by the disciples as a symbol of healing and consecration. It reflects a tradition rooted in Jewish customs and signifies the presence and power of God.

5. The Region of Galilee
The broader context of this passage is Jesus' ministry in Galilee, where He sent out the disciples to extend His work.
Teaching Points
Authority in Christ
The disciples' ability to cast out demons and heal the sick was not of their own power but through the authority given by Jesus. Believers today are called to operate under Christ's authority in their spiritual endeavors.

The Role of Faith and Obedience
The disciples' actions were a direct result of their obedience to Jesus' command. Our faith is demonstrated through obedience to God's Word and His calling in our lives.

Symbolism of Anointing Oil
Anointing with oil is a physical act that symbolizes spiritual truths, such as healing, consecration, and the Holy Spirit's presence. It reminds us of the importance of both physical and spiritual care.

Compassionate Ministry
The disciples' ministry to the sick and demon-possessed reflects Jesus' compassion. As followers of Christ, we are called to show compassion and care for those who are suffering.

Spiritual Warfare
The casting out of demons highlights the reality of spiritual warfare. Christians are called to be vigilant and equipped with the armor of God to stand against spiritual forces.

What Does Mark 6:13 Mean
They also drove out many demons

“They also drove out many demons” (Mark 6:13).

• Jesus had just given the Twelve “authority over impure spirits” (Mark 6:7). That authority was real and effective; the disciples exercised it immediately.

• The phrase “many demons” highlights the prevalence of spiritual bondage and the sufficiency of Christ’s delegated power. Compare Mark 1:34 and Luke 10:17, where evil spirits submit in His name.

• Deliverance ministry here is not theatrics; it is evidence that the kingdom of God has come near (Luke 11:20).

• Acts 8:7 and Acts 16:18 show this same authority continuing in the early church, affirming that Christ’s power over darkness did not cease with the Twelve.

and healed many of the sick

“…and healed many of the sick” (Mark 6:13).

• Physical healing accompanies spiritual deliverance, demonstrating the holistic salvation Jesus brings (Isaiah 53:4–5; Matthew 8:16–17).

• The disciples mirror their Master, who “healed all who were ill” (Matthew 12:15).

• In Mark 5:34 Jesus tells the woman, “Your faith has healed you.” Likewise, the disciples’ ministry called people to faith while confirming God’s compassion.

• Acts 3:6–8 continues this pattern, showing that in Jesus’ name cripples walk. Healing, then, is not an optional extra but a tangible sign that the gospel addresses body and soul.

anointing them with oil

“…anointing them with oil” (Mark 6:13).

• Oil served practical, medicinal purposes (Luke 10:34) and symbolized the Spirit’s presence and consecration (1 Samuel 16:13). Here it functions as a visible act pointing to God’s invisible grace.

• The practice foreshadows James 5:14: “Is any one of you sick? He should call the elders of the church to pray over him and anoint him with oil in the name of the Lord.”

• The disciples did not trust in oil itself; they anointed “in the name of the Lord,” relying on His authority.

• This tangible sign helps sufferers receive ministry with expectancy, much as laying on of hands does (Mark 16:18; Acts 28:8).

summary

Mark 6:13 shows the Twelve acting under Jesus’ delegated authority, casting out demons, healing the sick, and using oil as a meaningful sign. Together these acts authenticate the gospel, portray Christ’s compassion, and model ongoing church ministry that addresses both spiritual and physical needs in His name.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Anointed with oil.--St. Mark is the only Evangelist who mentions this as the common practice of the disciples, but we learn from James 5:14 that it was afterwards in use, at least, in the churches of Jerusalem and other Jewish communities. It was partly analogous to our Lord's treatment of the blind and deaf (Mark 7:33; Mark 8:23; John 9:6), i.e., it was an outward sign showing the wish to heal, and therefore a help to faith; but as the use of oil was more distinctly that of an agent recognised as remedial in the popular therapeutics of the time, it had also the character of uniting (and devout minds have since so regarded it) the use of natural outward means of healing with prayer for the divine blessing. It need scarcely be said that it had not the slightest affinity with the mediaeval so-called sacrament of extreme unction, which, though it may still retain, in theory, a partial secondary connection with the cure of the diseases of the body, is practically never administered till all hope of cure is abandoned. The development of the latter aspect of the usage was obviously the after-growth of a later time, when the miraculous gift of healing was withdrawn, and when it became necessary to devise a theory for the retention of the practice.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - And anointed with oil many that were sick, and healed them. It is hardly possible to separate this from the reference to the use of oil for the sick, in James 5:14. Unction was employed extensively in ancient times for medicinal purposes. It is recorded of Herod the Great by Josephus ('Antiq.,' 17:6, 5) that in one of his sicknesses he was "immersed in a bath full of oil," from which he is said to have derived much benefit. The apostles used it, no doubt not only on account of its supposed remedial virtues, but also as an outward and visible sign that the healing was effected by their instrumentality in the name of Christ, and perhaps also because the oil itself was significant of God's mercy, of spiritual comfort and joy" the oil of gladness." Neither this passage nor that in St. James can properly be adduced to support the ceremony of "extreme unction;" for in both these cases the result was that the sick were restored to health. The so-called sacrament of" extreme unction "is administered immediately before death, when the sick person is in articulo morris.
