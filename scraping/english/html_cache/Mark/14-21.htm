The Son of Man will go just as it is written about Him
This phrase refers to Jesus' self-identification as the "Son of Man," a title with deep roots in Jewish apocalyptic literature, particularly in Daniel 7:13-14, where the Son of Man is depicted as a heavenly figure given authority and dominion. Jesus uses this title to emphasize both His humanity and His divine mission. The phrase "just as it is written about Him" indicates the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies concerning the Messiah's suffering and death, such as Isaiah 53 and Psalm 22. These prophecies highlight the predetermined plan of God for redemption through the suffering of the Messiah.

but woe to that man by whom He is betrayed!
This part of the verse introduces a solemn warning to Judas Iscariot, the disciple who would betray Jesus. The term "woe" is a pronouncement of judgment and sorrow, often used by prophets in the Old Testament to declare divine displeasure and impending doom (e.g., Isaiah 5:8-23). The betrayal by Judas is a pivotal event in the Passion narrative, fulfilling prophecies like Psalm 41:9, which speaks of betrayal by a close friend. Despite the necessity of the betrayal for the fulfillment of God's redemptive plan, Judas's personal responsibility and culpability are underscored.

It would be better for him if he had not been born.”
This hyperbolic statement emphasizes the gravity of Judas's actions and the severe consequences of his betrayal. It reflects the ultimate tragedy of rejecting and betraying the Messiah, leading to eternal separation from God. The phrase underscores the seriousness of sin and the eternal implications of one's choices. In the broader biblical context, it serves as a warning to all about the consequences of turning away from God's will and the importance of faithfulness to Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Son of Man
This title refers to Jesus Christ, emphasizing His role as the Messiah and His fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies.

2. The Betrayer
This refers to Judas Iscariot, one of the twelve disciples, who would betray Jesus to the religious authorities.

3. The Event of Betrayal
This is the act of Judas handing Jesus over to the chief priests and scribes, leading to His arrest and crucifixion.
Teaching Points
The Sovereignty of God
Despite the betrayal, God's plan for redemption through Jesus was not thwarted. This teaches us about God's ultimate control over human history.

The Seriousness of Sin
Judas' betrayal serves as a stark reminder of the destructive nature of sin and the severe consequences it can have.

The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Jesus' reference to Scripture being fulfilled underscores the reliability and divine inspiration of the Bible.

Personal Responsibility
While God's plan is sovereign, individuals are still responsible for their actions, as seen in Judas' choice to betray Jesus.

The Cost of Discipleship
Following Jesus requires commitment and integrity, contrasting Judas' failure to remain faithful.

What Does Mark 14:21 Mean
The Son of Man

• Jesus applies Daniel 7:13–14 to Himself, declaring His divine authority and messianic identity.

• The title highlights both His humanity (Mark 10:45) and His exalted role as Judge (John 5:27).

• In Mark’s Gospel, “Son of Man” consistently points to the One who must suffer, die, and rise (Mark 8:31; 9:31; 10:33–34).

Will go

• “Will go” speaks of His upcoming departure through death, resurrection, and ascension (John 13:1; Acts 1:9).

• Jesus is not trapped by events; He voluntarily walks the path set before Him (John 10:18).

• Even in betrayal, He remains sovereign, moving steadily toward the cross (Luke 9:51).

Just as it is written about Him

• Scripture foretold the Messiah’s sufferings: Psalm 22; Isaiah 53; Zechariah 12:10.

• Specific prophecies include betrayal for thirty pieces of silver (Zechariah 11:12–13; Matthew 26:14–16).

• Jesus’ confidence in the written Word underscores its absolute reliability (Luke 24:25–27, 44).

But woe to that man

• “Woe” is a lament of judgment, not mere sorrow (Isaiah 5:20; Matthew 23:13).

• Jesus warns that human responsibility remains, even when God’s plan is being fulfilled (Acts 2:23).

• Divine sovereignty never excuses human sin; it exposes it (Romans 9:19–20).

By whom He is betrayed

• Judas fulfills Psalm 41:9—“Even my close friend… has lifted up his heel against me.”

• Betrayal from within intensifies the injustice of the cross (John 13:18–27).

• The kiss in Gethsemane (Mark 14:45) epitomizes hypocrisy and hardened unbelief.

It would be better for him if he had not been born

• Jesus issues a sober verdict on Judas’s eternal fate (Hebrews 10:29–31).

• The statement implies conscious, lasting punishment (Matthew 25:46; Revelation 20:14–15).

• The gravity of rejecting Christ surpasses all earthly tragedy (John 17:12; Acts 1:25).

summary

Mark 14:21 unites God’s unbreakable plan with human accountability. The Son of Man moves unfailingly toward the cross exactly as Scripture promised. Yet Judas’s freely chosen betrayal incurs severe judgment, underscoring both the trustworthiness of God’s Word and the sobering cost of opposing His Son.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Good were it for that man.--St. Mark, it will be noted, omits the fact recorded by St. Matthew, that the last "Is it I?" was uttered by the Traitor.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - For the Son of man goeth (ὑπάγει) - goeth, departeth from this mortal scene: the reference is, of course, to his death - even as it is written of him; as, for example, in Psalm 22 and Isaiah 41 It was foreordained by God that he was to suffer as a victim for the sins of the whole world. But this predestined purpose of God did not make the guilt any the less of those who brought the Savior to his cross. Good were it for that man if he had not been born. The Greek is καλὸν η΅ν αὐτῷ εἰ οὐκ ἐγεννήθη ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐκεῖνος: literally, good were it for him, if that man had not been born. Better not to have lived at all than to have lived and died ill. Existence is no blessing, but a curse, to him who consciously and wilfully defeats the purpose of his existence. St. Matthew (Matthew 26:25) here introduces Judas as asking the question, "Is it I, Rabbi?" And our Lord answers him affirmatively, "Thou hast said." This was probably said in a low voice. Had it been said so as to be heard by others, such as Peter and John, they might have risen at once to inflict summary vengeance upon the apostate traitor.
