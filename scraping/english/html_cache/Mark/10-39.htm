Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“We can,” the brothers answered.
James and John, the sons of Zebedee, respond confidently to Jesus' question about their ability to share in His suffering. This reflects their ambition and perhaps a misunderstanding of the nature of Jesus' mission. Their response is reminiscent of Peter's bold declarations of loyalty, highlighting a common theme of overconfidence among the disciples. This moment underscores the human tendency to underestimate the cost of discipleship and the depth of commitment required to follow Christ.

“You will drink the cup that I drink,” Jesus said,
The "cup" is a metaphor for suffering and divine wrath, often used in the Old Testament (e.g., Psalm 75:8, Isaiah 51:17). Jesus refers to His impending suffering and crucifixion. By stating that James and John will also drink this cup, Jesus prophesies their future trials and martyrdom. James would later be martyred by Herod Agrippa (Acts 12:2), and John would face persecution and exile. This phrase emphasizes the cost of following Jesus and the shared destiny of suffering for His sake.

“and you will be baptized with the baptism that I undergo.”
The term "baptism" here symbolizes being overwhelmed by suffering, akin to being submerged in water. Jesus speaks of His impending death and the overwhelming nature of His sacrifice. This baptism is not one of water but of suffering and death, aligning with the imagery of baptism as a form of identification with Christ's death and resurrection (Romans 6:3-4). The disciples' future experiences would mirror this, as they would face persecution and trials for their faith. This phrase highlights the theme of identification with Christ in His sufferings, a key aspect of Christian discipleship.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is responding to the request of James and John, the sons of Zebedee, regarding positions of honor in His kingdom.

2. James and John
The sons of Zebedee, also known as the "Sons of Thunder," who are seeking positions of prominence in Jesus' kingdom.

3. The Cup and Baptism
Metaphors used by Jesus to describe the suffering and trials He will endure, which James and John will also experience.

4. The Disciples
The broader group of Jesus' followers who are present and later react to the request of James and John.

5. Jerusalem
The city where Jesus is heading, and where His ultimate suffering, death, and resurrection will occur.
Teaching Points
Understanding the Cost of Discipleship
Following Jesus involves sharing in His sufferings. The "cup" and "baptism" symbolize the trials and sacrifices inherent in true discipleship.

The Nature of True Greatness
Jesus teaches that greatness in His kingdom is not about positions of power but about serving others and being willing to suffer for the sake of the Gospel.

The Role of Suffering in the Christian Life
Suffering is not only expected but is a means through which believers are conformed to the image of Christ. It is a participation in His redemptive work.

Faithfulness in Trials
Like James and John, believers are called to remain faithful through trials, trusting in God's purpose and strength.

The Promise of Resurrection
Just as Jesus' suffering led to His resurrection, believers can have hope that their trials will lead to spiritual growth and eternal life.

What Does Mark 10:39 Mean
“We can,” the brothers answered.

• James and John, stirred by their desire for honor (Mark 10:35-37), confidently affirm they are able to follow Jesus wherever He leads.

• Their words show zeal, yet also a lack of understanding about the cost of discipleship—much like Peter’s pledge in John 13:37 and the disciples’ earlier assertion in Matthew 26:35.

• Scripture warns that self-assurance, when detached from dependence on God, is unreliable (1 Corinthians 10:12).

• Still, Jesus receives their pledge, knowing He will shape their immature courage into steadfast faith (Philippians 1:6).

“You will drink the cup that I drink,”

• In Scripture, “cup” often pictures a determined portion of suffering or judgment (Psalm 75:8; Isaiah 51:17).

• Jesus’ own cup is the path to the cross—voluntary, substitutionary suffering for sinners (Mark 14:36; John 18:11).

• He foretells that James and John will share in His sufferings:

– James will be executed by Herod’s sword (Acts 12:2).

– John will endure exile on Patmos for the testimony of Jesus (Revelation 1:9).

• This promise underlines the pattern of Christian life: “If we suffer with Him, we will also be glorified with Him” (Romans 8:17; 2 Timothy 3:12).

“and you will be baptized with the baptism that I undergo.”

• Jesus is speaking of an overwhelming immersion into suffering (Luke 12:50).

• Just as baptism pictures a complete submersion, so the Lord will be engulfed by agony, death, burial, and ultimately resurrection (Romans 6:3-4; Colossians 2:12).

• James’ martyrdom and John’s long persecution fulfill this prophecy, demonstrating that union with Christ brings both a share in His pain and, ultimately, in His victory (Philippians 3:10-11; Revelation 2:10).

• Their experiences confirm Jesus’ words and show that God’s purposes for each believer include a unique, appointed measure of trial (1 Peter 4:12-13).

summary

Mark 10:39 reveals that following Jesus is not a path of earthly privilege but of sacrificial fellowship with Him. James and John’s confident “We can” is met by Jesus’ sober assurance that they indeed will share His cup and baptism of suffering. Their later lives prove the reliability of His word and illustrate the call to every disciple: embrace the Savior, endure the cost, and anticipate the glory that follows faithful suffering with Him (1 Peter 5:10; Romans 8:18).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(39) And with the baptism.--Here, as before, the clause is omitted in the best MSS. of St. Matthew, and is therefore, strictly speaking, peculiar to St. Mark.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 39. - James and John seem to have understood the meaning of the cup; and perhaps also of the baptism. They both of them drank the cup, though in different ways. St. James, preaching Christ more boldly and fervently, became an early martyr, having been slain by the sword of Herod (Acts 12:2). St. John also drank of this cup, and was baptized with this baptism, when, if we may trust the authority of Tertullian ('De Praescript.' c. 36.). He he was cast by order of Domitian into a caldron of boiling oil, before the Porta Latina at Rome, although the oil had no power to hurt him. Another legend states that he drank a cup of poison, and took no harm. On this account he is frequently represented with a cup in his hand.
