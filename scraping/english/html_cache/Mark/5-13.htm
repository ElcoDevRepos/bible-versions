Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He gave them permission
This phrase highlights Jesus' authority over the spiritual realm. In the context of Mark 5, Jesus encounters a man possessed by a legion of demons. The demons recognize Jesus' power and authority, requesting permission to enter the pigs. This demonstrates Jesus' sovereignty, as even the forces of darkness must submit to His will. This authority is consistent with other biblical accounts, such as Jesus' temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1-11) and His command over nature (Mark 4:39).

and the unclean spirits came out
The term "unclean spirits" refers to demonic entities, emphasizing their impurity and opposition to God's holiness. The expulsion of these spirits signifies Jesus' power to cleanse and restore individuals from spiritual bondage. This act is a fulfillment of prophecies like Isaiah 61:1, which speaks of liberation for the captives. It also prefigures the ultimate defeat of evil through Christ's death and resurrection.

and went into the pigs
Pigs were considered unclean animals according to Jewish law (Leviticus 11:7). The demons' entry into the pigs underscores the theme of uncleanness and the separation between the holy and the profane. This event also serves as a visual representation of the destructive nature of evil, as the pigs' fate mirrors the spiritual destruction the demons intended for the possessed man.

and the herd of about two thousand
The large number of pigs indicates the magnitude of the demonic presence within the man, as the term "legion" suggests a multitude. This detail emphasizes the severity of the man's condition and the comprehensive nature of Jesus' deliverance. The number also highlights the economic impact on the local community, which may have contributed to their fear and request for Jesus to leave (Mark 5:17).

rushed down the steep bank into the sea
The geographical setting near the Sea of Galilee provides a vivid backdrop for this dramatic event. The steep bank suggests a sudden and uncontrollable descent, symbolizing the inevitable downfall of evil when confronted by divine authority. This imagery parallels other biblical instances where water signifies judgment and chaos, such as the Flood (Genesis 7) and the Red Sea crossing (Exodus 14).

and drowned in the water
The drowning of the pigs serves as a powerful symbol of the ultimate fate of evil. Water, often associated with chaos and destruction, becomes the means by which the unclean spirits meet their end. This act foreshadows the final judgment and eradication of evil, as depicted in Revelation 20:10. It also reinforces the theme of Jesus as the deliverer who brings order and peace to a world marred by sin.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus demonstrates His authority over the spiritual realm by permitting the unclean spirits to enter the pigs.

2. Unclean Spirits
These are demonic entities that possessed a man and, upon Jesus' command, entered a herd of pigs.

3. The Pigs
A herd of about two thousand pigs that became the vessels for the unclean spirits and subsequently rushed into the sea.

4. The Sea
The body of water into which the pigs rushed and drowned, symbolizing the destructive nature of the demonic forces.

5. The Gerasenes
The region where this event took place, highlighting the Gentile setting and the broader reach of Jesus' ministry.
Teaching Points
Authority of Christ
Jesus' command over the unclean spirits demonstrates His supreme authority over all creation, including the spiritual realm. Believers can find comfort and assurance in His power and sovereignty.

Destructive Nature of Evil
The destruction of the pigs illustrates the destructive intent of demonic forces. Christians are reminded to be vigilant and to rely on God's protection against spiritual warfare.

Value of Human Life
Jesus' willingness to allow the destruction of the pigs for the sake of one man's deliverance highlights the immense value He places on human life and spiritual freedom.

Spiritual Warfare
This passage serves as a reminder of the reality of spiritual warfare and the need for believers to be equipped with the armor of God as described in Ephesians 6:10-18.

Witness to the Gentiles
The setting in the Gentile region of the Gerasenes points to the inclusive nature of Jesus' ministry, foreshadowing the Great Commission to make disciples of all nations.

What Does Mark 5:13 Mean
He gave them permission

• Jesus’ sovereign authority is front and center. The demons cannot make a move without His explicit word, echoing Mark 1:27, “He commands even the unclean spirits, and they obey Him.”

• This scene previews the sweeping claim of Matthew 28:18—“All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me.”

• Like God limiting Satan in Job 1:12, Christ’s permission underscores that evil operates only within boundaries set by the Lord.

the unclean spirits came out

• The exit of the spirits demonstrates immediate obedience to Christ’s command, just as in Mark 1:25-26 and Acts 16:18.

• Their departure fulfills 1 John 3:8, “The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil.”

• Deliverance is total; no partial victory exists when Jesus speaks.

went into the pigs

• Pigs were ceremonially unclean (Leviticus 11:7), fitting vessels for unclean spirits.

• Matthew 8:31 records the demons requesting this very transfer, revealing they would rather inhabit unclean animals than face the abyss (cf. Luke 8:31).

• The move exposes the destructive nature of demons: even beasts suffer when evil gains entry.

the herd of about two thousand

• The sheer number mirrors “Legion, for we are many” (Mark 5:9).

• Quantity underscores Jesus’ mastery over every force, whether one demon or thousands.

• It also hints at the depth of the man’s prior bondage—an army of spirits held him, yet a single word of Christ set him free (Matthew 12:29).

rushed down the steep bank

• Immediate, violent destruction aligns with John 10:10: “The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy.”

• Evil’s endgame is always ruin; given freedom, the demons speed toward death, illustrating where sin unchecked will lead (James 1:15).

into the sea

• The Sea of Galilee becomes a stage for judgment, reminiscent of Pharaoh’s army swallowed by the Red Sea (Exodus 14:28).

• Throughout Scripture, the sea often symbolizes chaos or opposition to God (Psalm 89:9; Revelation 13:1); here chaos consumes itself under divine oversight.

drowned in the water

• Physical drowning anticipates the ultimate fate of evil in the lake of fire (Revelation 20:10).

• Micah 7:19 pictures God casting sins “into the depths of the sea,” a parallel display that what destroys us is cast away by His power.

• The man is left clothed and in his right mind (Mark 5:15), while his tormentors perish—clear evidence of spiritual deliverance costing him nothing yet costing evil everything.

summary

Mark 5:13 showcases Jesus’ absolute authority, the destructive nature of demons, and the completeness of Christ’s deliverance. With a single permission, thousands of unclean spirits depart, rush to their own destruction, and leave the once-possessed man fully restored. The episode invites confidence in the Savior who sets boundaries for evil, liberates the captive, and ultimately consigns all wickedness to ruin.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) They were about two thousand.--The number, which is peculiar to St. Mark, may be noted as another instance of his graphic accuracy in detail.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - The unclean spirits came out, and entered into the swine: and the herd ran violently down a steep place (κατὰ τοῦ κρημνοῦ) - literally, down the steep - into the sea,... and were choked in the sea. By this Christ shows of how little worth are earthly possessions when set in the balance with the souls of men. The recovery of this demoniac was worth far more than the value of the two thousand swine.
