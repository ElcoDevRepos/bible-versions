You have disregarded the commandment of God
This phrase highlights the tension between divine commandments and human traditions. In the context of Mark 7, Jesus addresses the Pharisees and scribes who prioritize their traditions over God's commandments. This reflects a broader biblical theme where God's laws are often contrasted with human customs (Isaiah 29:13). The commandment of God refers to the Torah, the divine law given to Moses, which was central to Jewish life and worship. The disregard for God's commandment is a recurring issue in the Old Testament, where Israel often strayed from God's laws, leading to prophetic rebukes and calls for repentance (Jeremiah 7:23-24).

to keep the tradition of men.
The tradition of men refers to the oral traditions and interpretations developed by Jewish leaders over centuries, which were intended to safeguard the Torah but often became burdensome and overshadowed the original intent of God's law. These traditions included various purity laws and rituals not explicitly found in Scripture. Jesus criticizes these traditions because they can lead to legalism and a focus on external compliance rather than internal righteousness. This critique is echoed in other parts of the New Testament, such as Colossians 2:8, where Paul warns against being taken captive by human traditions. The conflict between divine commandments and human traditions is a central theme in Jesus' ministry, emphasizing the need for a heart aligned with God's will rather than mere ritual observance.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is addressing the Pharisees and scribes, challenging their adherence to human traditions over God's commandments.

2. Pharisees and Scribes
Religious leaders of the time who are known for their strict observance of the law and traditions. They are the recipients of Jesus' rebuke in this passage.

3. Disciples
Followers of Jesus who are present during this confrontation and are learning from His teachings and interactions with the religious leaders.

4. Jerusalem
The city where many of these religious leaders come from, representing the center of Jewish religious life and authority.

5. Tradition of the Elders
Refers to the oral traditions and interpretations of the law that had been passed down and were held in high regard by the Pharisees and scribes.
Teaching Points
Prioritizing God's Commandments
Believers are called to prioritize God's commandments over human traditions. This requires discernment and a commitment to Scripture as the ultimate authority.

The Danger of Legalism
Legalism can lead to a focus on external compliance rather than internal transformation. Christians should seek a heart aligned with God's will rather than mere outward conformity.

Tradition vs. Scripture
While traditions can be valuable, they must be evaluated against Scripture. Traditions should not overshadow or contradict the clear teachings of the Bible.

Heart Over Ritual
God desires genuine worship from the heart rather than empty rituals. Believers should examine their motives and ensure their worship is sincere and heartfelt.

Continuous Reformation
The church and individual believers must continually reform and align their practices with Scripture, ensuring that traditions serve to enhance, not hinder, their faith.

What Does Mark 7:8 Mean
You have disregarded

Jesus speaks directly to religious leaders who have made a habit of pushing aside God’s clear commands. This is not a casual oversight; it is a deliberate choice.

• “Disregarded” points to willful neglect (James 1:22–24, “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves”).

• It exposes a heart posture like that warned in Proverbs 28:9: “He who turns his ear away from listening to the law—even his prayer is detestable.”

• Mark’s parallel account in Matthew 15:3 echoes the same rebuke: “Why do you break the command of God for the sake of your tradition?”.

The Lord sees more than actions; He sees the motives behind them. When we know what God says yet choose another path, we “disregard” Him personally.

the commandment of God

Here Jesus highlights the supreme authority of Scripture.

• God’s commandments are not suggestions (Exodus 20:1–17). They carry His very character and will.

• Deuteronomy 6:6–7 instructs parents to teach these commands diligently, implying that God expects them to shape daily life.

• Psalm 19:7 reminds us, “The law of the LORD is perfect, reviving the soul.”

By elevating man-made rules above divine revelation, the leaders undermine the perfection, goodness, and binding nature of God’s Word.

to keep

“To keep” shows purpose and devotion. The Pharisees held tightly to something—but it was the wrong something.

• We all “keep” what we treasure (Matthew 6:21, “Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also”).

• Jesus contrasts two kinds of loyalty: clinging to God’s voice or clinging to human inventions. Both cannot occupy the same throne (Joshua 24:15).

• Galatians 1:14 highlights Paul’s former zeal “for the traditions of my fathers,” proving that even passionate religiosity can miss God.

Choosing what we keep determines the direction of our worship, our ethics, and ultimately our eternity.

the tradition of men

Traditions are not inherently bad; Scripture itself affirms sound tradition (2 Thessalonians 2:15). The issue arises when human traditions displace or contradict God’s commands.

• Isaiah 29:13 foretold this problem: “This people draw near with their mouths... yet their hearts are far from Me, and their worship of Me is but rules taught by men.”

• Colossians 2:8 warns, “See to it that no one takes you captive through philosophy and empty deception, according to human tradition… rather than Christ.”

• In Mark 7:11–13, Jesus cites the Corban rule as a case study: a man-made loophole that nullified the fifth commandment to honor father and mother (Exodus 20:12).

Whenever tradition trumps truth, the result is spiritual bondage, hypocrisy, and a loss of genuine love for God and neighbor.

summary

Mark 7:8 exposes the danger of exchanging divine command for human custom. Jesus condemns any practice—even long-standing religious practice—that forces us to sideline God’s clear Word. Our calling is to treasure Scripture, obey it wholeheartedly, and evaluate every tradition by its light. In doing so, we keep God’s commandment and honor the One who gave it.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) As the washing of pots and cups.--Many of the better MSS. omit the whole of the latter part of this verse. On internal grounds, however, it is hardly likely that such words should have been added as a note, and it is likely enough that the passage should have been altered by a transcriber, to make it agree with the report in St. Matthew.
