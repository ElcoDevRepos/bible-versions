When Jesus had again crossed by boat to the other side
This phrase indicates a recurring pattern in Jesus' ministry, where He frequently traveled across the Sea of Galilee. The "other side" refers to the western shore, likely near Capernaum, a central hub for His ministry. This movement highlights Jesus' intentional outreach to various communities, emphasizing His mission to reach both Jewish and Gentile populations. The Sea of Galilee, a freshwater lake, was a significant geographical feature in the region, facilitating travel and trade. Jesus' use of a boat underscores His connection with the local fishermen, some of whom were His disciples.

a large crowd gathered around Him
The gathering of a large crowd reflects Jesus' growing popularity and the widespread interest in His teachings and miracles. This crowd likely consisted of people from various backgrounds, including Jews and possibly Gentiles, drawn by His reputation as a healer and teacher. The presence of such crowds often set the stage for significant teachings and miracles, demonstrating the public's hunger for spiritual truth and physical healing. This also foreshadows the challenges Jesus would face from religious leaders who felt threatened by His influence.

beside the sea
The setting "beside the sea" is significant as it often served as a natural amphitheater for Jesus' teachings, allowing His voice to carry over the water to the gathered crowds. The Sea of Galilee was a familiar and accessible location for many in the region, making it an ideal place for Jesus to reach a diverse audience. This setting also connects to various Old Testament prophecies and narratives where bodies of water play a crucial role, such as the parting of the Red Sea, symbolizing deliverance and divine intervention. The sea's proximity underscores the theme of Jesus as the Lord over creation, as seen in His calming of the storm and walking on water.

Teaching Points
Jesus' Accessibility
Jesus made Himself available to the people, crossing boundaries to reach those in need. We should strive to be accessible and open to serving others in our communities.

The Draw of Jesus
The large crowd signifies the magnetic pull of Jesus' presence and message. As believers, our lives should reflect Christ in such a way that others are drawn to Him through us.

Crossing Boundaries
Jesus' movement across the sea symbolizes His mission to reach all people, not just the Jews. We are called to break down barriers and share the Gospel with everyone, regardless of background.

The Importance of Gathering
The crowd gathered around Jesus, eager to hear and see Him. We should prioritize gathering with other believers for worship, teaching, and encouragement.

Faith in Action

What Does Mark 5:21 Mean
When Jesus had again crossed by boat to the other side

• “When Jesus had again crossed by boat to the other side…” (Mark 5:21) places the moment immediately after His deliverance of the demoniac in the region of the Gerasenes (Mark 5:1–20).

• The word “again” reminds us of repeated crossings, showing Christ’s intentional movement to reach people on both shores of the Sea of Galilee (Mark 4:35; Matthew 9:1).

• Each crossing highlights His sovereignty over creation—He calmed a storm on a previous voyage (Mark 4:39)—and His purposeful mission to preach, heal, and deliver (Luke 4:43).

• This literal boat journey underscores Christ’s willingness to enter every context—Gentile or Jewish—to rescue the lost (John 3:17; Ephesians 2:17).

a large crowd gathered around Him

• The verse continues, “…a large crowd gathered around Him…” (Mark 5:21), showing Jesus’ public ministry drawing multitudes eager for healing, teaching, and hope (Mark 1:33; 3:9).

• Crowds illustrate human need and Christ’s compassion (Matthew 9:36). Whereas demons begged Him to leave (Mark 5:17), people now flock toward Him, revealing differing responses to His authority.

• His readiness to engage the multitudes points to fulfilled prophecy that Messiah would shepherd His people (Ezekiel 34:23; Isaiah 40:11).

• The scene prepares readers for two urgent pleas that follow—Jairus for his daughter and a woman for healing (Mark 5:22–34)—emphasizing personal faith amid pressing throngs.

beside the sea

• Mark notes the setting: “...around Him beside the sea” (Mark 5:21). The shoreline of the Sea of Galilee served as an outdoor classroom where sound carried and space accommodated large gatherings (Luke 5:1–3).

• Physical proximity—people pressing close while Jesus stands near water—pictures His accessibility; no barrier separates Creator from creation (John 1:14).

• The sea itself, recently calmed by His command (Mark 4:39), silently testifies to His deity even as crowds now seek His help (Psalm 89:9).

• Ministry in open, public venues fulfills the pattern of light shining before all (Matthew 5:14–16), encouraging believers today to live faith openly in everyday places.

summary

Mark 5:21 records a literal return voyage, an eager crowd, and a shoreline gathering that frame the miraculous events to follow. The verse showcases Jesus’ intentional movements, His drawing power over hearts, and His readiness to meet human need wherever He steps ashore.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - Jesus now crosses over the sea again, and apparently in the same boat, to the other side, the opposite shore, near to Capernaum. St. Matthew (Matthew 4:13) distinctly tells us that he had left Nazareth, and was now dwelling at Capernaum, thus fulfilling the ancient prophecy with regard to Zebulun and Nephthalim. The circumstances under which he quitted Nazareth are given by St. Luke (Luke 4:16-31). St. Matthew (Matthew 9:1) calls Capernaum his own city. Thus as Christ ennobled Bethlehem by his birth, Nazareth by his education, and Jerusalem By his death, so he honored Capernaum by making it his ordinary residence, and the focus, so to speak, of his preaching and miracles. When Jesus returned, a great multitude was gathered unto him; and he was by the sea. St. Luke says that the people welcomed him, for they were waiting for him. Again he placed himself by the sea, probably for the conveniences of addressing a multitude, and of relieving himself of the pressure, as before, by taking refuge in a boat.
