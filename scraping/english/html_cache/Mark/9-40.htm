For whoever is not against us
This phrase emphasizes the inclusivity of Jesus' mission and the broadness of His kingdom. In the context of Mark 9, Jesus is addressing His disciples who were concerned about someone casting out demons in His name but not following them. This reflects a common theme in Jesus' teachings where He challenges the exclusivity and elitism that can arise within religious communities. The phrase suggests that those who are not actively opposing Jesus' work are, in a sense, supporting it. This can be connected to the broader biblical principle found in Matthew 12:30, where Jesus states, "Whoever is not with me is against me," highlighting the importance of allegiance to Christ. The cultural context of the time was one of division and sectarianism, and Jesus' statement would have been radical, promoting unity and cooperation among those who might not be part of the immediate group of disciples but still work in His name.

is for us.
This part of the verse underscores the idea of unity and support within the body of Christ. It suggests that those who are not opposing the work of Jesus are contributing to it, even if indirectly. This can be seen as a call to recognize and appreciate the diverse ways in which people can contribute to the mission of the Gospel. Historically, this would have been a significant teaching for the early church, which was composed of a diverse group of Jews and Gentiles, each bringing different perspectives and practices. Theologically, this phrase can be linked to the concept of the body of Christ as described in 1 Corinthians 12:12-27, where Paul speaks of the church as one body with many parts, each with its own role and function. This teaching encourages believers to focus on common goals and shared faith rather than differences, fostering a spirit of cooperation and mutual support.

Teaching Points
Inclusivity in Ministry
Jesus teaches that those who are not actively opposing His work are considered allies. This encourages a broader acceptance of diverse ministries that honor Christ.

Unity in Christ
The passage emphasizes the importance of unity among believers, even if they are not part of the same group or denomination. The focus should be on the shared mission of advancing God's kingdom.

Discernment and Wisdom
While inclusivity is important, discernment is necessary to ensure that actions align with biblical truth and the character of Christ.

Avoiding Division
Jesus' teaching warns against creating unnecessary divisions among believers. The focus should be on common goals rather than differences.

Encouragement in Service
This passage encourages believers to support and affirm those who serve in Jesus' name, recognizing their contributions to the kingdom.

What Does Mark 9:40 Mean
Context

- Just before this statement, John told Jesus, “Teacher, we saw someone driving out demons in Your name and we tried to stop him, because he was not one of us” (Mark 9:38).

- Jesus replied, “Do not stop him. For no one who does a miracle in My name can in the next moment speak evil of Me” (Mark 9:39).

- The lesson echoes Moses’ words when Joshua complained about unauthorized prophecy: “Would that all the LORD’s people were prophets” (Numbers 11:26-29).

- Similar scenes appear in Luke 9:49-50 and Philippians 1:15-18, where Paul rejoices that Christ is preached even by rivals.

For

- “For” links Jesus’ command (“Do not stop him”) to the reason that follows.

- It grounds the disciples’ behavior in Jesus’ own authority, not personal preference (cf. Colossians 3:17).

- The verse is not an invitation to unchecked pluralism; it is a rationale for welcoming genuine Christ-honoring work.

Whoever

- The broad term removes ethnic, social, and institutional limits (cf. Acts 10:34-35; Galatians 3:28).

- “Whoever” reminds us that God often works outside our familiar circles (see 2 Kings 5:2-3; Jonah 3:5).

- The door is open to any person whose words and deeds truly exalt Jesus (1 Corinthians 12:3).

Is not against us

- “Against” pictures open hostility or opposition, not mere difference in style or affiliation (Matthew 12:30’s opposite warning makes the contrast clear).

- Alignment is measured by loyalty to Jesus’ name and message (2 Timothy 2:19; 1 John 2:22-23).

- When someone ministers in Christ’s name and bears good fruit, the default stance is acceptance, not suspicion (Luke 6:44).

Is for us

- Neutrality toward Jesus is impossible; absence of opposition reveals practical support (Romans 8:31).

- Those advancing Christ’s kingdom—though unknown to us—stand on the same side in the spiritual battle (Ephesians 6:12-13; Revelation 12:11).

- Their success brings honor to the same Lord, benefiting the whole body (1 Corinthians 3:5-9).

Implications for fellowship and ministry

- Welcome servants of Christ who uphold the gospel, even if they are “not one of us.”

- Celebrate victories wherever Christ’s name is honored, resisting turf wars (Philippians 2:3-4).

- Maintain discernment; support what is clearly for Jesus while rejecting teachings that deny Him (Jude 3-4).

- Pray for unity centered on truth so the world may believe (John 17:20-23).

summary

Mark 9:40 affirms that anyone not actively opposing Jesus is, by definition, on His side. The verse calls Christ’s followers to recognize and rejoice in authentic kingdom work beyond their immediate circles, fostering unity rooted in loyalty to the Lord and His gospel.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 40. - For he that is not against us is for us. In St. Matthew (Matthew 12:30) we find our Lord using a somewhat similar expression, only in an inverted order. He there says, "He that is not with me is against me." The lesson which both these apothegms teach is the same, that there is no such thing as neutrality in reference to Christ and his cause. We must be either with him or against him. Dr. Morison on St. Mark in this place says, "When in applied morals we sit in judgment on ourselves, we should in ordinary circumstances apply the law obversely and stringently,' he who is not with Christ is against him.' But when we are sitting in judgment on others, into whose hearts we cannot look directly, we should in ordinary circumstances apply the law reversely and generously, ' He that is not against Christ is with him.'"
