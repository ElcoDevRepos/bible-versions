Now because Jesus was doing these things on the Sabbath
The Sabbath, a day of rest and worship, was central to Jewish religious life, rooted in the creation narrative (Genesis 2:2-3) and codified in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:8-11). Jesus' actions on the Sabbath, such as healing, challenged the Pharisaic interpretation of Sabbath laws, which had become burdensome with numerous restrictions. His works on the Sabbath highlighted His authority over the law and pointed to His identity as the Lord of the Sabbath (Matthew 12:8). This phrase underscores the tension between Jesus' ministry and the religious leaders' legalism.

the Jews began to persecute Him
The term "the Jews" often refers to the Jewish religious leaders, such as the Pharisees and Sadducees, who were influential in the Sanhedrin. Their persecution of Jesus was rooted in His perceived violation of the Sabbath and His claims of divine authority, which they saw as blasphemous (John 5:18). This opposition is a recurring theme in the Gospels, illustrating the growing conflict that would eventually lead to Jesus' crucifixion. The persecution also fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the suffering Messiah (Isaiah 53:3), and it foreshadows the trials faced by early Christians as they spread the Gospel.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
Central figure in the New Testament, the Son of God, who performed miracles and taught about the Kingdom of God. In this context, He is performing acts of healing on the Sabbath.

2. The Jews
Refers to the Jewish leaders and authorities who were responsible for maintaining religious laws and traditions. They were often in conflict with Jesus over His interpretation and application of the Law.

3. The Sabbath
A day of rest and worship observed from Friday evening to Saturday evening, as commanded in the Mosaic Law. It was a central aspect of Jewish religious life.

4. Healing at the Pool of Bethesda
The event preceding this verse where Jesus heals a man who had been an invalid for 38 years, which sparked the controversy over Sabbath observance.

5. Persecution
The response of the Jewish leaders to Jesus' actions, which they viewed as a violation of the Sabbath law, leading to increased hostility towards Him.
Teaching Points
Understanding the Purpose of the Sabbath
The Sabbath was intended as a gift for rest and worship, not as a burden. Jesus demonstrates that acts of mercy align with the true spirit of the Sabbath.

Jesus' Authority
Jesus asserts His authority over religious traditions, emphasizing that His mission transcends legalistic interpretations of the Law.

Responding to Opposition
Jesus' example shows that obedience to God's mission may lead to persecution, but faithfulness is paramount.

Balancing Law and Grace
The tension between Jesus and the Jewish leaders highlights the need to balance adherence to religious practices with the grace and compassion that Jesus exemplifies.

The Role of Miracles
Jesus' miracles serve as signs pointing to His divine identity and mission, challenging us to see beyond the immediate act to the greater spiritual truth.

What Does John 5:16 Mean
Now

- The verse opens with a word that ties us directly to the events that have just taken place in John 5:1-15, where Jesus healed the lame man at the pool of Bethesda.

- Scripture reports this sequence as factual history (Luke 1:1-4). We are meant to read what follows as the literal continuation of that moment.

because Jesus was doing these things

- “These things” points back to the miraculous healing and the command, “Pick up your mat and walk” (John 5:8-9).

- Miracles validated Jesus’ divine authority (John 3:2; Acts 2:22).

- His works always served the Father’s purpose (John 4:34), revealing compassion and confirming His identity as the promised Messiah (Isaiah 35:5-6).

on the Sabbath

- The Sabbath command was given for rest and worship (Exodus 20:8-11).

- Jesus never violated God’s Law; He challenged human traditions that distorted it (Mark 7:8-9).

- He declared Himself “Lord of the Sabbath” (Mark 2:27-28), affirming that doing good and giving life are fully in harmony with God’s intent for that day (Matthew 12:11-12).

the Jews began to persecute Him

- The term refers to the religious leaders who had authority over synagogue life (John 9:22).

- They saw Jesus’ actions as a threat to their system (John 11:47-48).

- Persecution here includes:

• Questioning His motives and authority (John 5:18, 7:1)

• Seeking ways to silence Him (Mark 3:6)

• Plotting eventual execution (John 11:53)

- Ironically, their hostility fulfills prophecy about the suffering Servant (Psalm 22:6-8; Isaiah 53:3).

summary

John 5:16 records the moment hostility against Jesus crystallized because He healed on the Sabbath. By literally recounting the events, Scripture shows: Jesus’ miracles were deliberate acts of mercy, fully lawful under God’s design; religious leaders opposed Him not for breaking God’s Law, but for upending their traditions; and this rising persecution set in motion the redemptive plan that would culminate at the cross (Acts 4:27-28).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) The words, "and sought to slay Him," should be omitted. They have been inserted in some MSS. to explain the first clause of John 5:18. For "He had done," read He was doing. The word is in the imperfect tense, expressing continuance or custom. It is either that from this one instance they generalise a law of practice to justify their persecution, or that some of the earlier unrecorded miracles were also performed on the Sabbath. (Comp. Luke 6:1-11.)
