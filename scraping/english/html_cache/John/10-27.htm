My sheep
In biblical times, sheep were a common metaphor for God's people, reflecting their need for guidance and protection. The imagery of sheep is prevalent throughout Scripture, symbolizing believers who are under the care of the Good Shepherd, Jesus Christ. This metaphor is rooted in the pastoral culture of ancient Israel, where shepherds played a crucial role in the community. The Old Testament frequently uses this imagery, such as in Psalm 23, where the Lord is depicted as a shepherd.

listen to My voice;
Listening to the voice of Jesus implies a relationship characterized by recognition and obedience. In the context of the Gospel of John, this listening is not merely auditory but involves a deep, spiritual understanding and acceptance of His teachings. This phrase connects to the broader theme of Jesus as the Word of God (John 1:1) and the importance of hearing and responding to His message. The ability to discern His voice is a mark of true discipleship and is contrasted with those who do not recognize Him (John 10:5).

I know them,
The knowledge Jesus speaks of here is intimate and personal, reflecting a deep relationship between the shepherd and His sheep. This echoes the covenant relationship God has with His people, as seen in passages like Jeremiah 1:5, where God knows His people even before they are formed. In the New Testament, this knowledge is reciprocal, as believers are also called to know Christ (Philippians 3:10). The Greek word for "know" (γινώσκω) implies an ongoing, dynamic relationship rather than mere intellectual awareness.

and they follow Me.
Following Jesus involves a commitment to His teachings and a lifestyle that reflects His example. This phrase emphasizes the active response required from believers, who are called to walk in His footsteps (1 Peter 2:21). The concept of following is central to discipleship, as seen in Jesus' call to His first disciples (Matthew 4:19). It also implies trust and reliance on Jesus as the shepherd who leads His flock to safety and provision, resonating with the promise of eternal life and security found in Him (John 10:28).

Teaching Points
Listening to Jesus' Voice
Believers are called to discern and listen to the voice of Jesus amidst the noise of the world.

This requires regular engagement with Scripture and prayer to recognize His guidance.

Intimacy with Christ
Jesus knows His followers personally and intimately, which should encourage believers to cultivate a deeper relationship with Him.

This relationship is marked by trust, obedience, and love.

Following Jesus
True discipleship involves actively following Jesus, not just acknowledging Him.

This means aligning one's life with His teachings and example.

Security in Christ
As the Good Shepherd, Jesus provides security and assurance to His sheep, promising eternal life and protection.

Believers can find peace in knowing they are held securely by Christ.

Community of Believers
The imagery of sheep implies a community; believers are part of a flock and should support and encourage one another in their walk with Christ.

What Does John 10:27 Mean
My sheep

Jesus opens with ownership. We belong to Him. Just as a shepherd counts every lamb, He counts us as His own. Psalm 23:1 echoes, “The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want.” In John 10:14-16 He says, “I am the good shepherd. I know My sheep … I must bring them in as well.” Peter adds, “You were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd” (1 Peter 2:25).

Key truths:

• Identity: We are defined first by whose we are, not by what we do.

• Security: The shepherd guards His flock (Ezekiel 34:11-16).

• Unity: One flock under one Shepherd—no believer is left outside the fold.

listen to My voice

Sheep survive by recognizing their shepherd’s call. Jesus says, “The sheep listen for his voice” (John 10:3-4). Across Scripture God keeps speaking:

• Revelation 3:20—He knocks and calls us to table fellowship.

• Hebrews 3:7-8—“Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts.”

• Isaiah 30:21—A whisper behind you, “This is the way; walk in it.”

Listening means:

– Attentiveness: tuning out competing noises.

– Obedience: not just hearing but responding with trust.

– Relationship: we hear because we are already His sheep.

I know them

Knowledge here is intimate and personal. 2 Timothy 2:19 assures, “The Lord knows those who are His.” Jesus is not merely aware of data about us; He loves us deeply. Galatians 4:9 reminds that we are “known by God.” Nahum 1:7 promises He “cares for those who trust in Him.”

Practical comfort:

• He knows every weakness and still claims us.

• He guides with perfect wisdom because nothing about us is hidden.

• His knowledge guarantees eternal security—He will not lose a single sheep (John 10:28-29).

and they follow Me

Hearing leads to motion. The true mark of belonging is discipleship: “The sheep follow him because they know his voice” (John 10:4-5). Jesus later says, “Whoever serves Me must follow Me” (John 12:26).

Following involves:

– Daily surrender: “Take up his cross daily and follow Me” (Luke 9:23; Matthew 16:24).

– Separation: refusing the voice of strangers and false shepherds.

– Perseverance: Revelation 14:4 pictures saints who “follow the Lamb wherever He goes,” even through hardship.

It is not optional; it is the inevitable outcome of real hearing and real knowing.

summary

John 10:27 paints a complete portrait of the believer’s relationship with Christ. We are His possession (“My sheep”), His attentive listeners (“listen to My voice”), His intimately loved ones (“I know them”), and His obedient followers (“they follow Me”). The verse invites confident rest in His care and active commitment to walk wherever the Shepherd leads.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27, 28) The reference to those who believe not because they were not of His sheep, introduces the contrast between them and those who were, and the position of the true members of the flock is expanded in this pair of parallel clauses. One member of each pair refers to the act or state of the sheep; and the other to the act or gift of the Shepherd. The pairs proceed in a climax from the first response of the conscience which recognises the divine voice to the eternal home which is in the Father's presence.

(1)"My sheep hear My voice," . . "and I know them;"

(2)"And they follow Me" . . "and I give unto them eternal life;"

(3)"And they shall never perish" . . . "neither shall any man pluck them out of My hand."

By reading successively the clauses placed on the left side of the page, we trace the progress of the human act and state; by reading, in the same way, the clauses on the right side of the page, we trace the progress of the divine gift; by reading each pair in the order of the text, we see how at each stage the gift is proportioned to the faculty which can receive it.

The earlier clauses are familiar to us from the preceding discourse, but some expressions will need a word of explanation.

Eternal life.--Comp. John 10:10, where the word "eternal" is added in some readings. Here the verb is in the present, "I give (am now giving) them." (Comp. John 3:15; John 5:24; John 6:47 et seq.). We cannot be too careful to observe that our Lord's thoughts of "eternal life" is never of the future only. It is a development, rather than a simply future existence. We shall live eternally, because we now live spiritually in communion with the Spirit who is Eternal. . . .
