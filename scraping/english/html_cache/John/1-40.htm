Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Andrew, Simon Peter’s brother
Andrew is introduced as the brother of Simon Peter, highlighting his familial connection to one of the most prominent apostles. This relationship is significant as it underscores the close-knit nature of the early disciples. Andrew is often seen as a bridge to Peter, who would become a leading figure in the early church. The mention of Andrew first suggests his role in bringing Peter to Jesus, as seen later in the Gospel. This familial link also emphasizes the theme of evangelism within families, a common occurrence in the New Testament.

was one of the two
This phrase refers to Andrew being one of the two disciples of John the Baptist who first followed Jesus. The identity of the other disciple is not explicitly mentioned, but it is traditionally believed to be John, the author of the Gospel. This highlights the importance of personal witness and testimony in the spread of the Gospel message. The fact that Andrew is one of the first to follow Jesus indicates his openness to spiritual truth and readiness to embrace the Messiah.

who heard John’s testimony
John the Baptist's role as a forerunner to Christ is crucial here. His testimony about Jesus as the "Lamb of God" (John 1:36) prepared the hearts of his disciples to follow Jesus. This emphasizes the prophetic role of John the Baptist as foretold in Isaiah 40:3 and Malachi 3:1, serving as a bridge between the Old and New Testaments. The act of hearing and responding to testimony is a recurring theme in the Gospel, illustrating the power of spoken witness in leading others to Christ.

and followed Jesus
The decision to follow Jesus marks a pivotal moment of discipleship and commitment. This action signifies a transition from being a disciple of John the Baptist to becoming a follower of Christ, indicating a deeper understanding and acceptance of Jesus' identity and mission. The concept of following Jesus is central to the Christian faith, symbolizing a life of obedience, learning, and transformation. This phrase also connects to the broader theme of discipleship found throughout the Gospels, where following Jesus often involves leaving behind former ways of life and embracing a new path.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Testimony
Andrew's decision to follow Jesus was influenced by John the Baptist's testimony. This underscores the power of personal testimony in leading others to Christ.

Bringing Others to Jesus
Andrew is known for bringing his brother Peter to Jesus. This highlights the role of personal relationships in evangelism and discipleship.

Immediate Response to Jesus
Andrew's immediate decision to follow Jesus after hearing John's testimony serves as a model for responding promptly to the call of Christ.

The Role of Humility in Discipleship
Andrew is often overshadowed by his brother Peter, yet he faithfully serves in the background, illustrating the importance of humility in serving Christ.

What Does John 1:40 Mean
Andrew

• John presents Andrew as an individual with a name worth remembering. By naming him first, Scripture reminds us that God notes personal faith decisions (see John 6:8; John 12:22).

• Andrew’s very name appears again and again linked to bringing others to Jesus, hinting that discipleship isn’t a private hobby but an outward-looking mission.

Simon Peter’s brother

• Andrew is identified through his relationship to Simon Peter (Matthew 4:18; 1 Corinthians 1:12). Peter will later dominate the narrative, but here Andrew shines first.

• The wording teaches that kingdom significance isn’t measured by fame. God often works through the “less prominent” to launch great movements (cf. 1 Corinthians 12:22-24).

• Family ties become gospel bridges: Andrew’s first instinct is to introduce his brother to Christ (John 1:41-42).

was one of the two

• The “two” refers back to John 1:35-39, where two of John the Baptist’s disciples spend a day with Jesus. One is Andrew; the other is likely the unnamed disciple who later writes this Gospel.

• This subtle note underscores eyewitness reliability: John records what he personally experienced (John 19:35).

• God’s pattern: He often starts small—two seekers become pillars who help turn the world upside down (Acts 17:6).

who heard John’s testimony

• Faith comes by hearing (Romans 10:17). Andrew listened to John the Baptist’s declaration: “Look, the Lamb of God!” (John 1:36).

• John’s role was never to gather followers for himself but to direct them to Christ (John 3:30). Andrew’s response demonstrates the proper reaction to faithful preaching: move toward Jesus, not merely admire the messenger.

• The chain of witness continues: John testifies to Andrew, Andrew to Peter, Peter to thousands (Acts 2:41).

and followed Jesus

• “Followed” marks a decisive shift of allegiance—Andrew leaves the forerunner for the Messiah (Luke 5:11).

• Discipleship in John always carries relational and transformative weight (John 8:12; 10:27). Andrew’s step models immediate obedience.

• Jesus never turns away sincere seekers (John 6:37). One simple act of following opens a lifetime of revelation (John 1:39) and fruitfulness (John 6:9-13).

summary

John 1:40 spotlights Andrew’s quiet but pivotal moment: hearing faithful testimony, he personally responds and becomes a link in God’s unfolding plan. The verse teaches that God values individual choices, uses ordinary relationships for extraordinary influence, and calls every listener to move from hearing about Jesus to actively following Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(40) One of the two.--The Evangelist will even here draw the veil over his own identity (see Introduction). The one is Andrew, even now marked out as brother of the better-known Simon Peter. On these names comp. Note on Matthew 10:2-4; but it should be observed here, that on this first day, as the earnest of the harvest to come, we have the two pairs of brothers, the sons of Zebedee (comp. next verse), and sons of Jonas, who are ever leaders in the apostolic band.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 40-49. -

(2) The naming and convictions of the disciples. Verse 40. - One of the two who heard from John that Jesus was the Son of God and the Lamb of God, and who, on that astounding intelligence, and at their teacher's own suggestion, followed (became henceforth followers of, ἀκόλουθοι) him, was Andrew, the brother of Simon Peter (notice a similar construction at John 6:45, where a clause commences with the copula). The other disciple, with the studied reticence ever preserved about his own designation, is left unnamed by the writer. "Simon Peter" is here spoken of as the better-known man. The bestowment of this designation on Andrew shows that the Gospel was written when Peter's greater name was widely recognized, and the reference is made without the faintest touch of depreciation. Simon Peter's reputation gives force and importance to the record of Andrew's faith. The evangelist's intimate friend Andrew is thus lifted out of his comparative obscurity among the apostolate, not by his association with John, but by his relationship with Simon.
