Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Will you lay down your life for Me?
This question from Jesus to Peter highlights the theme of sacrificial love and commitment. In the cultural context of the time, laying down one's life was the ultimate act of loyalty and devotion. Jesus is challenging Peter's understanding of true discipleship, which requires self-denial and a willingness to follow Christ even unto death. This echoes Jesus' earlier teachings in John 12:25, where He speaks of losing one's life to keep it for eternal life.

Jesus replied.
The direct response from Jesus indicates His awareness of Peter's impending failure. It underscores Jesus' omniscience and His understanding of human frailty. This moment is pivotal as it sets the stage for Peter's later restoration and transformation, showing that Jesus' knowledge of our weaknesses does not preclude His love and plans for us.

Truly, truly, I tell you,
The double "truly" (or "amen, amen" in Greek) is a solemn affirmation used by Jesus to emphasize the certainty and importance of what He is about to say. This phrase is used throughout the Gospels to introduce significant truths, underscoring the reliability and authority of Jesus' words.

before the rooster crows,
The mention of the rooster crowing is a time marker, indicating the early morning hours. In the cultural and historical context, the rooster's crow was a familiar sound marking the end of the night and the beginning of a new day. This detail adds urgency and specificity to Jesus' prediction, highlighting the immediacy of Peter's denial.

you will deny Me three times.
The prediction of Peter's threefold denial is significant in its repetition, symbolizing completeness and emphasizing the gravity of Peter's forthcoming actions. This prophecy is fulfilled in the accounts of the Gospels (e.g., Matthew 26:69-75), illustrating the weakness of human resolve without divine strength. It also serves as a powerful reminder of the need for humility and reliance on God's grace. The number three often signifies completeness in biblical literature, further underscoring the totality of Peter's denial.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who is speaking to His disciples during the Last Supper. He is preparing them for His impending crucifixion and resurrection.

2. Peter (Simon Peter)
One of Jesus' closest disciples, known for his impulsive nature and strong declarations of loyalty. In this passage, Jesus predicts Peter's denial.

3. The Last Supper
The final meal Jesus shares with His disciples before His crucifixion, during which He institutes the Lord's Supper and delivers important teachings.

4. The Rooster Crowing
A significant event that marks the fulfillment of Jesus' prediction about Peter's denial. It serves as a symbol of Peter's failure and the beginning of his repentance.

5. Denial of Jesus
The act of Peter denying any association with Jesus, which occurs three times before the rooster crows, as Jesus predicted.
Teaching Points
Human Weakness and Divine Foreknowledge
Jesus' prediction of Peter's denial demonstrates His divine knowledge and understanding of human frailty. We must recognize our own weaknesses and rely on God's strength.

The Importance of Humility
Peter's overconfidence serves as a warning against pride. We should approach our faith with humility, acknowledging our need for God's grace and guidance.

Repentance and Restoration
Peter's account does not end with his denial. His repentance and restoration by Jesus illustrate the power of forgiveness and the opportunity for redemption.

The Role of Prayer in Spiritual Warfare
Jesus' prayer for Peter highlights the importance of prayer in overcoming spiritual challenges. We should be diligent in prayer, both for ourselves and others.

Faithfulness in Trials
Despite Peter's failure, he ultimately becomes a pillar of the early Church. This encourages us to remain faithful, even when we falter, trusting in God's plan for our lives.

What Does John 13:38 Mean
Will you lay down your life for Me?

• Jesus picks up Peter’s confident pledge in the previous verse (John 13:37) and echoes it back to him.

• Peter’s intent is genuine, as seen when he later draws a sword in Gethsemane (John 18:10), yet zeal alone cannot sustain faith under pressure (cf. Matthew 26:33–35).

• The question exposes the gap between human resolve and divine foreknowledge: God knows the future and our frailty even when we feel strongest (Psalm 139:1–4).

• By asking, Jesus readies Peter—and us—to see that self-reliance must give way to dependence on Christ (Proverbs 3:5–6; John 15:5).

Truly, truly, I tell you

• This double “amen” marks a solemn, unquestionable truth (John 1:51; 5:24).

• Jesus’ words carry absolute authority; what He now predicts will unfold exactly as spoken (Numbers 23:19).

• The phrase also shifts the focus from Peter’s promise to Jesus’ certainty, reminding believers that Scripture’s declarations stand above human intentions (Isaiah 55:11).

before the rooster crows

• Jesus sets a precise time marker, underscoring His control over unfolding events (Mark 14:30).

• The approaching dawn mirrors the spiritual darkness that will soon surround the disciples (Luke 22:53).

• Even in this detail, Scripture shows that God’s timeline is exact, reinforcing trust in every prophetic word (2 Peter 1:19).

you will deny Me three times.

• The prophecy proves literal: Peter later disowns Jesus, growing more emphatic with each denial (Luke 22:54–62).

• Three failures highlight complete breakdown; yet Jesus already anticipates Peter’s restoration (Luke 22:31–32; John 21:15–17).

• The warning teaches that anyone can stumble, but grace abounds for repentance (1 John 1:9; 2 Timothy 2:12–13).

• It also reveals Christ’s unwavering love: He foresees the sin yet still heads to the cross for Peter—and for us (Romans 5:8).

summary

John 13:38 pairs Peter’s bold claim with Jesus’ certain prophecy, contrasting human confidence with divine omniscience. Christ’s question exposes self-reliance; His “truly, truly” affirms the unfailing authority of His word; the rooster’s crow sets God’s precise timetable; and the triple denial displays both human weakness and the Savior’s ready mercy. The verse calls believers to trust not in their own resolve but in the Lord who knows, warns, forgives, and restores.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(38) wilt thou lay down thy life for my sake?--Comp. for this phrase Note on John 10:11. The pronouns are emphatic, and there is a solemn emphasis in the repetition of what St. Peter had said. He was using words of which he knew not the full meaning. He spoke of laying down his life for his Lord. He would hereafter be able to follow, because his Lord would lay down His own life for him.

For the remainder of the verse, comp. Notes on Matthew 26:34; Mark 14:30; and Luke 22:34.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 38. - With infinite pathos and pity Christ took up the words of Peter: Jesus answereth, Wilt thou lay down thy life for my sake? Verily, verily, I say unto thee, The cock shall not have crowed, till thou hast denied me thrice. In Matthew 26:31-35 and Mark 11:27-31 the announcement of Peter's fate is made on the way to the garden of Gethsemane; Luke's account (Luke 22:31, etc.) may harmonize chronologically with this statement of John; but from all we know of Peter, it is probable that, after his long silence maintained during the discourse of John 14-17, his love may have been so quickened and deepened as to have once more induced the reiteration of his fidelity and his willingness to die for and with his Master, only to receive again a more explicit warning of his weakness. Towards the close of the sixteenth chapter of this Gospel, the Lord warns all his disciples of their inability to stand the tremendous test to which they would soon be exposed. If we reject the "harmony," and refuse to double the prediction, we should be strongly inclined, with Meyer and Lucke, to accept the higher credibility of John's chronology than that of Matthew or Mark. The extraordinary character of this prediction, recorded in all four Gospels, is one of the most vivid proofs of our Lord's supernatural power, and in its detail and definiteness places him among those who claim attention from their absolute knowledge, and not their vague guess of the future. Yet there was no fate in this prediction; for Peter is afterwards warned, entreated, prayed for even, by Immanuel.
