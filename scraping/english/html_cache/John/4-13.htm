Jesus said to her
This phrase highlights Jesus' direct engagement with the Samaritan woman at the well, breaking cultural norms of the time. Jewish men typically did not speak publicly with women, especially Samaritan women, due to longstanding animosity between Jews and Samaritans (John 4:9). This interaction underscores Jesus' mission to reach all people, transcending social and ethnic barriers, as seen in His ministry to Gentiles and marginalized groups (Matthew 15:21-28).

Everyone who drinks this water
Here, "this water" refers to the physical water from Jacob's well, a significant historical and religious site for both Jews and Samaritans. The well symbolizes the old covenant and the limitations of the Law, which could not provide eternal satisfaction or salvation. The reference to "everyone" emphasizes the universal human condition of spiritual thirst and the inadequacy of earthly solutions to fulfill spiritual needs.

will be thirsty again
This phrase illustrates the temporary nature of physical and worldly solutions to spiritual problems. It echoes the theme of human insufficiency and the need for something greater, as seen in Ecclesiastes 1:8, where human desires are never fully satisfied. The statement foreshadows Jesus' offer of "living water" (John 4:14), which represents the Holy Spirit and eternal life, fulfilling prophecies like Isaiah 55:1-3 about God's provision of spiritual sustenance.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who offers spiritual truths and eternal life.

2. The Samaritan Woman
A woman from Samaria who encounters Jesus at Jacob's well. Her interaction with Jesus reveals deep spiritual truths.

3. Jacob's Well
A historical and significant location in Samaria where Jesus meets the Samaritan woman. It symbolizes the old covenant and physical sustenance.

4. Samaria
A region between Judea and Galilee, often avoided by Jews due to historical enmity, highlighting Jesus' breaking of social barriers.

5. The Disciples
Followers of Jesus who are absent during the initial conversation but later return, witnessing the impact of Jesus' teaching.
Teaching Points
The Temporary vs. the Eternal
Earthly solutions and pleasures are temporary and cannot satisfy our deepest needs. Only Jesus offers eternal satisfaction.

Spiritual Thirst
Recognize the spiritual thirst within us that only Jesus can quench. Pursue a relationship with Him to find true fulfillment.

Breaking Barriers
Jesus' interaction with the Samaritan woman teaches us to break social and cultural barriers to share the Gospel.

Living Water
Understand that the living water Jesus offers is the Holy Spirit, who indwells believers and provides continual spiritual nourishment.

Evangelism
Like the Samaritan woman, once we encounter Jesus, we are called to share our experience and lead others to Him.

What Does John 4:13 Mean
Jesus said to her

- The conversation begins with the Savior initiating dialogue. This reminds us that, just as in Luke 19:10—“For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost”—Jesus always takes the first step toward us.

- He addresses a Samaritan woman, crossing cultural and moral barriers. Acts 10:34-35 shows that “God does not show favoritism,” underscoring the same truth.

- By speaking directly, He demonstrates personal care. Psalm 139:1-4 affirms that God “discerns my thoughts from afar,” highlighting His intimate knowledge of each heart.

Everyone who drinks this water

- Jesus points to the well in front of them. The statement is literal: anyone drawing from Jacob’s well will physically drink. Yet He’s also exposing the limits of earthly provision.

- Ecclesiastes 2:10-11 records Solomon’s search for satisfaction “yet all was vanity,” illustrating how earthly pursuits—like literal water—meet needs only temporarily.

- In Matthew 6:31-32, Jesus cautions about being consumed by material needs, reminding us “your heavenly Father knows that you need them.” Physical water is necessary, but never ultimate.

- The universal “everyone” confirms that the issue touches every life. Romans 3:23—“all have sinned”—echoes the same inclusiveness.

will be thirsty again

- Physical water solves a momentary problem; the body will soon demand more. Proverbs 27:20 notes, “Sheol and Abaddon are never satisfied; so the eyes of man are never satisfied,” capturing this ongoing craving.

- By exposing recurring thirst, Jesus sets up His later promise in John 4:14 that “whoever drinks the water I give him will never thirst.”

- John 6:35 reinforces it: “Whoever believes in Me will never be thirsty.” Christ alone provides what the well cannot.

- Jeremiah 2:13 warns Israel for forsaking “the fountain of living water.” Earthly wells (career, wealth, pleasure) leak; only Jesus overflows.

- Revelation 21:6 seals the promise: “To the thirsty I will give freely from the spring of the water of life.” Our future satisfaction is guaranteed.

summary

Jesus begins by reaching out personally, then pivots from the literal well to the universal problem of unending thirst. Earthly water, like every temporal solution, always runs dry. In contrast, He will soon offer living water that fully, eternally satisfies. John 4:13 exposes the emptiness of self-reliance and prepares us for the abundance found only in Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13, 14) Whosoever drinketh of this water.--Jesus does not answer her question, but asserts the universal recurrence of thirst, after even the water of Jacob's well, to lead her to the thought that His "living water" is something widely different.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Jesus answered and said to her - leaving the question of his superiority to "our father Jacob" to be settled when she should understand him better - Every one who drinketh (is in the habit of drinking) from this water, or any similar fountain, will thirst again. Earthly desires obtain temporary satisfaction, and then resume their sway. Our whole life is made up of intermittent desires and partial satisfaction, of passion and satiation, of ennui and then of some new longing. This flow and ebb, ebb and flow, of desire belong to the very nature of human appetite. More than that, human desire is never really satiated. Our souls can never be at rest till they find rest in God. This water, even from the well of Jacob, is no exception to the rule.
