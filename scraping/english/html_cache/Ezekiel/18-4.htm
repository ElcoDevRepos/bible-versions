Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Behold, every soul belongs to Me;
This phrase emphasizes God's sovereignty and ownership over all human life. In the biblical context, it reflects the monotheistic belief that God is the creator and sustainer of all souls, as seen in Genesis 2:7, where God breathes life into man. This ownership implies accountability to God, aligning with the covenant relationship established with Israel. The use of "behold" serves as a call to attention, underscoring the importance of the statement.

both father and son are Mine.
This highlights the individual responsibility of each person before God, regardless of familial relationships. In ancient Israel, family lineage and heritage were significant, often determining one's social and religious standing. However, this phrase asserts that each person, whether father or son, is directly accountable to God. This challenges the prevailing cultural notion of collective punishment or blessing based on ancestry, as seen in Exodus 20:5-6, and emphasizes personal responsibility.

The soul who sins is the one who will die.
This statement introduces the principle of individual moral responsibility, a key theme in Ezekiel's prophecy. It counters the belief that children bear the punishment for their parents' sins, as suggested in the proverb mentioned earlier in Ezekiel 18:2. The concept of death here can be understood both physically and spiritually, reflecting the consequences of sin as outlined in Romans 6:23. This principle foreshadows the New Testament teaching of personal repentance and salvation through Jesus Christ, who offers life to those who turn from sin, as seen in John 3:16.

Teaching Points
Personal Responsibility
Each individual is accountable to God for their own actions. This challenges the belief that one can blame their circumstances or heritage for their spiritual state.

God's Ownership
Recognize that every soul belongs to God, which implies a divine claim over our lives and a call to live in accordance with His will.

The Consequence of Sin
Sin leads to spiritual death, underscoring the importance of repentance and seeking God's forgiveness through Jesus Christ.

Generational Accountability
While generational influences exist, each person must choose their path, emphasizing the need for personal faith and obedience.

Hope in Repentance
Despite the dire warning, there is hope in turning away from sin and towards God, who offers life and restoration.

What Does Ezekiel 18:4 Mean
Behold

– The opening word is a divine wake-up call, urging God’s people to stop and pay close attention.

– Scripture often uses “Behold” to front-load something weighty—think of Isaiah 42:1 or John 1:29.

– Here, the Lord is about to correct a flawed proverb in Israel (Ezekiel 18:2) and lay down His own unchanging standard.

Every soul belongs to Me

– God stakes an absolute claim: every person—without exception—originates from, exists for, and answers to Him (Psalm 24:1; Job 12:10).

– This truth undercuts any idea that we can negotiate our own moral terms. Ownership means authority.

– Because the Creator owns every soul, He alone sets the rules for life and judgment (Romans 14:11-12).

Both father and son are Mine

– The repetition zooms in on family lines, a hot topic in Ezekiel’s day. People blamed their hardships on their ancestors’ sins (Jeremiah 31:29-30).

– God’s double mention—father and son—levels the playing field: shared lineage does not equal shared guilt (Deuteronomy 24:16; 2 Kings 14:6).

– By asserting equal ownership, the Lord dismantles fatalism and invites each generation to seek Him personally.

The soul who sins is the one who will die

– The statement is straightforward: personal sin brings personal judgment. No scapegoats, no inherited penalty (Romans 6:23; James 1:14-15).

– “Die” points first to physical death in Israel’s covenant context (Ezekiel 18:13), but it also foreshadows eternal separation for unrepentant sinners (Revelation 20:14-15).

– The verse implicitly highlights hope: if judgment is individual, so is mercy. Later in the chapter God pleads, “Repent and live!” (Ezekiel 18:32).

summary

Ezekiel 18:4 dismantles generational blame by affirming God’s universal ownership and each person’s direct accountability to Him. Fathers and sons alike belong to the Lord; therefore, every individual must reckon with personal sin and its wages. Yet the same individual focus that brings sober warning also opens the door to personal repentance and life.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) All souls are mine.--This is the basis of the subsequent teaching. Since all alike belong to God and are absolutely in His power. He has no occasion to punish one lest another should escape; and again, since all are His, He loves and would save them all, and inflicts punishment only when it is deserved and His grace is rejected. Four cases are now discussed separately: (1) That of the righteous man who honestly seeks to follow the ways of the Lord (Ezekiel 18:5-9); (2) that of his wicked son (Ezekiel 18:10-13); (3) that of the righteous son of the wicked (Ezekiel 18:14-20); (4) that of a change of character in the individual, whether from sin to righteousness or the reverse (Ezekiel 18:21-29). The word "soul" throughout the chapter does not mean exclusively the immortal part of our nature, but, as so often in Scripture, is equivalent to man, or person, or self; and the word "die" is used, as often elsewhere, in the broad sense of suffer punishment.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - Behold, all souls are mine, etc. The words imply, not only creation, ownership, absolute authority, on the part of God, but, as even Calvin could recognize (in loc.), "a paternal affection towards the whole human race which he created and formed." Ezekiel anticipates here, and yet more fully in ver. 32. the teaching of St. Paul, that "God willeth that all men should be saved" (1 Timothy 2:4). The soul that sinneth, it shall die. The sentence, though taken from the Law, which ordered capital punishment for the offences named, cannot be limited to that punishment. "Death" and "life" are both used in their highest and widest meaning - "life" as including all that makes it worth living, "death" for the loss of that only true life which is found in knowing God (John 17:3).
