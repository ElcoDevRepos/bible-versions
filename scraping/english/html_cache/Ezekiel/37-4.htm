Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And He said to me
This phrase indicates divine communication, emphasizing the role of Ezekiel as a prophet who receives direct revelation from God. In the context of the Babylonian exile, God speaks to Ezekiel, providing hope and a future for Israel. This divine interaction underscores the authority and authenticity of the message.

Prophesy concerning these bones
The act of prophesying involves speaking forth God's word, often with a future focus. The "bones" symbolize the house of Israel, which is in a state of despair and hopelessness during the exile. This imagery of dry bones is a powerful metaphor for spiritual death and the need for revival. The command to prophesy suggests that God's word has the power to bring life and restoration.

and tell them
Ezekiel is instructed to address the bones directly, signifying that the message is intended for the people of Israel. This direct address highlights the personal nature of God's message and His desire for His people to hear and respond. It also reflects the prophetic tradition of speaking to the people on behalf of God.

‘Dry bones
The dry bones represent the spiritual and national condition of Israel at the time. They are lifeless and scattered, symbolizing the disunity and desolation experienced by the Israelites in exile. This imagery is consistent with other biblical passages that use bones to depict death and decay, such as in Psalm 22:14-15.

hear the word of the LORD!
The call for the dry bones to "hear" signifies the power and authority of God's word to effect change. In biblical tradition, hearing implies obedience and response. The phrase underscores the transformative potential of divine revelation, as seen in other scriptures where God's word brings about creation, healing, and restoration (e.g., Genesis 1, John 1:1-3). This command foreshadows the resurrection and renewal that God promises to His people, ultimately pointing to the hope of spiritual revival and the coming of the Messiah, who is the Word made flesh.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet of God, Ezekiel is the central human figure in this passage. He is called to deliver God's message to the people of Israel during their Babylonian exile.

2. The Valley of Dry Bones
This is the setting of the vision given to Ezekiel. It symbolizes the spiritual and national death of Israel, scattered and lifeless in exile.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The divine speaker in this passage, commanding Ezekiel to prophesy. Yahweh is the covenant God of Israel, who has the power to bring life from death.

4. Dry Bones
Represent the house of Israel, which feels hopeless and cut off. They symbolize the spiritual desolation and despair of the people.

5. The Vision
This event is a prophetic vision given to Ezekiel, illustrating God's promise to restore Israel both physically and spiritually.
Teaching Points
The Power of God's Word
God's word has the power to bring life where there is death. Just as Ezekiel was commanded to prophesy to the dry bones, believers are called to speak God's word into situations of hopelessness and despair.

Hope in Restoration
The vision of the dry bones is a powerful reminder that no situation is beyond God's ability to restore. In times of personal or communal desolation, we can trust in God's promise of renewal.

Faith and Obedience
Ezekiel's obedience to God's command to prophesy demonstrates the importance of faith and action. Believers are encouraged to act in faith, trusting in God's power to fulfill His promises.

Spiritual Renewal
The transformation of dry bones into a living army symbolizes spiritual renewal. Christians are called to seek and pray for revival in their own lives and communities.

God's Sovereignty and Faithfulness
This passage underscores God's sovereignty and faithfulness to His covenant people. It reassures believers of God's unchanging nature and His commitment to His promises.

What Does Ezekiel 37:4 Mean
And He said to me

God Himself initiates the entire vision. Ezekiel does not dream this up; the Lord speaks first, underscoring divine authority and reliability (2 Timothy 3:16–17; Exodus 4:12).

• When God speaks, His servants listen—this is the pattern from Genesis onward.

• The command is personal: “to me.” God deals directly with individuals before He works through them for others (Isaiah 6:8).

Prophesy concerning these bones

The Lord commands Ezekiel to proclaim His message to a scene of utter hopelessness—a valley filled with dry, scattered bones.

• Prophecy here means declaring God’s word, not predicting Ezekiel’s own ideas (Jeremiah 1:7–10).

• Speaking to lifeless bones illustrates that the power for change rests entirely with God (Romans 4:17).

• The bones represent Israel in exile, literally cut off from land, temple, and kingdom (Ezekiel 37:11), yet God plans real national restoration.

and tell them

God wants the prophet to address the bones directly. Silence will not do; truth must be verbalized (Isaiah 58:1; Matthew 10:27).

• Faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of Christ (Romans 10:17).

• The wording stresses responsibility: Ezekiel must relay the message exactly as given (Deuteronomy 18:18).

Dry bones

Their condition is emphasized—they are not merely bones; they are “very dry” (Ezekiel 37:2). No moisture, no marrow, no chance of spontaneous recovery.

• Symbolizes complete spiritual and national death (Psalm 22:15).

• Mirrors humanity’s state apart from Christ: “dead in your trespasses” (Ephesians 2:1).

• Sets the stage for God’s unmistakable intervention.

hear the word of the LORD!

The command pivots from death to life. Hearing God’s word is the channel through which resurrection power flows (John 5:24; Hebrews 4:12).

• The word brings faith and obedience, even to things that appear beyond hope (Isaiah 55:3, 11).

• In context, Israel will be regathered to the land, revived physically, and renewed spiritually—a literal fulfillment already begun in history and climaxed in the coming Messianic kingdom (Ezekiel 37:21–28; Romans 11:26).

summary

Ezekiel 37:4 showcases God’s sovereign initiative, the necessity of proclaiming His word, and the life-giving power that word carries—even over situations that seem irreversibly dead. Dry bones can live because the Lord commands it, the prophet obeys, and the word accomplishes what He pleases.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) Prophesy upon these bones.--"Prophesy" is here used (as frequently) in its original sense of "speak on God's behalf," and does not convey the idea of prediction.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - Prophesy upon (or, over) then bones. This instruction - which shows Jehovah regarded the prophet's answer as equivalent to an admission that the revivification of the bones lay within his (Jehovah's) power - was not a mere command to predict, as in Ezekiel 6:2 and Ezekiel 11:4, but an injunction to utter the Divine word through which the miracle (of creation, as it really was) should be performed. "The significance of the command lies in the fact that it taught the prophet that he was himself to be instrumental in the great work of resuscitation. He who had been so often troubled with the sense of impotence and failure, who had heard the people say of him, 'Both he not speak parables?' who had been to them as the lovely song of one that hath a pleasant voice, and nothing more than that, was at last to learn that the word of the Lord,' spoken by his lips, was mighty, and would not return to him void" (Plumptre).
