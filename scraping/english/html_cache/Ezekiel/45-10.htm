You must use honest scales
This phrase emphasizes the importance of integrity and fairness in commerce and daily life. In ancient Israel, scales were used to measure goods for trade, and dishonest scales were a common means of cheating. The call for honest scales reflects God's desire for justice and righteousness among His people. This command aligns with other biblical teachings on honesty, such as in Leviticus 19:36 and Proverbs 11:1, which condemn dishonest practices. The use of honest scales is a metaphor for living a life of integrity, reflecting God's character and His expectations for His people.

a just ephah
An ephah is a unit of dry measure used in ancient Israel, roughly equivalent to about 22 liters or 5.8 gallons. The call for a just ephah underscores the need for fairness in trade and economic transactions. This requirement is part of the broader Mosaic Law, which sought to ensure equity and prevent exploitation. The emphasis on a just ephah is also a reminder of God's concern for the poor and vulnerable, who were often the victims of dishonest practices. This principle is echoed in the prophetic literature, such as Amos 8:5, where God condemns those who manipulate measures to defraud others.

and a just bath
A bath is a unit of liquid measure, approximately 22 liters or 5.8 gallons, similar to the ephah but for liquids. The insistence on a just bath highlights the importance of consistency and fairness in all forms of measurement. This reflects the broader biblical theme of justice and righteousness, as seen in passages like Micah 6:11, where God questions the use of dishonest measures. The call for just measures in both dry and liquid forms indicates a comprehensive approach to justice, covering all aspects of life and commerce. This principle points to the ultimate justice and righteousness found in Jesus Christ, who fulfills the law and embodies God's perfect standard.

As followers of Christ, we are called to uphold honesty and fairness in every aspect of our lives, reflecting God's character.

Holiness in Worship and Life
Just as the Israelites were called to purity in temple practices, we are called to live lives of holiness, ensuring our actions align with God's standards.

Justice as a Reflection of God's Nature
God's call for just measures is a reflection of His just nature, and we are to mirror this attribute in our interactions with others.

What Does Ezekiel 45:10 Mean
You must use honest scales

• Ezekiel’s phrase immediately recalls the Lord’s long-standing command for integrity in trade (Leviticus 19:35-36, Deuteronomy 25:13-15).

• “Honest scales” speak to the very tools we use every day. If the scale itself is skewed, every subsequent transaction is corrupted. Proverbs 11:1 declares, “Dishonest scales are an abomination to the LORD, but a just weight is His delight.”

• By singling out the scales first, God pinpoints the heart: the measuring device represents motive.

• Notice that in Ezekiel 45 the context is the restored land and temple. God is preparing His people for worship in a future setting, insisting that economic life line up with spiritual life (see Ezekiel 45:9).

• The principle reaches all the way to us: whatever our “scales” are—tools, software, contracts—they must reflect the character of a truthful God (James 1:17).

A just ephah

• An ephah was the standard dry measure for grain or flour—about 22 liters. Commanding “a just ephah” ensures that the quantity promised equals the quantity delivered (Amos 8:5 condemns those who “skimp the measure”).

• The Lord is not satisfied with broad promises of honesty; He drills down to specific units. Micah 6:11 asks, “Can I excuse dishonest scales or bags of false weights?” The implied answer is no.

• Practical takeaways:

– In business, invoices must match the goods shipped.

– In ministry, attendance or donation figures must never be inflated for appearance’s sake.

– In the home, representations of our time, talent, and resources need the same precision.

• God links the ephah to worship offerings in Ezekiel 45:13-17. Shortchanging a neighbor would ultimately shortchange the Lord.

And a just bath

• A bath measured liquids—about 22 liters as well. Pairing it with the ephah shows God’s comprehensive concern: whether dry or wet, visible or hidden, all transactions come under His eye (Job 31:4).

• 1 Peter 2:12 urges believers to “conduct yourselves honorably among the Gentiles.” Honest measures with both solids and liquids safeguard our witness.

• In daily life:

– Accurate fuel pumps at the gas station.

– Truthful reporting of digital data usage.

– Consistency in charitable disbursements.

• Luke 19:8 illustrates the transformation God seeks. Zacchaeus, once a dishonest tax collector, volunteers to repay fourfold. A “just bath” in action.

summary

Ezekiel 45:10 teaches that authentic worship requires authentic weights. God commands truthful tools (“honest scales”), truthful quantities of solids (“a just ephah”), and truthful quantities of liquids (“a just bath”). From ancient markets to modern spreadsheets, His standard remains: integrity in every measurement because the Measuring God will one day weigh us.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - The exhortation addressed to the princes to practice justice and judgment now extends itself so as to include their subjects, who are required, in all their commercial dealings, to have just balances and just measures - a just ephah for dry goods, and a just bath for liquids (compare the prescriptions in Leviticus 19:35, 36 and Deuteronomy 25:13 - 15, and contrast the practices in Hosea 12:7; Amos 8:5; Micah 6:10, 11; see also Proverbs 16:11).
