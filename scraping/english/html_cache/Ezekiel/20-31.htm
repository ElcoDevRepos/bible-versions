Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
When you offer your gifts, sacrificing your sons in the fire
This phrase refers to the detestable practice of child sacrifice, which was prevalent among the Canaanite religions, particularly in the worship of the god Molech. This practice was strictly forbidden in the Mosaic Law (Leviticus 18:21, Deuteronomy 12:31). The Israelites' participation in such rituals indicates a severe departure from their covenant with Yahweh, reflecting a syncretism with pagan practices. This act of offering gifts in such a manner was seen as a profound violation of the sanctity of life and a direct affront to God’s commandments.

you continue to defile yourselves with all your idols to this day
Idolatry was a persistent issue for Israel, as seen throughout the Old Testament. The worship of idols was not merely a religious misstep but a moral and spiritual defilement that corrupted the people and their relationship with God. The phrase "to this day" underscores the ongoing nature of their rebellion, highlighting a pattern of unfaithfulness that persisted despite numerous warnings from prophets. This defilement through idolatry is a recurring theme in Ezekiel, emphasizing the need for repentance and return to true worship.

So should I be consulted by you, O house of Israel?
This rhetorical question underscores the audacity of the Israelites in seeking guidance from God while simultaneously engaging in practices that are abhorrent to Him. It reflects the hypocrisy of attempting to maintain a relationship with God while living in blatant disobedience. The "house of Israel" is a collective term for the nation, indicating that this was a widespread issue, not limited to a few individuals. The question challenges the sincerity of their faith and their understanding of what it means to be in covenant with God.

As surely as I live, declares the Lord GOD
This phrase is an oath formula, emphasizing the certainty and seriousness of God's declaration. It is a solemn affirmation of His authority and the truth of His words. The use of "as surely as I live" underscores the eternal and unchanging nature of God, contrasting with the fickle and transient nature of human faithfulness. It serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and His ultimate control over the affairs of humanity.

I will not be consulted by you!
God's refusal to be consulted by the Israelites is a direct consequence of their persistent idolatry and rebellion. It signifies a withdrawal of His guidance and favor, a severe judgment for a people who have continually rejected His ways. This statement reflects the broken relationship between God and Israel, a theme that runs throughout the book of Ezekiel. It serves as a warning of the consequences of disobedience and the importance of genuine repentance and faithfulness to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet during the Babylonian exile, tasked with delivering God's messages to the Israelites.

2. House of Israel
Refers to the collective people of Israel, who were in rebellion against God during this period.

3. Idolatry
The worship of idols, which was a significant issue for the Israelites, leading them away from God.

4. Sacrifice of Sons
A reference to the abominable practice of child sacrifice, which some Israelites engaged in, influenced by surrounding pagan cultures.

5. The Lord GOD
The sovereign God of Israel, who is addressing the people's unfaithfulness and idolatry.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Idolatry
Idolatry leads to spiritual blindness and moral decay. We must guard our hearts against modern forms of idolatry, such as materialism or self-worship.

The Sanctity of Life
The practice of child sacrifice underscores the value God places on human life. We are called to protect and cherish life in all its forms.

God's Righteous Judgment
God’s refusal to be consulted by the Israelites highlights His holiness and the seriousness of sin. We must approach God with reverence and repentance.

The Call to Repentance
Despite Israel's unfaithfulness, God continually calls His people to repentance. We are invited to turn back to God, no matter how far we have strayed.

The Importance of Obedience
Obedience to God's commands is crucial for a faithful relationship with Him. We are encouraged to live according to His Word.

What Does Ezekiel 20:31 Mean
When you offer your gifts

• Israel kept bringing offerings meant for the Lord, yet the formality was empty because the heart was corrupt (Isaiah 1:11-13; Amos 5:21-23).

• The outward act of “gifts” could not mask inner rebellion; God always asks for obedience over ritual (1 Samuel 15:22; Micah 6:6-8).

• Even today, external religion without genuine devotion remains offensive to God (Matthew 15:8).

Sacrificing your sons in the fire

• This refers to child sacrifice to Molech, a practice God had explicitly forbidden (Leviticus 18:21; Deuteronomy 12:31).

• Such cruelty showed how far the nation had drifted; they adopted the darkest customs of the surrounding culture (2 Kings 16:3; Jeremiah 32:35).

• The horror underscores that sin blinds and hardens, leading people to do the unthinkable while believing they are still religious (Romans 1:24-25).

You continue to defile yourselves with all your idols to this day

• Ezekiel delivered this message during exile, yet idolatry still persisted in hearts and homes (Ezekiel 14:3-5; 23:30).

• Defilement was not a past event but an ongoing pattern; sin unconfessed compounds over time (Psalm 106:36-39; Hosea 4:17).

• The phrase “to this day” warns that habitual sin, if unchecked, can become a generational stronghold (Exodus 20:5-6).

So should I be consulted by you, O house of Israel?

• God questions the logic of people seeking guidance while clinging to sin (Proverbs 1:28-30; Isaiah 59:1-2).

• Unrepentant idolatry blocks fellowship; prayer becomes hypocrisy when hearts remain obstinate (Psalm 66:18; Ezekiel 20:3).

• Genuine consultation with God requires surrender and repentance, not mere inquiry (James 4:8-10).

As surely as I live, declares the Lord GOD, I will not be consulted by you!

• The solemn oath “as surely as I live” stresses the certainty of God’s refusal (Hebrews 6:13).

• Divine silence is a grave judgment: when God stops speaking, disaster soon follows (1 Samuel 28:6; Lamentations 2:9).

• Yet this refusal is disciplinary, meant to drive the people to repentance so the relationship can be restored (Ezekiel 33:11; Hosea 5:15).

summary

Ezekiel 20:31 exposes the hypocrisy of presenting offerings while committing the most detestable sins, including child sacrifice. God calls out the ongoing defilement of idolatry and declares that unrepentant hearts forfeit the privilege of His counsel. The passage warns that external worship cannot coexist with hidden rebellion; it urges genuine repentance so that fellowship with the living God can be renewed.
