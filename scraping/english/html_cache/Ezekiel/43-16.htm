The altar hearth shall be square at its four corners
The design of the altar hearth being square at its four corners reflects the precision and orderliness that God commands in worship. The square shape symbolizes stability and completeness, often associated with the divine order. In biblical architecture, the square is a common motif, seen in the design of the tabernacle and the New Jerusalem (Revelation 21:16). The four corners may also represent the universality of God's covenant, reaching to the four corners of the earth, indicating that the worship of God is intended for all nations.

twelve cubits long and twelve cubits wide
The dimensions of twelve cubits by twelve cubits are significant in biblical numerology. The number twelve is often associated with God's people, as seen in the twelve tribes of Israel and the twelve apostles. This measurement underscores the connection between the altar and the covenant community of Israel. It also points to the completeness and perfection of God's plan for His people. The use of cubits, an ancient measurement, ties the altar to the historical and cultural context of the Israelites, emphasizing the continuity of God's presence and worship from the past to the future.

What Does Ezekiel 43:16 Mean
The altar hearth

• Ezekiel is shown “the altar hearth”—the very surface on which sacrifices will burn (Ezekiel 43:13). In the Mosaic system the altar was the center of worship; here, in the future temple vision, it still occupies center stage.

• God’s fire fell on earlier altars to confirm His pleasure, as with Elijah on Carmel (1 Kings 18:38) and at the tabernacle’s dedication (Leviticus 9:24). By specifying a hearth, the Lord reminds His people that atonement continues to require a place where sin is faced and covered.

• Hebrews 13:10 says, “We have an altar from which those who serve at the tabernacle have no right to eat.” Christ fulfills the meaning, yet a literal altar in the millennial temple will visibly point back to the cross just as Old Testament offerings pointed forward.

Shall be square

• Shape matters to God. Just as the bronze altar in the wilderness “shall be square” (Exodus 27:1), this hearth is perfectly symmetrical.

• A square conveys balance, stability, and wholeness—qualities essential for an unshakable meeting place between God and man (Psalm 102:25–27).

• The New Jerusalem is also laid out as a perfect square (Revelation 21:16), underscoring the continuity of God’s design from earthly altar to eternal city.

At its four corners

• Mentioning the corners emphasizes total coverage; no part of the hearth is left out (cf. Exodus 30:2, where horns extend from each corner).

• Scripture often uses “four corners” to picture the whole earth (Isaiah 11:12; Revelation 7:1). In the same way, this altar proclaims that God’s offer of cleansing reaches every direction, embracing all who come (John 1:29; Acts 10:34-35).

• The visibility of four corners invites worshipers to draw near from every side, echoing Solomon’s prayer that “all the peoples of the earth may know Your name” (1 Kings 8:60).

Twelve cubits long and twelve cubits wide

• Twelve cubits—about 21 feet—are literal measurements, not approximations. God is giving blueprints for a real altar in a real future temple (Ezekiel 40 – 48).

• The number twelve signals covenant fullness:

– Twelve tribes encamped around the tabernacle (Numbers 2).

– Jesus chose twelve apostles to sit on twelve thrones (Matthew 19:28).

– The heavenly city has twelve gates and twelve foundations (Revelation 21:12-14).

• By matching length and width, the altar forms a perfect square. This symmetry communicates that God’s provision for sin is complete and equitable—no side extended, none diminished (Romans 2:11).

• The large size assures capacity. In the millennial kingdom, multitudes will stream to Jerusalem (Isaiah 2:2-3); the altar’s generous dimensions guarantee room for every offering brought in grateful remembrance of Christ’s once-for-all sacrifice (Hebrews 10:10).

summary

Ezekiel 43:16 reveals that the coming temple’s altar is precisely designed by God: a sizable, perfectly square hearth with four distinct corners, measuring twelve cubits on each side. Every detail underscores certainty—atonement has a fixed, reliable place; completeness—the square shape and twelve-by-twelve dimensions mirror covenant fullness; and universality—the four corners declare that cleansing through the Messiah extends in every direction. The verse affirms that God’s redemptive plan is exact, ample, and open to all who will draw near.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 16, 17. - The measurements that now begin concern the breadth of the altar, and proceed from above downwards. First the altar, or, hearth of God (Hebrew, ariel) was twelve cubits long and twelve broad, i.e. was square in the four squares (or, sides) thereof, or a perfect square (comp. Exodus 27:1; Revelation 21:16). Next the settle, or, enclosure (Hebrew, הָעֲזָרָה) of ver. 14, was fourteen cubits long, and fourteen broad in the four squares (or, sides) thereof; the fourteen being made up of the twelve cubits of the altar-hearth's side with one cubit of ledge from the settle all round. The only question is to which "settle," the upper or the under, reference is made. Some expositors, identifying the greater Azarah with the Harel, i.e. the "upper settle," with "the mount of God" or the base of the hearth, make the altar height only seven cubits from the ground to the hearth. The general belief, however, is that they cannot be so identified. Among interpreters who distinguish them, Kliefoth, with whom Smend agrees, holds the "settle" in this verse to be the harel, or "mount of God," which extended (Smend says with a hek. or "gutter") one cubit on each side beyond the ariel, or "hearth of God," so that the "mount of God," on which the" hearth of God" rested, was fourteen cubits square. Then, assuming a similar extension of one cubit at each stage - in the greater azarah, the lesser azarah, and the hek, or ground bottom - he finds the surface of the greater azarah to be sixteen, of the lesser azarah eighteen, and of the ground bottom twenty cubits square. Keil, with whom Schroder and Currey agree, objects to this as involving too much of arbitrary assumption, and takes the" settle" of this verse to mean the lower azarah; so that no additional measurements are required beyond those given in the text. If the square surface of the greater azarah be considered as having been the same as that of the harel, so that their sides were continuous, then, as the "ground bottom" extended one cubit on each side beyond the lower azarsh, the altar at its base was a square of sixteen cubits. Comparing now these measurements with those of the altar of burnt offering in the tabernacle and the temple, one finds that the former was only five cubits square and three cubits high (Exodus 27:1), while the latter was twenty cubits broad, but only ten cubits high (2 Chronicles 4:1), which awakes the suspicion that the different views above noted have been insensibly influenced by a desire on the part of their authors to make them harmonize with the measurements of the temple. But there does not appear sufficient reason why the measurements of Ezekiel's altar should have agreed with those of Solomon's rather than with those of Moses', The border (or, parapet) of half a cubit which ran round the ledge, or bottom, of a cubit, at the foot of the lower azarah was clearly designed, not for the protection of the priest officiating, but for ornament. The stairs (or, steps), mention of which closes the description, mark a departure, not from the pattern of the Solomonic temple, in which the altar must have had steps (see Keil's 'Biblische Archaologie,' p. 141), but from the pattern of the tabernacle, in which altar-steps were disallowed (Exodus 20:26) and did not exist (Exodus 38:1-7). But if, as Jewish tradition asserts, the pest-exilic altar had no steps as Ezekiel's had, having been reached by an inclined plane, because in the so-called book of the covenant steps were forbidden, how does this harmonize with the theory that Ezekiel's vision temple was designed as a model for the post-exilic temple? And why, if the priest-code was the composition of a writer who worked in the spirit and on the lines of Ezekiel, should it have omitted to assign steps to the tabernacle altar?
