Therefore tell them that this is what the Lord GOD says:
This phrase emphasizes the authority of the message, coming directly from the Lord GOD, indicating divine intervention. In the context of Ezekiel, a prophet during the Babylonian exile, this underscores the seriousness of the message. Prophets often served as God's mouthpieces, delivering messages of warning, judgment, or hope. The use of "Lord GOD" (Adonai Yahweh) highlights God's sovereignty and covenant relationship with Israel.

‘I will put an end to this proverb,
The proverb in question, "The days go by and every vision comes to nothing," reflects a skeptical attitude among the Israelites who doubted the fulfillment of prophetic visions. This skepticism arose during a time of prolonged waiting and suffering in exile. By declaring an end to this proverb, God is asserting that His words and prophecies are true and will come to pass, countering the disbelief and cynicism of the people.

and in Israel they will no longer recite it.’
This indicates a change in the collective mindset of the Israelites. The cessation of the proverb signifies a shift from doubt to recognition of God's active role in history. Israel, representing God's chosen people, will witness the fulfillment of prophecies, leading to a renewed understanding of God's faithfulness and power. This also serves as a warning to those who dismiss God's words.

But say to them: ‘The days are at hand
This phrase conveys urgency and immediacy. The "days" refer to the time when God's prophetic words will be realized. In the historical context, this points to the impending judgment and events that Ezekiel prophesied, such as the fall of Jerusalem. Theologically, it underscores the concept of God's timing, which may seem delayed to humans but is perfectly orchestrated in His divine plan.

when every vision will be fulfilled.
This assurance of fulfillment highlights God's faithfulness to His word. The visions given to prophets like Ezekiel were not mere predictions but divine revelations that would come to pass. This phrase connects to other scriptures where God's promises are fulfilled, such as the coming of the Messiah, Jesus Christ, who embodies the ultimate fulfillment of God's redemptive plan. It reassures believers of the certainty of God's promises, encouraging faith and trust in His divine will.

Teaching Points
God's Word is Certain
Despite human skepticism, God's promises and prophecies are sure and will come to pass in His timing.

The Danger of Cynicism
The Israelites' use of the proverb reflects a cynical attitude that can lead to spiritual complacency and disbelief.

The Urgency of Repentance
With the fulfillment of God's visions being near, there is an urgent call for repentance and readiness.

Trust in God's Timing
Believers are encouraged to trust in God's perfect timing, even when it seems delayed from a human perspective.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Ezekiel serve as God's messengers, and their words should be heeded as they convey divine truth.

What Does Ezekiel 12:23 Mean
Therefore tell them that this is what the Lord GOD says

God instructs Ezekiel to confront popular skepticism with a firm “thus says the LORD.”

• Divine speech carries final authority; compare Jeremiah 1:7–9 and 2 Timothy 3:16, where God assures that His word is delivered exactly as intended.

• The command “tell them” reminds us that revelation demands proclamation, not silence (Amos 3:7).

• By addressing the people directly, the Lord removes any excuse that they “didn’t know” (Romans 1:20).

Application: When God speaks, debate ends. Our task is to listen, receive, and relay the message unchanged (Deuteronomy 4:2).

I will put an end to this proverb

Israel had coined a cynical saying that visions dragged on without fulfillment (see Ezekiel 12:22; also Ezekiel 11:3). God declares He will silence that mockery.

• The Lord defends His reputation (Isaiah 48:11).

• Proverbs that contradict truth crumble before the God who cannot lie (Numbers 23:19; Titus 1:2).

• The decisive “I will” underscores divine initiative; the people’s unbelief cannot delay His purposes (Romans 3:3–4).

Application: God terminates unbelieving narratives. Modern doubts (“Nothing ever changes,” “Judgment won’t come”) will likewise be shattered when the Lord acts (2 Peter 3:3–10).

and in Israel they will no longer recite it

Once God moves, the false proverb becomes obsolete—no one dares repeat it.

• Similar nationwide shifts occurred after Elijah’s Mount Carmel victory, when Israel cried, “The LORD, He is God!” (1 Kings 18:39).

• Collective memory is corrected by God’s unmistakable intervention (Psalm 126:1–2).

Application: Testimonies replace taunts when people see God fulfill His word (Luke 1:20, 64). Expect Him to rewrite cultural slogans that oppose truth.

The days are at hand when every vision will be fulfilled

The Lord promises immediate realization of prophetic warnings—no more delay.

• Ezekiel repeats this theme in 12:25,28; compare Habakkuk 2:3, where the vision “hastens toward the goal.”

• Historically, Babylon soon besieged Jerusalem (2 Kings 25), validating Ezekiel’s visions.

• God’s timetable may seem slow to us, but it is perfect (2 Peter 3:9). When He signals “at hand,” urgency is warranted (Mark 1:15; Revelation 1:3).

Application: Because God keeps every promise, we live watchfully, knowing that both judgment and restoration arrive exactly as foretold (Matthew 24:42, 44).

summary

Ezekiel 12:23 dismantles Israel’s dismissive proverb by asserting God’s immediate and unstoppable fulfillment of every prophetic vision. He authorizes Ezekiel to announce His word, ends the cynical saying, ensures it will never be repeated, and affirms that the time for fulfillment is now. Doubt is silenced, divine credibility is upheld, and God’s people are called to trust and obey without delay.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) The effect of every vision.--The sense would be made clearer by rendering "the accomplishment" of every vision.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - The prophet meets the current proverb with a counter proverb of his own: "The days are not far off, but have come near." Compare the language of the Baptist (Matthew 3:2), of our Lord (Matthew 4:17), of St. Paul (Romans 13:11). For the true prophet there is always a near fulfilment, though there may be also an ultimate and more complete reality of which that is the pledge and earnest. The "vision" shall not fail; every word (so in the Hebrew) shall become a reality.
