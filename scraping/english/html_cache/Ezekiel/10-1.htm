Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And I looked
This phrase indicates the prophet Ezekiel's active engagement in the vision. Ezekiel is often referred to as a "watchman" (Ezekiel 3:17), emphasizing his role in observing and conveying God's messages to the people of Israel. His visions are a crucial part of his prophetic ministry, providing insight into divine realities.

and saw above the expanse
The "expanse" refers to the firmament or sky, reminiscent of the creation account in Genesis 1:6-8. In Ezekiel's vision, the expanse signifies a separation between the earthly realm and the divine, highlighting the transcendence of God. This imagery is consistent with other biblical visions, such as in Daniel 7:9-10, where the heavenly court is depicted.

above the heads of the cherubim
Cherubim are angelic beings associated with the presence and glory of God. They are first mentioned in Genesis 3:24, guarding the way to the Tree of Life. In the context of the temple, cherubim are depicted as guardians of the divine presence (Exodus 25:18-22). Ezekiel's vision emphasizes the cherubim's role in supporting the divine throne, symbolizing God's majesty and holiness.

the likeness of a throne
The throne represents God's sovereign rule and authority. In biblical visions, the throne is a central element, as seen in Isaiah 6:1 and Revelation 4:2. The "likeness" suggests that Ezekiel is witnessing a representation of divine reality, emphasizing the mystery and majesty of God's presence.

of sapphire
Sapphire is a precious stone often associated with divine beauty and purity. In Exodus 24:10, the pavement under God's feet is described as being like sapphire, indicating the glory and holiness of God's presence. The use of sapphire in Ezekiel's vision underscores the splendor and transcendence of the divine throne, pointing to the incomparable nature of God's kingdom.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
The prophet and priest who is the author of the book. He is in exile in Babylon and receives visions from God.

2. Cherubim
Angelic beings associated with the presence and glory of God. In Ezekiel's vision, they are seen as part of the divine chariot.

3. Expanse
The firmament or sky above the cherubim, symbolizing the separation between the divine and earthly realms.

4. Sapphire Stone
A precious stone, often associated with divine presence and authority. In this vision, it resembles a throne, indicating God's sovereignty.

5. Throne
Represents God's authority and rule. The appearance of the throne above the cherubim signifies God's supreme power and presence.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty
The vision of the throne above the cherubim reminds us of God's ultimate authority over all creation. We are called to trust in His sovereign will, even when circumstances seem uncertain.

Divine Presence
The sapphire stone and the throne symbolize God's holy presence. As believers, we are invited to seek and dwell in His presence through prayer and worship.

Holiness and Reverence
The imagery of the cherubim and the throne calls us to approach God with reverence and awe, recognizing His holiness and majesty.

Vision and Obedience
Ezekiel's vision encourages us to be attentive to God's revelations in our lives and to respond with obedience and faithfulness.

Heavenly Perspective
The expanse above the cherubim serves as a reminder to maintain a heavenly perspective, focusing on eternal truths rather than temporary earthly concerns.

What Does Ezekiel 10:1 Mean
And I looked

– Ezekiel records a deliberate, conscious observation: “I looked.” The prophet is not dreaming; he is witnessing real events unfolding in real time.

– Scripture frequently highlights a servant of God “looking” to receive revelation (Exodus 3:2; Daniel 7:2; Acts 10:4). Each time, the gaze signals readiness and obedience. Ezekiel, standing firmly on the reliability of God’s Word, reminds us that divine visions are factual, not fanciful.

and saw above the expanse

– The “expanse” (firmament) recalls Genesis 1:6–8, the solid canopy God formed to separate waters. Ezekiel tells us this same firmament is present in the heavenly realm, anchoring the vision in the literal structure of creation.

– John likewise sees an expanse “like crystal” before God’s throne (Revelation 4:6). Both men describe the same reality from two vantage points, affirming the consistency of Scripture.

above the heads of the cherubim

– Cherubim appear first guarding Eden (Genesis 3:24) and later carved upon the mercy seat (Exodus 25:18–22). In every scene they serve, guard, and uphold God’s holiness.

– Here, their heads lie beneath the expanse, which in turn supports a higher glory. This order stresses that even mighty cherubim are subordinate to the Sovereign enthroned above (Psalm 99:1).

the likeness of a throne

– A “likeness” indicates that the throne, while real, transcends human vocabulary. Ezekiel employs comparison, yet he clearly sees an actual seat of authority.

– Thrones signify rule (1 Kings 22:19; Isaiah 6:1). By placing a throne at the center, God declares His unchallenged dominion over Israel’s circumstances—especially vital as Jerusalem nears judgment (Ezekiel 10:18).

of sapphire

– Sapphire, a deep, brilliant blue, evokes heaven’s purity and majesty (Exodus 24:10). The gemstone’s durability speaks of the permanence of God’s rule.

– Revelation 21:19 lists sapphire among the foundations of the New Jerusalem, linking Ezekiel’s vision to the future kingdom where God dwells with His people forever.

summary

Ezekiel physically beholds the true heavenly court: above the firmament, above exalted cherubim, rests a sapphire throne—the unmistakable seat of the living God. Each detail underscores His supremacy, holiness, and unchanging authority. Amid national collapse and impending exile, the vision assures God’s people that the Lord remains enthroned, active, and unshaken.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) As it were a sapphire stone.--Comp. Ezekiel 1:26. No mention is here made of a being upon the throne, but it is implied by the "he spake" of the following verse. The word cherubim corresponds throughout this chapter to the living creatures of Ezekiel 1.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1, 2. - Then I looked, etc. There follows on the work of judgment another theophany, like that of Ezekiel 1:15-28. In the "expanse," or firmament, like the "terrible crystal," there is seen as before the likeness of a sapphire throne (see Ezekiel 1:26, note). The form of the man who is the manifestation of Jehovah is implied, though not named. It is he who speaks to the captain of the six ministers of vengeance, himself the seventh, and bids him go in beneath the "whirling wheels" that are beneath the cherub (collective singular, as in Ezekiel 9:9), and fill his hands with coals of fire (Ezekiel 1:13), and scatter them over the city, as the symbol of its doom. We are reminded of Isaiah's vision (Isaiah 6:6); but there the work of the fire was to purify, here simply to destroy. Vers 3, 4. - Now the cherubim stood, etc. The position of the cherubim is defined, with a vivid distinctness of detail, which once more reminds us of Dante. They had been standing on the right, i.e. the southern side of the sanctuary. What follows is probably a reproduction of the change of positions described in Ezekiel 9:3, and the verbs should be taken, therefore, as pluperfects. The cloud of glory, as in 1 Kings 8:10, 11 and Isaiah 6:1, 2, the Shechinah, that was the taken of the Divine presence, filled the court, but the glory itself had moved to the threshold at the first stage of its departure.
