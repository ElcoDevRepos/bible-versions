For they were arranged in three stories
The arrangement of the chambers in three stories reflects the architectural design common in ancient Near Eastern structures, particularly in temple complexes. This tripartite structure may symbolize completeness or divine order, as the number three often holds significance in biblical numerology. The three stories could also represent different levels of holiness or function within the temple precincts, similar to the divisions seen in the Tabernacle and Solomon's Temple.

and unlike the courts, they had no pillars
The absence of pillars in these chambers contrasts with other parts of the temple, such as the courts, which often featured pillars as structural and symbolic elements. Pillars in biblical architecture can symbolize strength and stability, as seen in the pillars of Jachin and Boaz in Solomon's Temple (1 Kings 7:21). The lack of pillars here might indicate a different purpose or function for these chambers, possibly related to storage or priestly activities rather than public worship.

So the upper chambers were set back further than the lower and middle floors
The setback design of the upper chambers suggests a tiered structure, which could have practical purposes such as allowing light and air to reach each level. This architectural feature might also symbolize a progression towards greater sanctity or separation from the profane, as one moves upward. The concept of ascending to higher levels can be seen in other biblical contexts, such as the ascent to the mountain of the Lord (Psalm 24:3) or the spiritual ascent in the Christian life (Philippians 3:14).

The intricate details of the temple's design reflect God's attention to detail and His specific plans for His people. We can trust that God has a detailed plan for our lives.

The absence of pillars in the upper chambers may symbolize reliance on God's strength rather than human support. In our lives, we should depend on God's strength rather than our own.

The three accounts of the chambers can symbolize the progressive revelation of God's plan throughout history, culminating in Christ. We should seek to understand God's unfolding plan in our lives.

What Does Ezekiel 42:6 Mean
Three stories arranged

“For they were arranged in three stories”

• Ezekiel is recording an exact architectural detail of the temple’s side-chambers, underscoring that God’s designs are orderly and intentional (cf. Exodus 25:9; 1 Chronicles 28:11-12).

• Three levels echo the pattern in Solomon’s temple, where chambers were also stacked vertically (1 Kings 6:5-6). This repetition signals continuity: God’s holiness and provision for priestly ministry remain constant from the first temple to Ezekiel’s future temple.

• Practically, multiple stories maximize sacred space without expanding the footprint—an efficient, divinely sanctioned design (Ezekiel 41:6).

No pillars like the courts

“…and unlike the courts, they had no pillars.”

• Outer courts in ancient temples often featured colonnades (2 Chronicles 3:4; John 10:23). By contrast, these chambers rely on the surrounding walls for support.

• The absence of pillars highlights separation: the courts accommodate crowds and public worship, but these inner rooms are reserved for priests and holy articles (Ezekiel 42:13-14).

• It also points to God’s sufficiency. The chambers stand firm without visible columns, a subtle reminder that the Lord Himself upholds His house (Psalm 127:1; Colossians 1:17).

Upper chambers set back

“So the upper chambers were set back further than the lower and middle floors.”

• As the stories rise, each floor recedes, creating wider lower rooms and narrower upper rooms—just as in Solomon’s temple where “the structure narrowed as it ascended” (1 Kings 6:6).

• This stepped design provides stable support: each higher level rests partly on the wall of the level below, offsetting weight without external pillars (Ezekiel 41:7).

• Spiritually, the upward progression that grows increasingly restricted pictures ascending holiness—the closer one moves toward God’s glory, the narrower the way (Matthew 7:14; Hebrews 10:19-22).

summary

Ezekiel 42:6 gives a literal snapshot of future temple side-chambers: three stacked levels, no supporting pillars, and tiered setbacks. These details affirm God’s precise blueprint, connect with earlier temple patterns, and symbolically portray stability, consecration, and the narrowing path into His holy presence.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) As the pillars of the courts.--This statement is introduced to show that as there was no external support for the galleries, they must have been taken from the width of the chambers; but it gives incidentally the interesting information that there were pillars in the courts. These could not have been the ornamental pillars at the entrance of the various porches, for the connection implies that they supported something. It is quite likely, therefore, that there were cloisters around the inside of the wall of the courts (on the pavement), as in the later Temple.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6 supplies the reason for this shortening of the upper stories. The chambers had not pillars (see on Ezekiel 40:49) as the courts had. Though it is not otherwise stated, these appear to have had colonnades like these in the Herodian (Josephus, 'Aut.,' 15. 11. 5) and probably also the Solomonic temple (Acts 3:11); and hence the second and third stories required to recede in order to find supports for their respective galleries.
