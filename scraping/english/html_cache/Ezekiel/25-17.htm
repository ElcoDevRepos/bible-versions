Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I will execute great vengeance against them
This phrase indicates God's direct intervention and judgment. In the context of Ezekiel 25, God is speaking against the nations surrounding Israel, specifically the Ammonites, Moabites, Edomites, and Philistines. These nations had rejoiced over Israel's misfortunes, and God is declaring His intention to punish them. The concept of divine vengeance is consistent with the Old Testament portrayal of God as a just judge who defends His people and upholds His covenant. This vengeance is not arbitrary but a response to the nations' actions against Israel, reflecting the principle of divine retribution found throughout scripture (e.g., Deuteronomy 32:35).

with furious reproof
The term "furious reproof" emphasizes the intensity and righteousness of God's judgment. It suggests a corrective action intended to address the wrongs committed by these nations. The use of "furious" underscores the seriousness of their offenses and the depth of God's anger. This aligns with the biblical theme that God is slow to anger but will act decisively against persistent sin (Exodus 34:6-7). The reproof is not only punitive but also serves as a warning to others about the consequences of opposing God's will.

Then they will know that I am the LORD
This phrase is a recurring theme in Ezekiel, highlighting the purpose behind God's actions. The recognition of God's sovereignty and identity is a central goal of His judgments. By executing vengeance, God reveals His power and authority, compelling the nations to acknowledge Him as the one true God. This acknowledgment is not merely intellectual but involves a recognition of His rightful place as the ruler of all nations. The phrase echoes the broader biblical narrative where God's actions in history serve to reveal His character and establish His kingdom (e.g., Exodus 7:5).

when I lay My vengeance upon them.’”
The final phrase reiterates the certainty and personal nature of God's judgment. The use of "My vengeance" indicates that this is not a random act of violence but a deliberate and just response from God Himself. It underscores His active role in history and His commitment to justice. This assurance of divine intervention provides comfort to the faithful, affirming that God will ultimately vindicate His people and uphold His covenant promises. The prophetic nature of this statement also points to the eschatological hope found in the New Testament, where God's final judgment will bring about the full realization of His kingdom (Revelation 19:11-16).

Teaching Points
Understanding Divine Justice
God's vengeance is not arbitrary but a response to sin and injustice. It serves as a reminder of His holiness and righteousness.

God's Sovereignty
The passage emphasizes that God is in control and that His actions are purposeful, leading to the acknowledgment of His lordship.

Trust in God's Timing
Believers are encouraged to trust in God's timing for justice, knowing that He sees all and will act according to His perfect will.

Call to Repentance
The warning of divine vengeance serves as a call to repentance, urging individuals and nations to turn back to God.

Hope in God's Justice
For those who suffer injustice, this passage offers hope that God will ultimately set things right.

What Does Ezekiel 25:17 Mean
I will execute great vengeance against them

- God personally announces His action; He is not delegating vengeance but taking it upon Himself (Deuteronomy 32:35; Romans 12:19).

- “Great” underscores the scale. This is not a mild correction but a decisive intervention, comparable to the plagues on Egypt (Exodus 7–12).

- Because He is holy and just, His vengeance is never excessive or arbitrary; it perfectly matches the gravity of the offense (Psalm 97:2; Revelation 15:3–4).

with furious reproof

- “Furious” points to righteous anger, not loss of control. The LORD’s wrath is pure and purposeful (Nahum 1:2–3).

- “Reproof” shows the corrective aim: exposing sin and bringing the offender face-to-face with divine justice (Proverbs 3:11–12; Hebrews 12:5–6).

- Together, these words reveal both intensity and moral clarity—sin is confronted, and the guilty are unmistakably disciplined (Isaiah 13:11).

Then they will know that I am the LORD

- Recognition of God’s identity is the result God seeks. Judgment that does not lead to revelation would miss the mark (Exodus 14:4; Ezekiel 6:7).

- This knowledge is experiential; the nations learn God’s character by what He does, not merely by what He says (Psalm 9:16).

- Even hostile peoples are brought to acknowledge His sovereignty, fulfilling His larger redemptive plan that every knee will bow (Isaiah 45:22–23; Philippians 2:10–11).

when I lay My vengeance upon them

- The repetition seals certainty: God’s promise moves from declaration to execution (Isaiah 46:10–11).

- “My vengeance” stresses ownership. Human retaliation is forbidden because vengeance belongs to God alone (Leviticus 19:18; 1 Thessalonians 5:15).

- By acting, He vindicates His name, protects His people, and sets a standard for all time that evil will not ultimately prevail (Psalm 94:1–3; Revelation 19:1–2).

summary

Ezekiel 25:17 portrays the LORD’s personal, measured, and righteous vengeance. His intervention is powerful (“great vengeance”), passionate yet controlled (“furious reproof”), purposeful (“then they will know”), and certain (“when I lay My vengeance”). Every element underscores His holiness, justice, and desire that all recognize Him as the sovereign LORD.
