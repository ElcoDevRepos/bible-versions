Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I will multiply the people upon you
This phrase reflects God's promise of restoration and blessing to the land of Israel. Historically, this promise comes after the Babylonian exile, a period of desolation and loss for the Israelites. The multiplication of people signifies not only a return to the land but also a divine blessing of growth and prosperity. This echoes the Abrahamic covenant in Genesis 12:2, where God promises to make Abraham's descendants numerous.

the house of Israel in its entirety
This indicates the reunification of the divided kingdoms of Israel and Judah. The phrase emphasizes the completeness of the restoration, suggesting that all tribes of Israel will be gathered together. This reunification is a recurring theme in prophetic literature, as seen in passages like Jeremiah 31:31-34, which speaks of a new covenant with the whole house of Israel.

The cities will be inhabited
The repopulation of cities signifies a return to normalcy and stability. In the ancient Near East, cities were centers of culture, commerce, and governance. The inhabitation of cities implies economic recovery and social order. This promise is a reversal of the judgment seen in Ezekiel 5:14, where cities were left desolate due to Israel's disobedience.

and the ruins rebuilt
Rebuilding ruins symbolizes restoration and renewal. This is not just physical reconstruction but also spiritual revival. The imagery of rebuilding is prevalent in prophetic texts, such as Isaiah 61:4, which speaks of rebuilding ancient ruins. This can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate restorer and rebuilder of broken lives, as reflected in His ministry of healing and redemption.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel's messages often focused on both judgment and restoration for Israel.

2. House of Israel
Refers to the collective people of Israel, God's chosen nation, who were in exile due to their disobedience.

3. Cities of Israel
The cities that had been destroyed or depopulated due to the Babylonian conquest and exile.

4. Babylonian Exile
The period when the Israelites were taken captive to Babylon, a significant event in Israel's history.

5. Restoration
The promise of God to bring back the people of Israel to their land and restore their cities and communities.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness in Restoration
Despite Israel's disobedience, God remains faithful to His covenant promises. This assures us that God is committed to restoring His people, even after periods of judgment.

The Importance of Repentance and Obedience
The promise of restoration comes after a call to repentance. This highlights the importance of turning back to God and living in obedience to His commands.

Hope in Times of Desolation
Just as God promised to rebuild the ruins of Israel, He can bring restoration and hope to areas of our lives that seem desolate or broken.

Community and Rebuilding
The multiplication of people and rebuilding of cities emphasizes the importance of community and working together in God's plans for restoration.

God's Sovereignty Over Nations
The prophecy underscores God's control over history and nations, reminding us that He can use even difficult circumstances for His purposes.

What Does Ezekiel 36:10 Mean
I will multiply the people upon you

“ I will restore the fortunes of Judah and the cities of Israel and rebuild them as in former times.” (Jeremiah 33:7)

• God speaks directly to the mountains of Israel (Ezekiel 36:8–9), promising a fresh surge of life.

• This multiplication is literal population growth, echoing the covenant promise to Abraham: “I will make you into a great nation” (Genesis 12:2).

• Such language also anticipates the future ingathering foretold in Isaiah 49:19–21, where the land becomes “too small” for all her returning children.

• The imagery assures Israel that the exile’s barrenness will be reversed; the land once stripped of people will overflow again (Zechariah 10:8–10).

—the house of Israel in its entirety

“For there will be a day when watchmen cry out… ‘Arise, let us go up to Zion, to the LORD our God!’ ” (Jeremiah 31:6)

• No tribe or family is left out. The whole covenant people—north and south, Judah and Ephraim—are embraced (Ezekiel 37:15–22).

• This points to God’s unwavering faithfulness: “I will gather you from all the nations” (Deuteronomy 30:3–4).

• The inclusion of every segment anticipates the climactic promise: “all Israel will be saved” (Romans 11:26).

The cities will be inhabited

“Their children will be as in days of old, and their congregation will be established before Me.” (Jeremiah 30:20)

• Empty streets will once again echo with community life—commerce, worship, festivals, daily routine (Zechariah 8:3–5).

• Inhabited cities signify security and peace; enemies no longer dominate (Ezekiel 36:12–13).

• This fulfills earlier warnings reversed: the siege-and-scatter judgments of Leviticus 26:33 find their antidote in gracious restoration (Isaiah 54:14).

and the ruins rebuilt

“They will rebuild the ancient ruins and restore the places long devastated.” (Isaiah 61:4)

• Physical reconstruction mirrors spiritual renewal; stones rise as hearts are renewed (Ezekiel 36:26).

• God not only repairs but beautifies: “I will plant them on their land, and they will never again be uprooted” (Amos 9:14–15).

• The transformation is so dramatic that neighboring nations acknowledge, “This land that was desolate has become like the garden of Eden” (Ezekiel 36:35).

summary

Ezekiel 36:10 promises a sweeping, tangible restoration: a multiplied people, the whole nation welcomed home, cities thrumming with life, and ruins turned into sturdy dwellings. The verse assures Israel—and every reader—of God’s power to reverse loss and fulfill His covenants down to the last stone and the last soul.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 10, 11. - I will multiply men upon you. Jehovah's promise contemplated a return of both sections of the Golah, the whole house of Israel, Ephraim as well as Judah (comp. Ezekiel 20:40), to the land from which they had been deported, and a restoration of the united kingdom to a condition of prosperity in which its cities should again be inhabited, its ruined homesteads repaired, its fields cultivated, and its flocks and herds multiplied (see Ezekiel 16:55; Isaiah 44:26; Isaiah 54:3; Isaiah 61:4) - a condition of prosperity so great that it should surpass any measure or degree of good fortune previously enjoyed (comp. Deuteronomy 30:5; Job 42:12).
