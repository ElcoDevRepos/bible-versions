Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Since, then, it remains for some to enter His rest,
This phrase suggests the ongoing availability of God's rest, a concept rooted in the creation narrative where God rested on the seventh day (Genesis 2:2). The "rest" here is both a present spiritual reality and a future promise, symbolizing salvation and eternal life. The idea of rest is also linked to the Israelites' journey to the Promised Land, which was a type of the ultimate rest believers find in Christ. The use of "remains" indicates that the promise of entering God's rest is still open, emphasizing God's enduring grace and the opportunity for salvation.

and since those who formerly heard the good news did not enter because of their disobedience,
This part references the Israelites who, despite hearing God's promises, failed to enter the Promised Land due to unbelief and disobedience (Numbers 14:22-23). The "good news" they heard was the promise of entering a land flowing with milk and honey, which parallels the gospel message of salvation through Christ. Their failure serves as a warning and a lesson for contemporary believers about the consequences of unbelief and disobedience. This historical context underscores the importance of faith and obedience in the Christian life, as echoed in other scriptures like Psalm 95:7-11, which is also cited earlier in Hebrews 3:7-11.

Persons / Places / Events

The original recipients of God's promise of rest, who failed to enter due to disobedience.

2. The Promised Land
A symbol of God's rest, initially promised to the Israelites.

3. The Author of Hebrews
Traditionally attributed to Paul, though the exact authorship is debated. The author writes to Jewish Christians.

4. The Audience
Jewish Christians who are being encouraged to remain faithful and obedient to enter God's rest.

5. God's Rest
A spiritual state of peace and fulfillment, symbolized by the Promised Land, and ultimately fulfilled in Christ.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Obedience
Disobedience prevented the Israelites from entering God's rest. Believers today must heed this warning and live obediently.

The Promise of Rest Remains
God's promise of rest is still available. It is not limited to a physical land but is a spiritual rest found in Christ.

Faith and Perseverance
Entering God's rest requires faith and perseverance. Believers are encouraged to hold fast to their faith.

The Danger of Unbelief
Just as unbelief led to the Israelites' downfall, it can prevent believers from experiencing God's rest today.

Encouragement to Enter Rest
The passage encourages believers to strive to enter God's rest, emphasizing the urgency and importance of this spiritual goal.

What Does Hebrews 4:6 Mean
Since, then, it remains for some to enter His rest

“Since, then, it remains for some to enter His rest”

• God is still holding the door open. The promise of rest is not a relic of the past (Hebrews 4:1).

• “His rest” points to both present fellowship in Christ (Matthew 11:28-29) and the future, eternal Sabbath with Him (Revelation 14:13).

• The word “some” highlights personal invitation. Just as Joshua eventually led a believing remnant into Canaan (Joshua 21:43-45), God now invites every hearer to step in by faith (Hebrews 4:3).

• This ongoing invitation underscores God’s patience (2 Peter 3:9). The clock hasn’t run out yet, so we can still respond.

and since those who formerly heard the good news did not enter because of their disobedience

“and since those who formerly heard the good news did not enter because of their disobedience”

• The “good news” echoed through the wilderness generation: God would bring them into a land of rest (Exodus 3:17; Numbers 13-14).

• They heard, but hearing alone was not enough. Unbelief produced disobedience (Hebrews 3:16-19).

• Disobedience showed up as

– doubting God’s character (Numbers 14:1-4)

– craving Egypt’s comforts over God’s promise (Acts 7:39)

– testing the Lord through complaint and idolatry (1 Corinthians 10:5-11).

• Their failure stands as a sober warning: the same gospel invitation can be missed today if it is met with a hard heart (Hebrews 4:7; Psalm 95:7-11).

summary

Hebrews 4:6 teaches that God’s promise of rest is wide open, yet entering it hinges on obedient faith. The past generation’s unbelief serves as a mirror: what kept them out can still keep us out. Responding now—trusting Christ and yielding to His voice—brings us into the rest God lovingly prepared and still offers.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) The substance of the preceding verses may be thus expressed: There is a rest of God, into which some are to enter with God,--a rest not yet entered at the time of the wandering in the wilderness, and therefore not that which followed the work of creation,--a rest from which some were excluded because of unbelief. These five particulars are repeated in substance in the present verse: "Seeing, therefore, it is (still) left that some should enter in, and they to whom formerly glad tidings were declared entered not in because of disobedience, He again," &c. "Disobedience"--though Hebrews 4:2 speaks of unbelief as the cause: see Note on Hebrews 3:18. In John 3:36, the transition from "believeth" to "obeyeth" is equally striking.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 6, 7. - Since therefore it remains that some should enter into it, and they to whom the good tidings were before preached entered not in because of disobedience, he again defineth a certain day, saying in David, after so long a time, To-day; as it hath been before said, To-day, if ye will hear his voice, etc. The continued openness of the rest, and the failure of the Israelites of old to enter it, are the reasons why a further day for entering was defined in the psalm. But here the thought is suggested that the Israelites had not finally failed, for that, though those under Moses did so, the next generation under Joshua did attain the promised land. No, it is replied; the rest of the promised land was but a type after all; it was not the true rest of God: otherwise the psalmist could not have still assigned a day for entering it so long after the arrival at Shiloh; -
