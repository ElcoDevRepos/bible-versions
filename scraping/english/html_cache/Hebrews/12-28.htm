Therefore, since we are receiving an unshakable kingdom
This phrase connects to the preceding verses, emphasizing the contrast between the temporary, shakable things of this world and the eternal, unshakable kingdom of God. The "unshakable kingdom" refers to the eternal reign of God, which is a central theme throughout Scripture, including prophecies in Daniel 2:44 and the teachings of Jesus in Matthew 6:33. The concept of an unshakable kingdom would resonate with early Christians facing persecution, offering them hope and assurance of God's ultimate sovereignty and victory.

let us be filled with gratitude
Gratitude is a recurring theme in the New Testament, reflecting a heart transformed by the grace of God. In 1 Thessalonians 5:18, believers are encouraged to give thanks in all circumstances, highlighting gratitude as a fundamental response to God's goodness and provision. This gratitude is not just an emotional response but a recognition of the grace and mercy received through Christ, which should permeate every aspect of a believer's life.

and so worship God acceptably
Worship in the biblical context involves both attitude and action. Acceptable worship is characterized by sincerity, truth, and alignment with God's will, as seen in John 4:24, where Jesus speaks of worshiping in spirit and truth. The Old Testament also provides examples of acceptable and unacceptable worship, such as Cain and Abel's offerings in Genesis 4. This phrase calls believers to examine their hearts and ensure their worship aligns with God's standards, not merely external rituals.

with reverence and awe
Reverence and awe are responses to the holiness and majesty of God. Throughout Scripture, encounters with God often evoke fear and reverence, as seen in Isaiah 6:5 and Revelation 1:17. These responses acknowledge God's transcendence and the believer's humility before Him. The call to worship with reverence and awe serves as a reminder of God's greatness and the appropriate posture of humility and respect in His presence.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Recipients of Hebrews
The letter is addressed to Jewish Christians who were familiar with the Old Testament and were facing persecution, encouraging them to remain steadfast in their faith.

2. The Kingdom of God
Refers to the spiritual reign of God, which is unshakable and eternal, contrasting with earthly kingdoms that are temporary and unstable.

3. Worship
The act of revering and honoring God, which in this context is to be done with gratitude, reverence, and awe.

4. Mount Zion
Earlier in Hebrews 12, Mount Zion is mentioned as a symbol of the heavenly Jerusalem, representing the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises.

5. The Old Covenant vs. The New Covenant
The letter contrasts the old covenant, given at Mount Sinai, with the new covenant, which is established through Jesus Christ and is unshakable.
Teaching Points
Gratitude as a Response to God's Kingdom
Recognize that receiving an unshakable kingdom should fill us with gratitude. This gratitude is not just an emotion but a lifestyle that influences our actions and attitudes.

Worship with Reverence and Awe
Worship is not merely a ritual but a heartfelt response to God's majesty and holiness. Our worship should reflect deep respect and awe for who God is.

The Stability of God's Kingdom
In a world full of uncertainty, believers can find peace and assurance in the stability of God's kingdom. This should encourage us to focus on eternal values rather than temporary circumstances.

Living as Citizens of an Unshakable Kingdom
Our lives should reflect the values of God's kingdom. This includes living with integrity, showing love and compassion, and prioritizing spiritual growth.

Contrast with Earthly Kingdoms
Earthly kingdoms and systems are temporary and often flawed. Understanding the permanence of God's kingdom should shift our focus from worldly pursuits to spiritual ones.

What Does Hebrews 12:28 Mean
Therefore

The little word “therefore” ties verse 28 to the warning just given: “His voice shook the earth then, but now He has promised, ‘Yet once more I will shake not only the earth, but heaven as well’” (Hebrews 12:26). Because the coming judgment will shake everything temporary, we must draw a practical conclusion. Other passages do the same: after rehearsing God’s mercies, Paul writes, “Therefore, I urge you…to present your bodies as a living sacrifice” (Romans 12:1). In Hebrews 10:19–23 the writer likewise says, “Therefore, brothers, since we have confidence…let us draw near.” Each “therefore” moves from doctrine to daily duty.

since we are receiving an unshakable kingdom

• The kingdom God gives cannot be toppled, unlike every earthly empire that has ever risen and fallen (Daniel 2:44; Revelation 11:15).

• “Receiving” is present tense: we taste that kingdom now (Luke 17:21; Colossians 1:13) but will inherit it fully when Christ returns (2 Timothy 4:18).

• Because the kingdom is His gift, we do not build it by our ingenuity; we receive it by grace through faith (Luke 12:32). This certainty steadies us when everything around us trembles.

let us be filled with gratitude

• Gratitude is the first and fitting response to grace. “In everything give thanks, for this is God’s will for you in Christ Jesus” (1 Thessalonians 5:18).

• Thanksgiving redirects our eyes from what is shaking to the One who is secure (Philippians 4:6–7).

• In worship gatherings or private prayer, gratitude keeps praise from becoming routine (Psalm 100:4; Colossians 3:15–17).

and so worship God acceptably

The verse links gratitude to acceptable worship: thankful hearts lead to worship God receives.

• Jesus taught that “true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and in truth” (John 4:23–24); sincerity and biblical fidelity both matter.

• Acceptable worship involves the whole life: “present your bodies as a living sacrifice—this is your spiritual worship” (Romans 12:1).

• God rejects worship offered on our terms (Malachi 1:10–11), but He delights in praise that flows from redeemed hearts (1 Peter 2:5).

with reverence and awe

• Reverence acknowledges God’s holiness; awe feels His greatness. Together they guard us from casual, self-centered religion (Psalm 2:11).

• Proverbs 9:10 calls the fear of the Lord “the beginning of wisdom.” We dare not lose that fear, for the next verse reminds us, “our God is a consuming fire” (Hebrews 12:29).

• Even the early church, filled with joy, “walked in the fear of the Lord” (Acts 9:31). Healthy awe and deep gladness travel together.

summary

Hebrews 12:28 invites believers who stand on unshakable promises to respond with unshakable gratitude, expressed in worship that is both heartfelt and holy. Because God is giving us a kingdom that cannot be moved, our lives should overflow with thanks, reverent wonder, and service that pleases Him now and forever.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) Receiving a kingdom.--These words clearly contain a reference to Daniel 7:18, "The saints of the Most High shall receive the kingdom." Nor can it well be doubted that the closing verses of Haggai 2 are also before the writer's mind; after Hebrews 12:21, which repeats the words of Hebrews 12:6, quoted above, the prophet declares the overthrow of earthly kingdoms, and continues to His servant Zerubbabel the Messianic promise. Christ has made His people kings; and when heaven and earth have passed away, they shall be found heirs of a kingdom that cannot be shaken (Hebrews 2:5-9).

Let us have grace.--Many render the last word thankfulness, but the ordinary translation is preferable. There is for us a "throne of grace" to which we may draw near and "find grace" (Hebrews 4:16). The characteristic of our Christian state is that we "have grace," and have not "fallen back from the grace of God" (Hebrews 12:15). Let us continue in this state and thus be enabled to offer our priestly service unto God (Hebrews 9:14; Hebrews 13:15). . . .
