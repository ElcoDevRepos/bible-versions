Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He was faithful to the One who appointed Him
This phrase highlights the faithfulness of Jesus Christ to God the Father, who appointed Him. The concept of faithfulness is central to the character of Jesus, as seen throughout the New Testament. In John 6:38, Jesus states, "For I have come down from heaven, not to do my own will but the will of him who sent me." This underscores His obedience and commitment to fulfilling the mission given by the Father. The term "appointed" suggests a divine commissioning, similar to the Old Testament prophets and leaders who were chosen by God for specific purposes. Jesus' faithfulness is a model for believers, emphasizing the importance of obedience and dedication to God's calling.

just as Moses was faithful in all God’s house
Moses is presented as a type of Christ, serving as a faithful servant in God's house, which refers to the people of Israel. Numbers 12:7 states, "My servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house." This comparison elevates the status of Jesus, showing that while Moses was a servant, Jesus is the Son over God's house (Hebrews 3:6). The "house" here symbolizes the community of believers, both in the Old Covenant with Israel and the New Covenant with the Church. Moses' faithfulness involved leading the Israelites out of Egypt, receiving the Law, and guiding them through the wilderness, all of which prefigure Christ's redemptive work. The cultural context of Moses' leadership during the Exodus is foundational for understanding the Jewish heritage and the continuity of God's plan through Jesus.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure of the New Testament, recognized as the Son of God and the Savior. In this verse, He is highlighted for His faithfulness to God the Father.

2. God the Father
The One who appointed Jesus. This highlights the divine mission and authority given to Jesus.

3. Moses
A key figure in the Old Testament, known for leading the Israelites out of Egypt and receiving the Law from God. He is noted for his faithfulness in serving God’s house.

4. God’s House
Refers to the people of God, both in the Old Testament (Israel) and the New Testament (the Church). It symbolizes the community of believers over whom both Moses and Jesus have roles.
Teaching Points
Faithfulness as a Divine Attribute
Both Jesus and Moses exemplify faithfulness, a key attribute that believers are called to emulate. Faithfulness involves steadfastness, reliability, and trustworthiness in fulfilling God’s calling.

Jesus as Superior to Moses
While Moses was faithful, Jesus is presented as superior in His role and mission. This calls believers to recognize the supremacy of Christ in their faith and life.

The Role of Faithfulness in Leadership
Just as Moses was faithful in leading Israel, and Jesus in His mission, Christian leaders are called to be faithful in their responsibilities within God’s house.

Encouragement for Believers
The faithfulness of Jesus serves as an encouragement for believers to remain steadfast in their faith, trusting in God’s promises and His appointed leaders.

Understanding Our Role in God’s House
As part of God’s house, believers are encouraged to understand their role and be faithful in their service, contributing to the building up of the community of faith.

What Does Hebrews 3:2 Mean
He was faithful

- Jesus’ earthly life displayed unwavering obedience to the Father. He could say, “I always do what pleases Him” (John 8:29).

- His faithfulness ran from the wilderness temptation (Matthew 4:1-11) to Gethsemane where He prayed, “Not My will, but Yours be done” (Luke 22:42).

- Hebrews had just affirmed that the Son “had to be made like His brothers… so that He might become a merciful and faithful High Priest” (Hebrews 2:17). His fidelity is not abstract; it is proven in real time, under real pressure.

- For believers, this sets a pattern: “Be imitators of God… and walk in love, just as Christ loved us” (Ephesians 5:1-2).

to the One who appointed Him

- The Father “appointed” or commissioned the Son for the mission of redemption. Acts 2:23 speaks of Jesus being “delivered up by God’s set plan and foreknowledge.”

- John 20:21 records Jesus saying, “As the Father has sent Me, I also send you.” The appointment language emphasizes divine authority; the Son’s work is God-initiated, God-approved, and God-empowered.

- This reminds the reader that Jesus did not seize glory on His own terms. Philippians 2:6-8 describes Him humbly submitting to the Father’s plan, even to the point of death on a cross.

just as Moses was faithful

- The comparison is not a critique of Moses but an affirmation: Numbers 12:7 tells us, “My servant Moses… is faithful in all My house.”

- Exodus 40:16 testifies, “Moses did everything just as the LORD had commanded him.” His record of obedience in leading Israel, receiving the Law, and constructing the tabernacle was exemplary.

- By linking Jesus to Moses, the author bridges old-covenant history and new-covenant fulfillment, honoring Moses while preparing to show Christ’s surpassing glory (Hebrews 3:3-6).

in all God’s house

- “House” points to God’s covenant community. Under Moses it referred to Israel; under Christ it expands to all who believe, “whose house we are if we hold fast our confidence” (Hebrews 3:6).

- First Timothy 3:15 calls the church “the house of God, which is the church of the living God.” The image stresses belonging, order, and stewardship.

- Both leaders served faithfully within that same divine household, yet Jesus does so as the Son over the house, while Moses served as a servant within it (Hebrews 3:5-6).

summary

Hebrews 3:2 affirms that the Lord Jesus, fully obedient to the Father who commissioned Him, mirrors—and ultimately surpasses—the faithfulness shown by Moses in caring for God’s people. Christ’s flawless loyalty undergirds our confidence in Him as Savior and High Priest and calls us to the same steadfast devotion within the household of God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) Who was.--Rather, as being; or that He was. Not merely, fix your thought on Jesus; but also (and especially), think of Him as faithful to God (Hebrews 2:17).

Appointed him.--Literally, made Him, an expression which some ancient (Ambrose and other Latin fathers,--apparently also Athanasius) and many modern writers have understood as relating to the creation of the human nature of our Lord. It is probable, however, that 1Samuel 12:6 is in the writer's mind. "It is the Lord that made Moses and Aaron, and that brought your fathers up out of the land of Egypt." As there Samuel speaks of the raising up of Moses and Aaron, constituted by God deliverers of the people; so here our thought must rest on Him who constituted Jesus "Apostle and High Priest."

As also Moses.--These words, which give the key to the following verses, are quoted from Numbers 12:7, where Moses is placed in contrast with prophets in Israel to whom the Lord will make Himself known by vision or dream. "My servant Moses is not so, who is faithful in all mine house. With him will I speak mouth to mouth." The "house" or household is God's. people Israel. To others will God reveal Himself in various ways in regard to the many parts of the house, the many concerns of the household. Throughout the whole house Moses was the recipient of the divine commands, and was faithful--"faithful" (as one of the Targums paraphrases), "as chief of the chiefs of my court."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - Who was faithful (or, as being faithful) to him that appointed (literally, made) him, as also Moses was in all his house. The reference is to what was said of Moses (Numbers 12:7), "My servant Moses is not so, who is faithful in all mine house," and serves aptly to introduce the intended comparison of Christ with him. In respect of faithfulness to him who constituted him in his office, Christ resembles Moses; in respect to his office itself, it is to be shown that he is far above him. Observe

(1) that "his house" means God's house, as' is plain from the text cited, i.e. the house of him who appointed him;

(2) that "in all his house" has reference to Moses only, not to Christ; for the main point of what follows is that Christ is over God's house, not in it, as Moses was. As to the verb ποιήσαντα (translated in A.V. "appointed "), it may have been suggested by 1 Samuel 12:6, where the LXX. reads Κύριος ὁ ποίησας τὸν Μωυσῆν καὶ τὸν Ἀαρὼν, the Hebrew verb being עַשׂה, which seems to mean in this case "constitute," not "create" (so Gesenius). The preceding words, ἀπόστολον καὶ ἀρχιερέα, though it is not necessary to supply them as understood, may be taken here to rule the meaning of ποιήσαντι (cf. for a similar use of the verb without a second accusative following, Mark 3:14, καὶ ἐποίησε δώδεκα. Thus the Arian inference from the word, that Christ is represented as a creature, is groundless. Nor need reference be supposed to his human birth or conception, the temporalis generatio of the man Jesus (Athanasius, Ambrose, and other Fathers). Certainly not to his eternal generation (as Bleek and Lunemann); such reference is foreign to the idea of the passage; nor could the word ποιεῖν with any propriety be so used.
