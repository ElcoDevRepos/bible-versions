Therefore I am terrified in His presence;
Job expresses a deep sense of awe and fear before God. This reflects the biblical theme of the fear of the Lord, which is often associated with wisdom (Proverbs 9:10). In the context of Job, this fear is not just reverence but a profound terror due to the overwhelming power and holiness of God. Job's experience is reminiscent of other biblical figures who encountered God's presence, such as Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:6) and Isaiah's vision of the Lord (Isaiah 6:5). The Hebrew word for "terrified" here conveys a sense of trembling and dread, highlighting the intensity of Job's emotions.

when I consider this,
Job is reflecting deeply on his situation and the nature of God. The act of considering implies a thoughtful and deliberate meditation on God's character and actions. This introspection is a common theme in wisdom literature, where understanding and contemplation are valued (Psalm 77:12). Job's consideration leads him to a greater awareness of God's majesty and his own human limitations. This phrase underscores the importance of reflection in the believer's life, encouraging a thoughtful engagement with one's faith and circumstances.

I fear Him.
The fear of God is a central theme in the book of Job and throughout Scripture. It denotes a profound respect and acknowledgment of God's sovereignty and justice. This fear is not merely an emotional response but a recognition of God's ultimate authority and power. In Job's context, it is a fear born out of his suffering and the realization of God's inscrutable ways. This fear is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are called to work out their salvation with fear and trembling (Philippians 2:12). It also points to the reverence Jesus showed towards the Father, exemplifying perfect submission and obedience (Hebrews 5:7).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Job
A man described as blameless and upright, who feared God and shunned evil. He is the central figure in the Book of Job, experiencing intense suffering and questioning God's justice.

2. God
The Almighty, whose presence and actions are the focus of Job's fear and reverence in this passage.

3. Job's Suffering
The context of Job's statement is his immense suffering, having lost his wealth, children, and health, leading him to a deep existential and spiritual crisis.
Teaching Points
Reverence for God
Job's fear is rooted in a deep reverence for God's power and sovereignty. As believers, we are called to maintain a healthy fear of the Lord, recognizing His authority over our lives.

Understanding God's Sovereignty
Job's fear arises from contemplating God's control over all circumstances. We should trust in God's sovereignty, even when we do not understand our situations.

The Role of Fear in Faith
Fear of God is not about being scared but about having a profound respect and awe for His holiness and justice. This fear should lead us to live righteously and seek His will.

Facing Trials with Faith
Job's response to his trials teaches us to approach our own challenges with faith, acknowledging God's presence and purpose even in suffering.

Reflecting on God's Presence
Like Job, we should take time to consider God's presence in our lives, which can lead to a deeper understanding of His character and our relationship with Him.

What Does Job 23:15 Mean
Therefore I am terrified in His presence

Job’s first words capture the heart-thumping awe that overwhelms anyone who realizes who God truly is.

• Job has just affirmed that God “knows the way I have taken” (v.10); the One who sees every motive now stands before him. The weight of that thought makes him tremble, like Isaiah crying, “Woe to me, for I am ruined!” (Isaiah 6:5) or the psalmist urging, “Tremble, O earth, at the presence of the Lord” (Psalm 114:7).

• This is not panic without purpose but the rightful reaction to perfect holiness (Hebrews 12:28–29).

• Even while defending his innocence, Job bows to God’s sovereign right to examine him, knowing “no creature is hidden from His sight” (Hebrews 4:13).

when I consider this

Reflection intensifies reverence. “This” points back to all Job has been rehearsing: God’s unsearchable wisdom (Job 23:8-10), His fixed decrees (v.14), and His mysterious allowance of suffering.

• Meditation on God’s works often leads to holy fear—“I will consider all Your works and meditate on Your mighty deeds” (Psalm 77:12).

• Job weighs the gap between his limited understanding and God’s limitless rule, echoing Paul’s later cry, “Oh, the depth of the riches…how unsearchable His judgments” (Romans 11:33).

• Honest consideration silences complaints and nurtures humility, just as Moses urged Israel to “acknowledge and take to heart this day that the LORD is God in heaven above and on the earth below” (Deuteronomy 4:39).

I fear Him

Job’s closing confession shows that fear of God is not mere emotion; it is an enduring posture.

• Scripture portrays this fear as foundational—“The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge” (Proverbs 1:7).

• It is a fear that guards against presumption (Ecclesiastes 12:13) and anchors the soul amid suffering: better to stand trembling before God than to stand alone.

• The New Testament echoes the gravity: “It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God” (Hebrews 10:31), yet that same fear coexists with unwavering trust (Psalm 33:18).

summary

Job 23:15 reveals a man awakened to the stunning reality of God’s holy presence. Terror arises because the all-seeing Judge has drawn near; deeper reflection only amplifies that awareness; and the resulting fear is a healthy, lifelong reverence that keeps Job, and us, humble before the Almighty.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) Therefore am I troubled at his presence.--i.e., invisible though it be, and undiscoverable as He is on every hand (Job 23:8-9), Job is in a strait betwixt two (Philippians 1:23). The victim of an ever present paradox and dilemma; afraid of God, yet longing to see Him; conscious of His presence, yet unable to find Him; assured of His absolute justice, and yet convinced of his own suffering innocence. His history, in fact, to the Old World was what the Gospel is to the New: the exhibition of a perfectly righteous man, yet made perfect through suffering. It was therefore an effort, at the solution of the problem of the reconciliation of the inequality of life with the justice of God.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - Therefore am I troubled at his presence. The thought of these further afflictions troubles me, and makes me shrink from his unseen presence. I know not how soon he may lay a fresh burden upon me. When I consider, I am afraid of him. When I reflect on the many forms of suffering which I may still have to undergo, my fears increase, I tremble at the future.
