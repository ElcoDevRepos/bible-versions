For by these He judges the nations
This phrase highlights God's sovereignty and justice over all nations. In the context of Job, Elihu is speaking about God's use of natural phenomena, such as rain and storms, to execute His will. This reflects the biblical theme that God is the ultimate judge of all the earth (Genesis 18:25). The idea of God judging nations is also seen in the prophetic books, such as Isaiah and Jeremiah, where God uses various means to bring about judgment on nations for their sins. This phrase underscores the belief that God's judgments are righteous and purposeful, often serving as a call to repentance.

and provides food in abundance.
This part of the verse emphasizes God's provision and care for His creation. The imagery of God providing food in abundance is reminiscent of the agricultural blessings promised to Israel in Deuteronomy 28:1-14, where obedience to God results in prosperity and abundance. It also connects to the New Testament, where Jesus is seen as the provider, feeding the multitudes (Matthew 14:13-21) and teaching about God's provision in the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 6:25-34). This phrase reassures believers of God's ability to sustain and provide for their needs, reflecting His character as a loving and caring Creator.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Elihu
A young man who speaks to Job and his friends, offering a perspective on God's justice and power. Elihu's speeches are intended to defend God's righteousness and challenge Job's understanding of his suffering.

2. Job
A man of great faith and patience who endures severe trials and suffering. The Book of Job explores his struggle to understand his suffering and maintain his faith in God.

3. Nations
Refers to the various peoples and countries of the world. In this context, it highlights God's sovereignty over all nations and His ability to judge them.

4. God's Provision
The concept that God provides for His creation, ensuring that there is food and sustenance for all living beings.

5. Judgment
The act of God assessing and determining the fate of nations based on their actions and righteousness.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nations
God is in control of all nations and their destinies. He judges them according to His righteousness and purposes.

Divine Provision
God provides abundantly for His creation. We can trust in His provision for our needs, both physical and spiritual.

Understanding God's Justice
God's ways are higher than ours, and His judgments are just. We may not always understand His actions, but we can trust in His perfect justice.

Faith in Times of Uncertainty
In times of uncertainty or when facing national or global challenges, we can have faith that God is in control and will provide for His people.

Responsibility to Seek God
As individuals and nations, we have a responsibility to seek God and align ourselves with His will, knowing that He is the ultimate judge.

What Does Job 36:31 Mean
For by these

• “These” points back to the rain, clouds, lightning, and thunder Elihu has just described (Job 36:27-30).

• God’s mastery over weather is literal and ongoing; every drop of rain, every flash of lightning is governed by His hand (Psalm 147:8; Jeremiah 10:13).

• Scripture treats these natural forces not as random but as purposeful tools in the Lord’s management of His world (Amos 4:7).

He judges the nations

• Judgment is not limited to final, future events; God presently oversees and corrects entire peoples through the same weather patterns that sustain life.

– Droughts, floods, and storms can humble proud societies (Isaiah 19:1-10).

– Prosperous seasons can expose hidden idolatry when gratitude is withheld (Romans 1:21).

• Every nation remains accountable to the Creator who “does whatever pleases Him in the heavens and on the earth” (Psalm 135:6-7).

• Elihu’s claim underscores that God’s justice is public and global, not merely individual (Psalm 2:10-12).

and provides food in abundance

• The very elements that can chastise also nourish: rain soaks the soil, lightning fixes nitrogen, clouds shield crops—tangible evidence of God’s kindness (Acts 14:17).

• Abundance comes by His deliberate blessing, never by chance (Deuteronomy 11:13-15; Matthew 6:26-30).

• The verse pairs judgment with provision, revealing that God’s character is simultaneously holy and generous (Psalm 33:5).

• His supply is “in abundance,” reminding us that He delights to give more than the minimum (John 10:10b; 2 Corinthians 9:8).

summary

Job 36:31 assures us that the same sovereign God who uses weather to discipline wayward nations also uses it to lavishly feed the earth. Judgment and provision flow from one righteous hand, inviting every people group to revere Him and every believer to trust His unfailing care.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) For by them--i.e., these roots of the sea, these drops of water, these rain-clouds. "He judgeth peoples" by withholding them, or "giveth meat in abundance" by sending rain on the earth; or He may use them in excess, to chastise nations by inundations and the like. The change from roots of the sea to bottom of the sea in the Authorised Version has obscured the meaning of "them" in the next verse, unless, indeed, we understand it generally, by these things.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - For by them judgeth he the people. By his clouds God works two opposite effects. On the one hand, he executes judgment upon the peoples, destroying their crops, causing widespread ruin by inundations, smiting and slaying numbers with his thunderbolts; on the other, he giveth meat in abundance, restoring to the parched earth its fertility by means of copious and refreshing showers, stimulating vegetation, and so furthering the harvest.
