Though his face is covered with fat
This phrase suggests prosperity and abundance, as in ancient times, physical fatness was often associated with wealth and success. In the cultural context of the Ancient Near East, being well-fed was a sign of God's blessing. However, in the context of Job, this imagery is used to describe the wicked who prosper temporarily. This reflects the broader biblical theme that material wealth is not always indicative of divine favor. The imagery of a "covered face" can also imply a lack of spiritual insight or understanding, as seen in other scriptures where spiritual blindness is symbolized by physical conditions (e.g., Isaiah 6:10).

and his waistline bulges with flesh,
The emphasis on the waistline further underscores the idea of excess and indulgence. In biblical literature, the waist or loins are often associated with strength and vitality (e.g., Job 40:16). Here, the focus on bulging flesh suggests a life of ease and self-indulgence, contrasting with the biblical call to spiritual discipline and self-control (e.g., Galatians 5:22-23). This imagery can also be seen as a metaphor for moral and spiritual laxity, where physical excess mirrors a lack of spiritual restraint. The description serves as a warning against equating physical prosperity with spiritual well-being, a theme echoed in the teachings of Jesus, who warned against storing up treasures on earth (Matthew 6:19-21).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Eliphaz the Temanite
One of Job's three friends who comes to comfort him. In this chapter, Eliphaz is speaking, offering his perspective on Job's suffering and the nature of the wicked.

2. Job
The central figure of the Book of Job, a man who experiences immense suffering and loss. His friends, including Eliphaz, attempt to explain his suffering through their understanding of God's justice.

3. Teman
A region in Edom, known for its wise men. Eliphaz is identified as a Temanite, suggesting he is considered wise and knowledgeable.
Teaching Points
Understanding Misinterpretation
Eliphaz misinterprets Job's situation, assuming that physical prosperity or lack thereof is a direct indicator of one's righteousness or wickedness. We must be cautious not to judge others' spiritual states based on external circumstances.

The Danger of Pride
The imagery of being "covered with fat" symbolizes pride and self-indulgence. As Christians, we are called to humility and self-control, recognizing that our worth is not in material abundance but in our relationship with God.

The Complexity of Suffering
Job's account reminds us that suffering is not always a result of personal sin. We should approach those who are suffering with compassion and avoid simplistic explanations.

Spiritual Health Over Physical Prosperity
True spiritual health is not measured by physical or material abundance. Our focus should be on cultivating a heart that seeks after God, regardless of our external circumstances.

The Role of Friends in Times of Trouble
Eliphaz's approach to Job's suffering teaches us the importance of being sensitive and wise in our counsel to friends who are suffering. We should aim to support and uplift rather than judge and condemn.

What Does Job 15:27 Mean
Though his face is covered with fat

• Eliphaz paints the picture of a man who seems healthy, prosperous, and satisfied. In Scripture, visible fat often signals abundance (Deuteronomy 31:20) and freedom from want.

• The verse assumes literal truth: some people truly do grow plump in seasons of success. Yet that literal condition becomes a moral warning. Psalm 73:7 notes of the wicked, “From their callous hearts proceeds iniquity; their imaginations have no limits.” The full face here hints at the same unrestrained attitude.

• Pride is in view. A full face can symbolize smug self–confidence—think of Proverbs 28:25, “A greedy man stirs up strife, but he who trusts in the LORD will prosper.” Instead of trusting the Lord, this man trusts the comfort reflected in his mirror.

• Eliphaz’s larger argument (Job 15:20-35) is that outward ease does not cancel coming judgment. Psalm 17:10 speaks similarly: “They have closed their callous hearts; with their mouths they speak arrogantly.” A calloused, fat face hides a hardened heart.

and his waistline bulges with flesh

• The image shifts to the mid-section—symbol of appetite. Proverbs 23:21 warns, “For the drunkard and the glutton will come to poverty.” What now looks like safety can quickly become a snare.

• Excess around the waist points to unrestrained consumption. Luke 12:19 captures the same attitude: “Take life easy; eat, drink, and be merry.” Eliphaz insists that such complacency invites divine displeasure.

• Even literal weight can slow and hinder (Hebrews 12:1 urges us to “lay aside every weight”). Spiritually, this man carries layers of sin that keep him from repentance.

• Notice the contrast with Job’s gaunt, suffering figure (Job 16:8). Outward appearances deceive: Job is righteous yet thin; the wicked man is plump yet doomed (Psalm 37:35-36).

summary

Job 15:27 uses the literal picture of a plump face and bulging waist to spotlight the danger of prosperity without piety. Eliphaz argues that visible abundance can mask arrogance, unchecked appetite, and spiritual complacency. Scripture consistently affirms that such self-indulgence is temporary and invites judgment, while true security lies in humble trust and obedience to the LORD.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - Because he covereth his face with his fatness. The ground and origin of the wicked man's audacity is his luxurious and intemperate living. In the days of his prosperity he pampered his body, freely indulged all his carnal appetites, and gave himself up to gluttony and gourmandism. This depraved his moral nature, separated between him and God, and finally produced in him the insolence and presumption described in vers. 25, 26 And maketh collops of fat on his flanks. The same idea, only very slightly varied, as so often in the second member of a verse.
