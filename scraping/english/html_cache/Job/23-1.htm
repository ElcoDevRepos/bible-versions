Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then:
This word indicates a continuation of the dialogue within the narrative. Job's response follows the speeches of his friends, particularly Eliphaz in the previous chapter. The use of "then" situates this moment within the larger discourse, emphasizing the ongoing nature of the debate and Job's persistence in seeking understanding and justice.

Job:
Job is the central figure of the book, a man described as blameless and upright, who fears God and shuns evil (Job 1:1). His name is synonymous with patience and suffering. Job's character is a type of Christ in his innocence and suffering, prefiguring the ultimate suffering servant, Jesus, who also endured unjust suffering.

Answered:
This indicates that Job is responding to the accusations and counsel of his friends. The term suggests a defense or rebuttal, highlighting Job's active engagement in the dialogue. In the cultural context, dialogue and debate were common methods of exploring theological and philosophical issues. Job's response is part of a larger wisdom tradition, where questions of justice, suffering, and divine sovereignty are explored through discourse.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Job
A man described as blameless and upright, who feared God and shunned evil. He is the central figure in the Book of Job, known for his immense suffering and unwavering faith.

2. Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar
Job's three friends who visit him to offer counsel and comfort but often end up accusing him of wrongdoing as the cause of his suffering.

3. The Land of Uz
The setting of the Book of Job, traditionally considered to be in the region of Edom or northern Arabia.

4. God's Heavenly Court
The unseen realm where God and the "sons of God" (angels) discuss Job's faithfulness, leading to his trials.

5. Job's Suffering
The series of calamities that befall Job, including loss of wealth, family, and health, which set the stage for his dialogues and reflections.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Suffering
Suffering is a part of the human experience, even for the righteous. Job's account reminds us that faithfulness to God does not exempt us from trials.

The Importance of Honest Dialogue with God
Job's response shows the importance of bringing our honest questions and emotions before God. It is okay to express our struggles and seek understanding.

Perseverance in Faith
Job's perseverance amidst suffering is a model for us. We are encouraged to remain steadfast in our faith, trusting in God's ultimate justice and goodness.

The Role of Friends in Times of Trial
Job's friends initially come to comfort him, but their misguided counsel highlights the need for wisdom and compassion when supporting others in distress.

God's Sovereignty and Wisdom
Throughout Job's trials, the account underscores God's sovereignty and wisdom, reminding us that His ways and purposes are higher than ours.

What Does Job 23:1 Mean
Then

• The word signals sequence—Job responds immediately after Eliphaz’s harsh accusations in chapter 22 (Job 22:1–30).

• It keeps the courtroom-like rhythm of the book: Eliphaz speaks, then Job speaks, mirroring earlier exchanges (Job 4:1; 15:1; 16:1).

• God’s Spirit inspired the author to record every step in order, so we can trace the unfolding debate without guessing what came first (2 Peter 1:20-21).

• “Then” also reminds us that God always allows a right time for His children to speak (Ecclesiastes 3:7).

Job

• Scripture has already introduced him as “blameless and upright” (Job 1:1, 2:3), yet under severe testing.

• By naming him again, the text stresses personal accountability; Job alone must wrestle with God, friends, and his own suffering (Job 19:25-27).

• His perseverance foreshadows other righteous sufferers who maintained faith amid misunderstanding—such as David pursued by Saul (Psalm 57:1-2) and Jeremiah opposed by his countrymen (Jeremiah 20:7-9).

• James draws on Job’s endurance to encourage believers today (James 5:11).

answered

• Job does not remain silent; he “answered,” affirming the believer’s freedom to pour out honest words before God (Psalm 62:8).

• His reply rejects Eliphaz’s assumption that hidden sin caused his pain (Job 22:5). Answering clarifies truth and exposes error, aligning with Proverbs 15:28.

• The verb underscores dialogue, not monologue; God welcomes reasoning together (Isaiah 1:18), even when emotions run high (Job 7:11; 13:3).

• Each “answered” in the book pushes the narrative toward God’s eventual response from the whirlwind (Job 38:1).

summary

In just three simple words—“Then Job answered”—the Holy Spirit marks a new turn in the inspired conversation. The timing (“Then”) shows ordered progression, the name (“Job”) centers the spotlight on a tested yet righteous man, and the action (“answered”) highlights his courageous engagement with both friends and God. Job 23:1 thus sets the stage for one of the book’s most heartfelt speeches, reminding us that in every season God provides a moment, a voice, and an invitation for His people to speak truth back to Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XXIII.

(1) Then Job answered.--Job replies to the insinuations of Eliphaz with the earnest longing after God and the assertion of his own innocence; while in the twenty-fourth chapter he laments that his own case is but one of many, and that multitudes suffer from the oppression of man unavenged, as he suffers from the stroke of God.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-24:25. - Job replies to Eliphaz in a speech of no great length, which, though it occupies two chapters, runs to only forty-two verses. He begins by justifying the vehemence of his complaints, first, on the ground of the severity of his sufferings (ver. 2), and secondly, on the ground of his conviction that, if God would bring him to an open trial before his tribunal, he would acquit him (vers. 3-12). By the way, he complains that God hides himself, and cannot be found (vers. 3, 8, 9). He then further complains that God is not to be bent from his purpose, which is set against Job (vers. 13-17). In ch. 24. he goes over ground already trodden, maintaining the general prosperity of the wicked, and their exemption from any special earthly punishment (vers. 2-24). He winds up, finally, with a challenge to his opponents to disprove the truth of what he has said (ver. 25). Verses 1, 2. - Then Job answered and said, Even to-day is my complaint bitter; i.e. even to-day, notwithstanding all that has been said by my opponents against my right to complain, I do complain, and as bitterly as ever. And I justify my complaint on the following ground - my stroke is heavier than my groaning. If I complain bitterly, I suffer even more bitterly (comp. Job 6:2).
