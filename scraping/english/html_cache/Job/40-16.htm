See the strength of his loins
This phrase highlights the immense power and vitality of the creature described, often identified as Behemoth. In biblical context, the loins are frequently associated with strength and procreative power (Genesis 35:11). The loins symbolize the core strength of a being, emphasizing the creature's formidable nature. This imagery serves to remind readers of God's unmatched creative power, as He alone can create such a powerful being. The strength of the loins can also be seen as a metaphor for spiritual readiness and resilience, as seen in Ephesians 6:14, where believers are instructed to gird their loins with truth.

and the power in the muscles of his belly.
The reference to the muscles of the belly underscores the creature's physical might and robustness. In ancient Near Eastern culture, the belly was often seen as the center of emotions and inner strength. This description of Behemoth's belly muscles further illustrates the creature's formidable nature, which is beyond human control or understanding. The emphasis on physical power serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty over all creation, as even the most powerful creatures are under His dominion. This can be connected to the broader biblical theme of God's strength being made perfect in weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9), contrasting human frailty with divine omnipotence.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Power
The description of Behemoth's strength serves as a reminder of God's unmatched power in creation. We are called to trust in God's sovereignty, even when we do not understand His ways.

Humility Before God
Job's encounter with God's questions about creation, including the Behemoth, teaches us humility. We must recognize our limited understanding compared to God's infinite wisdom.

The Majesty of Creation
Observing the strength and intricacy of creatures like Behemoth should lead us to worship the Creator. It is an invitation to marvel at the natural world and acknowledge God's handiwork.

Trust in God's Wisdom
Just as Job is reminded of God's wisdom through the description of Behemoth, we are encouraged to trust that God knows what is best for us, even in times of suffering.

What Does Job 40:16 Mean
See the strength of his loins

“See the strength of his loins” (Job 40:16a) invites us to pause and look closely at the creature God calls Behemoth.

• “See” echoes God’s earlier challenge to Job: “Look at Behemoth, which I made along with you” (Job 40:15). The Lord is drawing Job’s eyes to unmistakable proof of His own creative might.

• The “loins” represent the animal’s hips and hindquarters—its base of propulsion. Scripture often uses the loins as a picture of foundational strength (cf. Job 39:19 where God asks, “Do you give the horse its strength?”).

• Psalm 18:32 celebrates this same source of vigor in people: “It is God who arms me with strength.” If human warriors owe their power to God, how much more does this massive beast?

• By directing Job to notice Behemoth’s loins, God underscores that such raw power is not random; it was intentionally “knit together” (Psalm 139:14) by the Creator.

• The lesson: when we feel small before overwhelming forces, we remember that even those forces are under God’s hand (Jeremiah 27:5).

and the power in the muscles of his belly

“and the power in the muscles of his belly” (Job 40:16b) shifts attention from visible hindquarters to the animal’s core.

• The “belly” houses vital organs and deep muscle layers; its strength means stamina, not just outward might. Proverbs 30:30 calls the lion “mighty among beasts,” hinting that real dominance flows from internal potency, exactly what God highlights here.

• Psalm 104:24–25 admires the diversity of God’s workmanship in land and sea creatures; their inner design reflects His wisdom. Behemoth’s strong abdomen proclaims the same message.

• God’s point to Job is straightforward: hidden strength still traces back to the Maker. Just as Psalm 33:6 credits creation to “the word of the LORD,” so the unseen power in Behemoth’s belly testifies to divine authorship.

• For believers the application is clear: what lies beneath the surface—our character, endurance, and spiritual “core”—must rest in God’s enabling (Ephesians 6:10, “be strong in the Lord and in His mighty power”).

• Thus, when trials press in on our own “belly,” we echo Psalm 73:26: “My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.”

summary

Job 40:16 guides Job—and us—to gaze at Behemoth as a living illustration of God’s unrivaled power. The robust loins emphasize visible propulsion, while the sinewy belly showcases hidden endurance. Together they declare that every aspect of strength, seen and unseen, comes from the Creator. Recognizing this humbles us, steadies our faith, and turns our eyes from our limitations to the God whose workmanship is perfect and whose authority is absolute.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Lo now, his strength is in his loins. The strength of the hippopotamus is its principal characteristic. Weighing often two thousand kilogrammes, and of a short thick make, when roused to anger it has a force which is irresistible. In the water it upsets large beats; on land it forces its way through dense thickets and fences of all kinds. The loins are especially strong, being deep, broad, and immensely muscular. And his force is in the navel of his belly; rather, in the muscles of his bell'i. The word used (שׁרידים) occurs only in this place. It is a plural form, and therefore cannot designate a single object, like the navel. The root seems to be the Syriac serir "firm," whence Schultens proposes to translate שׁרירים by firmitates.
