Who has appointed His way for Him
This phrase emphasizes the sovereignty and independence of God. In the context of the Book of Job, Elihu is speaking to Job and his friends, asserting that God's ways are beyond human understanding and control. This reflects the biblical theme that God is the ultimate authority, as seen in Isaiah 40:13-14, where it is stated that no one can instruct the Lord or be His counselor. The rhetorical question underscores that God’s plans and purposes are self-determined and not subject to human influence or direction.

or told Him, ‘You have done wrong’?
This part of the verse highlights the perfection and righteousness of God. It suggests that no one has the authority or the moral standing to accuse God of wrongdoing. This is consistent with the biblical portrayal of God as just and holy, as seen in Deuteronomy 32:4, which describes God as a "God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is He." The idea that God is beyond reproach is also echoed in Romans 9:20, where Paul questions the right of the created to question the Creator. This phrase serves to remind believers of the importance of humility and trust in God's perfect will, even when His actions are beyond human comprehension.

What Does Job 36:23 Mean
Who has appointed His way for Him

Job’s young friend Elihu fires off this rhetorical question to remind Job—and us—that God’s course is self-determined and unalterable. No created being plots His path or edits His calendar.

• Isaiah 40:13-14 asks, “Who has directed the Spirit of the LORD, or informed Him as His counselor?”—and the silence is deafening.

• Proverbs 19:21 notes, “Many plans are in a man’s heart, but the purpose of the LORD will prevail,” underscoring that every human intention is ultimately subordinate to God’s sovereign design.

• Romans 11:34-36 echoes the same truth and then bursts into praise: “For from Him and through Him and to Him are all things.”

The takeaway is humbling: since no one assigns God His route, trust that even baffling detours in our lives are still under His wise direction.

or told Him

The verse now shifts from planning God’s path to daring to instruct Him.

• Jeremiah 23:18 admits, “Who has stood in the council of the LORD to see and hear His word?”—a meeting to which no creature receives an invitation.

• First Corinthians 2:16 reminds believers, “Who has known the mind of the Lord so as to instruct Him?” The implied answer is none, yet we possess “the mind of Christ,” meaning we align ourselves with His revealed will, not the other way around.

Whenever we catch ourselves advising God on timing, method, or outcome, Elihu’s challenge calls us back to reverent silence and attentive listening.

‘You have done wrong’ ?

The climax of the verse confronts the audacity of accusing the Almighty of injustice.

• Job 34:10-12 already settled the matter: “Far be it from God to do evil, and from the Almighty to do wrong.”

• Deuteronomy 32:4 declares, “A faithful God without injustice, righteous and upright is He.”

• Romans 9:14-20 silences every charge: “Who are you, O man, to answer back to God?”

Elihu does not deny Job’s pain; he simply insists that personal agony never licenses us to indict God. Instead, suffering invites us to reaffirm that the Judge of all the earth still does right (Genesis 18:25).

summary

Job 36:23 strings together three unanswerable questions that highlight God’s unrivaled sovereignty, unmatched wisdom, and unblemished justice. No one maps His way, tutors His mind, or files a complaint that sticks. Resting in that truth steadies the heart, especially when life’s trials tempt us to question His character or competence.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - Who hath enjoined him his way? (comp. Job 34:13). While God is thus the universal and all-perfect Teacher, there are some who would fain instruct him, dictate the course which he ought to pursue, improve and amend his universe. Something of this spirit has appeared in Job's remonstrances, which seem to insinuate that the Divine government of the world might be carried on better than it is (see Job 9:22-24; Job 10:3; Job 13:20-26; Job 16:11-17, etc.). Elihu's intention is to reprove Job for his presumption. Or who can say, Thou hast wrought iniquity? Job has not said this; but he has gone near to saying it (Job 9:24; Job 10:3; Job 21:7-26; Job 24:2-12, etc.); compare the comment on Job 34:5-12.
