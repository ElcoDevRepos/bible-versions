Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He will not escape from the darkness;
This phrase suggests the inevitability of judgment and the inescapability of divine justice. In biblical context, "darkness" often symbolizes chaos, evil, or divine judgment (e.g., Exodus 10:21-23, where darkness is a plague upon Egypt). Theologically, it reflects the condition of those who live in opposition to God's will, as seen in John 3:19-20, where people loved darkness rather than light. The imagery of darkness also connects to the spiritual blindness and separation from God that sin causes.

the flame will wither his shoots,
The "flame" here symbolizes destruction and purification, often associated with God's judgment (Isaiah 66:15-16). The "shoots" represent the offspring or the works of a person, indicating that the consequences of one's actions will be consumed by divine fire. This imagery is reminiscent of John 15:6, where branches that do not remain in Christ are thrown away and burned. The agricultural metaphor underscores the futility of efforts that are not rooted in righteousness.

and the breath of God’s mouth will carry him away.
The "breath of God’s mouth" signifies the power and authority of God's word, as seen in Genesis 2:7, where God breathes life into man, and in 2 Thessalonians 2:8, where the Lord will overthrow the lawless one with the breath of His mouth. This phrase emphasizes the irresistible force of God's will and judgment. It also connects to the concept of divine sovereignty, where God's spoken word is sufficient to enact His purposes, reflecting the creative and destructive power of His command.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Eliphaz the Temanite
One of Job's three friends who comes to comfort him but ends up accusing him of wrongdoing. In this chapter, Eliphaz is speaking to Job, suggesting that the wicked will not escape God's judgment.

2. Job
The central figure of the Book of Job, a man who experiences immense suffering and seeks to understand the reasons behind it. Job is being addressed by Eliphaz in this passage.

3. Teman
A region in Edom, known for its wise men. Eliphaz is identified as a Temanite, indicating his background and supposed wisdom.

4. Darkness
Symbolic of judgment, calamity, or the absence of God's favor. Eliphaz uses this imagery to describe the fate of the wicked.

5. Flame
Represents destruction and purification. Eliphaz uses this metaphor to illustrate the consuming nature of God's judgment on the wicked.
Teaching Points
The Certainty of Divine Justice
Eliphaz emphasizes that the wicked cannot escape God's judgment. This serves as a reminder of the certainty and inevitability of divine justice.

The Consequences of Wickedness
The imagery of darkness and flame highlights the destructive consequences of living in opposition to God's ways. Believers are encouraged to pursue righteousness.

The Power of God's Word
The "breath of His mouth" signifies the power and authority of God's word. Christians are reminded of the transformative and authoritative nature of Scripture.

The Importance of Discernment
While Eliphaz's words contain truth about God's justice, his application to Job's situation is misguided. Believers should seek discernment in applying biblical principles to life situations.

Trusting in God's Sovereignty
Despite the harshness of Eliphaz's message, the overarching theme of Job is trusting in God's sovereignty, even when His ways are not understood.

What Does Job 15:30 Mean
He will not escape from the darkness

Eliphaz pictures the wicked stumbling into a night with no dawn.

• “Darkness” throughout Scripture signals God’s judgment and separation from His favor (Job 18:18; Proverbs 2:13; Jude 1:13).

• The verb “escape” underscores that no clever plan, wealth, or human strength can slip past the Lord’s verdict (Psalm 139:11–12; Amos 5:18–20).

• By affirming that the wicked “will not” escape, the verse echoes the certainty of divine justice promised in passages like Galatians 6:7 and Hebrews 9:27.

the flame will wither his shoots

The image shifts from darkness to scorching fire.

• “Flame” evokes God’s consuming holiness (Deuteronomy 4:24; Isaiah 33:14).

• “Shoots” point to a person’s prosperity, offspring, and future—everything that seems green and secure (Psalm 37:35–36; Hosea 9:16).

• Just as a tender plant shrivels under intense heat, so every outward success of the wicked collapses under judgment (Job 8:12; Isaiah 5:24).

• This sober warning contrasts sharply with the righteous, who are “like a tree planted by streams of water” (Psalm 1:3).

the breath of God’s mouth will carry him away

Judgment culminates not in impersonal fate but in a personal act of God.

• “Breath” recalls Genesis 2:7, where God gives life; here the same breath removes it (Job 4:9).

• “Carry him away” pictures chaff swept by wind (Psalm 1:4; Isaiah 40:24), emphasizing both ease and finality—God needs no army, only His breath.

• The wicked are uprooted from every worldly anchor, fulfilling warnings such as Proverbs 10:25 and Revelation 6:17.

summary

Job 15:30 paints a triple portrait of divine judgment: impenetrable darkness, consuming flame, and irresistible breath. Together they declare that the wicked cannot outrun, withstand, or outlast God’s righteous verdict. While Eliphaz misapplies this truth to Job, the verse itself stands as a timeless reminder: life apart from reverence for the Lord ends in unavoidable ruin, but those who trust Him find light, fruitfulness, and security instead (John 8:12; 1 Peter 1:3–5).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(30) He shall not depart out of darkness.--See Job 15:22. "By the breath of his mouth shall he go away." What this means is not very clear: probably as in Job 11:20; or, "When he expires it shall be the end of him; he shall leave nothing permanent that is destined to last;" or, "He shall pass away suddenly and completely, like his own breath."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 30. - He shall not depart out of darkness (comp. ver. 23, where the wicked man is threatened with "a day of darkness"). When the darkness once falls, it shall continue; there shall be no escaping out of it The flame shall dry up his branches; rather, a flame. The "flame" intended seems to be the wrath of God. ' And by the breath of his mouth; i.e. "of God's mouth" (comp. Job 4:9). Shall he go away; or, pass away; i.e. disappear, be consumed, perish.
