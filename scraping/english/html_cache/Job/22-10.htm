Therefore snares surround you
This phrase suggests a situation where one is trapped or ensnared by circumstances. In the context of Job, Eliphaz is accusing Job of being surrounded by troubles due to his alleged sins. Biblically, "snares" often symbolize the traps set by the wicked or the consequences of sin (Psalm 91:3, Proverbs 22:5). Theologically, it reflects the belief that sin leads to entrapment and spiritual danger. Historically, snares were literal traps used for hunting, symbolizing the cunning and deceit of those who set them. This imagery is used throughout Scripture to describe the spiritual and moral dangers that can entrap a person.

and sudden peril terrifies you
This phrase indicates an unexpected and overwhelming fear. In the narrative of Job, it reflects the sudden calamities that befell him, which were both physical and emotional. Theologically, it underscores the unpredictability of life and the human vulnerability to unforeseen disasters. This connects to other scriptures that speak of sudden destruction or fear, such as Proverbs 3:25 and 1 Thessalonians 5:3. Culturally, the ancient Near Eastern worldview often saw sudden disasters as divine retribution or tests. In a typological sense, this can be seen as a foreshadowing of the sudden trials faced by Christ, who, despite being sinless, experienced the terrors of betrayal and crucifixion.

Teaching Points
Understanding Misplaced Accusations
Eliphaz wrongly assumes Job's suffering is due to sin. We must be cautious not to judge others' circumstances without understanding God's broader plan.

The Reality of Spiritual Snares
While Eliphaz misapplies the concept, spiritual snares are real. Believers should remain vigilant and rely on God's guidance to avoid them.

Trusting God Amidst Peril
Despite the fear and danger described, believers are called to trust in God's protection and sovereignty, as seen in other scriptures.

The Role of Friends in Suffering
Eliphaz's approach teaches us the importance of offering support and empathy rather than judgment when others are in distress.

God's Sovereignty in Trials
While Eliphaz attributes Job's suffering to sin, the broader account of Job shows that God allows trials for His purposes, which may be beyond our understanding.

What Does Job 22:10 Mean
Therefore

Eliphaz has spent the first nine verses charging Job with hidden sin; the term “Therefore” ties every calamity Job faces to those accusations. Scripture often links consequence to conduct:

• “Be sure your sin will find you out” (Numbers 32:23).

• “Do not be deceived: God is not mocked. For whatever a man sows, that he will also reap” (Galatians 6:7).

Eliphaz believes that same principle is now operative in Job’s life. Although he misjudges Job’s innocence (Job 1:8), the Spirit-inspired record faithfully preserves the argument so we can weigh it against God’s later verdict (Job 42:7-8).

snares surround you

The imagery is of hidden traps closing in on a prey. Eliphaz pictures Job hemmed in on every side, unable to escape the consequences he assumes must flow from secret wickedness. Other passages employ similar language:

• “His own iniquities entrap the wicked man; the cords of his sin entangle him” (Proverbs 5:22).

• “He will rescue you from the snare of the fowler” (Psalm 91:3)—a promise contrasting the danger with God’s deliverance.

• “They spread out a net for my feet; my soul is despondent” (Psalm 57:6).

Eliphaz’s logic: since Job will not admit wrongdoing, those “snares” must now be God’s just punishment.

and sudden peril terrifies you

Eliphaz underscores the shock value—disaster strikes “suddenly,” rattling Job’s sense of security. Similar warnings appear throughout Scripture:

• “Do not fear sudden terror, nor the ruin that overtakes the wicked” (Proverbs 3:25-26), a verse promising God’s protection to the upright—again countering Eliphaz’s claim.

• “Destruction will come upon them suddenly, like labor pains on a pregnant woman, and they will not escape” (1 Thessalonians 5:3).

• “What the wicked dreads will overtake him” (Proverbs 10:24).

Eliphaz is certain that the dread now consuming Job proves divine judgment.

summary

Job 22:10 captures Eliphaz’s core accusation: Job’s losses and fears are the direct, deserved outcome of hidden sin. While Eliphaz wrongly applies this counsel to a righteous sufferer, the verse still teaches a timeless truth affirmed elsewhere—sin does entangle, and judgment can fall swiftly. Later chapters reveal that God allows such dialogues so readers can discern the difference between human assumptions and God’s perfect assessment.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10, 11) Snares . . . about thee.--That is, Fear troubleth thee, or darkness, &c. "If darkness and abundance of waters cover thee so that thou canst not see, is not God in the high heavens, though thou canst not see Him. God is too great to take note of the affairs of men, their sin or their good deeds. He is so far off that He cannot see what goes on in the earth, for His dwelling-place is in heaven." Eliphaz attributes to Job the kind of sentiments that he had himself attributed to the wicked man in the last chapter, Job 22:14, &c.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - Therefore snares are round about thee. As Bildad had threatened (Job 18:8-10), and as Job himself had acknowledged (Job 19:6). And sudden fear troubleth thee (comp. Job 3:25; Job 7:14; Job 13:21, etc.).
