Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
It will be paid in full before his time
This phrase suggests the concept of divine justice and retribution. In the context of the Book of Job, Eliphaz is speaking, implying that the wicked will face consequences prematurely. This reflects the ancient Near Eastern belief in immediate retribution, where one's actions directly influence their fate. The idea of "before his time" indicates a premature end, which contrasts with the biblical promise of a long life as a blessing (Exodus 20:12). This can be seen as a warning that God's judgment is inevitable and will occur at the appointed time, even if it seems early from a human perspective. Theologically, this aligns with the principle that God is sovereign over time and life (Psalm 31:15).

and his branch will not flourish
The imagery of a "branch" is often used in the Bible to symbolize offspring or prosperity (Psalm 128:3). In this context, it suggests that the wicked man's lineage or endeavors will not succeed. This can be connected to the biblical theme of the righteous flourishing like a tree (Psalm 1:3), while the wicked are compared to chaff that the wind drives away (Psalm 1:4). The failure to flourish implies a lack of divine blessing and favor, which is a recurring theme in wisdom literature. This also foreshadows the ultimate fulfillment of God's justice, where the unrighteous will not prosper eternally. The imagery of a branch can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is referred to as the "Branch" in prophetic literature (Isaiah 11:1), contrasting the flourishing of the righteous with the downfall of the wicked.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Eliphaz the Temanite
One of Job's three friends who comes to comfort him. In this chapter, Eliphaz is speaking, offering his perspective on Job's suffering and the nature of divine justice.

2. Job
The central figure of the Book of Job, a man who experiences profound suffering and seeks to understand the reasons behind it.

3. Teman
A region in Edom, known for its wise men. Eliphaz, being a Temanite, is considered wise and knowledgeable.

4. The Wicked
In this context, Eliphaz is referring to those who live in opposition to God's ways, suggesting that their punishment is inevitable.

5. Divine Justice
The overarching theme of this passage, where Eliphaz argues that God's justice will be served in due time.
Teaching Points
The Certainty of Divine Justice
Eliphaz emphasizes that God's justice is certain and will be fulfilled in its time. This serves as a reminder that while we may not see immediate consequences for wrongdoing, God's timing is perfect.

The Consequences of Wickedness
The imagery of a branch not flourishing symbolizes the ultimate futility of a life lived in opposition to God. This encourages believers to pursue righteousness and align their lives with God's will.

Trusting God's Timing
While Eliphaz's application may be misdirected towards Job, the principle remains that believers should trust in God's timing for justice and not take matters into their own hands.

The Importance of Discernment
Eliphaz's speech reminds us to discern the truth carefully. While his understanding of divine justice is correct, his application to Job's situation is flawed. We must seek God's wisdom in understanding and applying His truths.

What Does Job 15:32 Mean
It will be paid in full before his time

Eliphaz is declaring that the wicked person’s “bill” for sin comes due early—before he expects it. Scripture consistently affirms that God intervenes in history to settle accounts:

• Psalm 55:23 echoes, “Men of bloodshed and deceit will not live out half their days,” highlighting shortened life as a consequence.

• Proverbs 10:27 states, “The fear of the LORD prolongs life, but the years of the wicked are cut short,” reinforcing the same pattern.

• Galatians 6:7 warns, “Do not be deceived: God is not mocked. Whatever a man sows, he will reap,” reminding us that repayment is certain.

Eliphaz is applying this timeless truth to Job, though wrongly assuming Job fits the description. The principle itself, however, stands firm: divine justice is precise and often swifter than human expectation.

and his branch will not flourish

The image shifts from the individual to his future—his “branch,” or posterity. In biblical thought, a flourishing branch represents ongoing vitality, descendants, and legacy. When God withholds that flourishing, the wicked lose both influence and remembrance.

• Job 18:16 portrays the same fate: “His roots dry up beneath, and his branches wither above.”

• Psalm 37:2 observes that evildoers “wither like grass,” emphasizing the temporary nature of unrighteous success.

• Isaiah 14:20 describes the cut-off line of a tyrant, showing how sin can sever future generations from blessing.

• John 15:6, though spoken of spiritual fruitfulness, pictures branches gathered and burned, a vivid parallel to the denial of growth.

The lesson is plain: sin not only harms the sinner’s present life but also chokes the fruitfulness that would have extended beyond him.

summary

Job 15:32 teaches that God’s justice is neither delayed nor partial. The wicked person’s due is “paid in full” ahead of schedule, and his seeming prosperity ends with a barren branch. The verse assures believers that righteousness is never forgotten and unrighteousness never escapes notice, inviting us to trust God’s perfect timing and to pursue lives that bear lasting, godly fruit.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(32) It shall be accomplished.--That is, paid in full before its time.

The remainder of this chapter calls for little explanation. In it the speaker only repeats the orthodox and familiar saw that the wicked are punished in life, and therefore, by implication, the good rewarded: a maxim which fails utterly in the face of afflictions like those of Job, unless, as his friends insinuated, he was one of the wicked. After stating the doom of the ungodly, Eliphaz, in the last verse, sums up the character of those he has been denouncing. Not only are they evil in themselves, but they hatch evil; but it is evil that recoils on themselves.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 32. - It shall be accomplished before his time. "It [i.e. the recompense] shall be accomplished [or, 'paid in full '] before its time [i.e. before payment is due]." A vague threat, probably intended to signify that death will come upon the wicked man prematurely, before he has lived out halt the days of his natural life. And his branch shall not be green; i.e. he shall wither and fade, like a tree not planted by the waterside (Psalm 1:3).
