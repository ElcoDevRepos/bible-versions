Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
except Caleb son of Jephunneh.
Caleb, from the tribe of Judah, stands out as a figure of faith and obedience. His father, Jephunneh, is identified as a Kenizzite, suggesting a possible non-Israelite origin, which highlights the inclusivity of God's covenant. Caleb's faithfulness is contrasted with the rebellion of the other Israelites, emphasizing the importance of individual commitment to God.

He will see it,
This promise refers to the land of Canaan, the Promised Land. Caleb's faith and trust in God's promise during the exploration of Canaan (Numbers 13-14) earned him the privilege to enter the land, unlike the rest of his generation. This phrase underscores the biblical principle that faith leads to the fulfillment of God's promises.

and I will give him and his descendants the land on which he has set foot,
Caleb's inheritance is a tangible reward for his faithfulness. The specific land mentioned is Hebron, a significant city in biblical history, later associated with King David. This promise also extends to his descendants, illustrating the generational blessings of obedience to God.

because he followed the LORD wholeheartedly.”
Caleb's complete devotion to God is highlighted here. The phrase "wholeheartedly" indicates a total commitment without reservation, serving as a model for believers. This devotion is echoed in Joshua 14:8-9, where Caleb recounts his faithfulness. His life exemplifies the blessings that come from unwavering faith and obedience to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Caleb
A faithful spy from the tribe of Judah who, along with Joshua, trusted in God's promise to give Israel the Promised Land. His faithfulness is rewarded with the promise of inheritance.

2. Jephunneh
Caleb's father, mentioned to identify Caleb's lineage, which is significant in the context of Israel's tribal structure.

3. The Promised Land
The land of Canaan, which God promised to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Caleb is assured a portion of this land due to his faithfulness.

4. The Wilderness Generation
The Israelites who were led out of Egypt but failed to trust God to enter the Promised Land, resulting in their wandering for 40 years.

5. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who is faithful to His promises and rewards those who follow Him wholeheartedly.
Teaching Points
Wholehearted Devotion
Caleb is commended for following the LORD wholeheartedly. This teaches us the importance of complete devotion and trust in God, even when others doubt.

Faith Rewarded
Caleb's faithfulness resulted in a tangible reward. This illustrates that God honors those who remain steadfast in their faith and obedience.

Legacy of Faith
Caleb's inheritance was not just for him but for his descendants. Our faithfulness can have lasting impacts on future generations.

Standing Against the Majority
Caleb stood firm in his faith despite the majority's fear and unbelief. This encourages us to stand firm in our convictions, even when it is unpopular.

God's Faithfulness
The fulfillment of God's promise to Caleb reminds us of God's unwavering faithfulness to His word and His people.

What Does Deuteronomy 1:36 Mean
Except Caleb son of Jephunneh

• The backdrop is Israel’s refusal to enter Canaan after the spy report (Deuteronomy 1:26–32). In that moment of national unbelief, Caleb stood out.

• Numbers 14:24 echoes the same divine commendation: “But My servant Caleb, because he has a different spirit and has followed Me wholeheartedly, I will bring into the land where he has gone.”

• Joshua 14:6–8 later records Caleb reminding Joshua of this promise, showing its literal fulfillment.

• Caleb’s tribal identity (Judah) highlights God’s faithfulness to individuals within a larger covenant community, even when the majority fails.

He will see it

• God assures Caleb of personal experience, not a distant hope—he will literally lay eyes on the land.

• Forty years of wandering did not cancel that promise (Joshua 14:10–11).

• This “seeing” contrasts with Moses, who only viewed Canaan from Pisgah (Deuteronomy 34:4), underscoring the reward for faith versus the consequence of doubt.

I will give him and his descendants the land on which he has set foot

• The specific territory Caleb walked on during the spying mission became his inheritance: “So Joshua blessed Caleb … and gave him Hebron as an inheritance” (Joshua 14:13).

• Joshua 15:13–14 details Caleb taking possession, driving out the Anakim giants—proof that God’s grant included the strength to occupy.

• The promise extends generationally: “his descendants.” Psalm 37:29 affirms this wider biblical theme—“The righteous will inherit the land and dwell in it forever.”

Because he followed the LORD wholeheartedly

• Wholehearted obedience (literally “to fill after the LORD”) is the stated reason for Caleb’s blessing.

• The phrase repeats six times about Caleb (Numbers 14:24; 32:12; Deuteronomy 1:36; Joshua 14:8, 9, 14), emphasizing consistency, not a one-time act.

• 2 Chronicles 16:9 illustrates the principle: “For the eyes of the LORD roam to and fro … to show Himself strong on behalf of those whose heart is fully devoted to Him.”

• Caleb models faith that trusts God’s word over visible obstacles, encouraging believers today to do the same.

summary

Deuteronomy 1:36 spotlights Caleb as the exception amid national failure. God honors his steadfast faith with three tangible rewards: personal entry into Canaan, perpetual family inheritance, and public affirmation of wholehearted devotion. The verse reminds us that unwavering trust in God’s promises is never overlooked; it is met with fulfillment, blessing, and legacy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) Save Caleb.--Caleb is here placed by himself, as the one exception among the people. Joshua, as Moses' substitute, the exception among the recognised leaders, is named separately.
