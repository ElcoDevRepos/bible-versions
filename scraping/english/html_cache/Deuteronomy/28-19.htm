You will be cursed when you come in
This phrase signifies a comprehensive curse affecting all aspects of daily life. In the context of ancient Israel, "coming in" refers to entering one's home or city, symbolizing the beginning of daily activities. The curse implies a lack of peace and prosperity within the household and community. Historically, the Israelites understood blessings and curses as tangible expressions of their covenant relationship with God. This curse contrasts with the blessing in Deuteronomy 28:6, where obedience leads to blessings in all endeavors. Theologically, it underscores the consequences of disobedience to God's commandments, reflecting the covenantal framework established at Sinai.

and cursed when you go out.
"Going out" refers to leaving one's home or city, encompassing all external activities such as work, travel, and trade. This curse suggests that endeavors outside the home will also be fruitless and fraught with difficulty. In the cultural context of the ancient Near East, success in external affairs was crucial for survival and prosperity. The phrase echoes the comprehensive nature of the curse, affecting every aspect of life. Biblically, it parallels the curse of exile and dispersion found in later verses, where disobedience leads to being uprooted from the land. This reflects the broader biblical theme of exile as a consequence of sin, seen in the Babylonian captivity and ultimately pointing to the need for redemption through Christ, who reverses the curse by offering spiritual restoration and peace.

Teaching Points
The Seriousness of Disobedience
The curses in Deuteronomy 28 highlight the severe consequences of turning away from God's commands. This serves as a sobering reminder of the importance of obedience in our walk with God.

The Conditional Nature of Blessings and Curses
The blessings and curses are conditional upon the Israelites' obedience. This principle can be applied to our lives, understanding that our actions have spiritual consequences.

The Role of the Law in the Old Covenant
The law served as a guide and a standard for the Israelites. In the New Covenant, we are reminded that while we are not under the law, the principles of obedience and faithfulness remain.

Christ's Redemption from the Curse
Through Christ, we are redeemed from the curse of the law. This should lead us to live in gratitude and obedience, not out of fear of curses, but out of love for our Redeemer.

The Importance of Covenant Faithfulness
Just as the Israelites were called to be faithful to their covenant with God, we are called to be faithful to our relationship with Christ, living out our faith in every aspect of life.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:19 Mean
You will be cursed

Moses has just laid out the blessings for obedience (Deuteronomy 28:1-14) and now turns to the mirror image: “But if you do not obey… all these curses will come upon you” (v. 15). Verse 19 opens by stating plainly, “You will be cursed.”

• The verb is comprehensive, covering every facet of life—health, family, economy, security—echoed in the wider catalog of vv. 20-68.

• This is the covenant counterpart to the earlier promise, “Blessed shall you be” (v. 6). The Lord guarantees that the consequences of disobedience are as certain as the blessings of obedience (cf. Galatians 6:7-8; Leviticus 26:14-39).

• The curse is not random; it is God’s righteous response to deliberate rebellion (Proverbs 3:33).

when you come in

The first sphere Moses mentions is entry—arriving home, entering a city, stepping into any endeavor.

• In v. 6 the obedient Israelite was promised, “Blessed shall you be when you come in.” Now the inverse underscores that disobedience turns everyday routines into arenas of frustration (Deuteronomy 28:22-24).

• Scripture often uses “coming in” as shorthand for life’s ordinary beginnings (1 Samuel 18:16). The curse means loss of peace, productivity, and safety at every start (Isaiah 30:1-3).

• God’s people were to be His showcase nation; instead, their entrances would advertise divine displeasure, fulfilling warnings like Psalm 106:39-40.

and cursed when you go out

Departure is the other half of daily living—leaving home for work, setting out to war, journeying to worship.

• Again, v. 6 had promised, “Blessed shall you be when you go out.” Disobedience reverses that, so exits become vulnerable moments (Deuteronomy 28:25-26; 1 Kings 8:46).

• The phrase covers military campaigns (Numbers 27:17), commerce, and even emigration. Every outward step would feel the weight of God’s hand (Jeremiah 42:15-17).

• Psalm 121:8 proclaims, “The LORD will watch over your coming and going,” but here the same realm is surrendered to judgment because Israel would not let the LORD rule their hearts.

summary

Deuteronomy 28:19 warns that covenant-breaking turns the most routine movements—coming in and going out—into moments of divine chastening. Blessing or curse touches every entrance and every exit. The verse drives home the sobering truth: continual obedience invites God’s continual favor, while persistent disobedience invites His continual discipline.
