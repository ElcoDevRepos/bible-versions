Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But He brought us out from there
This phrase refers to God's deliverance of the Israelites from slavery in Egypt. It highlights God's power and faithfulness in fulfilling His promise to rescue His people. The Exodus is a central event in Israel's history, symbolizing salvation and liberation. It prefigures the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ, who frees believers from the bondage of sin (John 8:36).

to lead us in
God's purpose was not only to bring the Israelites out of Egypt but also to guide them into the Promised Land. This journey required faith and obedience, reflecting the Christian journey of sanctification. The wilderness period tested and refined the Israelites, similar to how believers are often tested in their spiritual walk (James 1:2-4).

and give us the land
The land represents God's covenant promise to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. It signifies a place of rest and blessing, a tangible expression of God's faithfulness. The land of Canaan is often seen as a type of the eternal inheritance believers receive through Christ (Hebrews 4:8-10).

that He had sworn to our fathers
This phrase underscores the covenantal nature of God's relationship with Israel. God's promises to the patriarchs were unconditional and based on His faithfulness, not human merit. This reflects the assurance believers have in God's promises through Christ, who is the fulfillment of the Abrahamic covenant (Galatians 3:16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites who is delivering God's commandments and promises to the people.

2. Israelites
The chosen people of God, who were enslaved in Egypt and are now on the journey to the Promised Land.

3. Egypt
The place of bondage from which God delivered the Israelites.

4. Promised Land (Canaan)
The land God swore to give to the forefathers of the Israelites, representing freedom and divine promise.

5. Forefathers (Patriarchs)
Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, to whom God originally made the promise of the land.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness
God is faithful to His promises. Just as He delivered the Israelites from Egypt and led them to the Promised Land, He is faithful to fulfill His promises in our lives.

Deliverance and Purpose
God delivers us from bondage (sin) not just for freedom's sake but to lead us into a life of purpose and promise. Reflect on what God has delivered you from and what He is leading you into.

Heritage of Faith
The promises of God are often generational. We are part of a larger account that began with the faith of our spiritual forefathers. Consider how your faith journey is connected to those who came before you.

Obedience and Trust
Just as the Israelites had to trust and obey God to enter the Promised Land, we must trust and obey God in our journey of faith. Evaluate areas in your life where you need to exercise more trust and obedience.

Spiritual Inheritance
The Promised Land symbolizes our spiritual inheritance in Christ. Reflect on the spiritual blessings and inheritance you have received through faith in Jesus.

What Does Deuteronomy 6:23 Mean
But He brought us out

- The opening phrase points immediately to the Lord’s personal initiative. Israel did not engineer its escape; “I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt” (Exodus 20:2) echoes here.

- Redemption is therefore historical and literal: God stepped into time, broke Pharaoh’s grip, and marched His people out (Deuteronomy 7:8; Exodus 12:51).

- Every future act of obedience in Deuteronomy 6 rests on this completed work of rescue.

from there

- “There” is Egypt—the furnace of slavery (Deuteronomy 4:20). Naming the place keeps the memory of bondage vivid.

- Remembering where God found us guards against pride (Deuteronomy 8:14) and fuels gratitude (Exodus 13:3).

- Spiritual parallel: believers recall being “rescued from the dominion of darkness” (Colossians 1:13), never minimizing the pit from which they were lifted.

to lead us in

- Deliverance had direction. God’s goal was not wilderness wandering but entrance to promise (Exodus 3:8).

- He personally shepherded them: “The LORD went before them” (Exodus 13:21) and “carried them, as a man carries his son” (Deuteronomy 1:31).

- Our salvation likewise includes guidance: “He who began a good work in you will perfect it” (Philippians 1:6).

and give us the land

- The land was gift, not trophy. “See, I have given you this land” (Deuteronomy 1:8).

- God’s generosity appears in its richness—“a land flowing with milk and honey” (Exodus 3:17)—and in its security, apportioned by tribe (Joshua 1:2-3).

- In Christ we taste the same grace: “an inheritance that can never perish” (1 Peter 1:4).

that He had sworn to our fathers

- The promise traces back to Abraham (Genesis 12:7), renewed to Isaac (Genesis 26:3) and Jacob (Genesis 28:13).

- God’s oath anchors faith; “God is not a man, that He should lie” (Numbers 23:19).

- Because He keeps covenant, His people can risk obedience and rest in hope (Hebrews 6:17-18).

summary

Deuteronomy 6:23 compresses the gospel pattern into one sentence: rescue (“brought us out”), remembrance (“from there”), purposeful guidance (“to lead us in”), lavish provision (“and give us the land”), and covenant faithfulness (“that He had sworn to our fathers”). The verse invites us to worship the God who acts in history, keeps every promise, and brings His people all the way home.
