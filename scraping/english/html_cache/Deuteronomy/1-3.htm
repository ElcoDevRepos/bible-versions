Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
In the fortieth year
This phrase marks a significant milestone in the Israelites' journey. The fortieth year signifies the end of the wilderness wanderings, a period of testing and preparation for entering the Promised Land. The number forty often symbolizes a period of trial or probation in the Bible, as seen in the forty days of rain during the flood (Genesis 7:12) and Jesus' forty days of fasting in the wilderness (Matthew 4:2).

on the first day of the eleventh month
This specific date situates the events in a historical context, providing a precise timeline for Moses' final speeches. The eleventh month, known as Shevat in the Hebrew calendar, is just before the Israelites' entry into Canaan. This timing underscores the urgency and importance of Moses' message as the people prepare to transition from wandering to conquest.

Moses proclaimed to the Israelites
Moses, as the leader and prophet, serves as the mediator between God and His people. His role is crucial in delivering God's commandments and guidance. This proclamation is part of Moses' farewell address, emphasizing his dedication to ensuring the Israelites understand God's laws before his impending death and their entrance into the Promised Land.

all that the LORD had commanded him concerning them
This phrase highlights the divine origin of Moses' message. The commandments are not merely Moses' words but are directly from the LORD, underscoring their authority and importance. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where obedience to God's commands is central. It also foreshadows the New Covenant, where Jesus, the ultimate prophet, delivers God's final revelation to humanity (Hebrews 1:1-2).

Teaching Points
The Importance of Obedience
Moses' role in proclaiming God's commandments underscores the necessity of obedience to God's word. Believers are called to listen and adhere to God's instructions in their lives.

God's Timing is Perfect
The specific timing of Moses' proclamation reminds us that God's plans unfold according to His perfect timing. Trusting in His timing is crucial for our spiritual journey.

Leadership and Responsibility
Moses' leadership highlights the responsibility of spiritual leaders to faithfully communicate God's truth. Believers in leadership positions should seek to guide others with integrity and faithfulness.

Preparation for Promised Blessings
The Israelites' journey prepares them for the blessings of the Promised Land. Similarly, believers are called to prepare their hearts for the blessings God has in store, through faith and obedience.

What Does Deuteronomy 1:3 Mean
In the fortieth year

“ In the fortieth year …” (Deuteronomy 1:3)

• The number forty in Scripture consistently marks completion of a testing period (Numbers 14:33-34; Psalm 95:10; Acts 7:36).

• God literally kept Israel in the wilderness a full forty years because of the first generation’s unbelief (Numbers 32:13).

• By the close of that span, every adult who refused to trust God at Kadesh-barnea had died (Deuteronomy 2:14-16), proving both the certainty of divine judgment and the patience of divine grace.

• The verse signals the end of wandering and the dawn of fulfillment: the promises given in Genesis 12:7 are about to materialize for their children.

On the first day of the eleventh month

“… on the first day of the eleventh month …”

• The date Isaiah 1 Shebat, only five weeks before Israel will camp opposite Jericho (Joshua 4:19) and a mere thirty-eight days before Moses’ death (Deuteronomy 34:8).

• This precise time-stamp shows God’s orderly planning: He schedules renewal of the covenant before His servant departs, just as He had scheduled Passover’s timing before the Exodus (Exodus 12:2-6).

• It reminds us that God’s purposes unfold on a real calendar, anchoring faith in verifiable history (Luke 1:1-4).

Moses proclaimed to the Israelites

“… Moses proclaimed to the Israelites …”

• Moses, the servant who spoke with God “face to face” (Exodus 33:11), now turns his full attention to the second-generation community.

• What he proclaims is not mere advice but covenant truth, the same pattern later mirrored when Ezra read the Law to post-exilic Israel (Nehemiah 8:1-8).

• The audience matters: these Israelites had not personally witnessed the Red Sea crossing (Deuteronomy 5:3), yet they still belonged to the covenant family.

• Moses models pastoral diligence—explaining, reminding, and urging obedience (Deuteronomy 1:5; 31:12-13).

All that the LORD had commanded him concerning them

“… all that the LORD had commanded him concerning them.”

• Moses holds nothing back; he relays the whole counsel of God (compare Acts 20:27).

• Faithful transmission marks every true servant:

– God’s charge to Moses (Exodus 34:27)

– Joshua’s later charge to keep the Book of the Law intact (Joshua 1:7-8)

– The prophets’ repeated refrain, “Thus says the LORD” (Jeremiah 1:7)

• Omitting or editing God’s word was forbidden (Deuteronomy 4:2; 12:32), a principle reaffirmed even at the close of Scripture (Revelation 22:18-19).

• Because Moses spoke exactly what God commanded, Israel could not claim ignorance; their response would be a matter of obedience or rebellion (Deuteronomy 30:15-20).

summary

Deuteronomy 1:3 roots the entire book in concrete time, place, and authority. After a literal forty-year season of testing, on a precisely dated day, God’s chosen leader gathers the new generation to deliver every word the Lord has spoken for their blessing and direction. The verse signals that wandering is over, covenant instruction is complete, and the people now stand poised to trust, obey, and enter all that God promised.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) And it came to pass in the fortieth year, in the eleventh month.--The "and" is the real beginning of Deuteronomy, and connects it with the previous books. The moral of these words has been well pointed out by Jewish writers. It was but eleven days' journey from Sinai to Kadesh-barnea--the place from whence Israel should have begun the conquest of the promised land; but not only eleven days of the second year of the exodus, but eleven months of the fortieth year found them still in the wilderness. "We see that they could not enter in because of unbelief."

(3, 4) Moses spake unto the children of Israel . . . after he had slain Sihon . . . and Og.--The conquest of these two kings and their territories was one of the exploits of the fortieth year. (See Numbers 21:21-35.) Before the eleventh month of that year, not only Sihon and Og, but also the five princes of Midian, "who were dukes of Sihon, dwelling in the country" (Joshua 13:21), had also been slain (Numbers 31). This completed the conquest, and was the last exploit of Moses' life. In the period of repose that followed he found a suitable time to exhort the children of Israel, "according unto all that the Lord had given him in commandment unto them" From Deuteronomy 34:8, we learn that "the children of Israel wept for Moses thirty days." These days would seem to be the last month of the fortieth year, for "on the tenth day of the first month" (probably of the next year, Joshua 4:19) they passed over Jordan. Thus the last delivery of the discourses recorded in Deuteronomy would seem to lie within a single month.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 3, 4. - Here is intimated the time when the following addresses were delivered to the people. It was on the first day of the eleventh month in the fortieth year; therefore near the end of their wanderings, and towards the close of the lawgiver's own career. He could thus speak to them according unto all that the Lord had given him in commandment unto them, i.e. in accordance with the legislative contents of the preceding books (comp. Deuteronomy 4:5 23; 5:28-33; 6:1). It was also after the destruction of Sihon and 'Og (Numbers 21:21-35). This also is significant. By the destruction of these kings, who sought to bar the access of the Israelites to the Promised Land, God had given proof that he would indeed fulfill his promise to his people, and had at once laid them under obligations to obedience, and given them encouragement to go forward on the course to which he had called them. The "he" here is Moses, who, at the command of God, had led the Israelites against Sihon and 'Og. Edrei, hod Draa (Numbers 21:33) was the second capital of 'Og; he "reigned in Ashtaroth and in Edrei" (Joshua 13:12). Here, however, it denotes the place where he was slain in battle, and the words "in Edrei" are to be referred to the verb "smote" and not to "dwelt" (cf. Deuteronomy 3:1: Numbers 21:33).
