When Moses had finished writing
Moses is traditionally credited as the author of the first five books of the Bible, known as the Pentateuch. This phrase indicates the completion of his role in documenting the law. The act of writing was significant in ancient times, as it was a means of preserving and transmitting God's commandments to future generations. This also underscores the importance of literacy and record-keeping in Israelite culture.

in a book
The term "book" here refers to a scroll, as the codex form of books was not yet in use. Scrolls were made from papyrus or parchment and were the primary medium for recording texts. This highlights the meticulous care taken to ensure the law was preserved accurately. The use of a book signifies the permanence and authority of the written word in conveying divine instruction.

the words of this law
This refers to the legal and covenantal instructions given to the Israelites, encompassing moral, ceremonial, and civil laws. The law served as a guide for the Israelites' relationship with God and with each other. It is foundational to understanding the covenant between God and His people, emphasizing obedience and holiness.

from beginning to end
This phrase indicates the comprehensive nature of the law, covering all aspects necessary for the life and governance of the Israelite community. It suggests completeness and thoroughness, ensuring that nothing was omitted. This mirrors the biblical theme of God’s word being complete and sufficient for guidance, as seen in other scriptures like 2 Timothy 3:16-17.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The central figure in this passage, Moses is the leader of the Israelites and the one who received the Law from God. He is responsible for writing down the Law as instructed by God.

2. The Israelites
The people to whom the Law is given. They are on the brink of entering the Promised Land, and the Law is meant to guide them in their new life there.

3. The Book of the Law
This refers to the written record of the laws and commandments given by God to Moses. It serves as a covenant document between God and His people.

4. The Promised Land
The land of Canaan, which God promised to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. The Israelites are about to enter this land under Joshua's leadership.

5. The Tent of Meeting
The place where Moses would meet with God and where the Book of the Law would be kept as a witness against the Israelites.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Scripture
The act of writing down the Law underscores the importance of having a tangible, unchanging record of God's commandments. This highlights the value of Scripture in our lives today.

The Role of Leadership
Moses' role in writing the Law demonstrates the responsibility of leaders to faithfully transmit God's Word to the people. Christian leaders today are called to uphold and teach Scripture with integrity.

The Covenant Relationship
The Book of the Law serves as a reminder of the covenant relationship between God and His people. Believers are called to live in obedience to God's Word as a reflection of their covenant with Him.

Scripture as a Guide
Just as the Israelites were to be guided by the Law, Christians are to be guided by the entirety of Scripture, allowing it to direct their paths and decisions.

The Enduring Nature of God's Word
The preservation and transmission of the Law through generations show the enduring nature of God's Word. It remains relevant and authoritative for all believers.

What Does Deuteronomy 31:24 Mean
When Moses had finished writing

- The verse opens with a moment of completion. Moses has reached the end of a God-given assignment that has spanned four decades (Exodus 17:14; Deuteronomy 31:22).

- Like the Creator who “finished His work” on the sixth day (Genesis 2:1-2), Moses models obedience carried through to the last detail.

- This closing act signals that the law is now a fixed, unalterable standard for the nation (Joshua 8:34-35).

in a book

- The law is not left to oral tradition; it is preserved in writing, giving it permanence and public accessibility (Exodus 24:7; Deuteronomy 17:18-19).

- A written record prevents distortion and enables every generation to read the same words Moses heard from God (Psalm 102:18).

- By placing the law “in a book,” Moses provides the first building block of what will become the canon of Scripture, foreshadowing the charge to Joshua to keep “this Book of the Law” always on his lips (Joshua 1:8).

the words of this law

- “Words” underscores that every statement—commands, promises, warnings—is inspired, authoritative, and binding (Deuteronomy 28:58; 30:10).

- The definite article (“this law”) points to the covenant given at Sinai and reiterated on the plains of Moab; nothing else qualifies as divine law for Israel (Nehemiah 8:1).

- The phrase affirms verbal inspiration: God’s exact words, not merely His ideas, are captured (2 Timothy 3:16 uses the same emphasis for all Scripture).

from beginning to end

- Moses records the law in its entirety; nothing is omitted, added, or left vague (Deuteronomy 31:9; Revelation 22:18-19).

- The completeness protects Israel from partial obedience; they must keep “all that is written” (Joshua 23:6).

- This totality foreshadows Christ’s declaration that He came to fulfill “everything written about Me in the Law of Moses” (Luke 24:44), affirming the enduring wholeness of God’s Word.

summary

Deuteronomy 31:24 captures the final sealing of God’s covenant instruction. Moses, having faithfully transcribed every divinely spoken word, delivers a complete, written law that will stand unchanged for all generations. The verse highlights divine inspiration, human obedience, and the sufficiency of Scripture—truths that remain foundational for every believer today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
Deuteronomy 31:24-28. DELIVERY OF THE BOOKS OF MOSES TO THE LEVITES.

(24) When Moses had made an end of writing.--This means the completion of the books of Moses as he delivered them to Israel; not merely Deuteronomy, as above, in Deuteronomy 31:9, but the whole, including the song mentioned in Deuteronomy 31:22. The song was probably the end of the book as delivered to them by Moses.

In a book.--'Al-sepher; upon a roll. The Pentateuch is written upon a single roll to this day.

(25) The Levites, which bare the ark.--Observe this, and comp. Deuteronomy 31:9, above.

(26) In the side of the ark.--More literally, beside, Rashi says, "The wise men of Israel differ about this in the treatise Baba Bathra (in the Talmud). Some of them say there was a leaf or slab projecting from the ark outside, and there the book was placed. Others say that it was placed beside the tables of the covenant in the ark itself." . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 24-29. - After the installation of Joshua, only one thing remained for Moses to do that all things might be set in order before his departure. This was the finishing of the writing of the Book of the Law, and the committing it finally to the priests, to be by them placed by the ark of the covenant, that it might be kept for all future generations as a witness against the people, whose apostasy and rebellion were foreseen. Whether this section is to be regarded as wholly written by Moses himself, or as an appendix to his writing added by some other writer, has been made matter of question. It is quite possible, however, that Moses himself, ere he laid down the pen, may have recorded what he said when delivering the Book of the Law to the priests, and there is nothing in the manner or style of the record to render it probable that it was added by another. What follows from ver. 30 to the end of the book was probably added to the writing of Moses by some one after his death, though, of course, both the song in Deuteronomy 32, and the blessing in Deuteronomy 33, are the composition of Moses (see Introduction, § 6).
