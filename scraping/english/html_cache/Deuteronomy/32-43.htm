Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Rejoice, O heavens, with Him, and let all God’s angels worship Him.
This phrase calls for a cosmic celebration, indicating that the events described have universal significance. The heavens and angels are often depicted in Scripture as witnesses to God's actions (Job 38:7, Psalm 148:2). The call for angels to worship aligns with Hebrews 1:6, which speaks of angels worshiping Christ, suggesting a messianic interpretation. This reflects the divine authority and majesty of God, who is worthy of worship from all creation.

Rejoice, O nations, with His people;
Here, the nations are invited to join in the rejoicing, indicating the global impact of God's actions. This foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles in God's redemptive plan, as seen in the New Testament (Romans 15:10). The phrase suggests a future time when all nations will recognize God's sovereignty and join His people in worship, pointing to the eschatological hope found in Revelation 7:9.

for He will avenge the blood of His children.
This phrase emphasizes God's justice and His role as the avenger of innocent blood. It reflects the biblical principle that God will not leave the guilty unpunished (Deuteronomy 32:35, Romans 12:19). The "children" likely refer to Israel, God's covenant people, but can also be seen as a type of the church, which God protects and vindicates.

He will take vengeance on His adversaries and repay those who hate Him;
God's vengeance is a recurring theme in Scripture, where He acts against those who oppose Him and His people (Isaiah 59:18, Nahum 1:2). This underscores God's holiness and justice, ensuring that evil does not go unpunished. It also serves as a warning to those who reject God, highlighting the seriousness of enmity against Him.

He will cleanse His land and His people.
The cleansing of the land and people signifies purification and restoration. This can be seen as a type of Christ's redemptive work, where through His sacrifice, believers are cleansed from sin (1 John 1:7). The land, often associated with Israel, represents God's promise and blessing, pointing to a future renewal and peace in the messianic kingdom (Isaiah 65:17).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The author of Deuteronomy, delivering his final song to the Israelites before his death.

2. Heavens and Angels
Spiritual beings called to rejoice and worship God, highlighting the cosmic scope of God's justice and glory.

3. Nations
Refers to the Gentiles, indicating that God's plan and justice extend beyond Israel to all peoples.

4. God's Children
The Israelites, whom God promises to avenge and protect.

5. Adversaries
Those who oppose God and His people, against whom God will execute judgment.
Teaching Points
Universal Call to Worship
God's glory and justice are reasons for all creation, including spiritual beings and nations, to worship Him. This calls us to recognize the universal scope of God's sovereignty.

God's Justice and Vengeance
God promises to avenge His people and execute justice on His adversaries. This assures believers of God's protection and the eventual triumph of righteousness.

Inclusion of the Nations
The call for nations to rejoice with God's people foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles in God's redemptive plan, encouraging us to embrace unity and diversity in the body of Christ.

Cleansing and Restoration
God's promise to cleanse His land and people points to His ongoing work of sanctification. Believers are called to pursue holiness and trust in God's purifying work.

Hope in God's Faithfulness
The assurance of God's vengeance and cleansing provides hope and encouragement for believers facing persecution or injustice, reminding us of God's ultimate faithfulness.

What Does Deuteronomy 32:43 Mean
Rejoice, O heavens, with Him, and let all God’s angels worship Him.

The song of Moses ends with a call for all creation beyond earth to celebrate God’s triumph.

• Heaven’s rejoicing underscores that God’s actions on earth display His glory to the unseen realm (Psalm 148:1-2; Luke 2:13-14).

• Hebrews 1:6 cites this line to show that angels worship the exalted Christ, affirming His deity and supremacy.

• The invitation is “with Him,” centering the praise on God Himself, not merely on His gifts.

Rejoice, O nations, with His people;

Earth now echoes heaven’s joy, inviting every ethnic group to join Israel in praise.

• Romans 15:10 applies this phrase to the gospel era, proving that Gentile blessing was always God’s plan (Genesis 12:3).

• When God’s people thrive, the nations are meant to recognize His goodness and rejoice (Psalm 67:3-4).

• This anticipates the multi-ethnic worship scene of Revelation 7:9-10, showing the missionary heartbeat of the Old Testament.

for He will avenge the blood of His children.

Joy rests on the certainty that God judges injustice.

• The martyrs’ cry, “How long?” (Revelation 6:10), is answered here: God will not overlook spilled innocent blood (Psalm 9:12).

• Earlier in the song He promised, “Vengeance is Mine” (Deuteronomy 32:35); this verse affirms He will keep that promise.

• Christ’s words about God bringing justice “speedily” (Luke 18:7-8) parallel this assurance.

He will take vengeance on His adversaries and repay those who hate Him;

God’s retribution is personal and precise.

• Romans 12:19 urges believers to leave vengeance to God, confident He will repay.

• The final reckoning described in 2 Thessalonians 1:6-9 and Revelation 19:11-21 displays this repayment on a global scale.

• Isaiah 59:17-18 shows that divine vengeance is part of His righteousness, not a flaw in His character.

He will cleanse His land and His people.

Judgment is never God’s last word; purification and renewal follow.

• Ezekiel 36:25-28 promises a cleansing water and a new heart for Israel, matching the hope here.

• Zechariah 13:1 pictures a fountain opened “to cleanse them from sin and impurity,” tying directly to this promise.

• Joel 3:21 and Revelation 21:3-5 affirm that God will dwell with a purified people in a restored creation.

summary

Deuteronomy 32:43 calls heaven, earth, angels, and nations into a chorus of praise because God’s righteousness will prevail. He rescues His children, punishes evil, and restores both land and people. The verse reaches from Moses’ day to the cross, the church age, and the final consummation, assuring every believer that justice, joy, and renewal are certain in the faithful hands of the LORD.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(43) Rejoice, O ye nations, with his people.[9]--This is cited by St. Paul to show that the Gentiles must also "glorify God for His mercy" in sending Jesus Christ. But it is not wholly fulfilled yet. "If the fall of God's people was the wealth of the world . . . what will the receiving of them be. but life from the dead?" (See Romans 11:12; Romans 11:15; Romans 15:10.)

[9] The LXX. have a longer version of this verse, "Rejoice, ye heavens, with Him, and let all the angels of God worship Him (Hebrews 1:7); Rejoice, ye Gentiles, with His people, and let all the sons of God be strong in Him; for," &c.

And will be merciful unto.--Literally, will reconcile or make atonement for His land, the land of His people, or for the land of His people. He will cleanse, forgive, and be merciful to it. The very last words speak of local restoration of the land to the people, and the people to the land. Of no other land has He said "The land is mine" "Israel" alone is called His "firstborn."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 43. - "As this song commenced with an appeal to heaven and earth to give glory to the Lord (vers. 1-3), so it very suitably closes with an appeal to the heathen to rejoice with his people on account of the acts of the Lord" (Keil). Rejoice, O ye nations, with his people. The Authorized Version here follows the LXX., εὐφράνθητε ἔθνη μετὰ τοῦ λαοῦ αὐτοῦ, and so St. Paul cites the passage in Romans 15:10. The Jewish interpreters generally render, Praise his people, O nations; and this several Christian interpreters adopt. But as Rosenmüller remarks, it is the Divine righteousness manifested in the vindication of his people from their enemies that is to be celebrated, and not the people themselves, as what follows shows. Here as elsewhere the nations and the people are in contrast.
