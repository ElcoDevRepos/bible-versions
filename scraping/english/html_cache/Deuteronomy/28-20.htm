Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The LORD will send curses upon you
This phrase indicates divine judgment and the consequences of disobedience to God's covenant. In the context of Deuteronomy, Moses is addressing the Israelites, warning them of the repercussions of turning away from God's commandments. The concept of blessings and curses is central to the covenant relationship between God and Israel, as seen in Leviticus 26 and Deuteronomy 27-28. The curses are a direct result of breaking the covenant, emphasizing the seriousness of maintaining faithfulness to God.

confusion and reproof in all to which you put your hand
Confusion and reproof signify mental and moral disarray, leading to failure in endeavors. This reflects the chaos that ensues when a nation or individual turns away from divine guidance. The Hebrew word for confusion can also imply panic or disorientation, which is a common theme in the Old Testament when describing the fate of those who oppose God (e.g., Genesis 11:7-9, the Tower of Babel). Reproof suggests correction or rebuke, indicating that their actions will be met with divine disapproval.

until you are destroyed and quickly perish
This phrase underscores the severity and finality of the consequences. The destruction is not just physical but also spiritual, highlighting the totality of the judgment. The rapidity of the perishing suggests an inevitable and swift downfall, reminiscent of the fate of other nations that opposed God, such as the Canaanites. This serves as a warning to Israel to remain faithful to avoid such a fate.

because of the wickedness you have committed
Wickedness here refers to the moral and spiritual corruption that results from forsaking God's laws. The Old Testament frequently associates wickedness with idolatry, injustice, and immorality. The prophets often called Israel to repentance, warning them of the consequences of their wickedness (e.g., Isaiah 1:4, Jeremiah 2:19). This phrase highlights the cause-and-effect relationship between sin and divine judgment.

in forsaking Him
Forsaking God is the ultimate act of unfaithfulness, breaking the covenant relationship. This abandonment is often linked to idolatry, as seen in the history of Israel's repeated turning to other gods (e.g., Judges 2:12-13). The call to remain faithful is a recurring theme throughout the Bible, with the prophets urging the people to return to God (e.g., Hosea 14:1-2). In the New Testament, Jesus emphasizes the importance of abiding in Him (John 15:4-6), drawing a parallel to the necessity of remaining faithful to God.

Teaching Points
The Seriousness of Disobedience
God's covenant with Israel included both blessings and curses, underscoring the seriousness of obedience and the dire consequences of forsaking Him.

The Nature of God's Justice
God's justice is evident in His response to Israel's disobedience. He is faithful to His word, whether in blessing or in judgment.

The Call to Faithfulness
Believers today are called to remain faithful to God, understanding that turning away from Him leads to spiritual consequences.

The Role of Repentance
While Deuteronomy 28 outlines severe consequences for disobedience, the broader biblical account emphasizes God's readiness to forgive and restore those who repent.

Christ's Redemption from the Curse
In Christ, believers are redeemed from the curse of the law, highlighting the grace available through faith and the importance of living in obedience to God's commands.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:20 Mean
The LORD will send curses upon you

Deuteronomy 28 opens with blessings for obedience, then pivots to the sobering reality of disobedience. Verse 20 begins with a personal, active subject—“the LORD.” He is not merely allowing consequences; He is sending them. This underscores:

• God’s covenant faithfulness: He keeps His word whether in blessing (v. 1-14) or in judgment (Numbers 23:19).

• The seriousness of sin: “The curse of the LORD is on the house of the wicked” (Proverbs 3:33).

• A direct parallel: Leviticus 26:14-16 lists similar penalties, confirming this is not a one-time threat but a consistent covenant principle.

confusion and reproof in all to which you put your hand

“Confusion” speaks of mental disarray and loss of direction (see 1 Samuel 14:20). “Reproof” describes continual setbacks and failures. God touches “all to which you put your hand,” meaning:

• Work, plans, and relationships will unravel (Haggai 1:6).

• National life will be disoriented (Isaiah 30:17).

• Personal peace will evaporate (Psalm 107:17; “Fools suffered affliction because of their rebellious ways”).

Instead of the ordered fruitfulness promised in Deuteronomy 28:8, disobedience reverses every endeavor.

until you are destroyed and quickly perish

The judgment is not momentary frustration but an escalating crisis “until” destruction. Two layers appear:

• Progressive devastation: crops fail (v. 24), enemies conquer (v. 25), disease spreads (v. 22).

• Sudden collapse: “quickly perish” echoes Psalm 73:19—“How suddenly they are brought to ruin!”

God’s patience has limits when repentance is refused (2 Chronicles 36:15-16). The verse warns that ignoring early rebukes ends in total ruin (Proverbs 29:1).

because of the wickedness you have committed in forsaking Him

The cause is crystal clear: wickedness and abandonment of the LORD. Covenant breach, not random fate, triggers the curse. Key insights:

• Sin is relational betrayal (Jeremiah 2:13).

• Forsaking God cancels protective blessing (2 Chronicles 15:2).

• Moral accountability is individual and corporate (Ezekiel 18:30-31).

Repentance remains the prescribed remedy (Deuteronomy 30:1-3), yet without it judgment runs its full course.

summary

• God Himself enforces covenant terms.

• Disobedience invites targeted, comprehensive disruption in every endeavor.

• Judgment intensifies until life collapses.

• The root issue is forsaking the LORD; returning to Him is the only escape (2 Chronicles 7:14).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) Cursing, vexation, and rebuke.--Deficiency, and anxiety, and failure in every enterprise, would convey the idea, according to another interpretation. There are two views of the derivation of the first of the three words employed. Probably the Authorised Version is right. The three words have each of them the definite article in the original, just as if they were so many diseases. "The curse, and the terror, and the rebuke" of the Almighty are terrible obstacles to any human undertaking.

In all that thou settest thine hand unto for to do.--Literally, in every putting forth of thine hand which thou makest, i.e., in every undertaking. This is the opposite of Deuteronomy 28:8.

Thou hast forsaken me.--Moses and Jehovah are here identified. This is characteristic. The prophets say, "Thus saith the Lord." Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face, sometimes exhorts Israel in His name without any such introduction. (Comp. the phrase "to forsake Moses" (literally, apostasy from Moses) in Acts 21:21.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 20-26. - First group. The curse should come upon them in various forms of evil, filling them with terror and dismay, and threatening them with utter ruin (cf. Malachi 2:2). Verse 20. - Vexation; rather, consternation; the deadly confusion with which God confounds his enemies. The same word is used in Deuteronomy 7:23; 1 Samuel 14:20. Rebuke; rather, threatening.
