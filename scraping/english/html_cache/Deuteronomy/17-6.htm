On the testimony of two or three witnesses
This phrase establishes a legal principle that emphasizes the need for corroboration in judicial matters. In ancient Israel, the requirement for multiple witnesses was a safeguard against false accusations and miscarriages of justice. This principle is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus and Paul both reference the necessity of two or three witnesses (Matthew 18:16, 2 Corinthians 13:1). It underscores the importance of truth and fairness in legal proceedings, reflecting God's justice.

a man shall be put to death
The context here is the administration of capital punishment under the Mosaic Law. The death penalty was prescribed for certain offenses, such as murder, idolatry, and blasphemy, reflecting the seriousness with which these transgressions were viewed. This phrase highlights the gravity of the judicial process and the need for certainty before such an irreversible sentence is carried out. It also points to the holiness and justice of God, who demands that sin be dealt with severely.

but he shall not be executed on the testimony of a lone witness
This clause reinforces the protection against wrongful execution by prohibiting the death penalty based on the testimony of a single individual. It reflects a deep concern for justice and the value of human life, ensuring that no one is condemned without sufficient evidence. This principle is consistent with the broader biblical theme of justice and mercy, and it serves as a reminder of the fallibility of human judgment. It also foreshadows the ultimate justice and mercy found in Jesus Christ, who bore the penalty for sin on behalf of humanity.

What Does Deuteronomy 17:6 Mean
On the testimony of two or three witnesses

• God establishes that justice in Israel must never rest on hearsay. A charge demanding the ultimate penalty requires multiple eyewitnesses, ensuring facts are confirmed and bias is curbed (Deuteronomy 19:15; Numbers 35:30).

• Jesus later appeals to the same standard when confronting His critics: “Even in your Law it is written that the testimony of two men is valid” (John 8:17). Paul echoes it, “Every matter must be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses” (2 Corinthians 13:1; see also Matthew 18:16).

• This principle teaches us to value corroboration—truth verified by more than one voice protects the innocent and upholds God-given justice.

a man shall be put to death

• The context of Deuteronomy 17 addresses idolatry and treason against the covenant. Capital punishment was not arbitrary; it recognized that certain sins threatened the whole nation’s relationship with the Lord (Numbers 15:30–36; Hebrews 10:28).

• By tying the death penalty to verified evidence, the Lord combines holiness with mercy. The severity of the sentence underscores the seriousness of sin, while the evidentiary safeguard preserves life when uncertainty exists.

• Civil authorities still bear responsibility to punish evil (Romans 13:1-4). Although methods differ today, the underlying principle of proportionate justice and careful proof remains.

but he shall not be executed on the testimony of a lone witness

• A single accuser could be mistaken, biased, or malicious. Requiring at least two witnesses reveals God’s concern for due process long before modern legal systems adopted similar safeguards (John 7:51; Acts 25:16).

• In church life, the same caution applies: “Do not entertain an accusation against an elder unless it is brought by two or three witnesses” (1 Timothy 5:19). Spiritual discipline and public reputation both demand careful verification.

• Practically, the verse warns believers to resist snap judgments. Whether in personal conflicts, social media claims, or congregational issues, God’s people are called to gather trustworthy evidence before pronouncing guilt.

summary

Deuteronomy 17:6 champions a balanced justice rooted in truth. Multiple witnesses confirm facts, capital punishment highlights sin’s gravity, and the ban on solitary testimony shields the innocent. From Israel’s courts to New Testament teaching, God’s standard remains: truth must be established, life protected, and justice carried out with both firmness and fairness.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 6, 7. - Only on the testimony of more than one witness could the accused be condemned (of Numbers 35:30); and the hand of the witnesses was to be first against him to put him to death - a rule which would tend to prevent accusations being lightly adduced, as none would venture to witness against any one unless so deeply convinced of his guilt that they were willing to assume the responsibility of inflicting on him the last penalty with their own hands. Worthy of death be put to death; i.e. adjudged or appointed to death; literally, the dead man shall die. מֵת, the part. of מוּת, to die, is here equivalent to בֶּן מָוֶת, son of death (1 Samuel 20:31), or אִישׁ מָוֶת, a man of death (1 Kings 2:26), i.e. one assigned to death, already the property of death, and so as good as dead. Put the evil away; literally, consume or sweep away the evil. The verb בָּעַר means primarily to consume by burning.
