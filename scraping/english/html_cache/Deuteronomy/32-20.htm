Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He said:
This phrase introduces the speaker, God, who is addressing the Israelites through Moses. It is part of the Song of Moses, a prophetic and poetic declaration given to the Israelites before they enter the Promised Land. The song serves as a witness against them, highlighting God's faithfulness and their potential unfaithfulness.

“I will hide My face from them;
In biblical terms, God hiding His face signifies a withdrawal of His favor and presence. This is a form of divine judgment, indicating that the Israelites will experience the consequences of their disobedience. The concept of God hiding His face is seen elsewhere in Scripture, such as in Isaiah 59:2, where sin creates a separation between God and His people.

I will see what will be their end.
This phrase suggests a period of testing or observation. God allows the Israelites to face the results of their actions to reveal their true character and the outcome of their choices. It reflects the principle of sowing and reaping found in Galatians 6:7-8, where actions have corresponding consequences.

For they are a perverse generation—
The term "perverse" indicates moral corruption and deviation from God's ways. This description echoes the language used in the New Testament, such as in Philippians 2:15, where believers are called to shine as lights in a "crooked and perverse generation." It highlights the contrast between God's holiness and human sinfulness.

children of unfaithfulness.
This phrase underscores the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where Israel is often depicted as God's children. Their unfaithfulness is akin to spiritual adultery, breaking the covenant established at Sinai. This theme is prevalent in the prophetic books, such as Hosea, where Israel's unfaithfulness is likened to an unfaithful spouse. The call to faithfulness is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who embodies perfect obedience and faithfulness to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The speaker of the song in Deuteronomy 32, Moses is delivering God's message to the Israelites.

2. Israelites
The audience of the song, they are described as a "perverse generation" and "children of unfaithfulness."

3. God
The one who declares He will hide His face from the Israelites due to their unfaithfulness.

4. The Song of Moses
A prophetic song given by Moses to the Israelites, warning them of the consequences of their disobedience.

5. The Promised Land
The land that the Israelites are about to enter, which is contingent upon their faithfulness to God.
Teaching Points
The Consequence of Unfaithfulness
God’s decision to hide His face is a direct response to the Israelites' unfaithfulness. This serves as a warning about the spiritual consequences of turning away from God.

The Nature of a Perverse Generation
The term "perverse" (Hebrew: "tahpukah") indicates a moral corruption and deviation from God's ways. Reflect on how this applies to contemporary society and personal life.

God’s Patience and Justice
While God is patient, there is a limit to His tolerance of sin. His justice requires that He respond to persistent disobedience.

The Importance of Faithfulness
Faithfulness is central to maintaining a relationship with God. Reflect on personal areas where faithfulness can be strengthened.

Hope for Restoration
Even when God hides His face, there is hope for restoration through repentance and returning to Him.

What Does Deuteronomy 32:20 Mean
“I will hide My face from them”

• The Lord announces a deliberate withdrawal of His felt presence. Just as in Deuteronomy 31:17–18, this hiding signals the loss of His favor and protection.

• Psalm 30:7 echoes, “You hid Your face, and I was dismayed,” showing how spiritually devastating such separation is.

• Isaiah 59:2 reminds us that sin creates this barrier: “Your iniquities have separated you from your God; your sins have hidden His face from you.”

• Yet the hiding is not random punishment—it is a sober response to persistent rebellion, aimed at awakening repentance (2 Chronicles 15:2).

“I will see what will be their end”

• God steps back to let consequences run their course, much like Romans 1:24 where He “gave them over” to their own desires.

• He is not ignorant of the outcome; Psalm 139:4 shows His omniscience. But He observes, allowing Israel to experience the fruit of disobedience (Galatians 6:7).

• Psalm 37:37-38 contrasts the peaceful end of the righteous with the destruction of transgressors, underscoring the certainty of divine justice.

“For they are a perverse generation—”

• Moses earlier said the same in Deuteronomy 32:5: “They are a crooked and perverse generation.” The charge is moral distortion—calling evil good and good evil (Isaiah 5:20).

• Jesus borrowed this description in Matthew 17:17 toward His contemporaries, linking Israel’s ancient failures to ongoing unbelief.

• Believers today are urged to shine “in the midst of a crooked and perverse generation” (Philippians 2:15), proving the relevance of this warning.

“children of unfaithfulness”

• The phrase exposes a heritage of infidelity—offspring who mirror their ancestors’ covenant breaches (Jeremiah 3:20).

• Hosea’s marriage imagery (Hosea 1:2) paints Israel as adulterous, illustrating spiritual unfaithfulness through literal family language.

• Isaiah 1:2 laments, “I reared children and brought them up, but they have rebelled against Me,” highlighting the heartbreak of a Father betrayed by His own household.

summary

Deuteronomy 32:20 portrays a solemn moment when God, grieved by ongoing rebellion, withdraws His protection, observes the inevitable consequences, and labels His people as twisted and unfaithful. The verse warns that divine patience has limits, yet the very act of hiding His face is designed to stir repentance and restoration. Choosing obedience keeps us under His smiling face; persisting in perversity invites the sorrowful silence of God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) A very froward generation.--Literally, a generation of perversities.

Children in whom is no faith.--Literally, children !--there is no relying on them. (Comp. Deuteronomy 5:5.) Faith is not used in the sense of "belief" or "confidence," but as in the expression to "keep faith," or to "break faith," children who will keep no faith with one.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - God himself comes forth to announce his resolution to withdraw his favor from them, and to inflict chastisement upon them; he would withdraw his protecting care of them, and see how they would fare without that; and he would also send on them the tokens of his displeasure. A very froward generation, etc.; literally, a generation of perversities, an utterly perverse and faithless race.
