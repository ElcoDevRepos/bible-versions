Deuteronomy 28
1. The blessings for obedience
15. The curses for disobedience














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Just as it pleased the LORD to make you prosper and multiply
This phrase reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where obedience leads to blessings. The prosperity and multiplication of Israel are seen as fulfillment of God's promises to the patriarchs, such as in Genesis 12:2, where God promises Abraham that He will make him a great nation. The prosperity of Israel is not merely material but also spiritual, as they are called to be a light to the nations (Isaiah 49:6). This reflects God's character as a loving and faithful provider.

so also it will please Him to annihilate you and destroy you
This part of the verse highlights the conditional nature of the covenant. Just as blessings follow obedience, curses follow disobedience. The language of annihilation and destruction is severe, emphasizing the seriousness of turning away from God. This reflects the Deuteronomic theme of blessings and curses (Deuteronomy 11:26-28). The concept of divine retribution is consistent with the holiness and justice of God, who cannot tolerate sin (Habakkuk 1:13).

And you will be uprooted from the land you are entering to possess
The land is central to Israel's identity and covenant with God. Being uprooted signifies exile, a punishment for covenant unfaithfulness. This prophecy was fulfilled historically in the Babylonian exile (2 Kings 25:1-21). The land is not just a physical territory but a place of divine promise and presence. The loss of the land symbolizes a broken relationship with God. This also foreshadows the ultimate restoration through Christ, who offers a new covenant and a spiritual inheritance (Hebrews 9:15).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is both the source of blessings and the enforcer of curses based on Israel's obedience or disobedience.

2. Israelites
The chosen people of God, recipients of the covenant blessings and curses outlined in Deuteronomy.

3. The Promised Land
The land of Canaan, which the Israelites are entering to possess, representing God's promise and blessing.

4. Moses
The leader of the Israelites who delivers God's laws and warnings to the people.

5. Covenant at Sinai
The agreement between God and Israel, where blessings and curses are contingent upon Israel's obedience to God's commandments.
Teaching Points
The Dual Nature of God's Pleasure
God takes pleasure in both blessing and disciplining His people. His actions are always just and rooted in His covenant promises.

The Seriousness of Disobedience
Disobedience to God's commands leads to severe consequences. This serves as a warning to remain faithful and obedient to God's Word.

The Importance of Covenant Faithfulness
The relationship between God and His people is covenantal. Faithfulness to this covenant is crucial for experiencing God's blessings.

The Reality of Divine Judgment
God's judgment is real and should not be taken lightly. It serves as a call to repentance and a return to God's ways.

Hope in Restoration
Even in judgment, there is hope for restoration. God's ultimate desire is for His people to return to Him and be restored.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:63 Mean
Just as it pleased the LORD to make you prosper and multiply

• God’s default posture toward Israel—and toward all who walk in covenant with Him—is blessing (Deuteronomy 28:1–14).

• From Abraham onward, He delights to give good gifts, fulfill promises, and increase His people (Genesis 12:2; Psalm 35:27).

• The verse reminds us that the Lord’s pleasure is rooted in His character: He is generous, faithful, and unchanging (James 1:17; Malachi 3:6).

• The prosperity spoken of here is not merely material; it includes spiritual vitality, national security, and an enduring legacy (Psalm 1:3; Joshua 23:14).

• When Israel obeyed, history showed abundant harvests, peace from enemies, and a reputation that drew nations to the God of Israel (1 Kings 4:20–25; Deuteronomy 4:6–8).

so also it will please Him to annihilate you and destroy you

• The same holy character that delights to bless also obliges God to judge persistent rebellion (Deuteronomy 28:15–68).

• Divine “pleasure” here is not cruel enjoyment but the satisfaction of perfect justice—God stays true to His word, whether in blessing or in curse (Numbers 23:19; Romans 11:22).

• Scripture consistently pairs love and holiness; God’s wrath is His settled opposition to sin (Psalm 5:4–5; Hebrews 10:26–31).

• Israel’s covenant included clear warnings: idolatry, injustice, and disregard for His commands would invite devastating consequences (Leviticus 26:14–39; 2 Chronicles 36:15–17).

• The severity underscores the seriousness of sin and the reliability of God’s promises on both sides of the ledger (Galatians 6:7; John 3:36).

And you will be uprooted from the land you are entering to possess

• Land was central to the covenant; exile signified the deepest form of loss—spiritual, national, and personal (Genesis 17:8; Deuteronomy 30:17–18).

• “Uprooted” pictures a farmer tearing out a plant that no longer bears fruit, echoing later prophetic imagery (Isaiah 5:1–7; Matthew 21:43).

• Fulfillment came in stages: Assyria carried off the northern kingdom (2 Kings 17:6), Babylon exiled Judah (2 Kings 25:11), and Rome ultimately scattered the nation (Luke 21:20–24).

• Even in judgment, God preserved a remnant and promised restoration for those who return to Him (Jeremiah 29:10–14; Romans 11:1–5).

• The warning applies broadly: any people or individual who rejects God’s rule forfeits His place of blessing (John 15:6; Revelation 2:5).

summary

Deuteronomy 28:63 holds blessing and curse in the same divine hand. The Lord genuinely delights to prosper an obedient people, yet He is equally committed to uphold justice when they persist in sin. His faithfulness means He keeps every promise—whether to plant or to uproot. The verse calls us to revere His holiness, rest in His goodness, and remain in wholehearted obedience so that His pleasure may rest on us for blessing, not for judgment.
