When the LORD your God has delivered it into your hand
This phrase emphasizes the divine sovereignty and providence in the victories of Israel. The Israelites were to recognize that their success in battle was not due to their own strength or strategy, but because God had granted them victory. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God as the ultimate deliverer, seen in passages like Exodus 14:14, where God fights for Israel. The phrase also underscores the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God promises to deliver their enemies into their hands as part of His covenant blessings (Deuteronomy 7:1-2).

you must put every male to the sword
This command reflects the ancient Near Eastern context of warfare, where the complete destruction of enemy combatants was often practiced to prevent future retaliation and ensure security. The instruction to put every male to the sword was a common practice in the ancient world, intended to eliminate the military threat. In the context of Deuteronomy, this command is part of the herem, or ban, which was a form of divine judgment against the Canaanite nations for their idolatry and wickedness (Deuteronomy 9:4-5). This action was not merely a military strategy but a fulfillment of divine justice. Theologically, it prefigures the ultimate judgment of God against sin, as seen in the eschatological visions of Revelation. Additionally, it serves as a type of Christ's ultimate victory over sin and death, where He will put all His enemies under His feet (1 Corinthians 15:25).

Teaching Points
Divine Sovereignty
Recognize that God is sovereign over nations and history. His commands, even those that are difficult to understand, are part of His divine plan.

Holiness and Judgment
Understand that the command to destroy the Canaanite cities was part of God's judgment on their sin and a call for Israel to remain holy and separate from pagan practices.

Obedience to God
Reflect on the importance of obedience to God's commands, even when they are challenging or counter-cultural.

Spiritual Warfare
Consider the spiritual application of this passage, recognizing that Christians are called to engage in spiritual warfare against sin and evil, using the "sword of the Spirit," which is the Word of God.

New Covenant Perspective
Balance the Old Testament commands with the teachings of Jesus, who calls His followers to love their enemies and seek peace.

What Does Deuteronomy 20:13 Mean
When the LORD your God has delivered it

• The initiative begins with the LORD, not Israel’s military prowess. He is the One who “delivered” Jericho to Joshua (Joshua 6:2) and “drove out nations greater and stronger” than Israel (Deuteronomy 7:1-2).

• God’s active role underscores His covenant faithfulness (Genesis 15:18-21). Just as He promised Abram the land, He now fulfills that promise in real time.

• The certainty of His action encourages confidence; Israel does not wonder if victory will come, but waits for the moment God grants it (Psalm 44:3).

into your hand

• “Into your hand” speaks of stewardship, not mere possession. What God grants must be handled according to His instructions (Judges 7:15).

• The phrase underscores accountability. Israel will answer to God for what it does with the victory He provides (1 Samuel 17:46).

• God’s transfer of the city prevents Israel from boasting in itself (Joshua 10:8). It is a gift that carries responsibility, not a license for self‐glory.

you must put every male to the sword

• In the context of Deuteronomy 20, this directive applies to hostile cities that reject Israel’s offer of peace (vv. 10-12). Refusal brings total defeat.

• Eliminating the fighting males removes the immediate military threat. Similar instructions appear in Numbers 31:7 and 1 Samuel 15:3.

• The command also protects Israel spiritually. God’s concern is that foreign warriors, left alive, would regroup and lure Israel into idolatry (Deuteronomy 20:16-18; 7:4).

• Though severe, the measure reflects divine justice against persistent, unrepentant aggression (Romans 1:18), not random cruelty. God’s holiness demands decisive action against sin when mercy is refused.

summary

Deuteronomy 20:13 reveals a threefold pattern: God secures the victory, entrusts it to His people, and prescribes how they must complete it. The verse affirms His sovereignty, Israel’s accountability, and the necessity of decisive judgment against entrenched evil.
