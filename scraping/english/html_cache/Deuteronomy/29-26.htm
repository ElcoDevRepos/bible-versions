They went and served other gods
This phrase highlights the Israelites' disobedience and idolatry, a recurring theme throughout the Old Testament. The act of serving other gods was a direct violation of the first commandment (Exodus 20:3). Historically, the Israelites were surrounded by nations with polytheistic beliefs, such as the Canaanites, whose gods included Baal and Asherah. This cultural influence often led Israel astray. The phrase also reflects the spiritual adultery that God condemned through prophets like Hosea, who used marriage as a metaphor for Israel's relationship with God (Hosea 1-3).

and they worshiped gods they had not known
This part emphasizes the foreign nature of these deities to the Israelites. Unlike the God of Israel, who revealed Himself through mighty acts and covenants, these gods were unknown and unproven. The Israelites' worship of these gods was not based on any historical relationship or divine revelation. This ignorance is condemned in Jeremiah 10:2-5, where the prophet warns against adopting the customs of the nations. The phrase also underscores the futility of idolatry, as these gods could not provide the guidance or protection that the true God offered.

gods that the LORD had not given to them
This phrase indicates that the true God had not sanctioned or provided these deities for worship. It underscores the exclusivity of the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God alone was to be worshiped (Deuteronomy 6:4-5). The LORD's provision of Himself as the sole deity is a recurring theme, as seen in the Shema. This exclusivity is further reinforced in the New Testament, where Jesus Christ is presented as the fulfillment of God's promises and the only way to the Father (John 14:6). The phrase also serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and the futility of seeking spiritual fulfillment outside His provision.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites who is delivering the message of Deuteronomy to the people. He is reminding them of their covenant with God and the consequences of disobedience.

2. Israelites
The chosen people of God who are being warned against idolatry and reminded of their covenant responsibilities.

3. Other gods
Refers to the false deities that the Israelites were tempted to worship, which were common among the surrounding nations.

4. Covenant at Moab
The renewal of the covenant between God and the Israelites, which is taking place in the plains of Moab before they enter the Promised Land.

5. The LORD (Yahweh)
The one true God who delivered the Israelites from Egypt and established a covenant with them.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Idolatry
Idolatry is not just the worship of physical idols but can include anything that takes the place of God in our hearts.

Faithfulness to the Covenant
Just as the Israelites were called to be faithful to their covenant with God, Christians are called to remain faithful to their relationship with Christ.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Turning away from God leads to spiritual and often physical consequences, as seen throughout Israel's history.

The Importance of Knowing God
Worshiping "gods they had not known" highlights the importance of truly knowing and understanding the character of the one true God.

Guarding Against Cultural Influences
The Israelites were influenced by surrounding nations; Christians today must be vigilant against cultural influences that lead away from God.

What Does Deuteronomy 29:26 Mean
They went and served other gods

Israel did not merely flirt with idolatry; they actively “went” after it—an intentional departure from the covenant love of the LORD (Exodus 32:8; Judges 2:12).

• The verb “went” implies movement of heart before any physical act; rebellion always begins inwardly (James 1:14–15).

• “Served” shows that idolatry always enslaves (Romans 6:16). What promised freedom ultimately bound them.

• The same warning had echoed through Moses’ sermons: “You shall not follow other gods, the gods of the peoples around you” (Deuteronomy 6:14). By chapter 29 they are being told what will happen when that warning is ignored.

and they worshiped gods they had not known

Worship belongs to relationship. The LORD had revealed Himself in fire, cloud, manna, and covenant love (Deuteronomy 4:35). These idols offered no such self-revelation.

• “Had not known” underscores that these deities were strangers; intimacy with the living God was traded for emptiness (Jeremiah 2:5).

• Psalm 81:9 voices the same grief: “There shall be no strange god among you.”

• In Acts 17:23 Paul points to an altar “To an Unknown God,” proving that worship without revelation is guesswork, not relationship. Israel already possessed revelation—and walked away from it.

gods that the LORD had not given to them

God alone designates proper worship (Deuteronomy 12:5–7). Anything else is self-made religion.

• Deuteronomy 4:19 speaks of heavenly bodies “which the LORD your God has allotted to all the nations,” a reminder that even created wonders are not to be worshiped.

• By stating “had not given,” Moses highlights both God’s sovereignty and His generosity. The LORD gives every good gift (James 1:17); idols give nothing.

• Deuteronomy 32:17 laments that they “sacrificed to demons, not to God,” showing that what God withholds, He withholds for their protection.

summary

Deuteronomy 29:26 exposes a tragic exchange: purposeful departure, worship without relationship, and devotion to unauthorized gods. The verse warns that idolatry is never innocent curiosity—it is covenant betrayal that forfeits the blessings God longs to give. Fidelity to the one true God is the only path to life.
