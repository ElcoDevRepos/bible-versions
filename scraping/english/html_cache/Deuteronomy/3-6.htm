We devoted them to destruction
This phrase refers to the practice of "herem," a term meaning to devote something to God by completely destroying it. This was a common practice in ancient warfare, especially in the context of the Israelites' conquest of Canaan. It was seen as a way to purify the land from idolatry and pagan practices, ensuring that the Israelites would not be led astray by the customs of the conquered peoples. This act was also a form of divine judgment against the Canaanites for their sins, as described in Genesis 15:16.

as we had done to Sihon king of Heshbon
Sihon was the king of the Amorites, and his defeat is recounted in Numbers 21:21-31. The Israelites' victory over Sihon was significant because it marked the beginning of their conquest of the Transjordan region. This victory demonstrated God's power and faithfulness in fulfilling His promises to Israel. The defeat of Sihon served as a precedent for the conquest of other territories, showing that God was with Israel in their battles.

utterly destroying the men, women, and children of every city
The complete destruction of the inhabitants of these cities was commanded by God as part of the herem. This was intended to prevent the Israelites from being influenced by the idolatrous practices of the Canaanites. While this command is difficult to understand from a modern perspective, it reflects the seriousness with which God viewed the threat of idolatry and the need for Israel to remain a holy people. This act also prefigures the final judgment, where sin and rebellion against God will be completely eradicated.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Judgment
The destruction of Og's kingdom demonstrates God's sovereign authority over nations and His right to execute judgment.

Obedience to God's Commands
The Israelites' complete destruction of their enemies reflects their obedience to God's specific instructions, emphasizing the importance of following God's will fully.

The Seriousness of Sin
The total destruction of these cities serves as a sobering reminder of the seriousness of sin and the consequences of living in opposition to God.

Trust in God's Promises
The victory over Og reinforces the reliability of God's promises, encouraging believers to trust in His faithfulness in their own lives.

Spiritual Warfare
Just as the Israelites faced physical battles, Christians are engaged in spiritual warfare, requiring vigilance and reliance on God's strength.

What Does Deuteronomy 3:6 Mean
We devoted them to destruction

Moses recalls how Israel, under God’s direct command, placed the cities of Bashan under the ḥerem—total destruction dedicated to the Lord (Deuteronomy 3:6: “We devoted them to destruction”).

• This act was not random violence but obedience to God’s judicial sentence on an irredeemably wicked culture (Genesis 15:16; Deuteronomy 9:5).

• The ḥerem principle protected Israel from being drawn into idolatry and moral corruption (Deuteronomy 7:2–4; Exodus 23:33).

• God’s holiness requires that sin be judged; here He uses Israel as His instrument just as later He will use Assyria and Babylon to judge Israel (Isaiah 10:5–7; Habakkuk 1:6).

as we had done to Sihon king of Heshbon

The phrase ties this victory to the earlier conquest over Sihon (Numbers 21:21–31; Deuteronomy 2:30–34).

• By reminding Israel of Sihon’s defeat, Moses underscores God’s consistent faithfulness and power (Joshua 24:8).

• These linked victories form a pattern: when Israel trusts and obeys, God grants triumph (Psalm 44:1–3).

• The repetition also reassures the next generation that entering Canaan is not a gamble; God’s past actions guarantee future success (Deuteronomy 31:6; Romans 15:4).

utterly destroying the men, women, and children of every city

This hard statement confronts modern readers, yet:

• It reflects a decisive, once-for-all judgment on cultures steeped in idolatry, child sacrifice, and sexual perversion (Leviticus 18:24–30; Deuteronomy 12:29–31).

• Scripture emphasizes God’s patience before judgment; centuries passed before this verdict fell (2 Peter 3:9; Romans 2:4–5).

• Total destruction prevented survivors from re-establishing practices that would entice Israel away from covenant faithfulness (Deuteronomy 20:16–18; 1 Kings 11:4–8).

• The action foreshadows the final, comprehensive judgment God will execute against all evil, vindicating His holiness and protecting His redeemed people (Revelation 19:11–16; 21:8).

summary

Deuteronomy 3:6 records Israel’s obedience in carrying out God’s righteous judgment on Bashan. The verse links the victory to earlier conquests, testifying to God’s unwavering faithfulness. Though sobering, the total destruction displays God’s holiness, reveals His patience before judgment, and safeguards His covenant people from corruption, pointing ultimately to the final triumph of God’s justice.
