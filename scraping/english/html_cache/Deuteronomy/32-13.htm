He made him ride on the heights of the land
This phrase signifies God's elevation of Israel to a place of prominence and blessing. Riding on the heights symbolizes victory, dominion, and the ability to see far and wide, suggesting a position of advantage and oversight. Biblically, this can be connected to the promises given to the patriarchs, such as in Genesis 12:2, where God promises to make Abraham a great nation. The imagery of riding on heights also reflects the blessings of obedience as outlined in Deuteronomy 28:1, where God promises to set Israel high above all nations if they obey His commands.

and fed him the produce of the field.
This phrase highlights God's provision and sustenance for Israel. The produce of the field represents the agricultural abundance of the Promised Land, a land "flowing with milk and honey" as described in Exodus 3:8. This abundance is a direct fulfillment of God's covenant promises, emphasizing His faithfulness. The produce also symbolizes spiritual nourishment, as seen in John 6:35, where Jesus refers to Himself as the "bread of life," indicating that true sustenance comes from God.

He nourished him with honey from the rock
Honey from the rock is a metaphor for unexpected blessings and provision from unlikely sources. In the arid and rocky terrain of the Near East, finding honey would be rare and precious, symbolizing God's miraculous provision. This can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the source of spiritual sweetness and sustenance, as reflected in Psalm 81:16, where God promises to satisfy His people with honey from the rock. It also points to the sweetness of God's word, as described in Psalm 119:103.

and oil from the flinty crag,
Oil, often associated with olive oil, was a staple in ancient Israel for cooking, anointing, and lighting lamps. The mention of oil from the flinty crag suggests abundance and prosperity even in hard and barren places. This imagery can be linked to the anointing of the Holy Spirit, as oil is a symbol of the Spirit's presence and blessing, seen in passages like 1 Samuel 16:13, where David is anointed by Samuel. The flinty crag, a hard and unyielding surface, underscores the miraculous nature of God's provision, echoing the theme of God bringing forth life and blessing from seemingly impossible situations.

Teaching Points
God's Provision in Abundance
God provides not just the essentials but in abundance, symbolized by honey and oil. Trust in His provision even in difficult circumstances.

Symbolism of the Rock
The rock represents stability and strength. God can bring forth blessings from the most unlikely places, reminding us of His sovereignty.

Faithfulness and Obedience
The Israelites' journey is a testament to God's faithfulness. Our response should be one of obedience and gratitude for His continued provision.

Spiritual Nourishment
Just as God nourished the Israelites physically, He offers spiritual nourishment through His Word and Spirit. Seek Him for sustenance in your spiritual journey.

Living in the Promised Abundance
As believers, we are called to live in the abundance of God's promises, not just materially but spiritually, embracing the fullness of life in Christ.

What Does Deuteronomy 32:13 Mean
He made him ride on the heights of the land

• Picture the Lord lifting Israel above danger and difficulty, much like an eagle carries its young (see Deuteronomy 32:11).

• “Ride on the heights” speaks of secure victory and elevated privilege—echoed in Habakkuk 3:19 and Isaiah 58:14, where God lets His people “walk on high places.”

• Deuteronomy 33:29 ties the image to conquering foes: “Your enemies will cower before you, and you will tread on their heights”.

• For us, the verse reminds that God does more than rescue; He positions His children to flourish, giving vantage and authority they could never win on their own.

and fed him the produce of the field

• Freedom in the land came with tangible provision. Moses had earlier described Canaan as “a land of wheat and barley… a land where bread will not be scarce” (Deuteronomy 8:7-10).

• God’s care is practical: grain in the barns, fruit on the vines, daily bread on the table (Psalm 104:14-15; Matthew 6:26).

• Note the sequence: elevation first, provision second. When God sets His people where He wants them, the resources follow.

He nourished him with honey from the rock

• Honey oozing from crags (Psalm 81:16) is unexpected sweetness in a harsh place. The Lord delights in surprising His people with pleasure where others expect only barrenness.

• Judges 14:8-9 shows Samson scooping honey from a lion’s carcass—another “impossible” source of sweetness. God presses goodness out of what looks dead or dry.

• In personal terms, He turns stony seasons into testimonies of His kindness, feeding faith with delights that could have come from nowhere else.

and oil from the flinty crag

• Olive trees often grow in rocky terraces; their oil was Israel’s staple for food, light, and healing (Deuteronomy 8:8; Leviticus 24:2; Isaiah 1:6).

• A “flinty crag” is the opposite of fertile soil, yet God draws rich, soothing oil from it—much like Job 29:6 recalls “the rock poured out for me streams of oil.”

• The point: even the hardest situations submit to the Lord’s purpose. He extracts blessing from bedrock, proving that nothing—no person, place, or problem—is beyond His provision.

summary

Deuteronomy 32:13 celebrates the Lord’s comprehensive care. He lifts His people to secure heights, supplies everyday needs, surprises them with sweetness in barren places, and draws healing abundance from the hardest ground. The verse invites confident trust: wherever God leads, He also provides—abundantly, creatively, and unfailingly.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - He made him ride on the high places of the earth. To ride over or drive over the heights of a country is figuratively to subjugate and take possession of that country (cf. Deuteronomy 33:29; Isaiah 58:14). Israel, having subjugated Canaan, could eat of its produce, the increase of the fields, as his own. Honey out of the rock, and oil out of the flinty rock. Canaan abounded in wild bees, which had their hives in crevices of the rock, and in olive trees, which grew on a rocky soil; as is still the case in Palestine.
