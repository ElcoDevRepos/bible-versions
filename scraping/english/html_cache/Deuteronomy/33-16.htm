with the choice gifts of the land and everything in it
This phrase highlights the abundance and fertility of the Promised Land, a recurring theme in the blessings given to the tribes of Israel. The "choice gifts" refer to the agricultural bounty, including grain, wine, and livestock, which were seen as direct blessings from God. This reflects the covenantal promise made to the patriarchs, particularly Abraham, about the land flowing with milk and honey (Exodus 3:8). The land's fertility is a sign of divine favor and fulfillment of God's promises.

and with the favor of Him who dwelt in the burning bush
This phrase connects to the theophany experienced by Moses in Exodus 3, where God revealed Himself in the burning bush. The "favor" indicates divine presence and blessing, emphasizing God's ongoing relationship with His people. The burning bush symbolizes God's holiness, power, and the mystery of His presence. This reference serves as a reminder of God's covenantal faithfulness and His role as the deliverer of Israel. It also foreshadows the coming of Christ, who embodies God's presence among His people (John 1:14).

May these rest on the head of Joseph
Joseph, one of the twelve sons of Jacob, receives a special blessing. His tribes, Ephraim and Manasseh, are often given prominence in Israel's history. The "head of Joseph" signifies leadership and honor, reflecting Joseph's role in saving his family during the famine (Genesis 45:7). This blessing acknowledges Joseph's faithfulness and God's providence in his life, serving as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate savior and provider for His people.

and crown the brow of the prince of his brothers
Joseph is referred to as the "prince of his brothers," highlighting his preeminence among the tribes. This title reflects his leadership and the double portion of inheritance given to his descendants. The imagery of a crown signifies authority and honor, pointing to the future leadership roles of Ephraim and Manasseh in Israel's history. This also prefigures Christ, the King of Kings, who is crowned with glory and honor (Hebrews 2:9) and holds preeminence over all creation.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joseph
One of the twelve sons of Jacob, Joseph is a central figure in the latter part of Genesis. He is known for his rise to power in Egypt and his role in saving his family during a famine.

2. Moses
The author of Deuteronomy, Moses is the leader of the Israelites who brought them out of Egypt. He is delivering blessings to the tribes of Israel in this chapter.

3. The Burning Bush
A significant event in Moses' life where God appeared to him in a burning bush, commissioning him to lead the Israelites out of Egypt (Exodus 3).

4. The Land of Israel
The "best gifts of the earth" refer to the promised land, a land flowing with milk and honey, which God promised to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

5. The Tribes of Israel
Joseph's blessing is part of a series of blessings Moses gives to the tribes of Israel before his death.
Teaching Points
God's Favor and Presence
The favor of God, as seen in the burning bush, signifies His presence and blessing. Believers are encouraged to seek God's presence in their lives, knowing that His favor brings spiritual and material blessings.

Blessings of the Earth
The "best gifts of the earth" remind us of God's provision. Christians are called to be good stewards of the resources God provides, using them for His glory and the benefit of others.

Leadership and Responsibility
Joseph is referred to as the "prince of his brothers," highlighting leadership. Believers in positions of influence should lead with integrity and humility, serving others as Joseph did.

Heritage and Legacy
The blessings on Joseph reflect the importance of heritage and legacy. Christians are encouraged to pass on their faith and values to future generations, ensuring a lasting spiritual legacy.

What Does Deuteronomy 33:16 Mean
The choice gifts of the land and everything in it

• Moses continues the blessing he began in Deuteronomy 33:13, picturing Joseph’s descendants (Ephraim and Manasseh) settled in exceptionally fertile territory (Joshua 16–17).

• “Choice gifts” echoes Jacob’s earlier words over Joseph: “blessings of heaven above… blessings of the deep” (Genesis 49:25–26). Grain, vineyards, olive groves, pastureland, and mineral wealth are all in view (Deuteronomy 8:7–9; 33:14–15).

• The verse reminds God’s people that material abundance is a gracious gift, not an entitlement (Deuteronomy 8:10–18; James 1:17).

• For believers today, it encourages gratitude and stewardship: whatever the land produces ultimately belongs to the Lord (Psalm 24:1; 1 Timothy 6:17–19).

And with the favor of Him who dwelt in the burning bush

• “Him who dwelt in the burning bush” points back to the LORD’s self-revelation to Moses in Exodus 3:2–6. The same holy God who called Israel out of bondage now pours out “favor” (grace, delight, protection) on Joseph’s line.

• That fiery bush burned yet was not consumed—a picture of God’s sustaining, purifying presence (Deuteronomy 1:31; Isaiah 43:2).

• The blessing therefore is more than fertile soil; it is the ongoing nearness of the covenant-keeping God who said, “I AM WHO I AM” (Exodus 3:14).

• New-covenant believers experience this favor most fully in Christ, the One who “dwelt among us” (John 1:14) and in whom the consuming fire of God’s holiness and the steadfast love of God’s grace meet (Hebrews 12:28–29).

May these rest on the head of Joseph

• “Rest” indicates permanence. The bounty of the land and the favor of the LORD are to settle on Joseph like a gentle but enduring weight (Psalm 133:2).

• The “head” represents the whole person; the blessing is comprehensive—spiritual, material, generational (Genesis 48:15–20).

• This language recalls Aaron’s priestly anointing (Leviticus 8:12) and foreshadows the Spirit’s anointing on all God’s children (1 John 2:20).

• It reassures believers that God’s gifts are not fleeting; His calling and His blessings are irrevocable (Romans 11:29; Philippians 1:6).

And crown the brow of the prince of his brothers

• Joseph was called “prince” long before Moses’ blessing (Genesis 45:8; 49:26). Though Judah would supply Israel’s kings, Joseph ruled in Egypt and, through his sons, led numerically and militarily in Israel (Numbers 1:32–35; Deuteronomy 33:17).

• A “crown” speaks of honor and authority (Proverbs 4:9). The image is that of blessings forming a diadem on Joseph’s head—public evidence of God’s favor (Psalm 103:4).

• In kingdom terms, every believer is made “a royal priesthood” (1 Peter 2:9); our true crown is the unfading glory given by Christ (2 Timothy 4:8; Revelation 2:10).

• Thus, as Joseph’s family enjoyed leadership and abundance, God’s people today are called to faithful service under the Lordship of Christ, the greater Prince (Acts 3:15).

summary

Deuteronomy 33:16 piles up images of rich soil, divine favor, lasting rest, and royal honor to assure Joseph’s descendants—and by extension all God’s people—of the LORD’s generous, steadfast blessing. The God who revealed Himself in the burning bush remains present, providing both material and spiritual abundance, establishing His gifts with permanence, and publicly crowning those who trust Him with dignity and purpose.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) The good will of him that dwelt in the bush--is a blessing peculiar to Moses. It contains an exquisite piece of interpretation. From the fact that Jehovah revealed Himself to Moses in a flame of fire in a bush, the man of God drew the thought that He presented Himself as dwelling in it; and thus he has furnished God's Church with this comfort for all ages, that His human temple, although it burn with fire, can never be consumed.

The last part of Deuteronomy 33:16 is taken direct from Genesis 49:26.

Separated from his brethren.--Heb., nazir. Is it altogether unreasonable to suppose that this particular feature in Joseph's history, when he was "sold into Egypt," and "separated from his brethren," may be part of the meaning of "Nazarene" when applied to our Lord in Matthew 2:23?
