These were the exploits of Benaiah son of Jehoiada
Benaiah, the son of Jehoiada, is introduced as a valiant warrior in the service of King David. His father, Jehoiada, was a priest, indicating Benaiah's noble lineage and possibly his upbringing in a religious environment. Benaiah's exploits are detailed earlier in the chapter, highlighting his bravery and skill in battle. He is noted for killing two Moabite warriors, a lion in a pit on a snowy day, and an Egyptian giant. These feats demonstrate his courage and strength, qualities highly valued in the ancient Near Eastern warrior culture. Benaiah's actions reflect the divine favor and empowerment often seen in the lives of those who serve God's purposes, similar to the exploits of other biblical figures like Samson and David himself.

who won a name along with the three mighty men
Benaiah's achievements earned him a reputation comparable to the "three mighty men," a group of elite warriors in David's army. These men were renowned for their extraordinary acts of valor and loyalty to David. The "three" refers to a select group within the larger assembly of David's mighty men, known for their unparalleled bravery and skill. Benaiah's inclusion in this context underscores his exceptional status and the high regard in which he was held. His recognition among these warriors highlights the biblical theme of God exalting those who are faithful and courageous. This phrase also connects to the broader narrative of David's reign, where loyalty and valor are key themes, and it foreshadows the ultimate victory and kingship of Jesus Christ, who is often typified by David and his mighty men.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Benaiah son of Jehoiada
A valiant warrior from Kabzeel, known for his bravery and significant military exploits. He was a leader among David's mighty men and later became the commander of Solomon's army.

2. The Three Mighty Men
A group of elite warriors who served King David. They were renowned for their extraordinary feats and loyalty to the king.

3. David's Mighty Men
A larger group of warriors who were loyal to King David, known for their courage and skill in battle. They played a crucial role in establishing and defending David's kingdom.
Teaching Points
Valor and Reputation
Benaiah's actions demonstrate the importance of courage and integrity. His reputation was built on his deeds, reminding us that our actions speak louder than words.

Loyalty and Service
Benaiah's loyalty to David and later to Solomon exemplifies the biblical principle of faithful service to those in authority, reflecting our ultimate service to God.

God's Recognition
While Benaiah was recognized by men, his true reward came from God. This teaches us that God sees and rewards our faithfulness, even when it goes unnoticed by others.

Legacy of Faithfulness
Benaiah's legacy was not just his military prowess but his faithfulness and integrity. We are encouraged to leave a legacy that honors God and inspires others.

What Does 2 Samuel 23:22 Mean
These were the exploits

“​These were the exploits…” (2 Samuel 23:22) points back to the vivid accounts in verses 20–21:

• Striking down two champions of Moab (compare 1 Chronicles 11:22).

• Descending into a snowy pit to kill a lion (Psalm 91:13 reminds us that God enables His servants to “tread on the lion and cobra”).

• Wresting a spear from a towering Egyptian and killing him with it (2 Samuel 23:21).

Taken together, the word “exploits” highlights repeated, courageous acts accomplished through God’s strength. Like Gideon’s victories (Judges 6:12) or Jonathan’s bold assault (1 Samuel 14:6–14), these feats demonstrate that “the battle belongs to the LORD” (1 Samuel 17:47).

of Benaiah son of Jehoiada

Benaiah’s name (“Yahweh has built”) and his father Jehoiada’s priestly lineage (1 Chronicles 27:5) tell us he grew up steeped in faith. Scripture later shows him:

• Serving as commander of David’s personal bodyguard, the Cherethites and Pelethites (2 Samuel 8:18; 20:23).

• Remaining loyal during Adonijah’s rebellion and executing Solomon’s justice (1 Kings 2:25, 34).

• Replacing Joab as commander of Israel’s army (1 Kings 2:35).

His life models how spiritual heritage and personal courage blend to forge godly leadership.

who won a name

“Won a name” means Benaiah gained lasting honor. Scripture consistently prizes a reputation formed by righteous deeds:

• “A good name is more desirable than great riches” (Proverbs 22:1).

• The early church recognized men “well spoken of” for leadership (Acts 6:3).

Benaiah’s name endures in God’s Word because he trusted the LORD and acted with unwavering integrity (2 Samuel 23:20–21). God still delights to magnify those who exalt Him (1 Peter 5:6).

along with the three mighty men

While Benaiah did not belong to the elite first tier—Josheb-basshebeth, Eleazar, and Shammah (2 Samuel 23:8–17)—he ranked immediately below them, “honored more than the Thirty” (23:23). This placement teaches:

• God acknowledges varying roles yet values every faithful servant (1 Corinthians 12:18–26).

• Humility opens doors to greater responsibility; Benaiah’s faithful service under David prepared him to lead under Solomon.

Just as the Three inspired the army, Benaiah’s exploits inspired the Thirty and the nation.

summary

2 Samuel 23:22 celebrates Benaiah’s God-empowered bravery, his priestly roots, the sterling reputation he earned, and his near-peer status with David’s greatest warriors. The verse reminds believers that courageous, faith-fueled obedience leaves a lasting legacy, honors the LORD, and positions us for greater service in His kingdom.
