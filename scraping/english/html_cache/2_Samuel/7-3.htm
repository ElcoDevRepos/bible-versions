And Nathan replied to the king
Nathan was a prophet during the reign of King David, serving as a spiritual advisor. His role was significant in guiding the king according to God's will. Prophets in ancient Israel were seen as God's mouthpieces, delivering divine messages. Nathan's relationship with David was one of mutual respect and trust, which is evident in their interactions throughout the biblical narrative.

Go and do all that is in your heart
This phrase indicates Nathan's initial approval of David's desire to build a temple for the Lord. It reflects the cultural understanding that kings often undertook grand projects to honor their deities. David's heart was aligned with a desire to glorify God, showing his devotion and commitment. However, this initial approval was based on Nathan's human understanding, not yet a direct revelation from God.

for the LORD is with you
This assurance highlights the special relationship between God and David. Throughout David's life, God's presence and favor were evident, from his anointing by Samuel to his victories in battle. The phrase underscores the biblical theme of God's covenant faithfulness. It also foreshadows the Davidic Covenant, where God promises to establish David's throne forever, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, the Son of David.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Nathan
A prophet during the reign of King David, Nathan served as a spiritual advisor and was instrumental in conveying God's messages to David. His role was crucial in guiding the king according to God's will.

2. King David
The second king of Israel, known for his deep faith and leadership. At this point in the account, David desires to build a house for the Ark of the Covenant, reflecting his devotion to God.

3. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who is central to the account. His presence and guidance are pivotal in the unfolding events of David's reign.

4. The Ark of the Covenant
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, it is the focal point of David's desire to build a temple, symbolizing God's presence among His people.

5. Jerusalem
The city where David established his kingdom and where he intended to build the temple, making it the spiritual and political center of Israel.
Teaching Points
The Role of Spiritual Advisors
Nathan's initial response to David underscores the importance of having godly counsel. Spiritual advisors can provide encouragement and guidance, but they must also be open to correction and further revelation from God.

Desires Aligned with God's Will
While David's desire to build a temple was noble, it was essential for him to seek God's will. Our plans, even when well-intentioned, should be submitted to God's greater purpose.

God's Presence and Guidance
Nathan's assurance that "the LORD is with you" reminds us of the importance of God's presence in our endeavors. We should seek His guidance in all our plans and actions.

The Importance of Listening to God
Nathan's later correction (not included in this verse) teaches us the importance of listening to God and being willing to change course when He reveals His will.

What Does 2 Samuel 7:3 Mean
And Nathan replied to the king

Nathan steps forward as God’s recognized prophet—someone whose words have proven trustworthy (1 Samuel 3:19; 2 Samuel 12:1). His willingness to speak shows:

• Respect for David’s authority yet confidence that the king wants God’s counsel (Proverbs 11:14).

• A relationship in which prophet and king jointly seek God’s honor (Amos 3:7).

By immediately responding, Nathan affirms that God’s word is not distant or inaccessible; He provides guidance through His appointed servants (Hebrews 1:1).

Go and do all that is in your heart

David longs to build a permanent house for the LORD (2 Samuel 7:1-2). Nathan’s initial endorsement recognizes several truths:

• David’s motive is godly: “It was in your heart to build a house for My Name… you did well” (1 Kings 8:18; 1 Chronicles 17:1-2).

• Acting from a heart aligned with God is normally encouraged (Psalm 37:4; Philippians 2:13).

• God often works through desires He places within His people (Nehemiah 2:12).

Yet the broader chapter shows that even sincere plans must remain submitted to further revelation; God will later redirect David (2 Samuel 7:4-7). That tension teaches dependence: proceed, but stay open to God’s clarifying voice (James 4:13-15).

for the LORD is with you

Nathan justifies his counsel by pointing to God’s proven presence in David’s life:

• “David became greater and greater, for the LORD God of Hosts was with him” (2 Samuel 5:10).

• Earlier victories (1 Samuel 17:37; 18:14) confirm the ongoing reality of Emmanuel—“God with us” (Romans 8:31).

This promise anchors confidence, not in human ability, but in divine companionship (Psalm 23:4). The same assurance frames the coming covenant promises: God will establish David’s house forever (2 Samuel 7:11-16).

summary

Nathan’s immediate affirmation in 2 Samuel 7:3 highlights the harmony between a prophet who speaks, a king who longs to honor God, and a Lord who is actively present. Nathan encourages David’s godly desire while modeling readiness to yield to further revelation. The verse assures believers that when motives align with God’s heart and His presence is evident, they may move forward confidently—always listening for any additional word the Lord may provide.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Go, do all that is in thine heart.--Nathan naturally considered that it must be right for David to execute his pious purpose; but he spoke only according to his own sense of right, and not by Divine direction.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Go, do all that is in thine heart. Nathan rashly approves. The king's purpose seems so pious that he does not doubt its acceptance by God.
