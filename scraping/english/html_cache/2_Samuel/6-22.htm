Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and I will humiliate and humble myself even more than this.
David's response to Michal's criticism highlights his willingness to lower himself in the eyes of others for the sake of honoring God. This reflects a heart of true worship, prioritizing God's glory over personal dignity. In biblical context, humility is a valued trait, as seen in Proverbs 22:4, which links humility with the fear of the Lord. David's actions prefigure Christ's humility, as described in Philippians 2:7-8, where Jesus humbles Himself by taking on human form and submitting to death on a cross. David's willingness to be undignified for God contrasts with Saul's concern for public image, illustrating a key difference between the two kings.

Yet I will be honored by the maidservants of whom you have spoken.”
David's statement indicates that true honor comes from God and those who recognize His presence, rather than from societal status or approval. The maidservants, representing the humble and lowly, are often more attuned to God's work, as seen in the Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55), where Mary, a humble servant, is honored by God. This reflects the biblical theme that God exalts the humble (James 4:10). David's confidence in being honored by the maidservants underscores the idea that God's perspective on honor differs from human perspectives, aligning with Jesus' teaching in Matthew 23:12 that those who humble themselves will be exalted.

What Does 2 Samuel 6:22 Mean
Setting the scene

2 Samuel 6 describes David bringing the Ark of the Covenant to Jerusalem. His exuberant dancing before the LORD draws criticism from his wife, Michal (v. 16, 20). David’s reply in v. 21–22 reveals his heart.

• David’s focus is worship, not royal dignity (v. 21; cf. Psalm 24:7–10; 1 Chronicles 15:27–29).

• Michal values regal decorum; David values God’s presence (Luke 10:41–42).

• This contrast prepares us to hear David’s statement in v. 22.

“and I will humiliate …”

“I will humiliate … even more than this.”

• David willingly embraces personal humiliation if it magnifies the LORD (Galatians 6:14).

• True worship often looks foolish to onlookers (1 Corinthians 1:27; Acts 2:13).

• His attitude mirrors Moses’ choice to suffer disgrace for God’s sake (Hebrews 11:24–26).

“and humble myself even more than this”

“and humble myself even more than this.”

• David’s humility is deliberate; he chooses lowliness (James 4:10; Philippians 2:5–8).

• He sees kingship as stewardship, not entitlement (Psalm 131:1–2).

• The phrase “even more” signals an ongoing readiness to decrease so God increases (John 3:30).

“Yet I will be honored by the maidservants of whom you have spoken.”

“Yet I will be honored by the maidservants …”

• Honor flows from God, not social status (1 Samuel 2:30; Proverbs 18:12).

• Those close to daily life (“maidservants”) recognize genuine devotion, even if elites do not (Mark 12:37b; Luke 7:44–50).

• God often exalts the humble in the eyes of the humble (Luke 1:52; 14:11).

summary

David declares that worship outweighs reputation. He gladly chooses deeper humility, confident that true honor comes from the LORD and is perceived by hearts attuned to Him. His words call us to value God’s presence above public image, knowing that God exalts those who lower themselves for His glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) Base in mine own sight.--The LXX., not understanding this expression, has changed it to "in thine eyes." But the meaning is, that while Michal had charged him with making himself base in the eyes of the maidservants (who were no fit judges of such matters), he was ready to abase himself in his own eyes, to do anything, however humbling it might seem even to himself, which should be for the honour and glory of God.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - And of the maidservants which thou hast spoken of, of them shall I be had in honour. These words have been variously interpreted, but their simplest meaning is also the best; that even the most uneducated women, though surprised at first at David's want of stateliness, would, on reflection, be led to a right understanding of the greatness of God; and would then feel that even a king was right in owning himself to be nothing in God's presence.
