and sent word through Nathan the prophet
Nathan the prophet plays a significant role in the life of King David, serving as a divine messenger. His involvement underscores the importance of prophetic authority in Israel, where prophets often acted as intermediaries between God and the king. Nathan previously confronted David about his sin with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 12:1-14), demonstrating his role in holding the king accountable. This phrase highlights the continued communication between God and David through Nathan, emphasizing the prophet's role in delivering God's messages and instructions.

to name him Jedidiah
The name "Jedidiah" means "beloved of the LORD." This naming is significant as it reflects God's favor and love towards Solomon, despite the circumstances of his birth. In the ancient Near Eastern culture, names held profound significance, often reflecting one's destiny or divine purpose. The naming by God through Nathan indicates a divine endorsement and a special relationship between Solomon and God. This act can be seen as a foreshadowing of Solomon's future role as king and builder of the temple, a task that David was not permitted to complete (1 Chronicles 22:9-10).

because the LORD loved him
This phrase emphasizes God's grace and favor towards Solomon, despite the sin associated with his parents' union. It reflects the theme of divine love and election, where God chooses individuals for specific purposes regardless of their background. This love is a precursor to Solomon's future wisdom and prosperity as king (1 Kings 3:5-14). It also connects to the broader biblical narrative of God's covenantal love for His people, which is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who is also called "beloved" (Matthew 3:17). Solomon's role as a wise king and temple builder serves as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate King and builder of the spiritual temple, the Church.

What Does 2 Samuel 12:25 Mean
Context of Restoration

David’s grievous sin with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 11) ended with confession and forgiveness, yet it carried harsh consequences, including the death of the couple’s first child (2 Samuel 12:13–18). Into that grief “David comforted his wife Bathsheba, and she bore a son and named him Solomon” (2 Samuel 12:24). By mentioning this sequence immediately before verse 25, Scripture frames Solomon’s birth as a tangible sign of divine mercy that follows genuine repentance (Psalm 51; 1 John 1:9).

The Lord’s Initiative

Verse 25 opens with the striking statement, “and the LORD loved him.” Love originates in God, not in human merit (Deuteronomy 7:7–8; 1 John 4:19). Solomon has done nothing to earn favor; grace simply flows from God’s heart into a family that has already tasted both discipline and forgiveness (2 Samuel 7:15).

Nathan’s Prophetic Role

God “sent word through Nathan the prophet.” The same prophet who had confronted David’s sin (2 Samuel 12:1–14) now becomes the messenger of blessing.

• God’s rebuke and God’s comfort come through the same faithful servant (cf. 2 Samuel 12:15; 1 Kings 1:11–14).

• Prophets do more than expose wrongdoing; they also unveil hope (Jeremiah 1:7–10). God’s Word pierces, then heals (Hosea 6:1).

The Name Jedidiah

Nathan is instructed “to name him Jedidiah because the LORD loved him.” Throughout Scripture, God-directed name changes reveal divine purpose (Genesis 17:5; 32:28; Luke 1:13; Matthew 1:21).

• Solomon receives two names: his parents’ choice, Solomon (“peaceful”), and God’s additional affirmation, Jedidiah (“loved by the LORD,” indicated by the text itself).

• The double naming hints that the forthcoming king will mediate peace (1 Chronicles 22:9) while standing under a banner of steadfast love (2 Samuel 7:16).

The Expression of Divine Love

By highlighting God’s love twice—once in verse 24 and again in verse 25—Scripture underscores a covenant reality: mercy triumphs over judgment (Psalm 103:10–13; Romans 5:20).

• God publicly identifies Solomon with love before any achievement (1 Kings 3:3–14).

• This love secures the Davidic line, anticipating the greater Son, Jesus, in whom the Father declares, “This is My beloved Son” (Matthew 3:17).

Implications for Solomon’s Future

The special naming sets the trajectory for Solomon’s mission. Cross-references unfold that path:

• God previously promised David “a son… who will build a house for My name” (2 Samuel 7:12–13).

• Nathan’s later word affirms Solomon as that chosen builder (1 Chronicles 22:9–10).

• The Temple, dedicated by Solomon, becomes a national witness to divine love (1 Kings 8:23).

Applications for Today

• God’s grace restores fallen people and weaves purpose from failure (Romans 8:28).

• When God speaks through faithful messengers, He offers both correction and comfort; we must heed both (Hebrews 12:5–11).

• Identity rooted in divine love precedes service; believers act out of acceptance, not for it (Ephesians 1:4–6).

• Naming children—or any stewardship—should be approached prayerfully, acknowledging God’s ultimate claim on their lives (Psalm 127:3).

summary

2 Samuel 12:25 showcases pure grace: the same Lord who chastened David now publicly delights in his newborn son. By sending Nathan to bestow the name Jedidiah, God stamps Solomon’s life with covenant love, guarantees the continuation of the Davidic promises, and reminds every reader that divine forgiveness can turn even the darkest chapters into stories of restoration and hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(25) Jedidiah.--It does not appear that this name (beloved of the Lord) was intended to do more than express the Divine acceptance of Solomon; and it never came into use as a personal title.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 25. - He sent. Some commentators make David the subject of the sentence, and translate, "And he, David, sent in the hand of Nathan, and called," etc. They suppose that this means that Nathan was entrusted with Solomon's education; but "in the hand" is the ordinary Hebrew preposition, meaning "by," and the sense plainly is that God sent a message by Nathan. David had already called the child Solomon, and now Jehovah, some years afterwards, gives him an indication of his special favour by naming him Yedidyah. The word is formed from the same root as David, that is, "lovely," with the addition of the Divine name. As we have already pointed out, this was no slight matter, but the virtual selection of Solomon to be David's successor, and probably, therefore, was delayed until he had given indica of his great intellectual gifts. His elder brothers would not be passed over without valid reasons.
