Zelek the Ammonite
Zelek is identified as an Ammonite, indicating he was from the region of Ammon, east of the Jordan River. The Ammonites were often in conflict with Israel, yet Zelek's inclusion among David's mighty warriors suggests a remarkable integration and acceptance into Israelite society. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God's kingdom being open to all nations. The presence of an Ammonite in David's elite group may also illustrate the fulfillment of prophecies regarding the inclusion of Gentiles, as seen in Isaiah 56:6-8.

Naharai the Beerothite
Naharai is described as a Beerothite, indicating his origin from Beeroth, one of the Gibeonite cities. The Gibeonites had a unique covenant relationship with Israel, as seen in Joshua 9, where they became servants to the Israelites. Naharai's role among David's warriors highlights the ongoing relationship and integration of the Gibeonites within Israel. This connection underscores the theme of covenant faithfulness and the importance of honoring agreements, as seen throughout the Old Testament.

the armor-bearer of Joab son of Zeruiah
Naharai served as the armor-bearer to Joab, the commander of David's army and son of Zeruiah, David's sister. This position was one of great trust and responsibility, indicating Naharai's skill and loyalty. Joab was a complex figure, known for his military prowess and sometimes ruthless actions. The role of an armor-bearer was crucial in battle, providing support and protection to the commander. This relationship can be seen as a type of Christ's role as our protector and the one who bears our burdens, as described in Matthew 11:28-30.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Zelek the Ammonite
Zelek is one of David's mighty warriors, listed among the valiant men who served King David. His inclusion highlights the diverse backgrounds of those who were loyal to David.

2. Nahari the Beerothite
Another of David's mighty warriors, Nahari is noted as the armor-bearer of Joab, the commander of David's army. Beeroth was a city of the Gibeonites, indicating Nahari's possible non-Israelite origin.

3. David's Mighty Warriors
This group of elite soldiers was renowned for their bravery and loyalty to King David. They played a crucial role in establishing and maintaining his kingdom.
Teaching Points
Diversity in God's Kingdom
The inclusion of Zelek the Ammonite and Nahari the Beerothite among David's mighty warriors demonstrates that God's kingdom is open to people from all backgrounds. This diversity is a strength and reflects God's inclusive nature.

Loyalty and Service
The mighty warriors exemplify loyalty and dedication to their leader. As Christians, we are called to serve Christ with the same commitment and faithfulness.

God's Sovereignty in Leadership
David's ability to unite a diverse group of warriors under his leadership is a testament to God's sovereign hand in establishing leaders according to His purposes.

The Role of Supportive Roles
Nahari's role as an armor-bearer highlights the importance of supportive roles in achieving greater goals. Every role in the body of Christ is vital and contributes to the mission.

What Does 2 Samuel 23:37 Mean
Zelek the Ammonite

• 2 Samuel 23:37 lists, “Zelek the Ammonite”, placing a man from Israel’s on-again, off-again enemies (cf. 2 Samuel 10:6–14) among David’s elite warriors.

• His inclusion underscores the kingdom’s magnetic pull; foreigners who embraced Israel’s God were welcomed into covenant life, much like Ruth the Moabitess (Ruth 1:16; Ruth 4:13–17).

• God’s faithfulness shines in this detail: He promised Abraham that “all nations” would be blessed through him (Genesis 12:3), and even a lone Ammonite in the ranks points forward to that global promise.

• Practically, Zelek’s name in Scripture honors loyalty over lineage. The text assures believers that wholehearted allegiance to the Lord outweighs background or birthplace (cf. Isaiah 56:3–7).

Naharai the Beerothite

• “Naharai the Beerothite” (2 Samuel 23:37) hails from Beeroth, one of the Gibeonite towns spared in Joshua 9:17. Those Gibeonites became permanent servants “for the altar of the LORD” (Joshua 9:27), and Naharai’s rise to the mighty-men roster shows how earlier covenant mercy kept bearing fruit generations later.

• Beeroth’s story also reminds us that God records histories we might overlook. Small places and obscure beginnings do not hide anyone from His notice (cf. Micah 5:2; John 1:46).

• 1 Chronicles 11:39 repeats Naharai’s name, confirming the accuracy of Samuel’s record and demonstrating how Scripture interprets Scripture.

the armor-bearer of Joab son of Zeruiah

• The verse finishes, “the armor-bearer of Joab son of Zeruiah.” Sharing Joab’s constant proximity meant absolute trust, as an armor-bearer carried weapons, guarded the commander’s life, and fought at his side (cf. 1 Samuel 14:6-13, Jonathan and his armor-bearer).

• Joab was Israel’s top general (2 Samuel 18:2); serving him placed Naharai at the nerve center of David’s military. That role highlights:

– Courage under fire—armor-bearers met danger first.

– Faithfulness in the shadows—Naharai’s deeds furthered Joab’s victories, yet Scripture singles him out so that unseen service receives eternal recognition (Matthew 6:4).

• David himself once filled a similar post for Saul (1 Samuel 16:21), so God’s pattern emerges: humble, behind-the-scenes faithfulness often precedes exalted usefulness (cf. Luke 16:10).

summary

2 Samuel 23:37 isn’t filler; it reminds us that God cherishes devoted servants from every background, whether foreign-born like Zelek or hometown regulars like Naharai. Their faithful, often unseen roles in David’s kingdom mirror our call to serve Christ’s kingdom with the same loyalty, courage, and humility—confident that the Lord records every act of obedience for His glory and our eternal good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) The Ammonite.--A foreigner, like "Igal of Zobah" (a Syrian), and "Ittai the Gittite," and "Uriah the Hittite," who rose to distinction in David's service, and all of whom were probably proselytes.

Armourbearer.--It appears from 2Samuel 18:15 that Joab had ten armourbearers. This one was probably their chief.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 37. - Zelek the Ammonite. The presence of an Ammonite among the thirty reminds us of the fidelity of Shobi, the son of Nahash the Ammonite king, to David (see 2 Samuel 17:27). Armourbearer. The written text has the plural, "armourbearers," for which the K'ri has substituted the singular. The plural is probably right, and if so, both Joab's chief armourbearers, or squires, were foreigners, Zelek being an Ammonite, and Nahari a Gibeonite (see note on 2 Samuel 4:2). In actual warfare we find Joab attended by ten esquires (2 Samuel 18:15).
