Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He was most honored among the Thirty
This phrase refers to Benaiah, son of Jehoiada, who was a distinguished warrior in King David's army. The "Thirty" were a group of elite warriors who served as David's personal bodyguards and were renowned for their bravery and skill in battle. Benaiah's distinction as "most honored" suggests he was held in high esteem for his valor and leadership. This honor reflects the biblical principle that God often elevates those who demonstrate faithfulness and courage, as seen in other figures like Joseph and Daniel.

but he did not become one of the Three
The "Three" refers to an even more elite group of warriors who were considered the mightiest among David's men. These three warriors performed extraordinary feats of bravery and were given the highest honor. Benaiah, despite his accomplishments, did not attain this level, which highlights the unique and exceptional nature of the Three. This distinction underscores the biblical theme of different roles and honors within God's kingdom, as seen in the New Testament with the apostles and early church leaders.

And David appointed him over his guard
David's appointment of Benaiah over his guard indicates a position of significant trust and responsibility. This role would have involved overseeing the protection of the king and his household, a testament to Benaiah's loyalty and capability. The appointment reflects the biblical principle of stewardship, where individuals are entrusted with responsibilities based on their proven character and abilities. This mirrors the way God appoints leaders within the church, as seen in the pastoral epistles, where qualifications for leadership are based on character and faithfulness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The second king of Israel, known for his leadership, military prowess, and heart for God. He appointed the individual mentioned in this verse over his guard.

2. The Thirty
A group of David's mighty warriors, known for their bravery and skill in battle. They were highly esteemed but distinct from "the Three," who were even more renowned.

3. The Three
A select group of David's warriors who were the most distinguished among his mighty men. They held a special place of honor and were known for extraordinary feats.

4. The Guard
Refers to the elite group responsible for the protection of King David. Being appointed over the guard was a position of significant trust and responsibility.

5. Benaiah
Although not explicitly named in this verse, the context of the surrounding passages identifies him as the one being honored. He was a valiant warrior known for his loyalty and courage.
Teaching Points
Honor and Distinction
Benaiah was honored more than the Thirty but not part of the Three. This teaches us that God recognizes and honors different roles and contributions within His kingdom. We should strive to fulfill our unique calling with excellence.

Faithfulness in Service
Being appointed over David's guard signifies trust and reliability. In our lives, we should aim to be faithful in the responsibilities entrusted to us, knowing that God values our dedication.

Leadership and Responsibility
Leadership is not just about position but about serving and protecting others. As Christians, we are called to lead by example, caring for those under our influence.

God's Empowerment
Just as David's warriors were empowered for their tasks, we too can rely on God's strength to accomplish what He has set before us. We should seek His guidance and empowerment in all we do.

What Does 2 Samuel 23:23 Mean
He was most honored among the Thirty

“ He was most honored among the Thirty ” (2 Samuel 23:23). In David’s roll call of warriors, the “Thirty” were a renowned corps of seasoned fighters (2 Samuel 23:24–39). Benaiah’s exploits—striking down two of Moab’s best men, killing a lion in a pit on a snowy day, and defeating a giant Egyptian with the enemy’s own spear (2 Samuel 23:20-21)—earned him top esteem within this group.

• Honor points to reputation, not self-promotion (Proverbs 22:1).

• Recognition comes after faithful service, not before (1 Samuel 18:5; Matthew 25:21).

• God keeps record of faithful deeds even when people overlook them (Hebrews 6:10).

Like the centurion of Luke 7:9, Benaiah shows that earthly rank can be paired with true humility and reliance on the Lord.

but he did not become one of the Three

“ …but he did not become one of the Three ”. David’s “Three” (Josheb-Basshebeth, Eleazar, and Shammah—2 Samuel 23:8-12) formed an even more elite circle, distinguished by breathtaking single-handed victories. Scripture does not say why Benaiah, though highly honored, was not counted with them. Several truths surface:

• God assigns differing roles within one body (1 Corinthians 12:18).

• Greatness is not a competition but a stewardship (John 21:21-22).

• Contentment with God’s placement guards the heart (Philippians 4:11).

Benaiah’s willingness to serve where he was placed mirrors John the Baptist’s attitude: “He must increase; I must decrease” (John 3:30).

And David appointed him over his guard

“ And David appointed him over his guard ”. As commander of the Cherethites and Pelethites—the king’s bodyguard (2 Samuel 8:18; 1 Chronicles 18:17)—Benaiah was entrusted with David’s personal safety.

• Proximity to the king required unwavering loyalty (2 Samuel 15:19-21).

• Protection of God’s anointed was a spiritual as well as military duty (1 Samuel 24:6).

• Faithfulness in smaller battles prepared Benaiah for greater responsibility, echoing Luke 16:10.

Later, Benaiah’s integrity positioned him to support Solomon, execute justice on traitors (1 Kings 2:25, 34, 46), and succeed Joab as commander-in-chief (1 Kings 2:35).

summary

Benaiah’s story in 2 Samuel 23:23 shows that God-honoring valor gains respect (“most honored among the Thirty”), humble contentment accepts God’s boundaries (“did not become one of the Three”), and proven faithfulness opens doors to greater trust (“David appointed him over his guard”). His life encourages believers to serve courageously, rest in God’s placement, and steward every assignment with integrity before the true King.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Set him over his guard.--The word translated guard means rather private audience. David either made him a member of, or set him over his council. If in 1Chronicles 27:34 "Jehoiada son of Benaiah" is an error for "Benaiah son of Jehoiada," his holding of this office is also mentioned there.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - David set him over his guard. We have already seen (upon 1 Samuel 22:14) that the words mean that David made him a member of his privy council. Literally the words are, and David appointed him to his audience. In 1 Chronicles 27:34 mention is made of "Jehoiada the son of Benaiah" as being next in the council to Ahithophel, and many commentators think that the names have been transposed, and that we ought to read, "Benaiah the son of Jehoiada."
