Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You have loved righteousness and hated wickedness;
This phrase highlights the moral and ethical purity of the subject, who is Jesus Christ. The love for righteousness and hatred for wickedness is a consistent theme throughout the Bible, reflecting God's own nature (Psalm 45:7). In the context of Hebrews, this underscores Jesus' perfect obedience and sinlessness, which qualifies Him as the ultimate High Priest and mediator of the New Covenant. The duality of loving righteousness and hating wickedness is a call to believers to emulate Christ's example, as seen in passages like Romans 12:9, which instructs believers to "abhor what is evil; cling to what is good."

therefore God, Your God, has anointed You
The anointing here signifies being set apart for a special purpose, often associated with kingship or priesthood in the Old Testament (1 Samuel 16:13). This anointing is not with oil, as was customary for kings and priests, but with the Holy Spirit, marking Jesus as the Messiah (Isaiah 61:1, Luke 4:18). The phrase "God, Your God" emphasizes the relationship within the Trinity, where Jesus, though divine, submits to the Father’s will, fulfilling His role in the divine plan of salvation. This anointing is a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy and typology, where figures like David were anointed as a foreshadowing of Christ.

above Your companions
This indicates the superiority of Christ over others, including angels and prophets, as the context of Hebrews 1 establishes. The "companions" could refer to other anointed figures in the Bible, such as kings and priests, or more broadly to humanity. Jesus' exaltation above His companions is due to His unique nature as both fully God and fully man, and His perfect fulfillment of God's will. Philippians 2:9-11 echoes this exaltation, where God highly exalts Jesus and gives Him the name above every name.

with the oil of joy.
The "oil of joy" symbolizes gladness and blessing, often associated with the presence and favor of God (Psalm 23:5, Isaiah 61:3). In the ancient Near East, oil was used in anointing ceremonies to signify consecration and celebration. For Jesus, this joy is linked to His resurrection and ascension, where He triumphs over sin and death, bringing salvation to humanity. This joy is also shared with believers, as they partake in the victory and blessings of Christ (John 15:11, Romans 15:13). The oil of joy signifies the eternal joy and satisfaction found in fulfilling God's purposes.

Teaching Points
Love for Righteousness
As followers of Christ, we are called to love righteousness and pursue it in our daily lives.

Hatred for Wickedness
A true commitment to God involves a rejection of sin and wickedness, aligning our values with those of Christ.

Anointing and Empowerment
Believers are anointed by the Holy Spirit to fulfill God's purposes, just as Jesus was empowered for His mission.

Joy in Obedience
True joy comes from living in obedience to God's will, as demonstrated by Jesus' life and ministry.

Christ's Supremacy
Recognize and honor the supremacy of Christ in all aspects of life, acknowledging His unique role and authority.

What Does Hebrews 1:9 Mean
You have loved righteousness

- This opening line spotlights the heart of Jesus. He doesn’t merely do righteous things; He loves righteousness itself (Psalm 11:7; John 4:34).

- His earthly life showed consistent delight in obeying the Father (Matthew 3:15; John 8:29).

- Because righteousness is what God Himself delights in, Christ’s affection for it reveals His full alignment with the Father’s character (Isaiah 11:5).

and hated wickedness

- Love for what is right always carries a corresponding hatred for what is evil (Romans 12:9). Jesus’ ministry demonstrated this hatred:

• Driving the money-changers from the temple (John 2:14-17)

• Condemning hypocrisy (Matthew 23:27-28)

• Remaining “holy, innocent, undefiled, separated from sinners” (Hebrews 7:26)

- He never made peace with sin, and that intolerance underscores His moral perfection.

therefore God, Your God

- “Therefore” shows cause and effect: Christ’s love of righteousness leads to divine exaltation.

- The phrase “God, Your God” highlights the mysterious beauty of the Godhead—Father addressing the Son (Psalm 2:7; John 20:17).

- It affirms both Christ’s deity and His willing submission during the incarnation (Philippians 2:6-8).

has anointed You

- Anointing marks someone for a special role. Jesus is the ultimate Anointed One—the Messiah.

- Isaiah 61:1 and Luke 4:18 show Him reading “The Spirit of the Lord is on Me, because He has anointed Me.”

- Acts 10:38 summarizes His ministry: “God anointed Jesus of Nazareth with the Holy Spirit and with power.”

above Your companions

- Companions can point to angels (context of Hebrews 1), or to redeemed humanity over which He now reigns. Either way, Christ stands supreme (Ephesians 1:20-22; Colossians 1:18).

- His elevation is the Father’s public declaration that Jesus is peerless: “God exalted Him to the highest place” (Philippians 2:9).

with the oil of joy

- Oil in Scripture speaks of consecration and refreshment (Psalm 23:5).

- Joy flows from perfect fellowship within the Trinity and from the victory of the cross (Psalm 16:11; John 15:11).

- Believers share in that joy through the Spirit (Romans 14:17; Isaiah 61:3), but Christ possesses it in overflowing fullness.

summary

Hebrews 1:9 assures us that Jesus’ unwavering love for righteousness and His total rejection of evil moved the Father to exalt Him uniquely and eternally. Anointed with immeasurable joy, He now reigns higher than all others, inviting us to share His righteousness, despise sin, and live in the same joy that fills His throne room.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) The King by divine election has been exalted by divine reward. (Comp. Hebrews 2:9, and Philippians 2:9-10.)

Therefore God.--It is possible, but not probable, that the words, both here and in the Psalm, should be rendered, Therefore, O God, Thy God hath anointed Thee.

Thy fellows.--In the first application, probably, these words point to other earthly kings. (Comp. Psalm 89:27.) Hence Ephesians 1:21 will be the best commentary upon them in their higher meaning.
