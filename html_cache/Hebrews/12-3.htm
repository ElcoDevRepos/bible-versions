Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Consider Him who endured such hostility from sinners
This phrase calls believers to reflect on Jesus Christ, who faced intense opposition and hostility from those who rejected Him. The hostility Jesus endured is documented throughout the Gospels, particularly in the events leading up to His crucifixion. This includes the Pharisees' and Sadducees' plots against Him, the betrayal by Judas, and the false accusations during His trial. The term "sinners" here refers to those who were opposed to God's purposes, highlighting the spiritual battle Jesus faced. This reflection serves as an encouragement to believers to remain steadfast in their faith, drawing strength from Christ's example.

so that you will not grow weary and lose heart
The purpose of considering Christ's endurance is to prevent believers from becoming discouraged in their own spiritual journey. The early Christian community, to whom the Book of Hebrews was addressed, faced persecution and trials, which could lead to spiritual fatigue and discouragement. The exhortation is to maintain perseverance, drawing a parallel to the endurance required in a race, as mentioned earlier in Hebrews 12:1-2. This encouragement is consistent with other New Testament teachings, such as Galatians 6:9, which urges believers not to grow weary in doing good. The phrase underscores the importance of focusing on Jesus as the ultimate source of strength and encouragement in the face of adversity.

What Does Hebrews 12:3 Mean
Consider Him

“Consider Him” focuses our attention on Jesus Christ Himself. Just a verse earlier we’re urged to keep our eyes on “Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of our faith” (Hebrews 12:2). The call is not to glance but to ponder, weigh, and meditate on who He is and what He has done. Hebrews 3:1 says, “Therefore, holy brothers, who share in the heavenly calling, set your minds on Jesus, the apostle and high priest of our confession.” When trials press in, deliberate contemplation of Christ steadies the heart far better than self-help slogans or sheer willpower.

Who endured such hostility from sinners

Christ’s earthly life culminated in a torrent of opposition—betrayal, false accusation, mockery, scourging, crucifixion. Luke 23:35-39 records the rulers sneering at Him while soldiers and a criminal hurled insults. Yet 1 Peter 2:23 testifies, “When He was reviled, He did not revile in return.” John 15:18 reminds us, “If the world hates you, understand that it hated Me first.” Reflecting on His endurance places our own struggles in perspective and provides the ultimate model of patient, righteous suffering.

So that you will not grow weary

Weariness hits when we carry burdens in our own strength. Galatians 6:9 encourages, “Let us not grow weary in doing good, for in due time we will reap a harvest if we do not give up.” Isaiah 40:31 promises renewed strength for those who wait on the Lord. Fixing on Jesus supplies fresh endurance; His past faithfulness fuels present perseverance.

And lose heart

Losing heart describes inward collapse—the point where discouragement silences faith. Paul faced crushing pressures yet wrote, “Therefore we do not lose heart. Though our outer self is wasting away, yet our inner self is being renewed day by day” (2 Corinthians 4:16). Psalm 27:13-14 echoes the same resolve: “I am still confident of this: I will see the goodness of the LORD in the land of the living.” Jesus’ triumph assures believers that despair never has the final word.

summary

Hebrews 12:3 urges believers to keep Jesus squarely in view. By thoughtfully remembering His patient endurance of intense hostility, we draw strength to keep running our race without succumbing to weariness or discouragement. His example transforms trials into opportunities for steadfast faith and sustained hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) The figure of the race is still continued, "For unless ye thus look unto Jesus ye will grow weary."

Consider.--Literally, compare; place your sufferings by the side of His.

Him that endured such contradiction.--Rather, Him that hath endured such gainsaying from sinners against themselves. The word "gainsaying," (Hebrews 6:16; Hebrews 7:7) is so frequently used in the LXX. for the rebelliousness of the people of Israel, that we need not here limit it to contradiction in words. The change of "Himself" into "themselves" (the reading of the oldest MSS.) is important, but it is not easy to say with what the last two words should be joined; for the meaning may be either "sinners against themselves" (comp. Numbers 16:38), or "gainsaying against themselves," In either case the force of the words will be that the sin or the opposition manifested against Him was really against themselves, since it was for their salvation that He came upon earth. To all His other sorrows were added the pain of their ingratitude and His grief over their aggravated guilt.

And faint.--Rather, fainting in your souls.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - For consider him that hath endured such contradiction of sinners against himself (or, of the sinners against him), lest ye be weary fainting in your souls. The word ἀντιλογία ("contradiction"), though strictly applicable to verbal gainsaying, and thus especially suggesting to our minds the blasphemies and false accusations against Christ, includes opposition of all kinds. It is used in the LXX. for "rebellion" (Hebrew, סְרַי), 2 Samuel 22:41; Proverbs 17:11, cf. Jude 1:11, τῇ ἀντιλογιᾴ τοῦ Κορέ. (Instead of εἰς ἑαυτόν (al. εἰς αὐτὸν) there is weighty manuscript authority for εἰς ἑαυτούς, equivalent to "against themselves.") "Lest ye be weary," etc., keeps in view the idea of getting tired in a race, the word ἐκλυεσθαι ("faint") being used primarily for corporeal, and figuratively for mental, lassitude (cf. Matthew 15:32, μήποτε ἐκλυθῶσι ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ).
