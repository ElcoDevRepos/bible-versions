I will cut the sorceries from your hand
This phrase indicates a divine action against practices considered abominable in the biblical context. Sorcery, or witchcraft, was strictly forbidden in the Law of Moses (Deuteronomy 18:10-12). The "hand" symbolizes power and control, suggesting that these practices were prevalent and influential among the people. The cutting off signifies a complete removal, reflecting God's desire for His people to rely solely on Him rather than on occult practices. This action is part of a broader theme in Micah of purifying Israel from idolatry and sin, aligning with the prophetic call to return to covenant faithfulness.

and you will have no fortune-tellers
Fortune-telling, like sorcery, was condemned in Israelite society as it involved seeking knowledge or guidance from sources other than God. This practice was common in surrounding pagan cultures, where divination was used to predict the future or gain insight into the will of the gods. The absence of fortune-tellers signifies a return to trusting in God's sovereignty and His revealed word. This aligns with the prophetic vision of a restored Israel, where reliance on God replaces dependence on human or demonic intermediaries. The eradication of such practices is a step towards the fulfillment of God's kingdom, where His people live in obedience and faithfulness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Micah
A prophet in the 8th century BC, Micah delivered messages of judgment and hope to the people of Judah and Israel. His prophecies often addressed social injustices and idolatry.

2. Judah and Israel
The divided kingdoms of God's chosen people during Micah's time. Both kingdoms were often guilty of idolatry and turning away from God.

3. Sorceries and Fortune-tellers
Practices condemned by God, involving attempts to predict the future or manipulate events through supernatural means, often associated with pagan religions.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over the Supernatural
God alone holds the power over the future and the supernatural realm. Believers are called to trust in His sovereignty rather than seeking guidance from forbidden practices.

Repentance and Purity
Just as God promised to cut off sorceries, believers are called to remove any practices or influences in their lives that are contrary to God's will.

The Danger of Idolatry
Sorcery and fortune-telling are forms of idolatry, placing trust in something other than God. Christians must be vigilant in identifying and removing idols from their lives.

The Call to Holiness
God desires His people to be set apart, living lives that reflect His holiness. This includes rejecting practices that are contrary to His commands.

What Does Micah 5:12 Mean
I will cut the sorceries from your hand

• The LORD announces a decisive intervention: “I will cut off sorceries from your hand” (Micah 5:12).

• “Cut off” pictures a clean, permanent removal—similar to Josiah’s purge of “mediums and spiritists” (2 Kings 23:24).

• By targeting what is “in your hand,” God exposes personal complicity and demands surrender of every occult practice. Believers in Ephesus responded in kind, burning their magic scrolls (Acts 19:19).

• Scripture consistently treats sorcery as rebellion: it is outlawed in De 18:10-12, listed among the “works of the flesh” in Galatians 5:20, and condemned in Revelation 22:15.

• The purpose is covenant fidelity. God alone deserves trust and worship (Exodus 20:3-4); anything else invites judgment like that pronounced on Babylon’s sorceries (Isaiah 47:9-13).

and you will have no fortune-tellers

• The promise shifts from removing practices to eliminating practitioners. The LORD will leave the land bereft of diviners—He “makes fools of diviners” (Isaiah 44:25).

• Without fortune-tellers, the people must seek God’s voice, not illicit substitutes. Isaiah warns, “Should the people not consult their God?” (Isaiah 8:19).

• This cleansing anticipates the Messianic age when “I will remove the prophets and the spirit of impurity from the land” (Zechariah 13:2), echoing Micah’s vision.

• New-covenant episodes confirm the same standard: Paul silences a fortune-telling slave girl (Acts 16:16-18), and Revelation excludes “sorcerers” from the New Jerusalem (Revelation 21:8).

• God’s goal is a community free from all counterfeit guidance, living by His revealed word alone (Psalm 119:105; 2 Timothy 3:16-17).

summary

Micah 5:12 promises that the LORD Himself will eradicate both occult practices and their practitioners among His people. By cutting off sorceries and removing fortune-tellers, He safeguards pure devotion, eliminating every rival source of spiritual power or guidance. The verse calls believers to renounce all forms of the occult, rely wholly on God’s word, and anticipate the complete righteousness of Christ’s coming kingdom.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - Witchcrafts. Magic and sorcery, which were much practised in Syria and Palestine, as in Chaldea, the literature of which country consists in great part of spells and charms. It is to the belief in the efficacy of such incantations that we owe the episode of Balak and Balaam (Numbers 22-24.), and the enactments in the Law; e.g. Deuteronomy 18:10, etc. (comp. Isaiah 2:6; Isaiah 47:12). Septuagint, τὰ φάρμακά σου, "thy poisons;" Vulgate, maleficia. Soothsayers; properly, cloud diviners, or storm makers; either persons who professed to divine by means of the shape and colour of clouds, or, as the old Scandinavian witches, charlatans who assumed the power of musing and directing storms. Cheyne compares the common name of sorcerers among savages, "rain makers."
