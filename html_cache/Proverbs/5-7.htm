So now, my sons,
This phrase indicates a direct address to the audience, emphasizing the importance of the message. In the context of Proverbs, "my sons" often refers to the younger generation or disciples who are being instructed in wisdom. The use of "sons" reflects the familial and covenantal relationship between the teacher and the learner, akin to the relationship between God and His people. This familial language is consistent with the broader biblical theme of God as a Father (e.g., Deuteronomy 32:6, Isaiah 63:16).

listen to me,
The call to "listen" underscores the importance of heeding wisdom and instruction. Listening in the biblical sense implies not just hearing but obeying and internalizing the teachings. This echoes the Shema in Deuteronomy 6:4-5, where Israel is called to hear and love the Lord with all their heart. The emphasis on listening is a recurring theme in Proverbs, highlighting the value of wisdom and the consequences of ignoring it.

and do not turn aside
This phrase warns against deviating from the path of wisdom. In biblical literature, the path is often a metaphor for one's way of life or moral conduct (e.g., Psalm 1:1, Proverbs 4:26-27). Turning aside suggests a deliberate choice to ignore or reject wise counsel, which can lead to destructive consequences. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative of choosing between the way of life and the way of death (Deuteronomy 30:19).

from the words of my mouth.
The "words of my mouth" refer to the teachings and commandments being imparted. In the context of Proverbs, these words are not merely human advice but are seen as divinely inspired wisdom. The emphasis on words reflects the power of speech in biblical thought, where words can create, bless, or curse (e.g., Genesis 1, James 3:5-10). This phrase also connects to the New Testament, where Jesus is described as the Word made flesh (John 1:14), embodying divine wisdom and instruction.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Solomon
Traditionally considered the author of Proverbs, Solomon was the son of King David and known for his wisdom. He addresses his audience as "my sons," indicating a paternal or mentoring role.

2. Sons
The term "sons" is used metaphorically to refer to the recipients of wisdom, suggesting a close, familial relationship between the teacher and the learners.

3. Wisdom Literature
Proverbs is part of the wisdom literature in the Bible, which includes practical teachings on how to live a life that is pleasing to God.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Listening
Listening is a key component of gaining wisdom. It requires humility and a willingness to learn from those who have walked the path before us.

Staying on the Path of Wisdom
The phrase "do not turn aside" suggests the need for perseverance and focus. Distractions and temptations can lead us away from the path of righteousness.

The Role of Mentorship
Solomon's address to "my sons" highlights the importance of mentorship and guidance in spiritual growth. Seek out and value godly mentors in your life.

Guarding Against Temptation
The broader context of Proverbs 5 warns against the allure of immorality. Staying true to wise teachings helps guard against such temptations.

The Value of Consistent Instruction
Regular engagement with Scripture and wise counsel is crucial for maintaining a life aligned with God's will.

What Does Proverbs 5:7 Mean
So now

The phrase signals a decisive moment. After painting the dangers of adultery in Proverbs 5:1–6, Solomon presses for immediate action.

• Urgency mirrors Hebrews 3:7, “Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts.”

• It echoes Joshua 24:15, where Joshua challenges Israel, “choose for yourselves this day whom you will serve.”

Takeaway: Wisdom is never for tomorrow; it always demands a present response.

my sons

A warm, familial address that reveals the heart of a loving father.

• Proverbs 1:8 opens the book with the same appeal—“My son, heed your father’s instruction.”

• Paul adopts similar language in 1 Timothy 1:2 when he calls Timothy “my true son in the faith.”

• In Christ, every believer receives this instructive love, as seen in 1 John 2:1, “My little children, I write these things to you so that you will not sin.”

Takeaway: God’s wisdom comes packaged in relationship, not mere rules.

listen to me

More than hearing—this is active, engaged obedience.

• John 10:27, “My sheep listen to My voice; I know them, and they follow Me.”

• James 1:22 warns, “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only.”

Practical steps:

– Create margin for Scripture before competing voices crowd in.

– Respond quickly when God convicts; delayed obedience dulls the ear.

and do not turn aside

A negative command that guards the positive. Staying straight requires refusing detours.

• Deuteronomy 5:32 exhorts, “Do not turn aside to the right or to the left.”

• Galatians 1:6 laments believers “so quickly deserting” the gospel.

Helps for staying the course:

– Keep trustworthy believers close (Proverbs 13:20).

– Set up visual reminders of God’s Word (Deuteronomy 6:9).

from the words of my mouth

The authority behind the appeal—God-breathed words delivered through Solomon.

• Deuteronomy 8:3 reminds, “man does not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the LORD.”

• Jesus affirms the permanence of divine speech in Matthew 24:35, “Heaven and earth will pass away, but My words will never pass away.”

Living it out:

– Memorize key verses that confront specific temptations.

– Teach God’s words to the next generation (Psalm 78:4).

summary

Proverbs 5:7 is a father’s urgent plea: seize this moment, recognize your beloved status, hear with intent, refuse detours, and anchor your life in God’s unchanging words. Obedience here preserves purity, honors God, and passes wisdom to those who follow.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(h). Eighth Discourse:--Against Adultery, and in Praise of Marriage (Proverbs 5:7-23).

(7) Hear me now therefore, O ye children.--In this verse Solomon apparently ceases to report the words of his father, and resumes his speech in his own person.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 7-14. - The ruinous consequences of indulgence in illicit pleasures. Verse 7. - The subject of which the teacher is heating demands the utmost attention of youth. Enough, it might be supposed, had been said to deter from intercourse with the "strange woman." She has been portrayed in her real colours, plunging recklessly into ruin herself, and carrying her victims with her; deceitful, full of intrigues, neither walking in nor knowing the way of life. But the warning is amplified and made more impressive. There is another side of the picture, the complete bodily and temporal ruin of her victim. The argumentum ad hominem is applied. There is an appeal to personal interest in the details which follow, which ought not to fail in holding youth back. The form of the address which is repeated is very similar to that in Proverbs 7:24. The plural form, "O ye children" (cf. Proverbs 4:1 and Proverbs 7:24), immediately passes into the singular for the reason mentioned before, that, though the address is made to all, yet each individually is to apply it to himself.
