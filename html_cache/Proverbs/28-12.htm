When the righteous triumph, there is great glory
This phrase highlights the celebration and joy that accompany the success of the righteous. Biblically, the triumph of the righteous is often associated with God's favor and blessing. In the Old Testament, figures like David and Solomon experienced periods of triumph that brought glory to Israel, reflecting God's covenant promises. The concept of "great glory" can be linked to the idea of God's glory being manifested through His people, as seen in passages like Isaiah 60:1-3, where the glory of the Lord rises upon His people, drawing nations to His light. The triumph of the righteous is not just a personal victory but a communal one, bringing peace and prosperity to the land, as seen in Proverbs 11:10.

but when the wicked rise, men hide themselves
This part of the verse contrasts the previous statement by illustrating the fear and oppression that occur when the wicked gain power. Historically, the rise of wicked rulers in Israel, such as Ahab and Manasseh, led to periods of idolatry, injustice, and suffering. The phrase "men hide themselves" suggests a time of danger and persecution, where people seek refuge from the tyranny of the wicked. This is reminiscent of the times of the judges, where Israel experienced cycles of oppression due to their disobedience (Judges 6:2). Theologically, this reflects the broader biblical theme of the struggle between good and evil, and the ultimate hope for deliverance through God's intervention, as seen in prophecies like Daniel 12:1, where deliverance comes for those who are wise and righteous.

What Does Proverbs 28:12 Mean
When the righteous triumph

• “When the righteous triumph…” (Proverbs 28:12) pictures godly people gaining positions of influence or winning a public victory.

• Scripture consistently links righteous leadership with blessing: “When the righteous thrive, the people rejoice” (Proverbs 29:2); “When the righteous prosper, the city rejoices” (Proverbs 11:10).

• Triumph for the righteous flows from God’s favor, not mere human skill (Psalm 75:6-7). It testifies that “righteousness exalts a nation” (Proverbs 14:34).

there is great glory

• The result of righteous victory is “great glory”—public celebration and honor.

– Joy spreads throughout the community (Nehemiah 12:43).

– God Himself receives glory as justice and truth shine (Isaiah 60:1-3).

• People feel secure under upright leadership, just as Israel rejoiced when David defeated Goliath (1 Samuel 17:52).

but when the wicked rise

• “But when the wicked rise…” warns that ungodly power is equally possible. Proverbs repeats the danger: “When the wicked rise, men hide themselves” (Proverbs 28:28).

• Wicked ascendency brings moral confusion: “The wicked freely strut when what is vile is honored” (Psalm 12:8).

• God allows such seasons as part of His sovereign plan, yet He never condones evil (Habakkuk 1:13).

men hide themselves

• Under wicked rulers, people withdraw—physically, emotionally, spiritually.

– Elijah hid from Ahab (1 Kings 19:3).

– Gideon threshed wheat in a winepress to escape Midianite oppression (Judges 6:11).

• Fear replaces celebration; oppression silences public life (Micah 3:1-4).

• Hiding also reflects a spiritual effect: light is suppressed, and darkness is preferred (John 3:19-20).

summary

Proverbs 28:12 teaches a clear contrast. Righteous leadership brings open, shared glory that lifts a whole community, while wicked rule drives people into fearful hiding. The verse encourages believers to pursue and support godliness in every sphere, confident that God honors the righteous and ultimately exposes and topples evil.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) When righteous men do rejoice--i.e., prosper, or triumph.

There is great glory.--Men rejoice, and array themselves in their gayest attire.

A man is hidden.--Literally, is sought for. They hide themselves for fear (comp. Proverbs 28:28), and must be sought for, in order to be found.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - When righteous men do rejoice, there is great glory (comp. Proverbs 29:2; Proverbs 11:10). "Rejoice," rather triumph, as conquerors, right prevailing and wickedness being overcome. Then there is great show of joy, and, as the expression implies, men put on their festal garments to do honorer to the occasion: See the description of Solomon's time (1 Kings 4:20, 25). If we take this verse in connection with ver. 2, we may see in it the triumph of order after a period of confusion and anarchy. Septuagint, "Through the help of righteous men great glory arises." But when the wicked rise, a man is hidden (comp. ver. 28, where, however, the verb is different). The Authorized Version m, one that when the wicked rise to power, people have to hide themselves in order to escape danger to life and property. The verb is more literally rendered, "are searched for," i.e. they have betaken themselves to hiding places, and have to be looked for; they fear oppression and injury, and venture no longer into the streets and open places. Vulgate,Regnantibus impiis ruinae hominum, "When evil men are m power, there is general ruin;" Septuagint, "In the places of the ungodly men are caught." Other interpretations of the proverb have been suggested, though none is so satisfactory as that given above. Thus some take the searching out to mean testing, in the sense that evil times try men's characters, and bring out their true nature (1 Corinthians 11:19). Others explain that, under the reign of the impious, men do not come forward to take part in public affairs, but retire sullenly into private life.
