The glory of young men is their strength
In biblical times, physical strength was highly valued, especially in young men who were often called upon for labor, warfare, and protection of their communities. This phrase acknowledges the vigor and vitality that characterize youth. The Bible frequently highlights the importance of strength in young men, as seen in the stories of Samson (Judges 13-16) and David (1 Samuel 17). Strength is not only physical but also represents potential and the ability to achieve great things. In a spiritual sense, young men are encouraged to use their strength for righteous purposes, as seen in 1 John 2:14, where young men are commended for overcoming the evil one.

and gray hair is the splendor of the old
Gray hair symbolizes wisdom, experience, and the honor that comes with age. In ancient cultures, including Israel, elders were respected for their life experience and knowledge. This respect is evident in Leviticus 19:32, which instructs people to rise in the presence of the aged and honor the elderly. The Bible often associates gray hair with a life well-lived and the accumulation of wisdom, as seen in Job 12:12, which states that wisdom is with the aged. This phrase emphasizes the value of the elderly in providing guidance and counsel, reflecting the biblical principle that wisdom is more valuable than physical strength.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Young Men
Represents the vigor, energy, and physical strength often associated with youth. In the context of ancient Israel, young men were often seen as warriors or laborers, contributing to society through their physical capabilities.

2. Old Men
Symbolizes wisdom, experience, and the honor that comes with age. In biblical times, elders were respected for their life experiences and the counsel they could provide.

3. Strength
Refers to the physical power and vitality that is typically at its peak during youth. It is a gift from God that can be used for His purposes.

4. Gray Hair
In the Hebrew context, gray hair is a metaphor for wisdom and honor. It signifies a life lived with experience and the accumulation of knowledge over time.

5. Glory and Splendor
These terms highlight the value and honor associated with both youth and old age. "Glory" (Hebrew: tiferet) and "splendor" (Hebrew: hadarah) suggest a divine quality or beauty bestowed by God.
Teaching Points
Value of Youthful Strength
Recognize and appreciate the physical and energetic capabilities of youth. Encourage young people to use their strength for God's glory and service.

Honor in Aging
Embrace the wisdom and experience that come with age. Encourage respect for elders and seek their guidance and counsel.

Intergenerational Relationships
Foster relationships between young and old, where each can learn from the other. The strength of youth and the wisdom of age can complement each other in the body of Christ.

God's Design for All Ages
Understand that God has a purpose for every stage of life. Whether young or old, each person has unique contributions to make to God's kingdom.

Spiritual Strength Over Physical
While physical strength is valuable, spiritual strength is eternal. Encourage all ages to grow in their relationship with God, which is the true source of strength and wisdom.

What Does Proverbs 20:29 Mean
The glory of young men

• Scripture celebrates the vigor of youth. David sprinting toward Goliath (1 Samuel 17:48) and the “strong ones” of Psalm 144:12 both show that raw energy can honor God.

• Solomon calls this “glory,” not to flatter, but to identify what is naturally commendable in younger years (Ecclesiastes 11:9).

• John writes, “I have written to you, young men, because you are strong, and the word of God abides in you” (1 John 2:14). Physical drive becomes spiritual glory when submitted to the Word.

Is their strength

• Physical power, endurance, quick recovery—these gifts are given for service. “Love the Lord your God with all… your strength” (Luke 10:27) directs that force toward worship.

• Strength without wisdom can turn reckless. Proverbs 24:5 reminds, “A wise man is strong,” tying muscle to mind.

• Encourage the young to channel energy into godly pursuits—missions, hard work, protecting the vulnerable (Isaiah 40:30-31).

And gray hair

• Scripture treats age with dignity: “You shall rise in the presence of the aged” (Leviticus 19:32).

• Gray hair is more than a pigment change; it signals seasons walked with God. “With the aged is wisdom” (Job 12:12).

• Proverbs 16:31 echoes today’s verse: “Gray hair is a crown of glory; it is attained along the path of righteousness”.

Is the splendor of the old

• Splendor points to honor earned through tested faith. Paul could say, “I have fought the good fight… I have kept the faith” (2 Timothy 4:7), showcasing a life well-spent.

• The church needs this brilliance: older men are urged to be “temperate, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith” (Titus 2:2) so they can mentor the next generation.

• Psalm 71:18 models the mission: “Even when I am old and gray, do not forsake me… until I proclaim Your power to the next generation”.

summary

God assigns unique beauty to each season. Youth shines in strength meant for wholehearted service; age shines in seasoned wisdom meant for wholehearted guidance. When these glories meet—energy guided by experience—the whole body of Christ flourishes.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(29) The beauty of old men is the grey head.--As suggesting the possession of experience and wisdom. It is the fault of the aged, therefore, if they do not receive the honour due to them, and this arises from their not having so spent their youth and middle age as to make their old age venerable.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - The glory of young men is their strength. That which makes the ornament (tiphereth) of youth is unimpaired strength and vigour, which can only be attained by due exercise combined with self-control. The moralist (Ecclesiastes 11:9) bids the young man rejoice in his youth, and let his heart cheer him in those happy days, but at the same time remember that he is responsible for the use which he makes of his powers and faculties, for for all these things God will bring him to judgment. The Greek gives a needful warning -

Μέμνησο νέος α}ν ὡς γέρων ἔσῃ ποτέ

"In youth remember thou wilt soon be old." Septuagint, "Wisdom is an ornament to young men." But koach is bodily, not mental, power. The beauty of old men is the grey head (Proverbs 16:31). That which gives an honorable look to old age is the hoary head, which suggests wisdom and experience (comp. Ecclus. 25:3-6). On the other hand, the Greek gnomist warns -
