A perverse man spreads dissension
The term "perverse" in this context refers to someone who is morally corrupt or deliberately deviating from what is considered right or good. In biblical terms, this aligns with the concept of sinfulness and rebellion against God's commandments. The act of spreading dissension is akin to sowing discord among people, which is condemned in Proverbs 6:16-19, where it is listed as one of the things the Lord hates. Historically, the ancient Near Eastern societies, including Israel, valued community harmony and unity, making the actions of a perverse man particularly destructive. This phrase warns against the influence of such individuals who disrupt peace and unity, which are essential for a godly community.

and a gossip divides close friends
Gossip, or the spreading of rumors and secrets, is portrayed as a destructive force that can sever even the strongest of relationships. The cultural context of ancient Israel placed a high value on loyalty and trust within friendships and family. The act of gossiping is seen as a betrayal of that trust, leading to division and strife. This is echoed in other scriptures such as James 3:5-6, which compares the tongue to a small fire that can set a great forest ablaze, illustrating the potential for words to cause significant harm. The division of close friends through gossip is a reminder of the power of words and the importance of using them wisely, as emphasized throughout the book of Proverbs.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Perverse Man
In the context of this proverb, a perverse man is someone who is morally corrupt or twisted in their ways. The Hebrew word used here, (tahpukot), implies a person who distorts or turns things upside down, often leading to discord.

2. Gossip
This refers to a person who spreads rumors or secrets, often causing harm to relationships. The Hebrew word (nirgan) suggests a whisperer or talebearer who sows seeds of division.

3. Close Friends
The proverb highlights the impact of gossip on intimate relationships, emphasizing the value and vulnerability of close friendships.
Teaching Points
Guard Your Tongue
Recognize the power of your words. Just as a perverse man spreads dissension, our words can either build up or tear down. Be intentional about speaking life and truth.

Value of True Friendship
Understand the preciousness of close friendships. Gossip can easily destroy these bonds, so protect and nurture your relationships by avoiding harmful speech.

Pursue Peace
As believers, we are called to be peacemakers. Actively seek to resolve conflicts and promote unity within your community.

Discernment in Listening
Be cautious about the information you receive. Not all that is shared is beneficial or true. Practice discernment and avoid participating in gossip.

Reflect Christ in Communication
Let your communication reflect the character of Christ. Speak with love, grace, and truth, aiming to edify those around you.

What Does Proverbs 16:28 Mean
A perverse man

“a crooked, morally warped person” (see Proverbs 2:12-15; 10:31-32)

• His heart has turned from God’s straight path, so his words and actions follow suit.

• Rather than building up, he delights in twisting truth and motives, much like the “schemer” in Proverbs 6:14 who “plots evil continually.”

• Such a person stands opposed to the God of order and peace (1 Corinthians 14:33) and therefore to the health of any community he enters.

spreads dissension

“spreads” pictures sowing seeds; conflict is the inevitable harvest (Proverbs 6:19).

• Methods include half-truths, subtle innuendo, and the cynical tone that makes suspicion sound sophisticated (James 3:14-16).

• Dissension doesn’t erupt overnight; it’s cultivated, like weeds in an untended field (Proverbs 26:20-21).

• God hates this practice because He loves unity among His people (Psalm 133:1; John 17:21).

and a gossip

“gossip” translates to the person who whispers behind backs (Proverbs 18:8).

• The gossip often disguises slander as concern, curiosity, or even a prayer request (Proverbs 26:22-25).

• Paul lists gossip with sins that destroy a church’s witness (Romans 1:29-30; 2 Corinthians 12:20).

• Unlike honest, loving confrontation (Ephesians 4:15), gossip hides in the dark; it cannot survive the light of truth.

divides close friends

The most vulnerable relationships are the deepest ones.

• Words once shared in confidence become weapons that fracture trust (Proverbs 17:9).

• The rupture affects more than the two parties; circles of family, church, and workplace feel the shock waves (Proverbs 11:9; 15:18).

• God’s design is covenant loyalty—friends who “stick closer than a brother” (Proverbs 18:24)—but gossip drives a wedge where God intends steadfast love.

summary

Proverbs 16:28 warns that twisted hearts produce twisted words, and those words wreck relationships God treasures. Pursue straight paths, speak truth in love, refuse every whisper that wounds, and you will cultivate the unity Christ died to secure (Ephesians 4:29-32).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) A froward man.--Who distorts the truth.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - A froward man soweth strife (Proverbs 6:14, 19). The verb means, literally, "sends forth," which may signify "scatters as seed" or "hurls as a missile weapon." The character intended is the perverse man, who distorts the truth, gives a wrong impression, attributes evil motives; such a one occasions quarrels and heartburnings. And a whisperer separateth chief friends (Proverbs 17:9). Nirgan is either "a chatterer," or "a whisperer," "calumniator." In Proverbs 18:8 and Proverbs 26:20, 22 it is translated "tale bearer." "Be not called a whisperer (ψίθυρος)," says the Son of Sirach (Ecclus. 5:14), speaking of secret slander. "Slanderers," says an old apothegm, "are Satan's bellows to blow up contension." Septuagint, "A perverse man sendeth abroad evils, and kindleth a torch of deceit for the wicked, and separateth friends." The alternative rendering of the second clause, "estrangeth a leader," i.e. alienates one leader from another, or from his army, is not confirmed by the authority of the versions or the best commentators.
