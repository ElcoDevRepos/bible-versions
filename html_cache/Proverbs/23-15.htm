My son
This phrase indicates a personal and intimate address, often used in wisdom literature to convey a fatherly or mentoring relationship. It reflects the familial context in which wisdom was traditionally passed down, emphasizing the importance of guidance and instruction within the family unit. This mirrors the relationship between God and His people, where God is seen as a Father to His children (Deuteronomy 1:31, Psalm 103:13).

if your heart is wise
The heart in biblical terms often represents the center of one's being, including mind, will, and emotions. Wisdom here is not merely intellectual but involves moral and spiritual discernment. The call for a wise heart aligns with the broader biblical theme that true wisdom begins with the fear of the Lord (Proverbs 9:10). This wisdom is practical, affecting how one lives and makes decisions, and is a reflection of a life aligned with God's will.

my own heart will indeed rejoice
The joy expressed here is deeply personal and reflects the profound satisfaction and fulfillment that comes from seeing one's teachings and values embraced and lived out by the next generation. This joy is not just emotional but is tied to the spiritual success and well-being of the son. It echoes the joy of God when His children walk in truth and righteousness (3 John 1:4). The rejoicing also points to the communal aspect of wisdom, where the well-being of one affects the joy of others, particularly within the family and faith community.

Emphasize the importance of seeking wisdom as a lifelong pursuit that pleases God and brings joy to those who guide us.

Encourage believers to cultivate a heart that seeks understanding and aligns with God's truth, as the heart is the wellspring of life.

Parental Joy in Godly Choices
Highlight the joy and satisfaction that parents and mentors experience when their children or disciples make wise, godly decisions.

Teach that true joy is found not in material success but in living a life characterized by wisdom and understanding.

Encouragement to Seek Wisdom
Urge believers to actively seek wisdom through prayer, study of Scripture, and the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

What Does Proverbs 23:15 Mean
My son,

- The verse opens with a tender, relational address. Solomon speaks as a caring father, echoing earlier appeals in Proverbs (see Proverbs 1:8; 3:1; 4:1).

- This father-child language reminds us that wisdom is meant to be passed on personally, generation to generation, just as Paul called the Corinthians “my beloved children” (1 Corinthians 4:14–15) and John wrote, “I have no greater joy than to hear that my children are walking in the truth” (3 John 4).

- Behind Solomon’s voice stands our heavenly Father, inviting every believer into the same intimate, instructive relationship (compare Hebrews 12:6-7).

if your heart is wise,

- Wisdom here is more than sharp thinking; it is a heart aligned with God’s ways (Proverbs 4:23; Deuteronomy 6:5).

• It embraces the “fear of the LORD” (Proverbs 9:10).

• It produces peaceable, gentle, obedient living (James 3:17; Colossians 1:9-10).

- By aiming at the heart, the verse stresses inward transformation, not mere outward compliance. When a child’s motives, loves, and decisions flow from godly wisdom, everything else follows.

my own heart will indeed rejoice.

- A parent’s deepest delight is seeing a child walk in wisdom (Proverbs 10:1; 23:24-25).

- Solomon’s joy foreshadows God’s joy when His children live rightly—like the shepherd rejoicing over the found sheep (Luke 15:7).

- This promise motivates both children to pursue wisdom and parents to nurture it, knowing that family harmony and divine pleasure are at stake.

summary

Proverbs 23:15 pictures a loving father addressing his child: “My son, if your heart is wise, my own heart will indeed rejoice”. The verse ties a child’s inner embrace of godly wisdom to the overflowing joy of the parent—and ultimately of God Himself. When our hearts choose wisdom, everyone’s heart rejoices.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - The moralist now addresses the disciple, and so to the end of the chapter. If thine heart be wise; become wise by profiting by discipline, and having its natural folly (Proverbs 22:15) eradicated. My heart shall rejoice, even mine. The pronoun is repeated for the sake of emphasis (as in Proverbs 22:19), the speaker thus declaring his supreme interest in the moral progress of his pupil.
