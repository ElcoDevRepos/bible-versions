Do not add to His words
This phrase emphasizes the sufficiency and authority of God's Word. Throughout Scripture, the importance of adhering strictly to God's commands is highlighted. In Deuteronomy 4:2 and Revelation 22:18-19, similar warnings are given against altering divine revelation. This reflects the belief that God's Word is complete and perfect, needing no human addition. Historically, the Israelites were surrounded by cultures with mutable religious texts, but they were called to maintain the purity of God's revelation. This principle underscores the importance of Scripture as the ultimate authority in faith and practice.

lest He rebuke you
The concept of divine rebuke is prevalent in the Bible, often associated with correction and discipline. In Hebrews 12:5-6, God's rebuke is seen as a form of loving discipline for His children. The rebuke here serves as a warning against the pride and presumption of altering God's Word. It reflects the biblical theme that God is actively involved in guiding and correcting His people, ensuring they remain faithful to His truth. This rebuke is not merely punitive but serves to realign individuals with God's will.

and prove you a liar
This phrase highlights the consequences of distorting God's Word. In biblical terms, a liar is someone who misrepresents the truth, as seen in John 8:44, where lying is associated with the devil. The integrity of God's Word is paramount, and any deviation from it is equated with falsehood. This serves as a reminder of the importance of truthfulness and the dangers of false teaching, which is a recurring theme in the New Testament, particularly in the pastoral epistles. The ultimate truth is found in Jesus Christ, who is described as "the way, the truth, and the life" in John 14:6, and any deviation from His teachings is considered a lie.

3. The Book of Proverbs

Teaching Points
The Sufficiency of Scripture
God's Word is complete and sufficient for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness. We must trust in its completeness and not feel the need to add our interpretations or traditions as equal to Scripture.

The Danger of Human Additions
Adding to God's Word can lead to false teachings and misrepresentations of His truth. It is crucial to discern between God's commands and human traditions or interpretations.

The Importance of Humility
Recognize our limitations in understanding and interpreting God's Word. Approach Scripture with humility, seeking the guidance of the Holy Spirit rather than relying solely on human wisdom.

The Call to Integrity
Uphold the integrity of God's Word in teaching and practice. Ensure that our lives and teachings align with Scripture, avoiding the temptation to alter it for personal gain or convenience.

The Role of Accountability
Be accountable to fellow believers and spiritual leaders to ensure that our understanding and application of Scripture remain true to its intended message.

What Does Proverbs 30:6 Mean
Do not add to His words

Proverbs 30:6 opens with a clear mandate: “Do not add to His words”.

• God’s revelation is complete and sufficient (2 Timothy 3:16–17).

• Adding human ideas dilutes divine authority; think of the warning Moses gave: “You shall not add to the word that I command you” (Deuteronomy 4:2).

• Jesus rebuked the Pharisees for piling traditions on top of Scripture, calling their worship “in vain” (Mark 7:7–13).

• The final book of the Bible echoes the same caution: “If anyone adds to them, God will add to him the plagues described in this book” (Revelation 22:18).

lest He rebuke you

The second phrase reminds us of God’s active defense of His word.

• A “rebuke” is more than correction; it is divine confrontation (Psalm 50:21).

• Scripture itself exposes error: “For the word of God is living and active… discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart” (Hebrews 4:12–13).

• When Job’s counselors spoke presumption, the LORD answered “out of the storm” to set the record straight (Job 38:1–2).

• God guards His message because it carries life-giving power (Isaiah 55:10–11).

and prove you a liar

Tampering with God’s word puts one in direct opposition to truth.

• God “cannot lie” (Titus 1:2); therefore, any contradiction with Him is a falsehood.

• False prophets were exposed when their words failed (Jeremiah 28:15–17).

• Ananias and Sapphira discovered the peril of deception before God (Acts 5:3–5).

• The test is simple: if a claim does not align with Scripture, it is a lie (1 John 2:21–23).

summary

Proverbs 30:6 is a safeguard around the treasure of divine revelation. We are called to receive Scripture as God has given it—nothing more, nothing less—knowing that He defends His word and exposes any who distort it. Holding fast to the Bible keeps us in the realm of truth and under the blessing of the One who speaks without error.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Add thou not unto his words. God's will, as announced in revelation, is to be simply accepted and acted upon, not watered down, not overstrained. This injunction had already been given in the old Law (Deuteronomy 4:2; Deuteronomy 12:32); it is repeated in the New Testament with awful emphasis (Revelation 22:18, 19). No human speculations or traditions may be mingled with God's words; the glosses and explanations and definitions, affixed by rabbinical ingenuity to plain enactments, and proved to be false in morality and fatal to vital religion, are a commentary on the succeeding sentence, Lest he reprove thee, and thou be found a liar. The reproof is found in the consequences of such additions; the results to which they lead are such as show that no who asserts that these things are contained in the Word of God is a liar.
