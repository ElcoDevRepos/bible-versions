Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then they began to question among themselves
This phrase indicates a moment of introspection and concern among the disciples. The context is the Last Supper, a significant event where Jesus shares a final meal with His disciples before His crucifixion. The disciples' questioning reflects their uncertainty and fear, as they are unaware of who among them could betray Jesus. This moment highlights the human tendency to doubt and the lack of understanding of Jesus' mission, despite being close to Him. It also sets the stage for the unfolding of the betrayal, a pivotal event in the Passion narrative.

which of them was going to do this.
The "this" refers to the betrayal of Jesus, which He had just predicted. The disciples' questioning shows their lack of awareness of Judas Iscariot's intentions, emphasizing the subtlety of Judas's betrayal. This moment is a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies, such as Psalm 41:9, which speaks of betrayal by a close friend. The act of betrayal is a type of the ultimate rejection of Christ by humanity, yet it is also part of God's sovereign plan for redemption. This phrase underscores the gravity of the situation and the unfolding of divine prophecy, as Jesus willingly goes to the cross for the salvation of mankind.

The final meal Jesus shares with His disciples before His crucifixion, during which He institutes the Lord's Supper and reveals the impending betrayal.

The act of delivering Jesus to the authorities, which is foretold by Jesus and causes the disciples to question who among them would commit such an act.

What Does Luke 22:23 Mean
Then

“Then” links this moment to Jesus’ grave announcement in the previous verse: “Indeed, the Son of Man will go as it has been determined, but woe to that man who betrays Him” (Luke 22:22).

• In Scripture, “then” often signals a turning point (see Matthew 26:22).

• The disciples move instantly from listening to reacting—showing how the Word of Christ always calls for a response (James 1:22–25).

• Their timeline is set by Jesus, not by circumstance; He reveals what will happen, and “then” the narrative unfolds exactly as He said (John 13:18–19).

they began to question among themselves

The conversation shifts from Jesus’ declaration to personal self-examination.

• The group dynamic mirrors Proverbs 27:17—interaction sharpens spiritual awareness.

• Though the Lord has just instituted the Lord’s Supper (Luke 22:19–20), the disciples are still processing His words rather than the symbols, echoing how believers can miss deeper meaning when focused on immediate concerns (Mark 9:31–32).

• Their questioning is not gossip but an earnest search of the heart, similar to Psalm 139:23–24.

which of them

The phrase shows each disciple recognizing the possibility of personal failure.

• No one assumes innocence automatically; humility is evident (1 Corinthians 10:12).

• Even the inner circle (Peter, James, John) are not exempt—illustrating Romans 3:23 in real time.

• This collective uncertainty affirms Jesus’ prior teaching that wheat and tares grow together until the harvest (Matthew 13:24–30).

was going to do this

“Do this” refers to the act of betrayal foretold in verse 22.

• The certainty—“was going to”—highlights divine sovereignty; the event is set, though human responsibility remains (Acts 2:23).

• Their ignorance of the betrayer’s identity underscores Judas’s outward conformity, warning that proximity to Jesus is not the same as loyalty to Jesus (1 John 2:19).

• The statement presses the disciples toward vigilance and faithfulness amid an approaching crisis (Ephesians 6:13–14).

summary

Luke 22:23 captures the disciples’ immediate, humble, and urgent self-examination after Jesus foretells His betrayal. The verse reminds believers that Christ’s Word provokes response, that sincere fellowship involves mutual heart-searching, that no one is above falling, and that God’s sovereign plan unfolds even through human choices.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23 - And they began to inquire among themselves, which of them it was that should do this thing. That all the disciples, on hearing this statement of their Master, should at once question their own hearts with the "Is it I?" (of St. Matthew's Gospel), shows with what cunning skill the arch-traitor must have concealed not merely his plans but his very sentiments. No suspicion on their parts ever seems to have fallen on Judas, their companion for so long a time. The direct colloquy of the Lord with the traitor, reported at length in the other Gospels on the occasion of dipping the sop into one of the Paschal dishes, was most probably carried on in a whisper (see John 13:26-29, where mention is specially made of the disciples' ignorance of the dread meaning of their Master's words to Judas).
