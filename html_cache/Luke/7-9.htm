When Jesus heard this
This phrase indicates Jesus' response to the centurion's message delivered through his friends. The centurion had expressed his unworthiness for Jesus to enter his home and his belief that Jesus could heal his servant with just a word. This demonstrates the centurion's understanding of authority and power, which is significant given his Roman military background.

He marveled at the centurion
Jesus' marveling is noteworthy as it is one of the few instances in the Gospels where Jesus is described as being amazed. This reaction highlights the extraordinary nature of the centurion's faith, especially considering he was a Gentile. The centurion's faith contrasts with the often lackluster faith of many Israelites, including the religious leaders of the time.

Turning to the crowd following Him
Jesus addresses the crowd, which likely included both His disciples and other followers, as well as onlookers. This public declaration serves to emphasize the lesson He is about to impart. The crowd's presence underscores the communal aspect of Jesus' ministry and the importance of witness and testimony in His teachings.

He said, “I tell you
This introduction signals the importance and authority of Jesus' forthcoming statement. It is a common phrase used by Jesus to preface significant teachings or revelations, underscoring His role as a teacher and prophet.

not even in Israel have I found such great faith.”
This statement is a profound commendation of the centurion's faith, especially given that Israel was the chosen nation, expected to be the epitome of faith in God. The centurion's faith is presented as a model, challenging the Jewish audience to reflect on their own faith. This also foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles in God's plan of salvation, a theme that is further developed in the New Testament. The centurion's faith is reminiscent of the faith of the patriarchs, such as Abraham, who believed God's promises without needing physical evidence.

Teaching Points
Faith Beyond Borders
The centurion's faith exemplifies that true belief in Jesus transcends ethnic and cultural boundaries. We are called to recognize and celebrate faith wherever it is found.

Authority of Christ
The centurion understood Jesus' authority over illness and distance. As believers, we should trust in Jesus' power and sovereignty in all areas of our lives.

Humility and Recognition
The centurion's humility in acknowledging Jesus' authority is a model for us. We should approach God with humility, recognizing His power and our need for His intervention.

Witness to Others
Jesus used the centurion's faith as a teaching moment for the crowd. Our faith can serve as a testimony to others, encouraging them to trust in Jesus.

Faith in Action
The centurion's faith was not passive; it prompted him to seek Jesus' help. Our faith should lead us to actively seek God's will and intervention in our lives.

What Does Luke 7:9 Mean
When Jesus heard this

• What Jesus “heard” (Luke 7:6-8) was the centurion’s message of humility—“Lord, do not trouble Yourself, for I am not worthy to have You come under my roof… just say the word, and my servant will be healed.”

• The centurion recognized Jesus’ absolute authority; like a commander giving orders, he believed Christ’s spoken word would suffice.

• Similar confidence appears in John 4:50, where Jesus tells the royal official, “Go; your son will live,” and the man “took Jesus at His word and departed.”

• Faith comes by hearing the word of Christ (Romans 10:17), and the centurion’s response is a living illustration of that truth.

He marveled at the centurion

• Scripture records only two occasions where Jesus “marveled”: at great faith here, and at great unbelief in His hometown (Mark 6:6).

• The Son of God, who knows all hearts (John 2:25), finds this Gentile soldier’s trust extraordinary.

• Hebrews 11:6 reminds us, “Without faith it is impossible to please God,” and the centurion’s reliance on Jesus alone pleases Him deeply.

• The moment underscores that sincere, child-like dependence on Christ is precious to Him, regardless of background.

Turning to the crowd following Him

• Jesus turns the centurion’s private request into a public lesson, ensuring the gathered Israelites do not miss the point.

• Throughout the Gospels He often pauses to teach crowds after a miracle (Matthew 5:1-2; Luke 14:25-27), using real-life encounters as living parables.

• By addressing the onlookers, He shows that faith is not merely personal; it carries a testimony meant to build up others (1 Timothy 4:12).

Not even in Israel have I found such great faith

• Israel possessed the covenants, promises, and prophecies (Romans 9:4-5), yet a Roman officer outshines them in trusting the Messiah.

• Jesus’ statement anticipates the grafting in of believing Gentiles (Acts 10:1-2; Romans 11:17-18) and warns that heritage without faith is empty (Matthew 21:43).

• The centurion’s “great faith” rests wholly on Jesus’ word, echoing Abraham’s example: “He believed the LORD, and it was credited to him as righteousness” (Genesis 15:6; compare Galatians 3:6-9).

• Christ’s commendation assures believers today that He still seeks and rewards the same wholehearted confidence in His authority.

summary

Luke 7:9 spotlights a Gentile soldier whose humility and confidence in Jesus’ word amaze the Lord Himself. By contrasting this faith with the widespread unbelief in Israel, Jesus teaches that true relationship with God is secured not by lineage but by wholehearted trust in His authority. The centurion’s example invites every reader to take Christ at His word, knowing that such faith delights the Savior and opens the door for His powerful work.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) I have not found so great faith, no, not in Israel.--It is, perhaps, characteristic of both the Evangelists that St. Luke omits the warning words which St. Matthew records as to the "many that shall come from the east and the west," and the exclusion of the children of the kingdom.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - When Jesus heard these things, he marvelled at him. Augustine strikingly comments here on the expression ἐθαύμασε, he marvelled: "Who had inspired that faith but he who now admires it?" In marvelling at it he intimated that we ought to admire. He admires for our good, that we may imitate the centurion's faith; such movements in Christ are not signs of perturbation of mind, but are exemplary and hortatory to us (St. Augustine, quoted by Bishop Wordsworth, on Matthew 8:10). I have not found so great faith, no, not in Israel. St. Augustine remarks here that "the Lord had found in the oleaster what he had not found in the olive."
