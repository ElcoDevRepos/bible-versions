Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But Jesus looked directly at them and said,
This phrase highlights Jesus' authoritative presence and His direct engagement with the religious leaders. In the context of Luke 20, Jesus is in the temple courts, teaching and being questioned by the chief priests and scribes. His direct gaze signifies His confidence and the seriousness of His message. This moment is a confrontation, as Jesus challenges their understanding and authority.

“Then what is the meaning of that which is written:
Jesus often used questions to provoke thought and reflection. Here, He refers to Scripture, emphasizing the importance of understanding the prophetic writings. This approach underscores the Jewish tradition of interpreting the Scriptures and highlights Jesus' role as a teacher who reveals deeper truths.

‘The stone the builders rejected
This phrase is a direct quotation from Psalm 118:22, a messianic psalm. The "builders" symbolize the religious leaders of Israel who, in their spiritual blindness, fail to recognize Jesus as the Messiah. The rejection of the stone is a metaphor for their rejection of Jesus, who does not fit their expectations of a political or military leader.

has become the cornerstone’?
The cornerstone is the most crucial stone in a building's foundation, ensuring stability and alignment. In biblical times, it was the first stone set in construction, determining the position of the entire structure. This imagery signifies Jesus' essential role in God's redemptive plan. Despite being rejected, He becomes the foundation of the Church. This concept is echoed in other New Testament passages, such as Ephesians 2:20 and 1 Peter 2:6-7, where Jesus is identified as the cornerstone, fulfilling Old Testament prophecy and establishing His preeminence in the new covenant.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is addressing the religious leaders who are questioning His authority. He uses a parable to convey a deeper spiritual truth.

2. Religious Leaders
These include the chief priests, scribes, and elders who are challenging Jesus. They represent the builders who have rejected the stone.

3. The Stone
Symbolically represents Jesus Himself, who is rejected by the religious leaders but becomes the cornerstone of God's redemptive plan.

4. The Cornerstone
A critical element in construction, symbolizing the foundation of the Church and the Kingdom of God, with Jesus as the essential and foundational element.

5. Psalm 118:22
The Old Testament scripture Jesus quotes, which is a messianic prophecy about the rejection and ultimate exaltation of the Messiah.
Teaching Points
The Rejection of Jesus
Recognize that Jesus was rejected by the religious leaders of His time, yet this rejection was part of God's sovereign plan for salvation.

Jesus as the Cornerstone
Understand that Jesus is the foundation of our faith and the Church. Our lives should be built upon Him, aligning with His teachings and example.

Prophetic Fulfillment
Appreciate the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies in the New Testament, affirming the reliability and divine inspiration of Scripture.

Personal Reflection
Consider areas in your life where you might be rejecting Jesus' authority or teachings, and seek to align more closely with His will.

Community and Unity
As believers, we are called to be part of the spiritual house built on Jesus. This calls for unity and cooperation within the body of Christ.

What Does Luke 20:17 Mean
But Jesus looked directly at them

• Luke places us in the temple courts where the chief priests, scribes, and elders have just challenged Christ’s authority (Luke 20:1-8).

• His steady gaze communicates divine discernment—He sees through their outward religiosity to the unbelief beneath (John 2:24-25; Hebrews 4:13).

• Eye contact also signals courage; He refuses to be intimidated by earthly power (Isaiah 50:7; Matthew 23:13-33).

• For believers, the scene reminds us that the Lord still looks “directly at” every heart, calling for sincerity over pretense (1 Samuel 16:7).

and said

• When Jesus speaks, heaven speaks (Luke 4:32; John 7:46).

• He does not offer speculation but issues a judgment that carries eternal weight (Matthew 24:35).

• His words confront these leaders—and us—with a choice: submit or resist (Deuteronomy 30:19-20; John 12:48).

Then what is the meaning of that which is written

• Christ immediately turns to Scripture, treating it as the final court of appeal (Psalm 119:160; John 10:35).

• By asking a question, He invites His opponents to read their own Bible honestly—something they prided themselves on yet failed to do (Matthew 22:29).

• The lesson: every spiritual dilemma is ultimately settled by returning to what “is written” (2 Timothy 3:16-17).

‘The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone’?

• Quoting Psalm 118:22, Jesus identifies Himself as “the stone.”

• “The builders” are Israel’s religious leaders—experts who should have recognized the Messiah yet are now discarding Him (Acts 4:11).

• Rejection is not defeat; God elevates the rejected stone to “cornerstone,” the essential foundation and alignment for God’s house (Ephesians 2:20-22).

• This prophecy unfolds literally in Christ’s crucifixion and resurrection, followed by the birth of the church (1 Peter 2:4-7).

• Those who stumble over Him face judgment (Isaiah 8:14-15; Luke 20:18), while those who rest on Him receive salvation (Romans 10:9-11).

summary

Jesus’ unwavering gaze, authoritative voice, and appeal to Scripture expose the blindness of religious self-confidence. Psalm 118 foretold that the very leaders charged with building God’s people would reject His chosen Stone. Yet God overrules their dismissal, making Jesus the Cornerstone of a new spiritual house. The verse calls every reader to move from rejecting to resting on Christ, for in Him alone is a sure and eternal foundation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) And he beheld them.--Better, He looked on them. The Greek verb implies the gaze turned and fixed on its object, in addition to the mere act of beholding.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 17, 18. - And he beheld them, and said, What is this then thai; is written, The stone which the builders rejected, the same is become the head of the corner? Whosoever shall fall upon that stone shall be broken; but on whomsoever it shall fall, it will grind him to powder. In spite of the deprecating expression, the severity of the tone of Jesus increases in his next words, when, looking at them with grave anger (ἐμβλέψας), he proceeds to speak of himself under the figure of the rejected stone. Quoting a well-known psalm (Psalm 118:22), and using the imagery of Isaiah 8:14, 15 and Daniel 2:44, he describes his fortunes under the imago of a corner-stone - that stone which forms the junction between the two most prominent walls of a building, and which is always laid with peculiar care and attention. In Luke 2:34 of our Gospel Simeon refers to the same well-known prophetic saying. The husbandmen who had just been described as vine-dressers are now described as builders, and the murdered son is reproduced under the image of a corner, stone tossed aside as useless. In the first part of the picture, the earthly humiliation of Messiah is portrayed when the stone is laid in the earth. In the second, the stone falling from the top of the building represents the crushing of all earthly opposition by Messiah in his glory. Woe to the builders, then, who had scornfully rejected him
