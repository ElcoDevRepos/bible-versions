Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All spoke well of Him
This phrase indicates the initial positive reception Jesus received from those in the synagogue. The people of Nazareth, His hometown, were impressed by His teaching. This reflects the common practice of Jewish synagogues where visiting teachers would be invited to read and expound on the Scriptures. The favorable response suggests that Jesus' reputation as a teacher and miracle worker had preceded Him, aligning with the accounts of His early ministry in Galilee.

and marveled at the gracious words that came from His lips.
The term "gracious words" highlights the wisdom and authority with which Jesus spoke. His teaching was characterized by grace, a hallmark of His ministry, as seen in John 1:14, where Jesus is described as "full of grace and truth." The marveling of the people suggests that His words were not only eloquent but also carried a depth and insight that was unexpected from someone they knew as a local carpenter. This reaction is consistent with other instances in the Gospels where Jesus' teaching astonished listeners (e.g., Matthew 7:28-29).

“Isn’t this the son of Joseph?” they asked.
This question reflects the skepticism and familiarity of the people of Nazareth. They knew Jesus as the son of Joseph, a carpenter, which made it difficult for them to reconcile His humble origins with the authority and wisdom He displayed. This skepticism is a recurring theme in the Gospels, where Jesus' identity and authority are questioned by those who knew Him before His public ministry. The reference to Joseph also underscores Jesus' humanity and His fulfillment of the prophecy of the Messiah coming from humble beginnings, as seen in Isaiah 53:2-3. This question foreshadows the rejection Jesus would face, as familiarity bred contempt among those who could not see beyond His earthly family ties.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is speaking in the synagogue in Nazareth, His hometown. His words and identity are the focus of the crowd's reaction.

2. Nazareth
A small town in Galilee where Jesus grew up. The setting is significant as it highlights the familiarity of the townspeople with Jesus' earthly family.

3. The Synagogue
The place of Jewish worship and teaching where Jesus reads from the scroll of Isaiah and delivers His message.

4. The Crowd
The people of Nazareth who are listening to Jesus. Their initial reaction is one of amazement and skepticism.

5. Joseph
Referred to as Jesus' earthly father, highlighting the crowd's perception of Jesus' humble origins.
Teaching Points
The Power of Jesus' Words
Jesus' words were described as "gracious," indicating the divine wisdom and authority with which He spoke. As believers, we should seek to speak with grace and truth, reflecting Christ in our conversations.

Familiarity Breeds Contempt
The people of Nazareth struggled to reconcile Jesus' divine authority with their knowledge of His humble beginnings. We must guard against allowing familiarity with Jesus or His teachings to lead to complacency or disbelief.

Recognizing Jesus' True Identity
The question, "Isn’t this the son of Joseph?" reveals a limited understanding of Jesus' true nature. As Christians, we are called to recognize and proclaim Jesus as the Son of God, not just a historical figure.

The Challenge of Preconceived Notions
The crowd's reaction highlights the challenge of overcoming preconceived notions about who Jesus is. We should be open to the transformative power of Christ, beyond our expectations or cultural biases.

What Does Luke 4:22 Mean
All spoke well of Him

• Luke pictures a real moment in Nazareth when the hometown crowd cannot deny what they see and hear—Jesus is impressive, and the approval is universal (Luke 4:15).

• This is the fulfillment of Luke 2:52, where Jesus “grew in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and man.” Years later, that favor is still evident.

• The scene echoes John 7:46, where officers sent to arrest Him admit, “Never has anyone spoken like this man!” The verbal witness of common people validates the Lord’s uniqueness.

• Genuine honor belongs to Christ, yet the text quietly reminds us that public opinion is fickle (cf. John 2:23-25). The same tongues praising Him now will soon doubt or even reject Him.

and marveled at the gracious words that came from His lips

• “Gracious” highlights both tone and content. Jesus has just read Isaiah 61:1-2 and declared its fulfillment (Luke 4:18-21). Listeners are stunned that prophecy is unfolding before their eyes—and ears.

• Psalm 45:2 anticipated Messiah: “Grace has anointed Your lips.” Luke shows that promise kept.

• The crowd’s amazement resembles Matthew 7:28-29, where other listeners are astonished because He teaches “as one who had authority.” Authority and grace are perfectly married in Him (John 1:14).

• His speech models what Colossians 4:6 commands of believers. The Savior’s words are truth delivered in divine kindness, attractive yet uncompromising.

“Isn’t this the son of Joseph?” they asked

• Admiration collides with familiarity. They know His family and trade (Matthew 13:54-55; Mark 6:3), so they try to fit supernatural authority into natural categories.

• The question is not mere curiosity; it carries skepticism. In John 6:42 similar words lead to outright unbelief. Nazareth voices the age-old stumbling block: “How can the ordinary be the vessel of the extraordinary?”

• Luke 3:23 has already told the reader that Jesus “was thought to be the son of Joseph,” yet His baptism and genealogy prove His divine Sonship. The crowd’s low view of Him reveals their incomplete knowledge, not Christ’s limited nature.

• The shift from praise to doubt warns modern readers: exposure to Jesus does not equal faith. Familiarity can breed contempt unless the heart bows to revealed truth.

summary

Luke 4:22 captures a swift movement of hearts—from unanimous praise, to astonished wonder, to skeptical questioning. The verse shows that Jesus’ gracious authority is undeniable, yet human pride resists the implications of His true identity. Belief requires more than admiration; it demands recognition that the hometown carpenter is, in fact, the promised Messiah and Son of God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) The gracious words.--Literally, the words of grace. It is noticeable that the latter noun does not occur at all in St. Matthew or St. Mark, becomes prominent in the Acts, and is afterwards the most characteristic word of the Epistles of St. Paul and St. Peter.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - And they said, Is not this Joseph's Son? Quickly the preacher caught the mind and feeling of his audience. Surprise and admiration soon gave place to a spirit of unbelief. Is not this who speaks to us such words, bright and eloquent with hope, often with a ring of sure triumph and certain victory in them - is it not the young Carpenter we have known so long in our village?
