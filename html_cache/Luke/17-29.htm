Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But on the day Lot left Sodom
This phrase refers to the biblical account of Lot, Abraham's nephew, who lived in the city of Sodom. The narrative is found in Genesis 19. Lot's departure from Sodom is significant as it marks the moment of divine judgment on the city. The urgency of Lot's exit, prompted by angelic messengers, underscores the immediacy of God's impending wrath. This event is a typology of deliverance, illustrating God's ability to rescue the righteous from judgment, as seen in 2 Peter 2:7-9.

fire and sulfur rained down from heaven
The destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah is described as a supernatural act of God, where fire and sulfur (often translated as brimstone) are used as instruments of judgment. This imagery is consistent with other biblical depictions of divine wrath, such as the plagues in Egypt (Exodus 9:23-24) and the final judgment in Revelation 20:10. Archaeological evidence suggests that the region around the Dead Sea, where Sodom is believed to have been located, has layers of sulfur deposits, supporting the biblical account.

and destroyed them all
The total destruction of Sodom serves as a sobering reminder of the consequences of sin and rebellion against God. It highlights the theme of divine justice, where God's patience with human wickedness has limits. This event is often referenced in Scripture as a warning, such as in Jude 1:7, which speaks of Sodom and Gomorrah as examples of those who suffer the punishment of eternal fire. The phrase also emphasizes the completeness of God's judgment, leaving no survivors, which parallels the finality of judgment at the end of the age.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Lot
A righteous man living in Sodom, Lot was Abraham's nephew. He is a central figure in the account of Sodom's destruction, representing God's mercy and deliverance for the faithful.

2. Sodom
An ancient city known for its wickedness and sinfulness. It serves as a symbol of God's judgment against sin.

3. Fire and Sulfur
The divine instruments of judgment used to destroy Sodom, symbolizing God's wrath and the finality of His judgment.

4. Destruction of Sodom
A historical event where God executed judgment on Sodom due to its pervasive sin, serving as a warning for future generations.

5. Day of the Lord
A prophetic theme in Scripture, often associated with judgment and deliverance, which Jesus alludes to in this passage.
Teaching Points
God's Judgment is Certain
Just as Sodom was destroyed, God's judgment on sin is inevitable. Believers should live with an awareness of this truth and strive for righteousness.

The Importance of Righteousness
Lot's deliverance highlights the importance of living a righteous life. Believers are called to be set apart from the sinful practices of the world.

Urgency of Repentance
The sudden destruction of Sodom serves as a reminder of the urgency to repent and turn to God before it's too late.

God's Mercy and Deliverance
Despite the judgment, God provided a way of escape for Lot. This illustrates God's mercy and His willingness to save those who are faithful.

Living in Expectation
Believers should live in expectation of Christ's return, being vigilant and prepared for the day of the Lord.

What Does Luke 17:29 Mean
But on the day

• Jesus singles out a particular, literal calendar day (Genesis 19:15, 23).

• His wording ties directly to the suddenness of future judgment (Luke 17:26, 30).

• This reminds us that God’s timetable is precise; nothing is random or delayed (2 Peter 3:8-10).

Lot left Sodom

• Lot’s departure marks the moment mercy ended for the city (Genesis 19:15-17).

• God delivered the righteous before unleashing wrath, a pattern seen with Noah (Genesis 7:1; 2 Peter 2:7-9).

• Obedience made the difference; lingering would have been fatal (Genesis 19:26; 2 Corinthians 6:17).

Fire and sulfur

• The judgment was tangible, not symbolic (Genesis 19:24; Deuteronomy 29:23).

• Fire pictures divine holiness consuming sin (Hebrews 12:29), while sulfur intensifies the image of perpetual ruin (Psalm 11:6; Revelation 21:8).

• Together they underscore that sin’s penalty is both severe and deserved (Romans 6:23).

Rained down from heaven

• Judgment originated from God, not human armies or natural chance (Genesis 19:24; Revelation 20:9).

• “Rained” mirrors the flood language (Luke 17:27), highlighting consistency in how God deals with rampant wickedness.

• Heaven’s involvement affirms that God oversees all creation and history (Psalm 103:19).

Destroyed them all

• No partiality: every unrepentant inhabitant perished (Genesis 19:25; Jude 7).

• Jesus links this total destruction to His future return—many will be caught unprepared (Luke 17:30-32; Matthew 24:39).

• The phrase warns that present moral climates do not postpone divine reckoning (2 Thessalonians 1:7-9).

summary

Luke 17:29 points to a real day when God rescued the righteous and executed complete judgment on persistent sin. Jesus uses that historical event to stress that His second coming will be just as sudden and decisive. The verse calls believers to live ready, trust God’s deliverance, and recognize that divine justice, while delayed in mercy, will inevitably fall.
