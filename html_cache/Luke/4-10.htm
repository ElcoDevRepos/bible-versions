For it is written:
This phrase indicates the use of Scripture to support a point or argument. In this context, it is part of the temptation of Jesus by Satan, who quotes Scripture to persuade Jesus. The phrase underscores the authority and importance of Scripture in Jewish and early Christian thought. It reflects the practice of using the Hebrew Scriptures (Old Testament) as a foundation for teaching and decision-making.

‘He will command His angels concerning You
This part of the verse is a quotation from Psalm 91:11, which speaks of God's protection over His faithful. The use of angels signifies divine intervention and care, a common theme in Jewish theology. Angels are often seen as messengers and protectors, acting on God's behalf. This reflects the belief in a spiritual realm actively involved in the affairs of humanity.

to guard You carefully,
The promise of protection is emphasized here, suggesting a vigilant and attentive safeguarding by the angels. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God's providence and care for His people. The careful guarding implies not just physical protection but also spiritual and moral guidance. This connects to the broader narrative of Jesus' mission, where divine protection is assured but not to be tested presumptuously.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is being tempted by Satan in the wilderness after fasting for forty days and nights.

2. Satan
The adversary who tempts Jesus, attempting to lead Him into sin by misusing Scripture.

3. Wilderness
The desolate place where Jesus is led by the Spirit and where the temptation occurs.

4. Angels
Heavenly beings mentioned in the verse, who are commanded by God to protect and serve His purposes.

5. Jerusalem
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, the temptation account includes the setting of the pinnacle of the temple in Jerusalem, where Satan challenges Jesus.
Teaching Points
Understanding Scripture in Context
Satan uses Scripture out of context to tempt Jesus. This teaches us the importance of understanding the full context of biblical passages to avoid misinterpretation.

Trust in God's Protection
While God promises protection, this does not mean we should test Him by putting ourselves in unnecessary danger. Trust in God should be accompanied by wisdom and discernment.

Resisting Temptation
Jesus' response to Satan's misuse of Scripture demonstrates the power of knowing and correctly applying God's Word to resist temptation.

Role of Angels
Angels are God's messengers and protectors, but our ultimate trust and reliance should be on God Himself, not solely on His created beings.

Spiritual Warfare
The temptation of Jesus is a reminder of the spiritual battles we face. We must be equipped with Scripture and the Holy Spirit to stand firm against the enemy.

What Does Luke 4:10 Mean
For it is written

Jesus meets the devil’s temptation with the unshakable authority of Scripture (Luke 4:4,‭ 8). By prefacing His response with these words, He shows that what God has already said is final.

• Psalm 119:89 reminds us, “Your word, O LORD, is everlasting; it is firmly fixed in the heavens.”

• Isaiah 55:11 promises that God’s word will not return void.

• 2 Timothy 3:16 underscores that “All Scripture is God-breathed.”

Because the written Word carries God’s own authority, it becomes both our shield (Ephesians 6:17) and our compass (Psalm 119:105) in moments of testing.

He will command His angels

The promise cited (Psalm 91:11) pictures God as the sovereign Commander of heaven’s armies, dispatching angels to serve His purposes.

• Hebrews 1:14 says angels are “ministering spirits sent to serve those who will inherit salvation.”

• In 2 Kings 6:17 Elisha’s servant sees the hills filled with horses and chariots of fire—proof that God’s help is often invisible but real.

• Daniel 6:22 shares that “My God sent His angel and shut the lions’ mouths,” highlighting divine intervention on behalf of the faithful.

If the Father mobilizes angels for His Son, believers can rest in His willingness to do the same for them as it aligns with His will.

concerning You

The care God pledges is personal. In the immediate context, the “You” refers to Jesus, the beloved Son (Luke 3:22).

• Isaiah 42:1 foretold a Servant in whom God delights.

• Matthew 3:17 echoes, “This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased.”

Yet the New Testament widens this intimacy to those in Christ. John 17:23 speaks of the Father loving believers “even as” He loves the Son. While we should not claim messianic promises out of context, we are assured that God’s attention is never generic but specific (Psalm 139:1-4).

to guard You carefully

Protection is the heart of the promise, but Satan twists it to promote presumption. Jesus answers in Luke 4:12, quoting Deuteronomy 6:16: “Do not test the Lord your God.”

• Psalm 121:3-8 paints a vigilant Keeper who “will neither slumber nor sleep.”

• Proverbs 30:5 tells us that “He is a shield to those who take refuge in Him,” not to those who tempt Him.

• 1 Peter 1:5 adds that we “are protected by the power of God through faith,” linking safety to trusting obedience rather than reckless display.

The lesson: God’s guardianship invites confident faith, not careless leaps.

summary

Luke 4:10 affirms the settled authority of Scripture, the Father’s sovereign command over angelic forces, His personal concern for His Son, and the promise of meticulous protection for all who walk in trusting obedience. Jesus models relying on God’s Word, not manipulating it, reminding us to rest in divine care while refusing to test the One who faithfully guards His own.
