Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
who had not consented to their decision or action
Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the Sanhedrin, did not agree with the council's decision to condemn Jesus. This highlights his righteousness and courage, as opposing the Sanhedrin could have led to severe consequences. His dissent aligns with the biblical principle of standing for truth, even when it is unpopular, as seen in Daniel's refusal to worship the king's image (Daniel 3).

He was from the Judean town of Arimathea
Arimathea is believed to be a town in Judea, though its exact location is uncertain. It is traditionally identified with Ramathaim-Zophim, the birthplace of Samuel (1 Samuel 1:1). This connection may suggest Joseph's noble heritage and his deep roots in Jewish history, emphasizing his role as a respected and devout Jew.

and was waiting for the kingdom of God
Joseph's anticipation of God's kingdom reflects a common Jewish hope for the Messiah's reign, as prophesied in the Old Testament (Isaiah 9:6-7). His faith in the coming kingdom aligns him with figures like Simeon and Anna, who also awaited the Messiah (Luke 2:25-38). This expectation underscores the belief in Jesus as the fulfillment of messianic prophecies, pointing to the establishment of God's spiritual kingdom through Christ's death and resurrection.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joseph of Arimathea
A member of the Jewish council (Sanhedrin) who did not agree with the decision to crucify Jesus. He is described as a righteous man who was waiting for the kingdom of God.

2. Arimathea
A city of the Jews, traditionally identified with Ramathaim-Zophim in the hill country of Ephraim. It is the hometown of Joseph, indicating his Jewish heritage and connection to the broader account of Israel.

3. The Sanhedrin's Decision
The Jewish council's decision to condemn Jesus to death. Joseph's dissent highlights his moral and spiritual integrity.

4. The Kingdom of God
A central theme in Jesus' teaching, representing God's sovereign rule and the fulfillment of His promises. Joseph's anticipation of this kingdom aligns him with the hope of Israel.

5. The Crucifixion of Jesus
The event surrounding this passage, where Jesus is sentenced to death. Joseph's role becomes significant as he later requests Jesus' body for burial.
Teaching Points
Courage in Faith
Joseph's dissent from the Sanhedrin's decision demonstrates the courage required to stand for truth, even when it is unpopular or dangerous.

Anticipation of God's Kingdom
Joseph's waiting for the kingdom of God reflects a deep faith and hope in God's promises. Believers are encouraged to live with this same anticipation.

Righteousness in Action
Joseph's actions show that true righteousness involves both belief and action. He not only disagreed with the council but also took steps to honor Jesus in His death.

The Role of the Remnant
Joseph represents the faithful remnant within Israel who recognized Jesus as the Messiah. This encourages believers to remain faithful even when in the minority.

The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Joseph's involvement in Jesus' burial fulfills Old Testament prophecy, reminding believers of God's sovereignty and the reliability of His Word.

What Does Luke 23:51 Mean
Not consenting to their decision or action

• Luke notes that Joseph “had not consented” to the Sanhedrin’s judgment against Jesus, meaning he actively disagreed with both the verdict and the rush to crucify (Luke 22:66–71; Mark 14:64).

• This sets Joseph apart from most of the Council, whose decision fulfilled Isaiah 53:8, yet Joseph chose righteousness over peer pressure (Proverbs 29:25; Acts 5:34–39).

• His dissent was costly—alignment with Jesus risked reputation and safety (John 9:22; 12:42)—but Joseph modeled courageous conviction like Moses, who “refused to be called the son of Pharaoh’s daughter” (Hebrews 11:24–26).

• Mark 15:43 confirms Joseph’s boldness: he “went boldly to Pilate to ask for Jesus’ body,” a public act declaring loyalty to the crucified Messiah.

He was from the Judean town of Arimathea

• Identifying Joseph’s hometown roots the narrative in real geography, underscoring Scripture’s historical accuracy (Luke 1:1–4).

• Arimathea (“Ramathaim” in 1 Samuel 1:1) lay in the Judean hill country, meaning Joseph traveled to Jerusalem to serve on the Council, then offered his own nearby tomb (Matthew 27:60).

• Owning property so close to the city fulfilled Isaiah 53:9—“He was assigned a grave with the wicked, but He was with a rich man in His death.” Joseph’s wealth and influence became instruments in God’s plan (1 Timothy 6:17–19).

• The detail also distinguishes him from Galilean disciples, showing that followers of Jesus emerged from every region and social class (Acts 6:7; Philippians 1:13).

Waiting for the kingdom of God

• Joseph lived in anticipation of the promised reign described by the prophets (Daniel 7:13–14; Isaiah 9:6–7). His hope mirrored that of Simeon, who also “was waiting for the consolation of Israel” (Luke 2:25–32).

• True expectation produced action: Joseph’s faith moved him to honor Jesus’ body, trusting that God’s kingdom purposes would prevail even in apparent defeat (Luke 23:52–53).

• Mark 15:43 repeats this trait, stressing that hope in God’s kingdom empowers believers to act with integrity when culture opposes Christ (Romans 14:17; 2 Peter 3:13).

• Joseph’s waiting was not passive; it was a lived-out belief that the crucified Jesus is the King whose resurrection would soon vindicate Him (Luke 24:1–7; Acts 2:32–36).

summary

Luke 23:51 highlights Joseph of Arimathea as a courageous dissenter, a real man from a real place, whose active hope in God’s kingdom propelled him to stand apart from an unjust decision. His example urges believers to resist unrighteous consensus, steward earthly resources for eternal purposes, and live expectantly for the full arrival of Christ’s reign.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(51) The counsel and deed of them.--The first word includes all the earlier stages of the action of the Sanhedrin, from the counsel given by Caiaphas (John 11:49) to the final condemnation; the second, the unofficial acts, such as the compact with Judas, and the delivery to Pilate.

Who also himself waited for the kingdom of God.--The description agrees in form with St. Mark, but not with St. Matthew or St. John. Nicodemus, who acted with him, is mentioned in the fourth Gospel only.
