Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then some of our companions went to the tomb
This phrase refers to the disciples who went to verify the women's report of the empty tomb. In the Gospel of John, Peter and John are specifically mentioned as the ones who ran to the tomb (John 20:3-4). This reflects the urgency and importance of confirming the resurrection, a pivotal event in Christian faith. The tomb, likely a rock-cut tomb typical of the period, was located outside Jerusalem, aligning with Jewish burial customs.

and found it just as the women had described.
The women, including Mary Magdalene, Joanna, and Mary the mother of James, were the first to discover the empty tomb (Luke 24:10). Their testimony, though initially met with skepticism due to cultural biases against women's testimonies, was crucial. The empty tomb is a central piece of evidence for the resurrection, fulfilling Jesus' prophecy about His rising on the third day (Luke 9:22).

But Him they did not see.
This highlights the initial confusion and lack of understanding among the disciples regarding the resurrection. Despite the empty tomb, Jesus' body was not there, which was perplexing to them. This absence underscores the miraculous nature of the resurrection, as Jesus later appears to His disciples in various forms, emphasizing His victory over death. This aligns with prophecies such as Psalm 16:10, which speaks of God not allowing His Holy One to see decay.

The role of the women and the disciples as witnesses to the resurrection highlights the importance of testimony in the Christian faith.

The disciples' journey to the tomb to verify the women's report teaches us about the balance between faith and seeking evidence.

The Reality of the Resurrection
The empty tomb is a foundational proof of Jesus' victory over death, encouraging believers to trust in the promise of eternal life.

The Role of Doubt and Belief
The initial doubt of the disciples can be a comfort to those who struggle with faith, showing that even the closest followers of Jesus needed reassurance.

The Power of the Resurrection

What Does Luke 24:24 Mean
Then some of our companions went to the tomb

• The speakers on the Emmaus road recall how certain disciples hurried to verify the women’s report. Luke 24:12 records, “But Peter got up and ran to the tomb; and bending down, he saw only the linen cloths”. John 20:3-4 adds that “Peter and the other disciple” ran together—first-hand confirmation that the empty tomb was not rumor.

• These men acted decisively, illustrating the natural human response to seek tangible evidence. Yet their very running affirms that the women’s testimony was already being treated seriously, overturning cultural norms that often dismissed female witnesses (compare Luke 24:1-10).

• This moment also fulfills Psalm 16:10, where David prophesied, “You will not abandon My soul to Sheol, nor will You let Your Holy One see decay”. The empty tomb marks the turning point from prophecy to observable history.

and found it just as the women had described

• Consistency of testimony strengthens credibility. The men saw the rolled-away stone, the discarded grave-cloths, and the absence of Jesus—exactly what Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and the others had reported (Luke 24:1-3, Mark 16:6).

• By confirming the women’s words, God underscores that He speaks through all His people, not just socially prominent voices. Acts 2:17 echoes this: “I will pour out My Spirit on all people…even on My servants, both men and women.”

• The precise agreement between independent witnesses reinforces the literal truthfulness of the resurrection narrative. If the tomb were not empty, every claim of resurrection would collapse (1 Corinthians 15:14).

But Him they did not see.

• An empty tomb alone does not equal faith; what matters is meeting the risen Lord. John 20:15-18 shows that Mary met Him only moments after finding the tomb empty, while the disciples in Luke 24:36-43 saw Him that same evening. God controls the timing of revelation.

• Jesus’ temporary concealment presses these disciples—and us—to move from mere evidence-gathering to trusting the promises spoken beforehand (Luke 24:6-8). As 1 Peter 1:8 reminds believers, “Though you have not seen Him, you love Him.”

• The absence of Jesus’ body, coupled with reliable witness, becomes a powerful apologetic: no body was ever produced because He was alive (Acts 2:32). The phrase points forward to the appearances that will soon erase every doubt, culminating in Thomas’s confession, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28).

summary

Luke 24:24 records an honest, step-by-step investigation: eyewitnesses hurry to the tomb, verify the women’s account, yet still lack a personal encounter with the risen Christ. The verse highlights (1) the harmony of multiple testimonies, (2) God’s validation of every faithful witness, and (3) the necessity of meeting Jesus Himself for faith to mature. The empty tomb is historical fact; the living Savior is everlasting reality.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) And certain of them which were with us.--The words have the interest of presenting an obviously undesigned coincidence with St. John's report of the visit of Peter and John (John 20:3). The naturalness of the manner in which the two Apostles are mentioned, but not named, "certain of them which were with us," may be noted, so far as it goes, as a sign of truthfulness. A later writer constructing a narrative would have brought in the two conspicuous names.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - And certain of them which were with us went to the sepulchre, and found it even so as the women had said: but him they saw not. Tholuck writes, "Does not their word sound as the language of those in whose heart the smoking flax yet glimmers, though nigh to extinction?"
