So keep watch at all times
This phrase emphasizes the importance of vigilance and spiritual alertness. In the context of Luke 21, Jesus is speaking about the end times and the signs that will precede His return. The call to "keep watch" is a recurring theme in the New Testament, urging believers to be prepared for the unexpected return of Christ (Matthew 24:42, 1 Thessalonians 5:6). The idea of watchfulness is also connected to the role of a watchman in ancient times, who was responsible for alerting the city of impending danger (Ezekiel 33:6).

and pray that you may have the strength to escape all that is about to happen
Prayer is presented as a vital means of obtaining divine strength and guidance. The phrase suggests that the events to come will be challenging and require spiritual fortitude. This echoes Jesus' instructions to His disciples in the Garden of Gethsemane to "watch and pray" to avoid falling into temptation (Matthew 26:41). The notion of escaping what is to come can be linked to the trials and tribulations described in the apocalyptic literature of the Bible, such as the Book of Revelation.

and to stand before the Son of Man.
The "Son of Man" is a title Jesus frequently used for Himself, drawing from Daniel 7:13-14, where the Son of Man is given authority and an everlasting kingdom. To "stand before" Him implies being in a position of acceptance and readiness at His return. This phrase suggests a final judgment or evaluation, where believers are called to be found faithful and worthy. The imagery of standing before a king or judge was common in ancient cultures, symbolizing accountability and the hope of receiving a favorable verdict.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker of this verse, providing guidance and prophecy to His disciples about the end times.

2. Disciples
The immediate audience of Jesus' teachings, representing all believers who are called to heed His warnings and instructions.

3. Son of Man
A title Jesus uses for Himself, emphasizing His role in the final judgment and His divine authority.

4. End Times
The period Jesus is referring to, characterized by trials and tribulations preceding His second coming.

5. Jerusalem
While not directly mentioned in this verse, it is the setting of Jesus' discourse in Luke 21, where He predicts the destruction of the temple and the signs of the end times.
Teaching Points
Vigilance in Faith
Believers are called to "stay awake" spiritually, maintaining a constant awareness of their faith and the times they live in.

The Power of Prayer
Prayer is essential for gaining the strength needed to endure trials and to remain faithful until the end.

Preparedness for Christ's Return
Christians should live in a state of readiness, understanding that the return of Christ could happen at any moment.

Standing Before the Son of Man
The ultimate goal is to be found worthy to stand before Jesus, which requires a life of holiness and dedication.

Escaping Tribulation
While trials are inevitable, Jesus provides a way of escape through spiritual vigilance and reliance on Him.

What Does Luke 21:36 Mean
So keep watch

Jesus has just described upheavals—wars, earthquakes, persecutions—and then He says, “So keep watch.”

• Watchfulness is active, not passive (Matthew 24:42–44).

• It means discerning the times, testing every spirit (1 John 4:1), and refusing spiritual drowsiness (Romans 13:11).

• It guards against surprise when the promised events unfold (1 Thessalonians 5:4–6).

at all times

The call is continuous.

• Consistent alertness keeps the heart soft and ready (Hebrews 3:13).

• Moments of complacency let temptation creep in (Mark 14:37–38).

• Everyday life—work, rest, recreation—falls under this “all times” umbrella (Colossians 3:17).

and pray

Watching and praying belong together.

• Prayer keeps dependence centered on God, not self (Philippians 4:6–7).

• It tunes the heart to God’s will, preventing fear when troubles intensify (Psalm 34:4).

• Persistent prayer is exactly what Jesus modeled in Gethsemane (Luke 22:40–44).

that you may have the strength

Strength here speaks of spiritual endurance.

• God supplies power through His Spirit (Ephesians 3:16).

• Endurance grows as we exercise faith daily (James 1:2–4).

• Strength is promised to the humble who rely on the Lord, not the self-reliant (2 Corinthians 12:9–10).

to escape all that is about to happen

Jesus refers to the severe judgments that precede His return (Luke 21:25–28).

• Escape is not avoidance of earthly hardship but rescue from divine wrath (1 Thessalonians 1:10).

• Noah and Lot illustrate God’s pattern—He preserves the righteous amid judgment (2 Peter 2:5–9).

• The command urges readiness for any form of deliverance God designs, including the rapture promised in 1 Thessalonians 4:16–17.

and to stand before the Son of Man

Standing speaks of acceptance and vindication.

• Believers appear before Christ’s judgment seat for reward, not condemnation (2 Corinthians 5:10).

• A blameless stance is secured by Christ’s righteousness (Jude 24).

• This meeting is personal—each life evaluated, every tear wiped away (Revelation 21:4).

summary

Luke 21:36 calls followers of Jesus to lifelong vigilance. Continuous watchfulness, saturated with prayer, brings God-given strength that delivers from coming judgment and prepares believers to stand confidently before Christ. This verse turns end-times discussion into daily discipleship: stay alert, stay prayerful, stay ready.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) Pray always.--The word is not the same commonly used for "pray," but occurs once only in the other Gospels (Matthew 9:38). St. Luke uses it fifteen times in the Gospel and Acts together, and St. Paul six times (2Corinthians 5:20; 2Corinthians 8:4; 2Corinthians 10:2, et seq.). It is not used by any other New Testament writer.

That ye may be accounted worthy . . .--See Note on Luke 20:35. The better MSS., however, give, "that ye may have strength to escape." . . .
