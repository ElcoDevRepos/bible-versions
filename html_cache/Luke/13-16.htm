Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then should not this daughter of Abraham
This phrase emphasizes the woman's identity as a "daughter of Abraham," highlighting her inclusion in the covenant community of Israel. It underscores the importance of the Abrahamic covenant (Genesis 12:1-3) and God's promises to His people. Jesus' use of this term indicates her rightful place in receiving God's mercy and healing. It also challenges the prevailing cultural and religious norms that often marginalized women, affirming her worth and dignity in God's eyes.

whom Satan has kept bound for eighteen long years
This phrase reveals the spiritual dimension of the woman's affliction, attributing her condition to Satan's influence. It reflects the biblical understanding of spiritual warfare, where Satan is often depicted as an adversary seeking to oppress God's people (Job 1:6-12, Ephesians 6:12). The mention of "eighteen long years" emphasizes the severity and duration of her suffering, highlighting the compassion of Jesus in addressing long-standing afflictions. It also serves as a reminder of the ultimate victory over Satan through Christ's ministry and sacrifice (1 John 3:8).

be released from her bondage
The concept of release from bondage is central to the message of the Gospel, symbolizing spiritual liberation and freedom from sin and oppression (Isaiah 61:1, Luke 4:18-19). Jesus' healing acts as a foretaste of the ultimate redemption and deliverance He offers. This phrase also connects to the theme of Jubilee in Leviticus 25, where debts are forgiven, and captives are set free, reflecting God's desire for restoration and wholeness.

on the Sabbath day?
The Sabbath was a day of rest and worship, instituted by God in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:8-11). Jesus' healing on the Sabbath challenges the legalistic interpretations of the religious leaders, who prioritized ritual observance over compassion and mercy (Mark 2:27-28). This act underscores the true purpose of the Sabbath as a time for doing good and bringing life, aligning with God's character and intentions. It also foreshadows the rest and peace found in Christ, who fulfills the law and offers eternal Sabbath rest to believers (Hebrews 4:9-10).

Persons / Places / Events

4. The Synagogue

5. The Sabbath
A day of rest and worship, central to Jewish law, and the point of contention in this account.
Teaching Points
The Value of Compassion Over Legalism
Jesus prioritizes compassion and mercy over strict adherence to the law, teaching us to value human need and suffering above ritualistic observance.

Spiritual Liberation
The healing of the woman symbolizes spiritual liberation from the bondage of sin and Satan, reminding believers of the freedom found in Christ.

Identity in Christ
Being a "daughter of Abraham" signifies a covenant relationship with God, encouraging believers to find their identity in their relationship with Christ.

The Purpose of the Sabbath
The Sabbath is intended for rest and restoration, not as a burden. Jesus demonstrates that doing good and bringing healing aligns with the true purpose of the Sabbath.

Confronting Spiritual Oppression
Jesus' authority over Satan's bondage encourages believers to confront spiritual oppression with faith and the power of Christ.

What Does Luke 13:16 Mean
Then should not…

- Jesus frames His response as a gentle yet undeniable “ought.” The logic is rooted in God’s revealed will: mercy is never out of place.

- Cross references: “The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath” (Mark 2:27); “It is lawful to do good on the Sabbath” (Matthew 12:12).

- By asking, He invites the leaders—and us—to see that divine compassion is not optional but obligatory when someone is suffering.

this daughter of Abraham

- Jesus highlights her covenant identity. She belongs to the line to whom God pledged blessing (Genesis 12:1-3).

- Calling her “daughter” elevates her dignity in a society that often minimized women.

- Cross references: When Zacchaeus believes, Jesus declares, “He too is a son of Abraham” (Luke 19:9); Paul later reminds, “Those who have faith are sons of Abraham” (Galatians 3:7).

whom Satan has kept bound

- Scripture presents Satan as a real personal enemy who oppresses both body and soul.

- Jesus never treats sickness and demonic affliction as merely figurative; He confronts them head-on (Acts 10:38).

- Cross references: “The Son of God appeared to destroy the works of the devil” (1 John 3:8); Job 2:6 shows Satan’s limiting permission under God’s sovereignty.

for eighteen long years

- The length underscores the depth of her misery; this was no temporary setback.

- God sees prolonged suffering and still acts at the right time (Psalm 103:13-14).

- Cross references: The paralytic at Bethesda languished thirty-eight years before Jesus spoke the freeing word (John 5:5-9).

be released from her bondage on the Sabbath day?

- The Sabbath commemorated redemption from slavery (Deuteronomy 5:15). Setting people free is therefore perfectly suited to the day.

- Isaiah connected true Sabbath delight with “loosing the chains of injustice” (Isaiah 58:6).

- Jesus embodies that prophecy: “He has sent Me to proclaim liberty to the captives” (Luke 4:18-19).

- The rhetorical question silences legalism: if livestock may be watered (Luke 13:15), how much more may a person be loosed from satanic chains?

summary

Luke 13:16 shows Jesus insisting that mercy is not merely permissible but required, even on the Sabbath. Because this afflicted woman is a covenant heir, and because Satan’s tyranny violates God’s purpose for His people, her release is both righteous and urgent. The passage affirms Christ’s authority over spiritual darkness and highlights the Sabbath as a day to celebrate God’s liberating grace.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Whom Satan hath bound.--The words imply the belief that there was another source than mere bodily disease for the infirmity--in part, at least, the belief that all disease--or very many forms of it--is directly or indirectly traceable to the power of the Enemy. So St. Paul's "thorn in the flesh"--assuming it to be some sharp bodily suffering--is "the messenger of Satan." (See Note on 2Corinthians 12:7.)

It is obvious that this narrative would have for one like St. Luke a special interest over and above that which like narratives had for the other Evangelists. We can scarcely fail to think of the "beloved physician" as practising his art for the good of men, his brothers, on the Sabbath, as on other days. In doing so he would doubtless be met, on the part of Jews and Judaisers, with words like those of the ruler of the synagogue, "There are six days on which men ought to work; do thy work of healing on them." For such a one it would be a comfort unspeakable to be able to point to our Lord's words and acts as sanctioning his own practice.
