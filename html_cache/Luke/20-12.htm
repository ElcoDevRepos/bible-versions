Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then he sent a third
In the parable of the wicked tenants, the owner of the vineyard represents God, and the servants sent to the tenants symbolize the prophets sent to Israel. The sending of a third servant highlights God's persistent and patient attempts to reach His people despite their repeated rejections. This reflects the long-suffering nature of God, who desires repentance and reconciliation. The number three often signifies completeness or finality in biblical terms, suggesting that this was a significant and decisive attempt by God to communicate with His people.

but they wounded him
The act of wounding the servant indicates the increasing hostility and violence of the tenants, representing the escalating rejection and persecution of God's messengers by Israel. This mirrors the historical treatment of prophets like Jeremiah and Zechariah, who faced severe opposition and suffering. The wounding also foreshadows the suffering of Jesus Christ, the ultimate messenger and Son of God, who would be rejected and crucified.

and threw him out
The act of throwing the servant out signifies the complete rejection and dismissal of God's message and authority by the tenants. This reflects the broader rejection of God's covenant and commandments by Israel throughout history. It also prefigures the rejection of Jesus by the religious leaders, who would ultimately cast Him out of Jerusalem to be crucified. This phrase underscores the theme of rejection and the consequences of turning away from God's appointed messengers, culminating in the rejection of Christ Himself.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Owner of the Vineyard
Represents God, who has entrusted His vineyard (Israel) to the tenants (the religious leaders).

2. The Tenants
Symbolize the religious leaders of Israel who were responsible for leading the people but failed to do so faithfully.

3. The Servants
These are the prophets sent by God to call Israel back to righteousness, often rejected and mistreated.

4. The Vineyard
Represents the nation of Israel, God's chosen people, whom He nurtured and expected to bear fruit.

5. The Third Servant
Another prophet sent by God, who, like the others, is mistreated and rejected by the tenants.
Teaching Points
God's Patience and Persistence
God repeatedly sends His messengers, demonstrating His patience and desire for repentance. We should be grateful for God's patience in our lives and respond to His call with obedience.

Rejection of God's Messengers
The rejection of the servants highlights the human tendency to resist God's message. We must examine our hearts to ensure we are receptive to God's Word and His messengers today.

Accountability and Stewardship
The tenants were entrusted with the vineyard but failed in their stewardship. As Christians, we are stewards of God's gifts and must use them to bear fruit for His kingdom.

The Cost of Disobedience
The parable warns of the consequences of rejecting God's messengers. We must heed God's warnings and align our lives with His will to avoid spiritual consequences.

What Does Luke 20:12 Mean
Then he sent a third

• The owner of the vineyard (God) has already sent two servants, each mistreated (Luke 20:10–11).

• Sending a third underscores God’s extraordinary patience and repeated calls to repentance (2 Peter 3:9; Nehemiah 9:30).

• As in Matthew 21:36 and Mark 12:4–5, multiple servants symbolize the long line of prophets sent to Israel (Jeremiah 7:25).

But they wounded him

• The tenants’ violence intensifies: the servant is not merely dismissed but physically harmed (Acts 7:52; 2 Chronicles 24:20–21).

• Wounding reveals hardened hearts—sin progresses when warnings are ignored (Hebrews 3:13).

• Scripture consistently records God’s messengers suffering for truth (Hebrews 11:36–38).

And threw him out

• Expelling the servant pictures outright rejection of God’s authority (Isaiah 5:4–5).

• “Out” hints at being cast outside the community, paralleling prophets driven away (Jeremiah 26:20–23) and foreshadowing Christ crucified “outside the city gate” (Hebrews 13:12).

• The action exposes the tenants’ intent to seize control of what is not theirs (Psalm 2:1–3).

Why the escalation matters

• Each successive servant meets harsher treatment, illustrating increasing rebellion (Romans 2:4–5).

• God’s patience is real yet not limitless; judgment will follow persistent rejection (Luke 20:15–16; Isaiah 65:2).

Refusing the owner’s patience

• God graciously offers chance after chance, but spurning grace invites wrath (Proverbs 29:1).

• The tenants mirror Israel’s leadership, yet the warning extends to all who resist God’s rightful claim (Romans 11:21–22).

Foreshadowing the Son

• The battered third servant sets the stage for the owner’s final move—sending his beloved son (Luke 20:13).

• The son’s coming will reveal hearts and become the decisive test (John 3:19).

• As predicted, the leaders will reject and kill Him (Psalm 118:22; Luke 20:17).

Lessons for today

• Take God’s repeated calls seriously; delaying repentance deepens peril (Hebrews 2:1–3).

• Honor every messenger of God’s Word—mistreating them is opposing the Sender (1 Thessalonians 5:12–13).

• Recognize the stewardship entrusted to us; the “vineyard” belongs to the Lord, not to us (1 Corinthians 4:2).

Summary

Luke 20:12 portrays the vineyard owner dispatching a third servant, only for the tenants to wound and expel him. The verse highlights God’s persistent grace through successive prophets, the escalating hostility of human rebellion, and the inevitable coming of the Son who will expose and judge unbelief. It calls believers to heed God’s Word promptly, respect His messengers, and live as faithful stewards under His rightful authority.
