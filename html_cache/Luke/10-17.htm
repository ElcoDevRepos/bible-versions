The seventy-two returned with joy
This phrase refers to the group of disciples Jesus sent out earlier in the chapter (Luke 10:1). The number seventy-two is significant, as it may symbolize the nations of the world, reflecting the table of nations in Genesis 10. Their return with joy indicates the success of their mission and the fulfillment of Jesus' instructions. This joy is a common theme in the New Testament, often associated with the presence and work of the Holy Spirit (Galatians 5:22).

and said, “Lord,
The use of "Lord" here acknowledges Jesus' authority and divinity. In the cultural and historical context, calling someone "Lord" was a sign of respect and recognition of their higher status. This title for Jesus is consistent throughout the New Testament, affirming His role as the Messiah and the Son of God (Philippians 2:11).

even the demons submit to us
This statement highlights the authority given to the disciples over demonic forces. In the Jewish context, demons were considered evil spirits opposed to God. The disciples' ability to command them demonstrates the power of Jesus' name and the coming of God's kingdom. This authority over demons is a sign of the messianic age prophesied in the Old Testament (Isaiah 61:1).

in Your name.”
The phrase "in Your name" signifies acting under the authority and power of Jesus. In biblical terms, a name represents the essence and authority of a person. The disciples' success in casting out demons underscores the power inherent in Jesus' name, as seen in other scriptures where His name is invoked for healing and miracles (Acts 3:6, Philippians 2:9-10). This reflects the early Christian understanding of Jesus as the source of divine power and authority.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Seventy-Two Disciples
These are the followers of Jesus who were sent out in pairs to every town and place where He was about to go. They were given authority to heal the sick and proclaim the kingdom of God.

2. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, who sent out the seventy-two disciples and empowered them to perform miracles in His name.

3. Demons
Spiritual beings opposed to God, often causing harm or possession in individuals. The disciples report that these beings submit to them when they invoke the name of Jesus.

4. The Return of the Seventy-Two
This event marks the moment when the disciples come back to Jesus, filled with joy over the success of their mission.

5. The Name of Jesus
The authority and power vested in Jesus' name, which the disciples used to command demons.
Teaching Points
Authority in Christ
Believers have authority in Jesus' name to confront spiritual forces. This authority is not of our own making but is granted through our relationship with Christ.

Joy in Ministry
The disciples returned with joy, emphasizing that serving God and witnessing His power brings profound joy and fulfillment.

The Power of Jesus' Name
The name of Jesus is powerful and effective against spiritual opposition. Believers should have confidence in the authority of His name.

Dependence on God
The success of the seventy-two was not due to their own abilities but their reliance on Jesus. This teaches us to depend on God in all aspects of ministry.

Spiritual Warfare
The reality of spiritual warfare is evident, and believers are called to engage with the confidence that comes from being in Christ.

What Does Luke 10:17 Mean
The seventy-two

Jesus “appointed seventy-two others and sent them two by two ahead of Him” (Luke 10:1). The number is literal: seventy-two real disciples empowered for a real mission, showing that gospel ministry is not limited to the Twelve. • Numbers 11:24-25 speaks of seventy elders filled with the Spirit, foreshadowing this broader delegation. • Luke 9:1-2 records a similar commissioning of the Twelve, proving Christ consistently entrusts His authority to obedient followers.

returned with joy

Their first emotion on coming back is exuberant delight. Genuine service for Christ produces deep, holy gladness (Philippians 4:4; 1 Peter 1:8). • Acts 13:52 notes that disciples “were filled with joy and with the Holy Spirit,” highlighting that joy is evidence of the Spirit’s work. • Isaiah 55:12 promises, “You will go out in joy,” a prophecy tasted in these missionaries’ experience.

and said

They report directly to Jesus, underscoring accountability and fellowship with the Master. • Mark 6:30 shows the apostles doing the same: “They reported to Him all they had done and taught.” Ministry is never autonomous; it always circles back to Christ for evaluation and celebration.

Lord

Calling Him “Lord” (Greek kurios) acknowledges His supreme authority. • John 20:28—Thomas exclaims, “My Lord and my God!” • Philippians 2:11 affirms that “every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ is Lord.” Their confession anchors the source of power and honor squarely in Him.

even the demons submit to us

They encountered literal demonic forces that literally obeyed. • Luke 9:1 says Jesus “gave them power and authority over all demons.” • Mark 16:17 predicts, “In My name they will drive out demons.” • 1 John 4:4 reminds believers, “He who is in you is greater than he who is in the world.” The disciples marvel that spiritual darkness cannot withstand the authority vested in them.

in Your name

The key phrase explains everything. Their victories came only by invoking Jesus’ name—His person, authority, and finished work. • Acts 3:6: “In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk!” • Acts 4:10 stresses that healing occurred “by the name of Jesus Christ.” • John 14:13 promises, “Whatever you ask in My name, I will do it.” His name is not a formula but a revelation of His sovereign power. The passage teaches dependence, not self-confidence.

summary

Luke 10:17 shows seventy-two commissioned believers returning to Jesus overwhelmed with joy because, by His authority, even demons obeyed them. Their experience underscores Christ’s willingness to share His power with obedient servants, the unmatched supremacy of His name over spiritual darkness, and the deep, contagious joy that flows from faithful, accountable service to the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) And the seventy returned again with joy.--It is obvious from the immediate sequence of the two facts that the mission of the Seventy was, as stated above, confined within narrow limits of space and time.

Lord, even the devils are subject unto us.--Better, the demons. The tone in which the disciples speak is that of a joyful surprise. They had not looked for such great and immediate results. They had thought that the power to cast out demons had been confined to our Lord's immediate action or to that of the Twelve, and they found that they too possessed the power to rescue the spirits of men from thraldom. With them, as with others, the consciousness of a new power was attended with a new pleasure, in this case, with that of high spiritual exultation.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - And the seventy returned again with joy, saying, Lord, even the devils are subject unto us through thy Name. How wavering and hesitating the faith of the chosen followers of Jesus was, even at this late period of his public ministry, is clear from this frank confession of surprise at their powers. They were contrasting the present with what had lately happened at the foot of the Mount of Transfiguration, where the disciples were utterly unable to heal the possessed boy. What a contrast do these true writers of the gospel story paint between themselves and their Master! They never seem to tire in their self-depreciatory descriptions. They describe with the same careful, truthful pen their slowness to understand what afterwards became so clear to them - their mutual jealousies, their covetous hopes of a brilliant future, their shrinking from pain and suffering, their utter failure when they try to imitate their Master; and now we find them marvelling at their own - to them - unexpected success in their imitation of him.
