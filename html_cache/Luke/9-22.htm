Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The Son of Man must suffer many things,
This phrase identifies Jesus with the title "Son of Man," a term used in Daniel 7:13-14, which refers to a messianic figure endowed with authority and glory. The necessity of suffering is emphasized by the word "must," indicating divine purpose and fulfillment of prophecy, such as Isaiah 53, which describes the suffering servant. This suffering is not incidental but integral to His mission.

He said.
This indicates Jesus is speaking directly to His disciples, providing them with a revelation of His mission. It underscores the importance of His words as a direct teaching moment, preparing them for the events to come.

He must be rejected by the elders, chief priests, and scribes,
The rejection by these groups highlights the opposition from the Jewish religious leadership. The elders, chief priests, and scribes represent the Sanhedrin, the ruling council in Jerusalem. This rejection fulfills prophecies like Psalm 118:22, where the stone the builders rejected becomes the cornerstone. It also reflects the broader theme of Jesus being a stumbling block to those who do not believe.

and He must be killed
This phrase points to the crucifixion, a central event in Christian theology. The necessity of His death is tied to the atonement for sin, as seen in passages like Hebrews 9:22, which states that without the shedding of blood, there is no forgiveness. Jesus' death is not a defeat but a divine plan for redemption.

and on the third day be raised to life.
The resurrection on the third day is a cornerstone of Christian faith, affirming Jesus' victory over death and His divine nature. This event is foreshadowed in Jonah 1:17, where Jonah spends three days in the belly of the fish, and it fulfills Jesus' own predictions, as well as Old Testament typology. The resurrection is the assurance of believers' future resurrection and eternal life, as discussed in 1 Corinthians 15.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Son of Man
This title, used by Jesus, emphasizes His role as the Messiah and His identification with humanity. It is rooted in Daniel 7:13-14, where the "Son of Man" is given authority and glory.

2. Elders, Chief Priests, and Scribes
These groups represent the Jewish religious leadership. The elders were respected leaders, the chief priests were responsible for temple rituals, and the scribes were experts in the Law.

3. Rejection and Suffering
Jesus foretells His rejection by the religious leaders, which is a pivotal event leading to His crucifixion. This rejection fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the suffering servant (Isaiah 53).

4. Death and Resurrection
Jesus predicts His death and resurrection, central events in Christian theology. His resurrection on the third day signifies victory over sin and death.

5. Jerusalem
While not mentioned directly in this verse, Jerusalem is the setting for these events, being the center of Jewish religious life and the location of Jesus' crucifixion and resurrection.
Teaching Points
Understanding Jesus' Mission
Jesus' prediction of His suffering and resurrection is central to understanding His mission. He came not just as a teacher or healer but as the Savior who would conquer sin and death.

The Necessity of the Cross
The use of "must" (Greek: dei) indicates divine necessity. Jesus' suffering and death were not accidents but essential to God's redemptive plan.

The Cost of Discipleship
Jesus' path of suffering sets a pattern for His followers. Christians are called to take up their cross, embracing sacrifice and self-denial in their walk with Christ.

Hope in Resurrection
Jesus' resurrection assures believers of their future resurrection and eternal life. It is a source of hope and encouragement in the face of trials.

Faithfulness Amid Rejection
Jesus' rejection by the religious leaders reminds believers to remain faithful even when facing opposition or misunderstanding from the world.

What Does Luke 9:22 Mean
The Son of Man must suffer many things

Jesus begins by naming Himself “Son of Man,” the Daniel 7 figure destined for everlasting dominion, yet He immediately ties that glory to suffering. The word “must” shows divine necessity, not human chance (Luke 24:26; Acts 2:23).

• Physical anguish – flogging, thorns, nails, thirst (Mark 15:15-20).

• Emotional anguish – betrayal, desertion, mockery (Psalm 69:20; Isaiah 53:3).

• Spiritual anguish – bearing sin’s weight (Hebrews 2:10; Isaiah 53:5).

Mark’s parallel confirms the plan: “He then began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things…” (Mark 8:31).

He must be rejected by the elders, chief priests, and scribes

The whole Sanhedrin—the very leaders meant to shepherd Israel—will spurn Him, fulfilling Psalm 118:22, “The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone”. John echoes it: “He came to His own, and His own did not receive Him” (John 1:11). Luke records the moment: “The council of the elders…both the chief priests and scribes, assembled” (Luke 22:66). Their unified rejection proves that Jesus is Messiah even in their denial.

He must be killed

Prophecy turns fatal: “He was pierced for our transgressions” (Isaiah 53:5). Jesus explains the purpose: “The Son of Man…came…to give His life as a ransom for many” (Mark 10:45).

• Literal death—“He bowed His head and gave up His spirit” (John 19:30).

• Substitutionary death—“Christ also suffered for sins once for all” (1 Peter 3:18).

• Predetermined death—“He was delivered up by God’s set plan” (Acts 2:23).

And on the third day be raised to life

Death is not the end. Jesus promises resurrection, echoing Jonah’s sign (Matthew 12:40). Paul affirms, “He was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures” (1 Corinthians 15:4).

• Historical fact—“He is not here; He has risen!” (Luke 24:6).

• Divine act—“God raised Him…because it was impossible for Him to be held in its clutches” (Acts 2:24).

• Living hope—“Because I live, you will live also” (John 14:19).

summary

Luke 9:22 maps Messiah’s mission: ordained suffering, official rejection, sacrificial death, and victorious resurrection. Each “must” highlights God’s unwavering plan, foretold by the prophets and fulfilled in Jesus. He suffers, dies, and rises so that all who trust Him may share in His life and glory.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - Saying, The Son of man must suffer many things, and be rejected of the elders and chief priests and scribes, and be slain, and be raised the third day. "See how," as Riggenbach, quoted by Godet, says ('Vie de Jesus,' p. 318), "Jesus was obliged, in the very moment of self-revelation, to veil himself, when he had lighted the fire to cover it again." This dark and terrible prediction came upon the disciples evidently as something new. It was their Master's reply to their confession of faith in him. It said in other words, "You are right in your conception of me and my work. I am the promised King Messiah; but this part of my reign will be made up of affliction and mourning and woe. The great council of the people will reject me, and I shall only enter into my grand Messianic kingdom through the gate of suffering and of death. But do you, my own, be of good cheer. Three days after that death I shall rise again." The enumeration of "elders, chief priests, and scribes" is simply a popular way of describing the great council of the Jewish nation, the Sanhedrin, which was composed of these three important and influential sections of the people.
