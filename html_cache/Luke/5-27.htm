Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
After this
This phrase indicates a continuation from previous events. In the context of Luke 5, Jesus had just performed several miracles, including the healing of a paralytic. This sets the stage for His authority and divine mission, which is further demonstrated in His calling of Levi.

Jesus went out
Jesus actively seeks out individuals, demonstrating His initiative in calling people to Himself. This reflects His mission to seek and save the lost, as seen in Luke 19:10.

and saw a tax collector
Tax collectors were often despised in Jewish society for their association with the Roman authorities and their reputation for corruption. This highlights the radical nature of Jesus' ministry, reaching out to those marginalized and considered sinners.

named Levi
Levi, also known as Matthew, is traditionally understood to be the author of the Gospel of Matthew. His calling signifies the transformative power of Jesus' invitation, turning a tax collector into an apostle and evangelist.

sitting at the tax booth
The tax booth was a place where taxes were collected, often located in busy areas like roads or ports. This setting underscores Levi's occupation and the societal disdain for his role, emphasizing the grace extended by Jesus.

“Follow Me,” He told him
This command is a direct call to discipleship, requiring immediate and total commitment. It echoes the calls of other disciples and signifies a break from past life, as seen in the responses of Peter, James, and John earlier in the chapter. Jesus' call to "Follow Me" is a central theme in the Gospels, representing a call to a new life and purpose.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who calls individuals to follow Him and become His disciples.

2. Levi
Also known as Matthew, a tax collector by profession. Tax collectors were often despised by the Jewish people for collaborating with the Roman authorities and for their reputation of being dishonest.

3. Tax Booth
The place where Levi was working when Jesus called him. It symbolizes Levi's old life and profession, which he left behind to follow Jesus.

4. Call to Discipleship
The event where Jesus invites Levi to follow Him, marking a significant transformation in Levi's life.
Teaching Points
The Power of Jesus' Call
Jesus' call to Levi demonstrates His authority and the transformative power of His invitation. When Jesus calls, it demands a response that can change the course of one's life.

Leaving the Old Life Behind
Levi's willingness to leave his tax booth signifies the importance of leaving behind our old ways and sins to follow Christ wholeheartedly.

Inclusivity of Jesus' Ministry
Jesus' choice of a tax collector as a disciple shows that His message and ministry are for everyone, regardless of their past or societal status.

Immediate Obedience
Levi's immediate response to Jesus' call is a model of obedience and faith. When Jesus calls us, our response should be prompt and decisive.

Transformation and Witness
Levi's transformation from a tax collector to a disciple of Christ serves as a powerful witness to others about the change Jesus can bring into a person's life.

What Does Luke 5:27 Mean
After this

• The phrase shows continuity with the healing of the paralytic that has just occurred (Luke 5:17-26), underscoring a rhythm in Jesus’ ministry: miracle, teaching, then movement to the next person in need.

• Jesus never lingers where the Father’s work is already complete; He presses forward, modeling purposeful mission (John 9:4).

• The timing hints that what follows is part of the same divine agenda of forgiveness displayed moments earlier (Mark 2:1-12).

Jesus went out

• Jesus initiates contact; He does not wait for sinners to come find Him (Luke 19:10).

• Stepping outside demonstrates His refusal to remain inside comfortable or religious spaces—He moves toward the margins, just as when He crossed towns to preach in “other places also” (Mark 1:38).

• His going out reminds believers that mission begins with deliberate steps toward people who would not normally enter a synagogue or church (Acts 10:23-24).

Saw a tax collector named Levi

• “Saw” is more than a glance; Christ’s gaze communicates worth to a man everyone else ignores or despises (John 1:48).

• Levi (also called Matthew, see Matthew 9:9) is a publican—viewed as a traitor for collecting Roman taxes—and yet Jesus notices him personally (Luke 18:13-14).

• By naming Levi, Scripture stamps his identity in God’s story, illustrating that grace records former outcasts among the saints (Revelation 21:12-14).

Sitting at the tax booth

• Levi is caught in the very act of his unpopular profession; Jesus meets him where he is, not after self-improvement (Romans 5:8).

• The booth symbolizes material security and social compromise; leaving it will cost Levi financially and socially (Philippians 3:7-8).

• This setting highlights the public nature of discipleship—others will witness the transition from tax gatherer to gospel writer (Mark 2:15-17).

“Follow Me,” He told him

• The call is direct and unconditional: Jesus asks for allegiance, not negotiation (Luke 9:59-62).

• “Follow” involves immediate surrender of career, reputation, and future plans—an echo of the fishermen’s earlier response (Luke 5:11).

• The power of the command rests in the Caller; Levi rises because Christ’s words carry authority (John 10:27).

• Discipleship here is relational—following a Person, not merely adopting principles (1 Peter 2:21).

summary

Luke 5:27 reveals a Savior who purposefully leaves familiar ground, fixes His loving gaze on a socially rejected man, meets him in the midst of sin-stained work, and issues a life-altering summons. The verse teaches that no occupation, stigma, or momentary setting can bar Christ’s call. His authoritative “Follow Me” still reaches into ordinary routines, offering sinners the same transforming grace that turned a tax collector into the Gospel writer Matthew.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 27-29. - The calf of Levi (Matthew the publican), and the feast that followed. Verse 27. - And after these things he went forth, and saw a publican, named Levi, sitting at the receipt of custom: and he said unto him, Follow me. Capernaum, as has been already noticed, had become, owing to its situation, a commercial centre of no small importance. It was on the great highway from the interior of Asia, and from Damascus to the seaboard Mediterranean cities, to Jerusalem, and to Egypt. The custom-house of Capernaum and the office of inland revenue there would naturally be under the control of officials of some importance. The local trade on the lake, too, we know at that period was very large. It has been frequently asked - What specially induced our Lord to select as one of his inner circle a man whose life-work was so hateful and unpopular to the Jewish people generally? why did he include in the twelve one who, from the nature of his detested office, had lost religious caste among the Jews, and who was compelled to consort with sinners, Gentiles, and persons who were considered, either from their birth or life and associations, outside the pale of the chosen people? Various replies to this question have been suggested, such as - by this open act he threw down the gauntlet to all that powerful Pharisee class who were beginning to suspect and to mistake his teaching and liberalism. Or was his apparently strange choice dictated by a simple desire to have, in the inner circle of his devoted friends, a business man - one who could manage the affairs and regulate the economy of the little growing society? but this seems to have been done by Judas; or was it simply done in obedience to a sudden impulse from on High? None of these seems satisfactory. Surely another motive, and that a deeper and a nobler one, suggested this enrolment of the despised publican in that glorious company of apostles. The Lord was determined to show, by this choice of his, that in his eyes all callings were equally honourable, all ways of life might lead to the city of the blessed. Never would the work ennoble the man, but only the way in which the work was done. The Baptist, as we have seen, first taught this Divine liberalism. The Baptist's Lord placed his seal of approval upon his servant's teaching by such acts as the calling of Matthew the publican, and feasting in his house with publicans and sinners.
