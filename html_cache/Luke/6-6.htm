On another Sabbath
The Sabbath was a day of rest and worship, observed from Friday evening to Saturday evening, as commanded in Exodus 20:8-11. It was a central aspect of Jewish life and religious practice. Jesus often performed miracles on the Sabbath, challenging the Pharisees' legalistic interpretations and emphasizing the spirit of the law over the letter (Mark 2:27-28).

Jesus entered the synagogue
Synagogues were local centers for worship, teaching, and community gatherings. They played a crucial role in Jewish religious life, especially after the Babylonian exile when the temple was less accessible. Jesus frequently taught in synagogues, as seen in Luke 4:16-21, where He read from Isaiah and declared His messianic mission.

and was teaching
Teaching was a primary aspect of Jesus' ministry. He taught with authority, often using parables and direct instruction to reveal the truths of God's kingdom (Matthew 7:28-29). His teachings challenged existing religious norms and offered a deeper understanding of God's will.

and a man was there whose right hand was withered
A withered hand would have been a significant disability, affecting the man's ability to work and participate fully in society. The mention of the "right hand" is notable, as the right hand was often associated with strength and skill (Psalm 137:5). This healing would demonstrate Jesus' compassion and power, fulfilling prophecies like Isaiah 35:5-6, which speak of the Messiah bringing restoration and healing.

What Does Luke 6:6 Mean
On another Sabbath

“On another Sabbath” (Luke 6:6) places this event within a rhythm of weekly rest that God instituted at Creation (Genesis 2:2-3) and codified in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:8-11).

• Scripture shows Jesus attending synagogue “as was His custom” (Luke 4:16), underscoring His faithfulness to the Law while revealing its true intent (Matthew 5:17).

• By choosing the Sabbath again (cf. Luke 4:31; 6:1-5; 13:10), Jesus highlights the day’s purpose: restoration and mercy (Isaiah 58:13-14).

• The repetition—“another”—signals that conflict with religious leaders over Sabbath interpretation is ongoing (Mark 2:27-28).

Jesus entered the synagogue and was teaching

• Synagogues functioned as community centers for Scripture reading and exposition (Acts 13:15). Jesus steps into this setting not merely to debate but to reveal God’s heart through teaching (Matthew 7:28-29).

• His presence fulfills the pattern of the Servant who brings justice and instruction (Isaiah 42:1-4).

• Teaching precedes the miracle, emphasizing that transformation begins with truth (John 8:31-32).

• Throughout the Gospels, Jesus couples teaching with compassionate action (Matthew 9:35-36), stressing that doctrine and mercy are inseparable.

a man was there whose right hand was withered

• Luke the physician (Colossians 4:14) notes the specific impairment and even the side—“right hand”—hinting at the man’s inability to work or worship fully (Psalm 137:5-6).

• Physical infirmity often carried social and religious stigma (John 9:1-2). Jesus’ focus on this marginalized man echoes His mission “to proclaim freedom for the prisoners and recovery of sight for the blind” (Luke 4:18-19).

• The withered hand symbolizes spiritual barrenness within a system more concerned with rule-keeping than restoration (Micah 6:6-8).

• By selecting a visible, public ailment, Christ exposes hardened hearts in the synagogue (Mark 3:5) while offering undeniable evidence of His authority (Luke 11:20).

summary

Luke 6:6 sets the stage for a clash between rigid religiosity and the life-giving Lord of the Sabbath. On God’s holy day, Jesus teaches truth that liberates, then targets a man’s crippling need to demonstrate that mercy, not ritual, defines genuine obedience. The verse reminds believers that weekly rest is a divine invitation to encounter Christ, hear His Word, and extend His healing compassion to those overlooked by society.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - And it came to pass also on another sabbath, that he entered into the synagogue and taught: and there was a man whose right hand was withered. This was the second part of his sabbath teaching. The first had taken place in the open country, in one of the corn-fields near the Lake of Gennesaret. The second was given in a synagogue possibly in the city of Capernaum. St. Luke inserts this scene, which may have taken place several weeks after the one above related, because it completes in a way the teaching of the Lord on this important point of the ceremonial law.
