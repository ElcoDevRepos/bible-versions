Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
for he will be great in the sight of the Lord.
This phrase indicates a divine perspective on greatness, contrasting with worldly views. The greatness of John the Baptist, as foretold by the angel Gabriel, is rooted in his role as the forerunner to the Messiah, preparing the way for Jesus (Isaiah 40:3, Malachi 3:1). His greatness is not measured by earthly standards but by his obedience and faithfulness to God's calling. This echoes the biblical theme that true greatness is found in serving God's purposes (Matthew 11:11).

He shall never take wine or strong drink,
This vow reflects the Nazirite lifestyle, a special consecration to God described in Numbers 6:1-21. Nazirites abstained from wine and strong drink as a sign of their dedication. John's abstinence underscores his prophetic role and separation for God's work. This lifestyle choice also connects him to figures like Samson (Judges 13:4-5) and Samuel (1 Samuel 1:11), who were similarly set apart for divine purposes.

and he will be filled with the Holy Spirit even from his mother’s womb.
This unique filling of the Holy Spirit from the womb signifies John's extraordinary role in salvation history. It highlights the divine orchestration of his life and mission, emphasizing the Holy Spirit's work in empowering and guiding God's servants. This pre-birth filling is unparalleled, indicating John's pivotal role in heralding the coming of Christ. It also foreshadows the New Testament theme of the Holy Spirit's indwelling presence in believers (Acts 2:4, Ephesians 5:18).

Persons / Places / Events
1. John the Baptist
The subject of this verse, John is prophesied to be great in the sight of the Lord. He is set apart for a special mission to prepare the way for Jesus Christ.

2. Zechariah and Elizabeth
John's parents, who were righteous before God. Elizabeth was barren, and their account is a testament to God's miraculous intervention.

3. The Angel Gabriel
The messenger who delivers this prophecy to Zechariah, announcing the birth of John the Baptist.

4. The Temple
The place where Zechariah receives the prophecy while performing his priestly duties.

5. The Holy Spirit
The third person of the Trinity, who fills John even from the womb, signifying his unique role and divine mission.
Teaching Points
Divine Purpose and Calling
God has a specific purpose for each of us, just as He did for John the Baptist. Understanding and pursuing our divine calling is crucial for living a life that honors God.

The Role of the Holy Spirit
Being filled with the Holy Spirit is essential for fulfilling God's mission. We should seek the Spirit's guidance and empowerment in our daily lives.

Living a Set-Apart Life
Like John, Christians are called to live lives that are distinct and holy, avoiding anything that might hinder our spiritual walk.

The Importance of Obedience
Zechariah and Elizabeth's obedience to God's commands set the stage for John's life and ministry. Our obedience can have far-reaching impacts on God's kingdom.

God's Sovereignty in Our Lives
God's plan for John was established before his birth, reminding us of God's sovereignty and the assurance that He is in control of our lives.

What Does Luke 1:15 Mean
For he will be great in the sight of the Lord

– Gabriel’s words identify John’s greatness not by human metrics but “in the sight of the Lord.” That shifts the lens from public applause to divine approval (1 Samuel 16:7).

• Jesus later echoes this when He says, “I tell you, among those born of women there is no one greater than John” (Luke 7:28).

• John’s greatness flows from his role: preparing the way for Christ (Luke 1:76; Isaiah 40:3).

• True greatness remains tied to humble obedience; John declares, “He must increase, but I must decrease” (John 3:30).

Application: Pursue God’s commendation above all, trusting that faithfulness—more than fame—defines real success (Matthew 25:21).

He shall never take wine or strong drink

– This lifelong abstinence recalls the Nazirite pattern (Numbers 6:2-3; Judges 13:5).

• The prohibition underlines separation unto God, marking John for a unique prophetic task.

• By avoiding intoxicants, John models clarity and self-control, virtues later commended to all believers: “Do not get drunk on wine... but be filled with the Spirit” (Ephesians 5:18).

• His austere lifestyle (Luke 1:80; Matthew 3:4) underscores singular devotion, contrasting worldly excess.

Application: While Scripture allows moderate use of lawful pleasures (1 Timothy 4:4-5), it calls every disciple to live distinctly, removing hindrances that dull spiritual sensitivity (Hebrews 12:1).

He will be filled with the Holy Spirit even from his mother’s womb

– John’s Spirit-filling begins before birth, highlighting God’s sovereign choice (Jeremiah 1:5; Galatians 1:15).

• Evidence appears when unborn John leaps as Mary greets Elizabeth (Luke 1:41-44).

• Spirit empowerment equips him to “turn many of the sons of Israel back to the Lord their God” (Luke 1:16) and to preach with courage (John 1:29-34).

• This verse affirms the personhood and value of life in the womb, cherished and purposeful in God’s plan (Psalm 139:13-16).

Application: Dependence on the Spirit, not personal charisma, fuels effective ministry (Acts 4:8, 31). Seek continual filling for bold witness and holy living (Romans 15:13).

summary

Luke 1:15 sketches John the Baptist as heaven’s definition of greatness: a man wholly consecrated and Spirit-empowered from the very start. His example urges us to prize God’s approval, embrace a set-apart lifestyle, and rely daily on the Holy Spirit for fruitful service.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) And shall drink neither wine nor strong drink.--The child now promised was to grow up as a Nazarite (Numbers 6:4), and to keep that vow all his life, as the representative of the ascetic, the "separated," form (this is the meaning of the term) of a consecrated life. He was to be what Samson had been (Judges 13:4), and probably Samuel also (1Samuel 1:11), and the house of Jonadab the son of Rechab (Jeremiah 35:6). The close connection between the Nazarite and the prophetic life is seen in Amos 2:11-12. The absence of the lower form of stimulation implied the capacity for the higher enthusiasm which was the gift of God. The same contrast is seen in St. Paul's words, "Be not drunk with wine, but be filled with the Spirit (Ephesians 5:18). . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - Great in the sight of the Lord. To the pious old Jewish priest the strange visitant's words would bear a deep signification. Zacharias would quickly catch the angel's thoughts. His son was not to be the Messiah of the people's hope, but was to be like one of those great ones loved of God, of whom the women of Israel sang on their solemn feast-days - one like Samson, only purer, or Samuel, or the yet greater Elijah. Could all this deep joy be true? Shall drink neither wine. The old curse then as now. God's heroes must be free from even the semblance of temptation. They must stamp their high lives, from the beginning, by the solemn vow of self-denial and abstinence. It is remarkable how many of the great deliverers and teachers of the chosen people were commanded from childhood to enroll themselves among the abstainers from all strong drink. Nor strong drink. The word δεήσις includes all kinds of fermented drink except that made from the grape; it was especially applied to palm wine.
