While they were puzzling over this
The women at the tomb were perplexed because they found it empty. This moment of confusion highlights the unexpected nature of the resurrection, despite Jesus having foretold it (Luke 9:22, 18:33). Their puzzlement reflects a common human reaction to divine mysteries and the fulfillment of prophecy, as seen in Isaiah 55:8-9, where God's ways are higher than human understanding.

suddenly two men in radiant apparel
The sudden appearance of these men emphasizes the divine intervention at this pivotal moment. The description of "radiant apparel" suggests their heavenly origin, aligning with other biblical accounts of angelic appearances, such as in Daniel 10:5-6 and Matthew 28:3. The radiance signifies purity and the glory of God, often associated with divine messengers.

stood beside them
The positioning of the angels beside the women indicates their role as messengers and witnesses to the resurrection. This aligns with the biblical pattern of angels providing guidance and revelation, as seen in Genesis 18:2 and Acts 1:10. Their presence serves to affirm the truth of the resurrection and to provide comfort and clarity to the bewildered women.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Women at the Tomb
These are the women who had come to the tomb early in the morning to anoint Jesus' body with spices. They were followers of Jesus and had witnessed His crucifixion and burial.

2. The Tomb
The place where Jesus was laid after His crucifixion. It was a new tomb, hewn out of rock, belonging to Joseph of Arimathea.

3. The Two Men in Dazzling Apparel
These are angelic beings who appeared to the women at the tomb. Their appearance was striking and served to convey a divine message.
Teaching Points
Divine Intervention
The presence of angels at the tomb highlights God's direct intervention in human history. It reminds us that God is actively involved in the fulfillment of His promises.

The Role of Women in the Resurrection Narrative
The women were the first to witness the empty tomb and hear the message of the resurrection. This underscores the important role women played in Jesus' ministry and the early church.

The Reality of the Resurrection
The empty tomb and the angelic message confirm the truth of Jesus' resurrection, which is the cornerstone of Christian faith. Believers are called to live in the power of the resurrection.

Faith in Unseen Realities
The women were perplexed by the empty tomb, yet they were given a message of hope. This teaches us to trust in God's promises even when circumstances are confusing or unclear.

What Does Luke 24:4 Mean
While they were puzzling over this

• The women had just discovered the empty tomb and the stone rolled away (Luke 24:1–3). Their confusion shows honest human reaction when facts defy expectations.

• Like the disciples on the road to Emmaus who “were discussing and debating” (Luke 24:15), these women needed divine illumination to understand the resurrection.

• Scripture often depicts God’s people in moments of uncertainty just before revelation arrives—consider Daniel as he “pondered the vision” before Gabriel appeared (Daniel 8:15–16).

• This pause underscores that faith does not eliminate questions; it invites God to answer them.

Suddenly two men in radiant apparel

• The word “suddenly” conveys God’s sovereign timing—He acts at the precise moment His people need clarity (Acts 2:2).

• “Two men” parallels other angelic pairings that confirm truth by the testimony of two witnesses (Deuteronomy 19:15; Acts 1:10).

• Their “radiant apparel” points unmistakably to heavenly origin, echoing the dazzling appearance of Jesus at the Transfiguration (Luke 9:29) and the angel who rolled back the stone (Matthew 28:3).

• God’s messengers are never mistaken for ordinary people; their glory highlights the glory of the risen Christ they announce.

Stood beside them

• The angels’ nearness signifies comfort and assurance. When the Lord sends help, it is not distant but right “beside” His children, just as He “stood beside Paul” in Corinth (Acts 18:9–10).

• Their posture—standing—conveys readiness to speak and act, reminiscent of the “Man dressed in linen… standing above the waters” who delivered the final word to Daniel (Daniel 12:6–7).

• The women would soon fall facedown in reverence (Matthew 28:5), yet the angels’ presence first dispels fear, preparing them to receive the joyful announcement: “He is not here; He has risen!” (Luke 24:6).

summary

Luke 24:4 captures the turning point between human perplexity and divine revelation. The women’s honest questions meet God’s timely answer through two angelic witnesses clothed in heavenly radiance and positioned right beside them. In one sentence, Scripture moves us from confusion to confidence, assuring believers that God draws near, provides clear testimony, and replaces doubt with the certainty of Christ’s resurrection.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) Two men stood by them.--St. Mark and St. Matthew mention one only. Had St. Matthew given the two, it might have been urged by adverse critics that this duplication of phenomena, as in the case of the demoniacs (Matthew 8:28), and the blind men at Jericho (Matthew 20:30), was an idiosyncrasy of his. As it is, we must suppose that each set of informants--the two Maries, and the "others" from whom it seems probable that St. Luke's report was derived--described what they themselves had seen. At such moments of terror and astonishment, perception and memory are not always very definite in their reports.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - And it came to pass, as they were much perplexed thereabout, behold, two men stood by them in shining garments. To one company of women one angel appeared: to another, two. Mary Magdalene, a little later, saw two angels in white sitting, as it were keeping watch and ward over the sepulchre for a short time after the sacred form had left it. The words which these beings from another sphere spoke to the mourning women were slightly different, but the teaching was the same in each case: "He is not here, but is risen. Do you not remember what he told you when he was yet with you?" Van Oosterzee and Farrar repeat a beautiful passage from Lessing on this: "Cold discrepancy-mongers, do you not, then, see that the evangelists do not count the angels?... There were not only two angels - there were millions of them. They appeared not always one and the same, not always the same two; sometimes this one appeared, sometimes that; sometimes on this place,- sometimes on that; sometimes alone, sometimes in company; sometimes they said this, sometimes they said that."
