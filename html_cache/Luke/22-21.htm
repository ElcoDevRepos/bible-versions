Look!
This word serves as an imperative, drawing attention to something significant. In biblical narratives, such calls to attention often precede important revelations or events. It emphasizes the gravity of the moment and the need for the disciples to be aware of what is about to unfold.

The hand of My betrayer
The phrase indicates a close relationship, as sharing a meal was a sign of fellowship and trust in ancient cultures. The betrayal by Judas Iscariot fulfills Old Testament prophecies, such as Psalm 41:9, which speaks of betrayal by a close friend. This act of betrayal is pivotal in the Passion narrative, leading to Jesus' arrest and crucifixion.

is with Mine
This highlights the intimate setting of the Last Supper, where Jesus and His disciples are gathered. The shared meal underscores the treachery of Judas's actions, as he is part of the inner circle. The phrase also reflects the fulfillment of Jesus' foreknowledge and the divine plan, as He is aware of the betrayal yet continues to offer grace and fellowship.

on the table.
The table setting is significant, as it is the Passover meal, a time of remembrance and covenant renewal for the Jewish people. This setting connects to the broader biblical theme of covenant, with Jesus instituting the New Covenant through His impending sacrifice. The table symbolizes both community and the impending division caused by Judas's betrayal.

Teaching Points
The Reality of Betrayal
Betrayal can come from those closest to us, as seen in Judas' actions. We must be vigilant and discerning in our relationships, always seeking God's wisdom.

Prophecy Fulfilled
Jesus' betrayal was foretold in the Scriptures, demonstrating God's sovereignty and the reliability of His Word. This should strengthen our faith in God's plan and promises.

The Presence of Sin
Even among the chosen disciples, sin was present. This reminds us of the pervasive nature of sin and the need for constant vigilance and repentance in our own lives.

The Importance of Forgiveness
Jesus knew of Judas' betrayal yet continued to show love and grace. We are called to forgive others, even when they wrong us, following Christ's example.

Self-Examination
Judas' betrayal prompts us to examine our own hearts and motives. Are we truly following Christ, or are there areas where we are betraying Him through our actions or inactions?

What Does Luke 22:21 Mean
Look!

Jesus opens with an urgent, eye-catching command. He wants the disciples—and us—to stop and notice an unexpected, sobering reality right in their midst. Similar wake-up calls appear in Luke 21:29–31 and John 13:19, where He alerts His followers ahead of time so their faith will be strengthened when events unfold exactly as foretold.

The hand

Hands in Scripture often symbolize action, intention, and fellowship.

• Eating together implied trust (cf. Genesis 31:54).

• By pointing to a hand, Jesus highlights a physical, deliberate act already in motion (Mark 14:18).

• Psalm 41:9, written centuries earlier, speaks of a close friend lifting up his heel, underscoring how betrayal can come from intimate quarters.

of My betrayer

Jesus identifies the traitor as “My” betrayer, showing:

• Personal pain—this is someone within His chosen circle (John 6:70-71).

• Fulfillment of prophecy—Acts 1:16 affirms the Holy Spirit foretold Judas’s role through David’s writings.

• Divine foreknowledge—nothing about the cross is accidental (Isaiah 53:12; Luke 22:22).

is with Mine

The betrayer’s hand is literally touching the same table space as Jesus’ hand.

• Proximity: treachery and divine love occupy the same setting (John 13:26).

• Sovereignty: Jesus remains in complete control; even His enemy cannot act outside the plan “delivered up by God’s set purpose and foreknowledge” (Acts 2:23).

• Mercy: Judas receives every opportunity to repent while still seated beside the Savior.

on the table

The table is the Passover table, soon to hold the symbols of the New Covenant (Luke 22:19-20).

• It recalls God’s past deliverance (Exodus 12:8) and points to the greater redemption about to occur.

• The table also pictures future fellowship with Christ (Revelation 3:20), making Judas’s presence there all the more tragic.

• Betrayal at the very site of covenant meal heightens the contrast between Jesus’ faithful love and human faithlessness.

summary

Luke 22:21 spotlights the startling closeness of betrayal to covenant grace. Jesus deliberately calls attention to a hand of treachery sharing His table, fulfilling prophecy, exposing the deceit hidden in plain sight, and displaying His sovereign foreknowledge. The verse reminds believers that God’s redemptive plan never wavers, even when sin operates right beside holiness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21-23) But, behold, the hand of him that betrayeth me . . .--See Notes on Matthew 26:21; Matthew 26:25; Mark 14:18; Mark 14:21; John 13:21; John 13:35. St. Luke's account is here the briefest, St. John's by far the fullest. There is again a slight discrepancy in the order of facts, St. Luke placing the mention of the Betrayal after, St. Matthew and St. Mark before, the institution of the memorial. St. John, who makes no mention of the institution, leaves the question open. On the whole, the order of the first two Gospels seems here the most probable. and agrees better with the fourth. The date before us do not enable us to say with certainty whether Judas partook of the memorial; but, if we follow the first two Gospels, it would seem probable that he did not. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verses 21-23. - The Lord's sorrowful allusion to Judas the traitor. Verse 21. - But, behold, the hand of him that betrayeth me is with me on the table. This is the second mention of the traitor in St. Luke's account of the Last Supper. From St. John's recital, we gather that Jesus returned several times in the course of that solemn evening to this sad topic. That one of his own little inner circle, so closely associated with him, should so basely betray him, was evidently a very bitter drop in the Lord's cup of suffering. In his dread experience of human sorrow it was needful that the Christ should fulfill in his own experience what even the noblest of the children of men - David, for instance - had felt of the falseness of friends. What suffering can be inflicted on a generous heart comparable to it? Surely he of whom it was written, "Whose sorrows are like unto my sorrows?" must make trial of this bitterness. Chrysostom thinks that the Master, in some of these repeated allusions during the "Supper," tried to win Judas over to a better mind.
