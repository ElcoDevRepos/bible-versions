Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But I tell you truthfully
This phrase emphasizes the authority and certainty of Jesus' statement. In the Gospels, Jesus often uses such expressions to underscore the importance and veracity of His teachings. It reflects His role as a prophet and teacher, speaking with divine authority.

that there were many widows in Israel
Widows in ancient Israel were often among the most vulnerable members of society, lacking a husband's protection and provision. The mention of "many widows" highlights the widespread suffering and need during this period. This sets the stage for understanding the significance of God's intervention through Elijah.

in the time of Elijah
Elijah was a prominent prophet in Israel during the 9th century BC, known for his powerful miracles and confrontation with the idolatrous practices of King Ahab and Queen Jezebel. His ministry is recorded in 1 Kings 17-19. Elijah's time was marked by spiritual decline and apostasy in Israel, making his prophetic role crucial.

when the sky was shut for three and a half years
This refers to the drought that Elijah prophesied as a judgment from God due to Israel's idolatry and sin (1 Kings 17:1). The "three and a half years" is a specific period also mentioned in James 5:17, symbolizing a time of divine testing and judgment. The drought was a direct challenge to Baal, the Canaanite storm god, demonstrating the LORD's supremacy.

and great famine swept over all the land
The famine was a natural consequence of the prolonged drought, affecting not only Israel but surrounding regions. This severe famine underscores the seriousness of God's judgment and the dire circumstances faced by the people. It also sets the stage for God's miraculous provision through Elijah, as seen in the story of the widow of Zarephath (1 Kings 17:8-16).

This passage, as a whole, serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and the importance of faithfulness to Him. It also foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles in God's plan, as Elijah was sent to a Gentile widow, prefiguring the broader mission of Jesus.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Judgment
The famine was a direct result of God's judgment on Israel for their unfaithfulness. This reminds us of God's control over nature and history.

Faithfulness in Times of Trial
Elijah's faithfulness during the famine serves as an example for believers to remain steadfast in difficult times, trusting in God's provision.

God's Provision for the Faithful
Despite the widespread famine, God provided for Elijah and the widow of Zarephath, illustrating His care for those who trust in Him.

The Importance of Prayer
Elijah's prayer life was instrumental in the events of his time, teaching us the power and importance of prayer in our own lives.

God's Grace Extends Beyond Israel
The mention of the widow of Zarephath, a Gentile, receiving God's provision highlights God's grace extending beyond the boundaries of Israel, foreshadowing the inclusion of Gentiles in the New Covenant.

What Does Luke 4:25 Mean
But I tell you truthfully

Jesus opens with a solemn assurance, signaling that what follows is not an opinion but an authoritative statement.

• Throughout the Gospels He uses this formula to underscore divine certainty (see John 14:6, “I am the way and the truth and the life,”).

• By prefacing His words this way, He confronts His hometown listeners who doubt His messianic mission, placing them on notice that God’s viewpoint—not local expectations—defines reality.

there were many widows in Israel

A vivid reminder that need was everywhere among God’s covenant people.

• Scripture repeatedly calls Israel to care for widows (Exodus 22:22; Deuteronomy 24:17).

• Yet Jesus’ point is that, despite their number, most were bypassed in the coming miracle.

• This exposes a heart issue: unbelief and hard-heartedness can cause a person—or a nation—to miss God’s intervention (compare Hebrews 3:12).

in the time of Elijah

Jesus anchors His illustration in a well-known historical moment: the prophetic ministry of Elijah.

• 1 Kings 17–18 records Elijah confronting Israel’s idolatry under Ahab and Jezebel.

• By naming Elijah, Jesus reminds listeners that God often sends wake-up calls when His people wander.

• The parallel is clear: Nazareth’s skepticism toward Jesus mirrors Israel’s resistance to Elijah’s call to repentance.

when the sky was shut for three and a half years

A literal drought imposed by God as judgment.

• 1 Kings 17:1: “As surely as the LORD lives… there will be neither dew nor rain in these years except at my word.”

• James 5:17 confirms the duration: “It did not rain on the land for three and a half years.”

• The physical heavens mirrored the spiritual barrenness caused by Baal worship—just as Jesus suggests a spiritual drought in His own generation.

and great famine swept over all the land

The natural consequence of prolonged drought: widespread hunger that touched every household.

• 1 Kings 18:2: “The famine was severe in Samaria.”

• God’s judgment was not random; it was a call to repentance. Likewise, Jesus implies that missing Him will lead to greater loss than any famine (compare Luke 13:3, “Unless you repent, you too will all perish”).

• The severity underscores both God’s holiness and His mercy—He disciplines so that hearts might turn back to Him.

summary

Luke 4:25 shows Jesus using Elijah’s drought to warn His listeners that familiarity with spiritual privilege does not guarantee God’s favor. Many widows lived in Israel, yet God’s miraculous provision went to a foreigner (v. 26). In the same way, Nazareth’s unbelief could shut them out of the blessings Christ brings. The verse calls every generation to humble faith, recognizing that God’s truth, not heritage or proximity, secures His gracious intervention.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 25-27. - But I tell you of a truth, many widows were in Israel in the days of Elias, when the heaven was shut up three years and six months, when great famine was throughout all the land; but unto none of them was Elias sent, save unto Sarepta, a city of Sidon, unto a woman that was a widow. And many lepers were in Israel in the time of Eliseus the prophet; and none of them was cleansed, saving Naaman the Syrian. In support of these assertions, Jesus proceeds to quote two well known incidents in the story of Israel. They must remember God's mercies in past times were not confined to Israel. There were many starving widows among the chosen people, not a few childless, desolate hearths; but their own great Elijah was sent to none of these, but to a despised Phoenician woman in Sarepta, hard by Sidon. Elisha, that loved man of God, who passed by the homes of the people continually, performed his famous miracle of healing on no child of Israel, though many a leper mourned his sad lot among the chosen people; but the one on whom Elisha worked his mighty miracle of mercy was the Syrian leper Naaman, the great foe of Israel.
