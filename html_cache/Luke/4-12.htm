Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But Jesus answered
In this passage, Jesus is responding to the devil during His temptation in the wilderness. This encounter occurs after Jesus has fasted for forty days and nights, a period that mirrors the 40 years the Israelites spent in the desert. Jesus' response is significant as it demonstrates His reliance on Scripture to counter temptation, setting an example for believers. His use of Scripture highlights the importance of knowing and applying God's Word in spiritual battles.

It also says
Jesus refers to the written Word of God, emphasizing the authority and sufficiency of Scripture. By saying "it also says," He acknowledges the comprehensive nature of God's Word, which provides guidance for all aspects of life. This phrase underscores the importance of interpreting Scripture with Scripture, ensuring a balanced understanding of God's commands and promises.

‘Do not put the Lord your God to the test.’
This quote is from Deuteronomy 6:16, where Moses warns the Israelites not to test God as they did at Massah. At Massah, the Israelites doubted God's provision and presence, demanding water and questioning His care for them. Jesus uses this command to reject the devil's challenge to throw Himself from the temple pinnacle, which would have been a presumptuous act demanding a miraculous sign from God. This highlights the principle of trusting God's faithfulness without requiring Him to prove Himself through extraordinary means. It also reflects Jesus' role as the obedient Son, fulfilling the law and succeeding where Israel failed.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is responding to Satan's temptation in the wilderness. He is the Son of God, fully divine and fully human, who came to fulfill God's redemptive plan.

2. Satan
The adversary and tempter, Satan is attempting to lead Jesus into sin by challenging Him to test God's protection and power.

3. Wilderness
The setting of this event, the wilderness is a place of testing and preparation. It is where Jesus fasted for forty days and was tempted by Satan.

4. The Temple
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, the temptation involved the pinnacle of the temple, where Satan suggested Jesus throw Himself down to prove His divinity.

5. Deuteronomy 6:16
The Old Testament scripture Jesus quotes, which originally warned the Israelites not to test God as they did at Massah.
Teaching Points
Trust in God's Sovereignty
Jesus' response to Satan underscores the importance of trusting God's plan and timing without demanding miraculous signs as proof of His care.

Scripture as a Defense
Jesus models the use of Scripture to counter temptation, demonstrating the power and authority of God's Word in spiritual battles.

Avoid Testing God
Testing God reflects a lack of faith and trust. Believers are called to live by faith, trusting in God's promises and character.

Recognize Temptation
Understanding the nature of temptation helps believers to recognize and resist it, following Jesus' example of reliance on Scripture.

Faithful Obedience
Jesus' obedience in the face of temptation is a model for believers to follow, emphasizing the importance of aligning our actions with God's will.

What Does Luke 4:12 Mean
But Jesus answered

- Jesus meets the devil’s challenge (Luke 4:9–11) with a direct verbal response, showing that the proper way to confront temptation is by speaking God’s word aloud, not by engaging in debate or relying on human reasoning (Matthew 4:4; Ephesians 6:17).

- His answer reveals immediate spiritual discernment: He identifies the devil’s twisting of Psalm 91 and refuses to let Scripture be misused (2 Peter 3:16).

- In doing so, Jesus models humble dependence on the Father, contrasting with Israel’s historic pattern of complaint and unbelief (Numbers 14:22).

It also says

- Jesus affirms that Scripture interprets Scripture. One passage (Psalm 91) cannot override another (Deuteronomy 6:16); the whole counsel of God must be held together (Acts 20:27).

- By adding “also,” He teaches us to balance promises with commands, preventing selective obedience (James 1:22–25).

- This keeps our faith from becoming presumptuous: confidence in God’s protection must remain within the bounds of His revealed will (1 John 5:14).

Do not put the Lord your God to the test

- Quoting Deuteronomy 6:16, Jesus points back to Massah, where Israel demanded proof of God’s care (Exodus 17:1–7). Testing God means forcing Him to act on our timetable, questioning His faithfulness.

- The devil urged Jesus to jump from the temple to make the Father prove His promise; Jesus rejects any act that manipulates God for self-exaltation (Philippians 2:6–8).

- Practical implications:

• Trust God’s character without requiring sensational signs (John 20:29).

• Obey first; let confirmations follow in God’s way (Proverbs 3:5–6).

• Recognize that demanding miraculous rescue can mask pride and unbelief (1 Corinthians 10:9).

summary

Luke 4:12 shows Jesus countering Satan’s misuse of Scripture by quoting another passage that forbids testing God. He demonstrates that true faith obeys God’s word in its entirety, trusts His character without manipulation, and resists temptation through humble, Scripture-saturated dependence.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - And Jesus answering said unto him, It is said, Thou shalt not tempt the Lord thy God. It is remarkable that in these crowning instances of temptation, which no doubt were originally recounted by the Lord himself to the inner circle of the disciples, and from them passed into the regular course of instruction adopted by the Christian teachers of the first days, the Redeemer, in each of his three answers to the devil, uses words taken from two chapters (the sixth and eighth) of Deuteronomy. It has been suggested that the thoughts and expressions of this book were fresh in the mind of the tempted Christ, as he had probably, specially during his sojourn in the wilderness, used for his own study and meditation a book which told the story of Israel's wanderings in the desert for forty years. It seems, however, more likely that the Lord simply chose to frame his answers from a book with which every Israelite from his earliest yearn had been acquainted. The maxims and precepts of Deuteronomy were used in the education of every Hebrew child. Its devout and beautiful maxims were written on the phylacteries or frontiers which so many pious Jews were in the habit of wearing.
