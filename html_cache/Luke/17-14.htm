When Jesus saw them
This phrase indicates Jesus' awareness and compassion. In biblical context, Jesus often noticed those whom society overlooked, such as lepers. His ability to see beyond physical appearances to the heart of individuals is a recurring theme in the Gospels. This reflects God's omniscience and care for all people, regardless of their social status or condition.

He said, “Go, show yourselves to the priests.”
In the cultural and historical context of first-century Judaism, lepers were required by Mosaic Law to present themselves to a priest for examination and declaration of cleanliness (Leviticus 14:2-32). This command by Jesus aligns with the law, showing His respect for the Law of Moses. It also demonstrates faith, as the lepers were to act on Jesus' words before seeing any evidence of healing. This act of obedience is a type of faith that is often required in the Christian walk, where believers are called to trust and act on God's promises.

And as they were on their way,
This phrase highlights the importance of obedience and faith in action. The lepers were healed not immediately, but as they obeyed Jesus' command. This suggests a process of healing that is contingent upon faith and obedience. It parallels other biblical narratives where faith is demonstrated through action, such as the healing of Naaman in 2 Kings 5:14.

they were cleansed.
The cleansing of the lepers is both a physical and spiritual metaphor. Leprosy in the Bible often symbolizes sin and its isolating effects. The cleansing signifies not only physical healing but also restoration to community and spiritual renewal. This act of cleansing is a type of the redemptive work of Christ, who cleanses believers from sin and restores them to fellowship with God. The healing also fulfills the messianic prophecy of Isaiah 61:1, where the Messiah is described as one who brings healing and liberation.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is the one who gives the command to the lepers. His authority and compassion are evident in His interaction with them.

2. The Ten Lepers
A group of men suffering from leprosy, a disease that caused them to be outcasts in society. They approach Jesus seeking healing.

3. The Priests
Religious leaders in Jewish society responsible for examining and declaring someone clean or unclean according to the Law of Moses.

4. The Journey to the Priests
The act of faith required by the lepers, as they were healed on their way to the priests, not before.

5. The Cleansing
The miraculous healing that occurs as the lepers obey Jesus' command, demonstrating the power of faith and obedience.
Teaching Points
Faith in Action
The lepers' healing occurred as they acted on Jesus' command. Faith often requires us to take steps of obedience before we see the results.

Obedience to God's Word
The lepers' willingness to follow Jesus' instructions, even when the outcome was not immediately visible, teaches us the importance of obeying God's Word.

Community and Restoration
The act of showing themselves to the priests was not just about physical healing but also about being restored to the community. Our faith journey often involves restoration in relationships and community.

Gratitude and Worship
Although not explicitly in this verse, the subsequent verses show that only one leper returned to thank Jesus. This highlights the importance of gratitude in our relationship with God.

The Role of Religious Leaders
The priests' role in declaring the lepers clean underscores the importance of spiritual authority and community validation in our faith journey.

What Does Luke 17:14 Mean
When Jesus saw them

• Jesus’ first response is not avoidance but notice. He looks directly at ten men ostracized by leprosy (Luke 17:12).

• His gaze consistently precedes compassion—“When the Lord saw her, He was moved with compassion” (Luke 7:13); “He saw a large crowd and felt compassion for them and healed their sick” (Matthew 14:14).

• The Lord still “searches every heart and understands every desire and every thought” (1 Chronicles 28:9), assuring the afflicted that they are seen, not forgotten.

He said

• Healing begins with Jesus’ word. “He sent out His word and healed them” (Psalm 107:20).

• The spoken command carries divine authority just as in “Young man, I say to you, get up!” (Luke 7:14) and “Take up your mat and walk” (John 5:8).

• Faith comes by hearing (Romans 10:17); the miracle starts the moment His voice meets their ears.

Go, show yourselves to the priests.

• Leviticus 14 prescribes priestly inspection as the only official declaration of cleansing from leprosy. Jesus honors the Law He came to fulfill (Matthew 5:17).

• Similar instruction was given to the cleansed leper in Mark 1:44: “Go, show yourself to the priest… as a testimony.”

• Obedience precedes evidence; they must act on His word before seeing its result, echoing Naaman’s direction to wash in the Jordan (2 Kings 5:10).

• The priests who once pronounced these men “unclean” (Leviticus 13:45-46) will now attest to Christ’s power, turning a private cure into a public witness.

And as they were on their way

• The miracle unfolds in motion, illustrating that faith is active: “By faith Abraham obeyed and went, even though he did not know where he was going” (Hebrews 11:8).

• Like the blind man told to “Go, wash in the Pool of Siloam” (John 9:7), their obedience is the channel through which grace flows.

• No delay or debate is recorded; immediate compliance positions them to experience the promised cleansing.

they were cleansed

• The healing is complete and instantaneous—“Immediately the leprosy left him” (Luke 5:13).

• Physical cleansing pictures spiritual reality: “If we walk in the light… the blood of Jesus His Son cleanses us from all sin” (1 John 1:7).

• Ten bodies restored testify that Christ’s power is not diminished by quantity; His grace abounds (Romans 5:20).

summary

Luke 17:14 reveals a Savior who notices the outcast, speaks with authority, honors God’s law, invites faith-filled obedience, and delivers complete cleansing. The lepers’ journey from isolation to restoration turns on trusting Christ’s word enough to walk it out, assuring every believer that obedience to Jesus’ voice is never wasted—healing grace meets us on the way.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) Go shew yourselves unto the priests.--On the meaning and object of this command, see Note on Matthew 8:4. Here, however, it may be noted, there was no accompanying touch as the outward means and pledge of healing, and the command was therefore, in a greater degree than it had been before, a trial and test of faith. It did not necessarily imply a journey to Jerusalem. Any priest in any town was qualified for the function of inspecting and deciding on the completeness of the cure. Suddenly, or by degrees, as they went, the taint of blood disappeared, and their flesh became as it had been in the days of health.
