I know what I will do
This phrase reflects a moment of realization and decision-making. In the context of the parable of the shrewd manager, it highlights the manager's cunning and resourcefulness. The manager's self-awareness and quick thinking are emphasized, which can be seen as a call for believers to be wise and discerning in their own lives. This moment of clarity is crucial, as it sets the stage for the actions that follow.

so that after my removal from management
The manager anticipates his dismissal, which reflects the precarious nature of employment and social status in the ancient world. This phrase underscores the theme of accountability and the consequences of mismanagement. It also serves as a reminder of the transient nature of earthly positions and possessions, echoing biblical teachings on stewardship and the importance of being faithful with what one has been given (Luke 16:10-12).

people will welcome me into their homes.
This phrase indicates the manager's plan to secure his future by ensuring he has a place in the community after losing his job. It highlights the cultural importance of hospitality and social networks in the ancient Near East. The manager's strategy involves creating goodwill and securing relationships, which can be seen as a metaphor for the eternal relationships and treasures that believers are encouraged to cultivate (Matthew 6:19-21). This also reflects the broader biblical theme of using worldly resources to invest in eternal outcomes.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Unjust Steward
A manager accused of wasting his master's possessions, facing imminent dismissal.

2. The Rich Master
The owner of the estate who calls the steward to account for his mismanagement.

3. Debtors
Individuals who owe the master and are approached by the steward to reduce their debts.

4. Homes
Symbolic of future security and relationships the steward seeks to secure through his actions.

5. Dismissal
The impending event that prompts the steward to act shrewdly to secure his future.
Teaching Points
Shrewdness in Stewardship
The steward's actions, though dishonest, demonstrate the importance of being wise and strategic in managing resources.

Planning for the Future
Believers are encouraged to think ahead and make decisions that align with eternal values, not just immediate gains.

Accountability
Just as the steward was called to account, Christians are reminded of their accountability to God for their actions and management of His resources.

Building Relationships
The steward's focus on securing relationships for future security underscores the value of investing in relationships that honor God.

Repentance and Change
The steward's realization and subsequent action can inspire believers to recognize their shortcomings and make necessary changes.

What Does Luke 16:4 Mean
I know

• The steward reaches a moment of clear-eyed awareness, much like the prodigal son who “came to his senses” in Luke 15:17.

• Scripture often highlights such wake-up moments that lead to godly action—Psalm 90:12 urges us to “number our days,” and Romans 13:11 reminds us that “our salvation is nearer now than when we first believed.”

• This phrase shows personal responsibility: he cannot blame circumstances; he must respond rightly (cf. Ezekiel 18:30).

what I will do

• He makes a concrete plan rather than drifting in fear. Proverbs 16:3 points us to committing plans to the LORD, and James 1:22 commands us to be doers, not hearers only.

• Jesus commends prudent foresight in Luke 12:42-43; the steward models that, even if his motives are mixed.

• Initiative reflects wisdom praised in Proverbs 6:6-8 (the ant prepares for the future).

so that

• Every choice aims toward a purpose; here the steward’s goal shapes his actions. Luke 16:9 echoes the same forward-looking mindset: “make friends for yourselves with worldly wealth, so that when it is gone, they will welcome you into eternal dwellings.”

• Galatians 6:7-8 underscores the principle: what we sow now determines what we reap later.

• Ephesians 5:15-16 urges believers to “make the most of every opportunity,” capturing the heart behind this phrase.

after my removal from management

• He knows the clock is ticking; stewardship is temporary. Luke 16:2 already announced, “give an account of your management.”

• This mirrors the universal accountability taught in Romans 14:12 and 2 Corinthians 5:10—each will stand before God.

• 1 Corinthians 4:2 insists, “Now it is required of stewards that they be found faithful,” highlighting the sober reality of termination for unfaithfulness.

• Hebrews 9:27 reminds us that a decisive end (death and judgment) awaits everyone, pressing us toward faithful living now.

people will welcome me into their homes

• In the parable, earthly hospitality pictures eternal reception. Jesus later applies the lesson to eternal dwellings (Luke 16:9).

• Hospitality marks righteous living—Matthew 25:35-40 shows Christ honoring those who welcome others, and Luke 14:12-14 promises blessing to those who invite the poor.

• Acts 4:34-35 displays believers practically opening homes and resources, embodying the kind of receptivity the steward seeks.

• 1 Timothy 6:18-19 connects generous use of wealth with “storing up treasure” for the future, tying present actions to coming reward.

summary

Luke 16:4 captures a steward’s moment of decisive, purposeful planning. He recognizes his impending dismissal, crafts a plan to secure future welcome, and acts before opportunity evaporates. Jesus uses this snapshot to urge believers toward wise, purposeful stewardship of every resource, living today in light of the eternal tomorrow where true “homes” await.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) I am resolved what to do.--More literally, I know, or even, I knew, as of a man to whom a plan occurs suddenly. The dramatic abruptness of the parable leaves us uncertain who "they" are that are to "receive" him. The context that follows immediately supplies the deficiency. What answers to this, in the interpretation, is the moment when a Church or party or an individual teacher, halts between two policies--one that of striving after righteousness, and the other of secular expediency--and makes up its mind to adopt that which promises the most immediate and most profitable results.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - I am resolved what to do. The first part of the parable teaches, then, this great and all-important lesson to men - that they will do well to provide against the day of dismissal from life. The second part points out very vividly how kindness, charity, beneficence, towards those poorer, weaker, more helpless than ourselves is one way, and that a very sure and direct way, cf. so providing against the inevitable dissmission, or death.
