Then Jesus said to the man,
This phrase highlights Jesus' direct interaction with the individual, emphasizing His personal approach to healing and ministry. In the context of Matthew 12, Jesus is in a synagogue, a place of worship and teaching, which underscores the religious and communal setting of this miracle. The man with the withered hand represents those marginalized by physical ailments, often seen as outcasts in Jewish society. Jesus' willingness to address him directly challenges the prevailing cultural norms and demonstrates His compassion and authority.

“Stretch out your hand.”
Jesus' command to the man is significant because it requires an act of faith. The man is asked to do something that seems impossible due to his condition. This mirrors other instances in the Gospels where Jesus requires a demonstration of faith before performing a miracle, such as when He tells the paralytic to take up his mat and walk (Mark 2:11). The act of stretching out his hand symbolizes obedience and trust in Jesus' power to heal.

So he stretched it out,
The man's response to Jesus' command is immediate and obedient, reflecting his faith. This action is a testament to the power of Jesus' words and the man's belief in them. It also serves as a model for believers, illustrating the importance of responding to Jesus' instructions with faith and action. The act of stretching out his hand, despite its previous condition, signifies a step of faith that leads to healing.

and it was restored to full use,
The restoration of the man's hand signifies the completeness of Jesus' healing power. This miracle not only addresses the physical ailment but also restores the man's ability to participate fully in society, as physical deformities often led to social and economic marginalization. The restoration is immediate and complete, showcasing Jesus' divine authority over creation and His ability to make whole what is broken.

just like the other.
This phrase emphasizes the totality and perfection of the healing. The man's hand is not only healed but is made identical to the other, healthy hand. This reflects the biblical theme of restoration and wholeness found throughout Scripture, such as in the prophecies of the coming Messiah who would bring healing and restoration (Isaiah 35:5-6). It also points to the eschatological hope of complete restoration in the new creation, where all things will be made new and perfect.

Teaching Points
Authority of Jesus
Jesus demonstrates His divine authority by healing on the Sabbath, challenging legalistic interpretations of the law.

Compassion Over Legalism
Jesus prioritizes compassion and human need over strict adherence to religious rules, teaching us to do the same.

Faith and Obedience
The man's act of stretching out his hand, despite its condition, exemplifies faith and obedience, which are crucial for experiencing God's power.

Restoration and Wholeness
Jesus' healing brings complete restoration, symbolizing the spiritual wholeness He offers to all who believe.

Sabbath as a Blessing
The Sabbath is intended as a blessing and a time for doing good, not as a burden or restriction.

What Does Matthew 12:13 Mean
Then Jesus said to the man

- In the synagogue on the Sabbath (Matthew 12:9-10), Jesus takes the initiative; compassion moves Him before anyone speaks up.

- The same voice that later says, “Peace, be still” to a storm (Mark 4:39) now addresses one hurting individual.

- Luke 19:10 reminds us that the Son of Man actively seeks out those in need.

Stretch out your hand

- A direct command that requires faith; the man’s hand is useless, yet Jesus calls him to do what he cannot do on his own.

- Parallel moments:

• “Get up, pick up your mat, and go home” (Mark 2:11)

• “Lazarus, come out!” (John 11:43)

- Obedience precedes the visible miracle, echoing Psalm 37:5 “Commit your way to the LORD; trust in Him, and He will act.”

So he stretched it out

- Immediate response—no debating, no delay.

- James 1:22 urges, “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only,” and this man lives it out.

- Hebrews 11 showcases the pattern: faith acts on God’s spoken word.

and it was restored to full use

- Restoration is instant and complete, displaying the Creator’s power (John 1:3).

- Isaiah 35:3-6 foretold that Messiah would strengthen weak hands; Jesus fulfills that prophecy on the spot.

- The physical healing pictures the spiritual healing promised in 1 Peter 2:24, “by His stripes you are healed.”

just like the other

- God’s work lacks nothing (Deuteronomy 32:4). The once-withered hand now matches the healthy one perfectly.

- The thoroughness of the miracle foreshadows the final renewal in Revelation 21:4-5, where every trace of brokenness is erased.

- Mark 3:5 notes that critics could only watch in silence; undeniable wholeness speaks for itself.

summary

Matthew 12:13 shows Jesus taking compassionate initiative, issuing an authoritative command, inviting obedient faith, and delivering total restoration. The episode proves that “the Son of Man is Lord of the Sabbath” (Matthew 12:8) and invites us to trust Him with our own withered places, act on His word, and expect His perfect, complete healing work.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Then saith he to the man.--St. Mark, with his usual vividness, adds the look and gesture and feeling which accompanied the words, "looking round about on them with anger, being grieved at the hardness of their hearts."

It was restored whole--i.e., as the tense implies, in the act of stretching the hand forth. The man's ready obedience to the command, which if he had not believed in the power of Jesus would have seemed an idle mockery, was, ipso facto, a proof that he had "faith to be healed."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Then saith he to the man, Stretch forth thine hand. He is bid use his strength before he is told that it is given. The intellectual difficulties that might have occurred to him lose themselves fir the action. In the somewhat similar ease in Matthew 9:5, 6 there had been the preparation of forgiveness of sins. And he stretched it forth; and it was restored whole, like as the other. Power is linked to obedience. Whole; i.e. sound, in complete health and vigour. The word comes more often in the account of the man healed at the pool of Bethesda than in all the rest of the New Testament.
