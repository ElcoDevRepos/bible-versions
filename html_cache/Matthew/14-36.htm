Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and begged Him
This phrase indicates the desperation and faith of the people seeking Jesus. In the cultural context of the time, begging was a sign of humility and recognition of one's need. The people recognized Jesus as a healer and miracle worker, which is consistent with His growing reputation throughout the region. This act of begging also reflects the acknowledgment of Jesus' authority and power, similar to the faith of the centurion in Matthew 8:5-13.

just to let them touch
Touching was a significant act in Jewish culture, often associated with the transfer of power or blessing. In the Old Testament, touching the Ark of the Covenant or the altar was considered sacred. Here, the act of touching Jesus' garment signifies faith in His divine power. This mirrors the faith of the woman with the issue of blood in Matthew 9:20-22, who believed that touching Jesus' cloak would heal her.

the fringe of His cloak.
The fringe, or tassel, of a cloak was part of the traditional Jewish garment, known as the "tzitzit," commanded in Numbers 15:38-39 as a reminder of God's commandments. By seeking to touch the fringe, the people were not only reaching out for physical healing but also symbolically connecting with the divine law and promises of God. This act underscores Jesus' role as the fulfillment of the Law and the Prophets.

And all who touched Him were healed.
This statement emphasizes the completeness and universality of Jesus' healing power. It reflects the messianic prophecy in Isaiah 53:5, which speaks of healing through the Messiah's suffering. The phrase also highlights the theme of faith leading to healing, a recurring motif in the Gospels. The healing of all who touched Him demonstrates Jesus' compassion and the accessibility of His power to those who believe, regardless of their status or background.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is the one whom the people seek for healing. His presence and power are the focus of the event.

2. The People of Gennesaret
These are the individuals who come to Jesus seeking healing. They demonstrate faith by believing that merely touching His cloak will heal them.

3. Gennesaret
A region on the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee. It is known for its fertile land and is the setting for this miraculous event.

4. The Fringe of His Cloak
This refers to the tassels (tzitzit) on the corners of a Jewish garment, which are commanded in the Law of Moses (Numbers 15:38-39). Touching the fringe symbolizes faith in Jesus' authority and power.

5. Healing
The miraculous event where all who touched Jesus' cloak were healed, demonstrating His divine power and compassion.
Teaching Points
Faith in Action
The people of Gennesaret demonstrate active faith by seeking Jesus and believing in His power to heal. Our faith should also lead us to action, trusting in Jesus' ability to meet our needs.

The Power of Jesus
This passage underscores the divine power of Jesus, who heals all who come to Him. We should recognize and rely on His power in our lives.

Symbolism of the Fringe
The fringe of Jesus' cloak represents His fulfillment of the Law and His authority. Understanding the cultural and religious significance can deepen our appreciation of His ministry.

Compassion of Christ
Jesus' willingness to heal all who came to Him reflects His compassion and love. We are called to emulate His compassion in our interactions with others.

The Role of Community
The people of Gennesaret came together to seek Jesus. Our faith journey is often strengthened in community, where we can support and encourage one another.

What Does Matthew 14:36 Mean
and begged Him

“They sent word into all the surrounding region and brought to Him all who were sick, and begged Him” (Matthew 14:35–36).

• The people recognize Jesus as the promised Messiah who has power over sickness (Isaiah 35:5-6; Matthew 8:16).

• Their begging shows humble dependence, a picture of faith that refuses pride and self-reliance (Luke 18:38-39; James 4:6).

• Jesus receives them; He never scolds those who come in genuine need (John 6:37).

just to let them touch

• Faith doesn’t always require elaborate ceremony—sometimes a single touch is enough (Mark 5:27-28; Luke 8:45-46).

• Asking only for permission underscores confidence in His sufficiency; they believe authority flows from His very person (Matthew 9:29; Acts 3:16).

• The request is simple, yet it stretches their belief beyond what eyes can see (Hebrews 11:1).

the fringe of His cloak

• The fringe (or tassel) reminded Israel of God’s commands (Numbers 15:38-39). Touching it linked their need to God’s covenant promises.

• For Jesus, even this humble edge carried healing virtue because He is Immanuel—God with us (Matthew 1:23; Colossians 2:9).

• Physical contact is not a magic act; it is a tangible expression of trust in the Savior’s authority (Acts 19:11-12).

And all who touched Him were healed

• “All” leaves no exceptions; Christ’s power is complete (Psalm 103:3; Matthew 12:15).

• Healing here is immediate and total, affirming both His compassion and His divine identity (Matthew 11:4-5; John 20:30-31).

• The scene previews the wholeness promised in His future kingdom, where sickness and pain are banished (Revelation 21:4).

summary

Matthew 14:36 portrays desperate people reaching in faith to the only One who can restore them. Their humble plea, a mere touch of Jesus’ garment, meets unlimited grace: everyone is healed. The episode invites us to bring our needs to Christ with the same confident dependence, assured that His power and compassion remain unchanged.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) That they might only touch the hem of his garment.--The wide-spread belief may be noted as the natural result of the miracle already recorded in Matthew 9:20-22, and as the touch implied the faith which was the condition of receptivity, it was now also, as before, effective.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 36. - And besought; and they besought (Revised Version); i.e. the sick, for probably the change of person takes place here and not at" that they might touch." Him that they might only touch the hem of his garment (Matthew 9:20, 21, notes): and as many as touched were made perfectly whole (διεσώθησαν); were made whole (Revised Version). For διά here is probably not intensive, but rather gives the thought of being brought out safe through the danger. In the LXX. διασώζεσθαι is a common rendering of נמלט, "escape."
