Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All things have been entrusted to Me by My Father.
This phrase emphasizes the authority and divine commission given to Jesus by God the Father. In the context of the Gospel of Matthew, this statement underscores Jesus' unique role in God's redemptive plan. The concept of being "entrusted" suggests a transfer of authority and responsibility, which aligns with the biblical theme of Jesus as the mediator between God and humanity. This echoes the Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah, such as in Isaiah 9:6-7, where the government is said to rest on His shoulders. The phrase also reflects the intimate relationship between the Father and the Son, a central theme in the New Testament, particularly in the Gospel of John (John 3:35, John 5:22-23).

No one knows the Son except the Father,
This part of the verse highlights the unique and exclusive relationship between the Father and the Son. The term "knows" in biblical context often implies a deep, intimate understanding rather than mere intellectual knowledge. This exclusivity points to the divine nature of Jesus, affirming His deity and pre-existence, as seen in John 1:1-2. The Father’s unique knowledge of the Son underscores the mystery of the Trinity, where each Person of the Godhead is distinct yet fully God. This relationship is foundational for understanding the nature of Jesus' mission and His teachings.

and no one knows the Father except the Son
Here, the text emphasizes that the Son has a unique and unparalleled understanding of the Father. This statement asserts Jesus' authority to reveal God to humanity, as He is the only one who truly comprehends the Father’s nature and will. This is consistent with the prologue of John's Gospel, where it is stated that the Son, who is in closest relationship with the Father, has made Him known (John 1:18). This exclusive knowledge is a testament to Jesus' divine origin and His role as the ultimate revelation of God.

and those to whom the Son chooses to reveal Him.
This phrase introduces the concept of divine revelation and election. It suggests that knowledge of God is not attained through human effort but is a gift granted by the Son. This aligns with the biblical theme of God's sovereignty in salvation, as seen in Ephesians 1:4-5, where believers are chosen in Christ before the foundation of the world. The idea of Jesus choosing to reveal the Father also speaks to the grace and mercy inherent in the gospel message, where salvation is offered not based on human merit but on divine initiative. This revelation is central to the mission of Jesus, who came to make the Father known and to reconcile humanity to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure in this verse, who speaks of His unique relationship with the Father and His authority to reveal the Father to others.

2. God the Father
The one who has entrusted all things to Jesus, highlighting the intimate and exclusive relationship between the Father and the Son.

3. The Disciples
While not directly mentioned in this verse, they are the primary audience of Jesus' teachings and the ones to whom He reveals the Father.

4. The Jewish Audience
The broader context includes Jesus addressing the people of Israel, who are familiar with the concept of God as Father but are being introduced to the unique Sonship of Jesus.

5. The Revelation Event
The act of Jesus choosing to reveal the Father to others, which is a divine and sovereign act.
Teaching Points
The Sovereignty of Jesus
Jesus has been entrusted with all things by the Father, indicating His divine authority and sovereignty. Believers can trust in His power and control over all aspects of life.

The Unique Relationship
The exclusive knowledge between the Father and the Son underscores the divine nature of Jesus. This relationship is foundational to understanding the Trinity and the nature of God.

Revelation through Jesus
The only way to truly know God the Father is through Jesus Christ. This calls for a personal relationship with Jesus as the means to understand and experience God.

The Gift of Revelation
Jesus chooses to reveal the Father to those He wills. This is a reminder of the grace involved in our understanding of God, prompting gratitude and humility.

Evangelism and Discipleship
As followers of Christ, we are called to share the revelation of God through Jesus with others, participating in His mission to make the Father known.

What Does Matthew 11:27 Mean
All things have been entrusted to Me by My Father

Jesus speaks of total authority handed to Him by the Father.

• This is not partial or temporary; it is comprehensive (“all things”), echoing Matthew 28:18: “All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me.”

• The Father’s delight in the Son is behind this transfer (John 3:35, “The Father loves the Son and has placed all things in His hands”).

• Because everything is in His care, Jesus alone can fulfill every promise of salvation, judgment, and provision (Ephesians 1:22; Colossians 1:17).

• For the believer, this means resting in Christ’s sufficiency—nothing is outside His sovereign reach.

No one knows the Son except the Father

Here Jesus unveils an exclusive, infinite intimacy within the Godhead.

• The Father’s knowledge of the Son is perfect and eternal (John 10:15, “just as the Father knows Me and I know the Father”).

• Human opinions about Jesus fall short unless the Father illumines the heart (Matthew 16:17).

• This truth guards against reducing Christ to a mere teacher; only the Father comprehends the full depth of His divine-human person.

No one knows the Father except the Son

Just as only the Father fully knows the Son, only the Son wholly knows the Father.

• John 1:18 affirms: “No one has ever seen God, but the one and only Son, who is Himself God… has made Him known.”

• The Son possesses unborrowed knowledge of the Father’s character, will, and glory (Hebrews 1:3).

• Any genuine understanding of God’s nature flows through Christ; separate from Him, the Father remains hidden (John 14:6).

And those to whom the Son chooses to reveal Him

Divine self-disclosure now extends to people—yet on Christ’s initiative.

• Revelation is a gift, not human discovery (John 6:37, 44).

• The Spirit applies this revelation to believing hearts (1 Corinthians 2:10).

• Salvation therefore rests on grace: Jesus willingly unveils the Father to the humble and dependent (Matthew 11:25-26; 2 Corinthians 4:6).

• This truth balances urgency in sharing the gospel with confidence that God opens eyes in His timing.

summary

Matthew 11:27 presents a sweeping portrait of the Son’s supremacy and the Father’s gracious purpose. All authority resides in Jesus; only the Father truly comprehends the Son, and only the Son truly comprehends the Father. Yet, in love, the Son sovereignly reveals the Father to those who come to Him. For believers, the verse invites deep assurance—our Savior governs all things and personally ushers us into genuine knowledge of God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) All things are delivered.--Literally, were delivered, as looking back on the moment of the gift. The "all things," though not limited by the context, are shown by it to refer specially to the mysteries of the kingdom implied in the word "reveal." The wider meaning of the words appears more clearly in Matthew 28:18, and in both passages we may trace a formal denial of the claim of the Tempter resting on the assertion that the power and glory of the world had been committed to him (Luke 4:6).

Neither knoweth any man the Father.--The Greek implies full and complete knowledge, and in that sense it was true that no one knew the Son as such in all the ineffable mystery of His being and His work but the Father; that no one fully entered into the Fatherhood of God but He whose relation to Him had been from eternity one of Sonship. To those only who knew God in Christ was the Fatherhood of which Jews and Gentiles had had partial glimpses revealed in all its completeness.

To whomsoever the Son will reveal him.--The Greek gives more than the mere future--is willing to reveal.
