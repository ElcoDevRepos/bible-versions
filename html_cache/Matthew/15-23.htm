Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But Jesus did not answer a word.
This silence from Jesus can be seen as a test of faith, both for the woman and the disciples. In the context of Jewish tradition, silence can be a powerful tool for teaching and reflection. Jesus' lack of immediate response may also highlight the cultural tensions between Jews and Gentiles, as the woman was a Canaanite. This moment echoes other instances in Scripture where God’s silence is a precursor to a greater revelation or miracle, such as in the story of Job or the period of silence before the birth of Christ.

So His disciples came and urged Him,
The disciples' reaction reflects their discomfort and perhaps their own cultural biases. In the Jewish context, interactions with Gentiles were often limited, and the disciples may have been influenced by societal norms. Their urging suggests a desire to maintain social boundaries, yet it also sets the stage for Jesus to teach them about the inclusivity of His mission. This moment is reminiscent of other times when the disciples misunderstood Jesus' intentions, such as when they rebuked those bringing children to Him.

“Send her away,
The disciples' request to send the woman away indicates their lack of understanding of Jesus' mission to all people, not just the Jews. This reflects a common theme in the Gospels where the disciples struggle to grasp the full scope of Jesus' ministry. The phrase can also be seen as a test for the woman, challenging her persistence and faith. It parallels other instances where Jesus initially seems to reject a request, only to later fulfill it in a way that demonstrates His power and compassion.

for she keeps crying out after us.”
The woman's persistence is a key element in this narrative, demonstrating her faith and desperation. Her continual crying out is reminiscent of the persistent widow in Luke 18, who also received what she sought through perseverance. This persistence is a model of faith for believers, showing that earnest and unwavering prayer can lead to divine intervention. The cultural context of a woman publicly pleading with a Jewish rabbi would have been unusual, highlighting her desperation and the breaking of social norms to seek help from Jesus.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
- The central figure in this passage, Jesus is approached by a Canaanite woman seeking healing for her daughter. His initial response is silence, which sets the stage for a deeper lesson on faith and persistence.

2. Canaanite Woman
- A Gentile woman from the region of Tyre and Sidon who approaches Jesus with a desperate plea for her demon-possessed daughter. Her interaction with Jesus highlights themes of faith and perseverance.

3. Disciples
- The followers of Jesus who urge Him to send the woman away, reflecting their initial lack of understanding of Jesus' mission to all people, not just the Jews.

4. Tyre and Sidon
- Regions known for their Gentile populations, emphasizing the cross-cultural nature of this encounter and foreshadowing the inclusion of Gentiles in the Gospel message.

5. Demon-Possessed Daughter
- The afflicted child of the Canaanite woman, whose condition prompts her mother's plea to Jesus, illustrating the power of intercessory prayer and faith.
Teaching Points
The Silence of God
Sometimes, God’s silence is a test of our faith and perseverance. Like the Canaanite woman, we must continue to seek Him even when He seems silent.

Faith Beyond Boundaries
True faith transcends cultural and ethnic boundaries. The Canaanite woman’s faith was recognized by Jesus, showing that God’s grace is available to all who believe.

Intercessory Prayer
The woman’s plea for her daughter teaches us the power and importance of intercessory prayer. We should persistently pray for others, trusting in God’s mercy and power.

Understanding Jesus’ Mission
The disciples’ initial reaction reflects a limited understanding of Jesus’ mission. We are called to embrace the full scope of the Gospel, which is for all people.

Persistence in Faith
The Canaanite woman’s persistence is a model for us. We should not be discouraged by initial setbacks or silence but continue to trust in God’s goodness and timing.

What Does Matthew 15:23 Mean
But Jesus did not answer a word.

• The Lord’s silence is deliberate, not indifferent. Isaiah 53:7 notes, “He opened not His mouth,” showing that purposeful silence can be part of God’s redemptive plan.

• Jesus often waits before acting—John 11:6 records Him lingering two extra days when Lazarus was ill—so that faith can mature and God’s glory can shine more brightly (cf. Psalm 13:1; James 1:3).

• For the Canaanite woman (Matthew 15:22), the quiet moment becomes a faith-stretching pause rather than a rejection.

So His disciples came

• The twelve repeatedly step in as intermediaries (Mark 6:36; Luke 9:12), reminding us that believers can misread situations while thinking they are helping.

• Their approach shows how quickly concern for order can eclipse compassion, revealing a mindset still shaped by cultural boundaries.

And urged Him

• “Urged” carries the flavor of pressing, even pestering. Similar pressure appears in Luke 18:39 when the crowd rebukes the blind beggar for shouting to Jesus.

• The disciples’ impatience contrasts with the Lord’s measured pace, highlighting the difference between human urgency and divine timing (2 Peter 3:9).

"Send her away"

• They want resolution through dismissal, not deliverance. Elisha’s servant once reacted the same way to the grieving Shunammite—“Push her away” (2 Kings 4:27)—yet Elisha uncovered a deeper need.

• The request exposes lingering ethnic prejudice; in their minds a Gentile woman has no rightful claim on Israel’s Messiah, despite earlier lessons like the Roman centurion’s faith (Matthew 8:10).

"For she keeps crying out after us."

• Her persistence mirrors the widow who “kept coming” to the unjust judge until she received justice (Luke 18:1-8).

• Repetition is not rude to God; it is commended—“Keep asking…keep seeking…keep knocking” (Matthew 7:7).

• Mark’s parallel account notes she “kept asking” (Mark 7:26), underscoring that determined faith often precedes divine breakthrough.

summary

Matthew 15:23 portrays a purposeful silence from Jesus that draws deeper faith, disciples who misinterpret the moment, and a Gentile woman whose relentless cries anticipate grace for all nations. The verse reminds believers that God’s delays are not denials, human impatience can obscure divine compassion, and persistent trust pleases the Lord who will soon speak and act.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) He answered her not a word.--Two alternative views present themselves as to our Lord's action in this matter. That which has found favour with nearly all ancient and most modern interpreters assumes that from the first He had purposed to comply with her request, and spoke as He did only to test and manifest her faith. Men have been unwilling to recognise the possibility of a change of purpose in the human nature of our Lord which they, unconsciously heretical, confused with the divine, and have preferred to fall back on the supposition of a simulated harshness. The truer and more reverential course, I venture to think, is to accept the impression which, apart from any a priori theory, the facts seem naturally to make, and to see, in what passed, the prevailing power of prayer working on the sympathy of Christ, and leading Him to pass beyond the ordinary limits of His appointed work. On this assumption, it is our work to trace, with all reverence, the successive stages of the process. And first, even the silence is significant, and implies a conflict. It would have been easy to dismiss her with a word. But the tenderness which He felt towards this sufferer, as towards others, forbade that course, and yet the sense of the normal limitation of His work forbade the other. Silence was the natural outcome of the equilibrium of these conflicting motives.

Send her away; for she crieth after us.--The disciples were clearly unable to enter into either of the two feelings which were thus contending for the mastery. Their words, as interpreted by our Lord's answer, were, in some sense, a plea in favour of the woman. They wished Him to grant what she asked for, and so to dismiss her. And yet we feel that their words were far harsher than their Master's silence. They wanted only to be rid of her presence, which had followed them from the streets into the house, to be freed from the loud eager cries which vexed them.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - Answered her not a word. The woman made no specific request; she had not brought the sufferer with her, and entreated Christ to exorcise the evil influence; she did not urge him to go to her house, and by his gracious presence work a cure. Simply she tells her affliction, and lets the woeful tale plead for itself. But there was no response. The Merciful is obdurate; the Physician withholds his aid; in the face of misery, to the voice of entreaty, the Lord is silent. It is the discipline of love; he acts as though he hears not, that he may bring forth perseverance and faith. Send her away. There is some doubt concerning the feeling of the apostles in thus addressing Christ. Did they wish him to grant her virtual petition or not? On the one hand, it is urged that they were thoroughly annoyed at her importunity. They had sought for quiet' and privacy, and now this woman was bringing a crowd around them, and occasioning the very notoriety which they wished to avoid. Their Jewish prejudices, too, were aroused by this appeal from a Canaanite; they could not endure the idea that favour should be extended to this Gentile of an abhorred race; hence they desire Christ to dismiss her at once, give her a decided rejection. On the other hand, the answer of Christ to their request leads to another explanation, as if he understood them to be asking him to grant her prayer. And this is undoubtedly what they did want, though they did not presume to prescribe the manner or to beg for a miracle. They range themselves on the woman's side, not from any genuine compassion, but from mere selfishness. The ground of their appeal is, She crieth after us. The appeal had been first made in the open street, and the Canaanite had followed them, as they moved, continuing her piteous cry, and thus attracting attention to them and defeating their hope of retirement and rest. So they, for their own peace and comfort, ask Christ to grant the prayer of this obstinate suppliant: "Give her what she wants, and have done with her."
