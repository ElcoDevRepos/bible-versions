Jesus replied
In this passage, Jesus is responding to the Pharisees and scribes who have questioned Him about His disciples not following the traditional handwashing rituals. Jesus often used questions to challenge the religious leaders, highlighting their misunderstandings or misapplications of the law. This method of teaching was common among rabbis, and Jesus, as the ultimate Rabbi, uses it effectively to reveal deeper truths.

And why do you break the command of God
Here, Jesus contrasts the divine commandments with human traditions. The "command of God" refers to the Mosaic Law, which was given to the Israelites as a covenant and is considered the ultimate authority. Jesus emphasizes the importance of adhering to God's commands over human traditions. This reflects the broader biblical theme that God's word is eternal and unchanging, as seen in passages like Isaiah 40:8, which states, "The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God will stand forever."

for the sake of your tradition?
The "tradition" mentioned here refers to the oral traditions and interpretations that had developed around the Mosaic Law. These traditions were meant to help people keep the law but had, over time, become burdensome and sometimes contradictory to the law itself. Jesus criticizes the Pharisees for allowing these traditions to take precedence over God's commands. This critique is consistent with other instances in the Gospels where Jesus challenges the Pharisees, such as in Mark 7:8, where He says, "You have let go of the commands of God and are holding on to human traditions." This highlights the danger of elevating human customs above divine instruction, a theme that resonates throughout the New Testament.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is addressing the Pharisees and scribes, challenging their adherence to human traditions over God's commandments.

2. Pharisees and Scribes
Religious leaders of the time who were known for their strict adherence to Jewish law and traditions. They often clashed with Jesus over interpretations of the law.

3. Tradition vs. Commandment
The event highlights a confrontation between Jesus and the religious leaders regarding the prioritization of human traditions over divine commandments.

4. Jerusalem
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the Pharisees and scribes came from Jerusalem, indicating the significance of this confrontation as they represented the religious authority of the time.

5. Command of God
Refers to the divine laws given by God, which Jesus emphasizes should take precedence over human traditions.
Teaching Points
Prioritizing God's Commandments
Believers are called to prioritize God's commandments over human traditions. This requires discernment and a commitment to Scripture as the ultimate authority.

Heart Over Ritual
True worship and obedience come from the heart, not merely external rituals. Our actions should reflect a genuine relationship with God.

Evaluating Traditions
It is important to evaluate our traditions and practices to ensure they align with biblical teachings and do not contradict God's commands.

Authority of Scripture
Scripture should be the guiding authority in our lives, and we must be cautious of allowing cultural or religious traditions to supersede biblical truth.

Challenging Religious Norms
Like Jesus, we should be willing to challenge religious norms that conflict with God's word, even if it means facing opposition.

What Does Matthew 15:3 Mean
Jesus replied

- The scene opens with the Lord answering Pharisees and scribes who had traveled from Jerusalem to accuse His disciples of ceremonial defilement (Matthew 15:1–2, Mark 7:1–5).

- By stepping into the conversation Himself, Jesus models righteous confrontation (cf. John 2:24–25) and shows that truth must address error head-on (2 Timothy 4:2).

- His reply carries divine authority; He is not offering an opinion but speaking as the incarnate Word who perfectly reveals the Father (John 1:14, Hebrews 1:1–2).

And why do you break

- “Break” underscores an active decision to violate—not a mere oversight (James 2:10, 1 John 3:4).

- Jesus places the burden of proof on the religious leaders: they accuse others, yet they themselves stand guilty before God’s standard (Romans 2:1).

- The question form invites self-examination, echoing the Lord’s pattern of exposing hearts (Luke 6:41-42).

the command of God

- God’s commands are clear, binding, and good (Psalm 19:7-8). Human inventions can never overrule them (Deuteronomy 4:2, Revelation 22:18-19).

- In the immediate context, the command in view is “Honor your father and mother” (Exodus 20:12), which the Pharisees had nullified by their vow system (Matthew 15:4-6, Mark 7:10-13).

- To tamper with a divine command is to rebel against the Lawgiver Himself (Isaiah 33:22, James 4:12).

for the sake of

- This phrase exposes motive: maintaining religious respectability, control, and human approval (John 12:43).

- Whenever obedience to God is sacrificed for any other cause—cultural comfort, personal gain, or institutional preservation—the heart has shifted allegiance (Matthew 6:24, Galatians 1:10).

- Jesus highlights the spiritual danger of elevating any agenda above God’s revealed will (1 Samuel 15:22-23).

your tradition?

- Tradition can be valuable when it transmits truth (2 Thessalonians 2:15), yet deadly when it supplants Scripture (Colossians 2:8).

- The Pharisaic “Corban” tradition sounded devout but actually excused neglect of parents, contradicting the very Law they claimed to honor (Mark 7:11-13).

- Jesus’ rebuke warns every generation: measure all customs—old or new—by the infallible Word (Acts 17:11, Isaiah 8:20).

summary

Matthew 15:3 reveals the Lord’s unwavering priority: God’s command stands supreme over all human tradition. Jesus challenges religious leaders—and every believer—to test motives, refuse man-made loopholes, and submit wholeheartedly to Scripture’s clear authority. When tradition aligns with God’s Word, keep it; when it competes, discard it, honoring the Lord above all.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) By your tradition.--Better, for the sake of your tradition. Our Lord's answer, it will be noted, is an indirect one, an argumentum ad hominem. He shows that their traditional casuistry was in direct opposition to the "commandment" of God, and the natural inference from that antagonism was that in itself, apart from the commandment, it had no binding authority as a rule of life.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - He answered. Christ does not formally defend his disciples, nor condemn the Pharisees for their ceremonial ablutions, but he turns to a matter of more importance, even a plain breach or evasion of a plain commandment. Ye also. If my disciples transgress a tradition of the ancients, ye too transgress, and that the commandment of God - an error of far graver character. His non-observance of these minutiae showed their unimportance, and called attention to the inward purity which they typified, and which could be maintained without these external ceremonies. At the same time, Jesus does not condemn such symbolical acts, even as he himself washed the disciples' feet before the last Supper. The evil in rabbinical teachings was that it superseded the spiritual view, and placed outward cleansing on a higher level than inward holiness. By (διὰ with accusative); on account of, in order to maintain. Your tradition. Tradition which is emphatically yours and not God's, a human gloss, not a revealed command. Jesus does not accept the assertion that these traditions are derived from the ancients; he gives them a more modern origin.
