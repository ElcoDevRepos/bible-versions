Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The crowd admonished them to be silent
In the context of Matthew 20, Jesus is on His way to Jerusalem, and a large crowd follows Him. The crowd's attempt to silence the blind men reflects a common cultural attitude of the time, where those with disabilities were often marginalized and seen as less deserving of attention. This moment highlights the societal barriers that Jesus frequently challenged. The crowd's reaction can also be seen as a misunderstanding of Jesus' mission, as they fail to recognize the importance of compassion and mercy that He embodies.

but they cried out all the louder
The persistence of the blind men is significant. Despite societal pressure to remain silent, their desperation and faith drive them to continue seeking Jesus' attention. This persistence is a recurring theme in the Gospels, where those who earnestly seek Jesus often overcome obstacles to receive His help. Their actions demonstrate a deep faith and recognition of Jesus' authority and ability to heal, which is a key aspect of His ministry.

“Lord, Son of David, have mercy on us!”
The title "Son of David" is a messianic term, acknowledging Jesus as the promised descendant of David who would establish God's kingdom. This recognition by the blind men is profound, as it shows their spiritual insight despite their physical blindness. The plea for mercy is a common biblical theme, reflecting a humble acknowledgment of one's need for God's grace. This cry for mercy is echoed in other parts of Scripture, such as the Psalms, where individuals seek God's compassion and intervention. The use of "Lord" signifies their recognition of Jesus' divine authority, further emphasizing their faith in Him as the Messiah.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Crowd
A group of people following Jesus, likely consisting of His disciples, followers, and onlookers. They play a significant role in the account by attempting to silence the blind men.

2. The Blind Men
Two individuals who are physically blind but spiritually perceptive, recognizing Jesus as the "Son of David" and seeking His mercy.

3. Jesus
Central to the event, He is addressed as "Lord" and "Son of David," acknowledging His messianic role and authority.

4. Jericho
The location near which this event occurs, a city with historical and spiritual significance in Jewish history.

5. The Roadside
The setting where the blind men are positioned, symbolizing their marginalization and need for divine intervention.
Teaching Points
Persistence in Faith
The blind men exemplify unwavering faith by continuing to cry out despite opposition. Believers are encouraged to persist in prayer and faith, even when faced with discouragement.

Recognizing Jesus' Authority
By calling Jesus "Son of David," the blind men acknowledge His messianic identity. Christians are reminded to recognize and submit to Jesus' authority in their lives.

Compassion Over Conformity
Jesus' response to the blind men contrasts with the crowd's attempt to silence them. Believers are called to prioritize compassion and mercy over societal pressures or norms.

The Power of a Desperate Cry
The blind men's desperate plea for mercy is heard and answered by Jesus. This teaches that God is attentive to the cries of those who earnestly seek Him.

Faith Beyond Sight
The physical blindness of the men contrasts with their spiritual insight. Christians are encouraged to develop spiritual vision and trust in God's promises, even when circumstances seem bleak.

What Does Matthew 20:31 Mean
The crowd admonished them to be silent

- The onlookers treated the blind men as an interruption, echoing earlier moments where people tried to manage who could approach Jesus (Matthew 19:13-14).

- This resistance highlights a recurring tension: human gatekeeping versus Christ’s open invitation. See also Mark 10:48 and Luke 18:39, the parallel accounts that confirm the historicity of this scene.

- When public opinion pressures the needy into silence, Scripture calls believers to remember that “the LORD is near to the brokenhearted” (Psalm 34:18) and that no one seeking Him is turned away (John 6:37).

but they cried out all the louder

- The verb tense points to persistent, escalating pleas. Physical blindness did not dull spiritual sight; faith intensified under opposition.

- Similar holy defiance appears when the apostles, ordered to stop preaching, replied, “We cannot stop speaking about what we have seen and heard” (Acts 4:18-20).

- Genuine faith refuses to be muted. Hebrews 11 showcases others who pressed on despite obstacles, while Philippians 3:14 urges believers to “press on toward the goal.”

- Their volume was not desperation alone; it was confidence that Jesus truly hears (Psalm 18:6).

“Lord, Son of David, have mercy on us!”

- “Lord” acknowledges Jesus’ authority; “Son of David” declares Him the promised Messiah (2 Samuel 7:12-16; Isaiah 11:1-5; Jeremiah 23:5-6). The blind men saw, by faith, what many sighted people missed.

- Their plea for mercy mirrors classic biblical prayers: “Have mercy on me, O God” (Psalm 51:1) and “Show us Your mercy, O LORD” (Psalm 123:3).

- Mercy, not merit, is their basis. Titus 3:5 affirms we are saved “not by works… but because of His mercy.”

- Jesus immediately responds (Matthew 20:34), fulfilling Isaiah 35:5—“Then the eyes of the blind will be opened.” The miracle validates both their confession and His messianic identity.

summary

Matthew 20:31 records a literal event that displays the clash between crowd control and persistent faith. The blind men ignored silencing voices, amplifying their cry to the rightful King. By calling Jesus “Lord, Son of David,” they affirmed His messiahship and leaned wholly on His mercy. Their bold persistence invites believers to bypass human barriers, trust Christ’s compassionate authority, and approach Him with confident, humble pleas for mercy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) The multitude rebuked them.--The silence of our Lord, the hushed reverence of the multitude, led men to look on the eager, clamorous supplication as intrusive. The entry of the Prophet about to claim His kingdom was not to be thus disturbed. But they were not to be silenced, and the litanies of Christendom for centuries have been modelled on the Kyrie Ele‹son ("Lord, have mercy upon us") which came from their lips.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - Rebuked them, because (ἵνα, in order that) they should hold their peace. The motive of the crowd, in thus silencing the blind men, has been explained in two ways - either they grudged that Christ should be addressed by the high title of "Son of David;" or they desired to spare him unseemly importunity and unreasonable interruption in his journey. As the multitude show no signs of hostility at this time, the latter suggestion seems most probable. They cried the more. The attempted check only made them more earnest in their entreaty. The opportunity now offered might never present itself again. The officious interference of unsympathizing bystanders was at once brushed aside. They could attract Christ's attention only by their passionate cry, and this they continued to utter with renewed energy. Faith resists opposition and triumphs over all impediments.
