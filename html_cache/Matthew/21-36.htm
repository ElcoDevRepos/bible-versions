Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Again, he sent other servants, more than the first group.
This phrase indicates the persistence and patience of the landowner, symbolizing God's continued outreach to His people. The sending of more servants represents the prophets sent by God throughout Israel's history, as seen in the Old Testament. Prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel were sent to call Israel back to faithfulness. The increase in the number of servants highlights God's abundant grace and mercy, despite Israel's repeated disobedience. This action reflects God's desire for repentance and reconciliation, as seen in 2 Chronicles 36:15-16, where God persistently sent messengers to His people.

But the tenants did the same to them.
The tenants' repeated mistreatment of the servants symbolizes Israel's historical rejection and persecution of the prophets. This behavior is a reflection of the hard-heartedness and rebellion of the people, as seen in passages like Nehemiah 9:26 and Acts 7:52, where the prophets were often met with hostility. The tenants' actions foreshadow the ultimate rejection of Jesus Christ, the Son, by the religious leaders of His time. This rejection is a fulfillment of the prophecy found in Isaiah 53:3, where the Messiah is described as being despised and rejected by men. The tenants' consistent behavior underscores the theme of human resistance to divine authority and the tragic consequences of rejecting God's messengers.

Teaching Points
God's Patience and Persistence
God continually reaches out to His people, even when they reject Him. This teaches us about His enduring patience and love.

Rejection of God's Messengers
The parable warns against the rejection of those who bring God's message. We should be open to hearing and heeding God's word.

Accountability and Stewardship
The tenants were entrusted with the vineyard but failed in their responsibility. We are called to be faithful stewards of what God has given us.

The Consequences of Rejection
The parable foreshadows the judgment that comes from rejecting God's messengers and ultimately His Son.

The Call to Produce Fruit
Just as the tenants were expected to produce fruit, we are called to bear spiritual fruit in our lives.

What Does Matthew 21:36 Mean
Again

“Again He sent other servants...” (Matthew 21:36)

• The word “again” shows the literal, historical patience of the landowner, picturing God’s persistent mercy toward His covenant people (2 Peter 3:9; Psalm 86:15).

• God does not give up after one rejected invitation; He repeats His call, reflecting the steadfast love that “never ceases” (Lamentations 3:22-23).

• Israel’s history records cycle after cycle of rebellion answered by renewed divine outreach (Judges 2:16-18).

He sent other servants

• The “servants” are the prophets—real men sent at specific moments to speak God’s word (Jeremiah 7:25-26; 2 Kings 17:13).

• Each prophet carried the same essential message: repent, return, bear fruit (Isaiah 1:18; Hosea 14:1-2).

• Their presence underscores that the Owner’s rights over His vineyard are unquestionable and that His appeal is always delivered through human messengers (Hebrews 1:1).

More than the first group

• The increase in number magnifies God’s grace. Instead of fewer warnings, He multiplies them (2 Chronicles 36:15-16).

• It also heightens accountability. With every added prophet, the tenants’ responsibility grows (Luke 12:48).

• Scripture lists this expanding line: Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, the twelve minor prophets, John the Baptist—each confirming God’s unwavering call (Malachi 4:5-6).

But the tenants did the same to them

• The tenants’ repeated violence reveals hardened hearts (Zechariah 7:11-12).

• Jesus confirms the literal pattern: “O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, you who kill the prophets” (Matthew 23:37; cf. Acts 7:52).

• Their refusal exposes the tragic irony: those entrusted with God’s vineyard resist the very servants sent to bless them (Nehemiah 9:26).

• Repetition of sin forms a trajectory that will culminate in the ultimate rejection of the Son (Matthew 21:37-39).

summary

Matthew 21:36 depicts God’s relentless, historical pursuit of His people through an ever-growing chorus of prophets, contrasted with the tenants’ equally persistent rejection. The verse highlights divine patience, human responsibility, and the escalating conflict that will soon center on God’s own Son.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) Other servants more than the first.--There is, perhaps, a reference here to the greater power and fulness of the work of the later prophets, such as Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel, stretching onward to that of the Baptist, as closing the whole line.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 36. - Other servants. God's loving kindness was not wearied out with the husbandmen's cruelty and violence. Each step of their wickedness and obstinacy was met with renewed mercy, with fresh calls to repentance. More (πλείονας). More in number. In the latter days the number of God's messengers was much greater than in earlier times; so it is unnecessary to take πλείονας in the sense of "more honourable," "of higher dignity," though such interpretation is supported by its use in Matthew 6:25; Mark 12:33; Hebrews 11:4. Likewise. They resisted these new envoys as they had resisted these first sent, treating them with equal cruelty and violence.
