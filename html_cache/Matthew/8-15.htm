So He touched her hand
In this passage, Jesus demonstrates His compassion and willingness to heal. The act of touching is significant, as it shows Jesus' personal involvement and care. In Jewish culture, touching someone with a fever could render a person ceremonially unclean, yet Jesus transcends these cultural norms to bring healing. This touch signifies the power and authority of Jesus over sickness, reflecting His divine nature. It also prefigures the way Jesus would later touch the lives of many through His ministry, showing that He is not distant but intimately involved with humanity.

and the fever left her
The immediate healing of Peter's mother-in-law illustrates Jesus' authority over physical ailments. Fever, often seen as a symptom of a more serious illness, was a common and sometimes deadly condition in the ancient world. The instantaneous nature of the healing underscores the miraculous power of Jesus, affirming His identity as the Messiah. This act fulfills the prophecy of Isaiah 53:4, which speaks of the Messiah bearing our sicknesses. It also serves as a testament to the Kingdom of God breaking into the world through Jesus' ministry, where sickness and suffering are overcome.

and she got up and began to serve Him
The response of Peter's mother-in-law is immediate and practical. Her service is a natural reaction to the healing she received, demonstrating gratitude and recognition of Jesus' authority. In the cultural context, hospitality was a significant virtue, and her actions reflect the proper response to divine intervention. This service can be seen as a model for discipleship, where those who have experienced Jesus' transformative power respond by serving Him and others. It also highlights the restoration not just to health but to community and purpose, as she resumes her role in the household. This mirrors the call for believers to serve Christ in response to His work in their lives.

Teaching Points
The Power of Jesus' Touch
Jesus' touch signifies His willingness to engage personally with our suffering. His touch brings healing and restoration, reminding us of His power and compassion.

Immediate Response to Healing
Peter's mother-in-law's immediate service after being healed is a model for us. When we experience God's work in our lives, our response should be one of gratitude and service.

The Role of Service in the Christian Life
Service is a natural response to God's grace. As believers, we are called to serve others, reflecting Christ's love and humility in our actions.

Faith and Action
This passage encourages us to have faith in Jesus' power to heal and transform. Our faith should lead to action, as seen in the service of Peter's mother-in-law.

Community and Healing
The setting in Peter's house highlights the importance of community in experiencing and witnessing God's work. We are encouraged to share our testimonies within our communities.

What Does Matthew 8:15 Mean
So He touched her hand

• Notice the gentle, personal nature of Christ’s action. Jesus does not need elaborate rituals; a simple touch communicates both authority and compassion (cf. Matthew 9:29; Mark 1:41).

• His willingness to enter a private home and physically reach out proclaims that no setting is too ordinary for His power (Luke 19:5).

• The same hand that upholds the universe (Colossians 1:17) rests on one fevered woman—highlighting the intimacy of divine care.

and the fever left her

• The departure of the fever is instantaneous and total, underscoring Jesus’ absolute dominion over sickness (Matthew 4:24; Luke 4:40).

• No recovery period or medicine is mentioned, paralleling other immediate healings such as the paralytic (Mark 2:12) and the leper (Luke 5:13).

• This moment verifies that physical illness is fully subject to the Creator’s word and touch, affirming Psalm 103:3—“He heals all your diseases.”

and she got up

• Her immediate strength reveals that the healing is complete; weakness or fatigue do not linger (cf. Acts 3:7–8).

• Rising at once shows faith in action—she believes she is well and behaves accordingly (John 5:9).

• The quick restoration hints at resurrection power, foreshadowing scenes like Jairus’s daughter rising (Luke 8:54–55).

and began to serve Him

• Service is the natural response to salvation and healing (Romans 12:1).

• She serves “Him,” not merely the household, illustrating personal gratitude to the Savior (John 12:2).

• Her ministry begins immediately, modeling that no believer is too newly blessed to serve (Galatians 5:13).

• In meeting practical needs—likely preparing food—she participates in kingdom work just as surely as the Twelve who preach (Matthew 10:7–8).

summary

Matthew 8:15 records a real event demonstrating Jesus’ tender touch, sovereign authority, and power to restore completely. Physical healing leads to immediate, grateful service—an enduring pattern for every believer touched by Christ’s redeeming hand.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) She arose, and ministered unto them.--The fact is stated as showing the completeness of the work of healing. The "great fever" had not left behind it its usual sequel of weakness and exhaustion.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - And he touched her hand. Perhaps with her, as with the leper (ver. 3), the word alone would not have been enough. In both cases the faith seems to have been below that of the centurion. And the fever left her: and she arose, and ministered unto them; Revised Version. him, with manuscripts. Serving them all (parallel passages), and him in particular. If it were her own house this would be doubly natural (cf Luke 10:40). The change of tense (aorist to imperfect) contrasts the single act of arising from her bed and her continued ministry at the meal (cf. Matthew 4:11).
