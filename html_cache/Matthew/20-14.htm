Take your pay and go.
This phrase is part of the parable of the workers in the vineyard, where a landowner hires laborers at different times of the day but pays them all the same wage. The instruction to "take your pay and go" reflects the landowner's authority and fairness in fulfilling his agreement. In biblical times, daily wages were common, and workers were paid at the end of each day (Leviticus 19:13). This reflects the immediate provision for daily needs, as seen in the Lord's Prayer, "Give us this day our daily bread" (Matthew 6:11). The command to "go" signifies the completion of the agreed-upon work and the fulfillment of the contract, emphasizing the landowner's right to distribute his resources as he sees fit.

I want to give this last man the same as I gave you.
This statement highlights the landowner's generosity and the theme of divine grace. The "last man" refers to those hired later in the day, who worked fewer hours but received the same wage. This challenges human notions of fairness and merit, illustrating God's sovereign grace and generosity. The parable reflects the kingdom of heaven, where God's grace is extended equally to all, regardless of their time of service or perceived worthiness. This mirrors the teaching in Ephesians 2:8-9, where salvation is described as a gift from God, not based on works. The landowner's desire to give equally underscores the principle that God's ways are higher than human ways (Isaiah 55:8-9), and His grace is not limited by human standards of justice.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Grace
God, like the landowner, has the right to dispense His grace as He sees fit. We must trust in His wisdom and fairness.

Equality in the Kingdom
The parable teaches that all believers, regardless of when they come to faith, receive the same reward of eternal life. This challenges our human notions of fairness and merit.

Contentment with God's Provision
We are called to be content with what God provides, trusting that He knows what is best for us.

Avoiding Comparison
Comparing our spiritual journey or rewards with others can lead to envy and dissatisfaction. Instead, focus on our personal relationship with God.

The Generosity of God
God's generosity is beyond human understanding. He gives abundantly and equally, reflecting His love and grace for all.

What Does Matthew 20:14 Mean
Take your pay

“Take your pay” shows the vineyard owner honoring the agreement (Matthew 20:2). God’s character is the same—He always keeps His word (Numbers 23:19, “God is not a man, that He should lie…”).

• A denarius for every worker highlights that eternal life is a settled gift, not a sliding scale of merit (Romans 6:23, “the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord”).

• No one receives less than promised (1 Corinthians 3:8); believers can trust that every promise in Christ is “Yes” and “Amen” (2 Corinthians 1:20).

• The owner pays first, then addresses complaints, reminding us that God’s faithfulness is never contingent on human approval (Deuteronomy 7:9).

and go

Having been paid, the early workers are told to “go.” The service day is finished; lingering only fuels discontent.

• God releases us from performance anxiety—our part is done, the reward secure (Luke 17:10, servants simply “have done our duty”).

• Grumbling stalls forward movement (Philippians 2:14, “Do everything without complaining”).

• The elder brother in Luke 15:28 refused to enter the celebration; similarly, comparison keeps believers outside the joy God intends (John 15:11).

• “Go” also signals continuing mission: once grace is received, we step out to share it (Matthew 28:19).

I want to give this last man the same as I gave you

Here the owner asserts both his right and his generosity.

• God’s grace is sovereign: “I will have mercy on whom I have mercy” (Romans 9:15).

• Length of service never earns greater salvation; the thief on the cross received the same paradise promised to lifelong disciples (Luke 23:43).

• Peter learned that God shows no partiality (Acts 10:34–35); Paul declared that Jews and Gentiles are “fellow heirs” (Ephesians 3:6).

• The statement answers the human cry for fairness with a higher principle—divine generosity. Salvation is “not by works, so that no one can boast” (Ephesians 2:9).

• The first-last reversal earlier in the parable (Matthew 19:30) cautions believers against measuring worth by tenure or visibility of service (1 Samuel 16:7).

summary

Matthew 20:14 underscores that God faithfully gives what He promises, sends His children forward instead of letting them stew in comparison, and delights to grant equal grace to all who come—whether early or late. Our calling is to receive the gift with gratitude, rejoice in others’ blessing, and keep moving in humble trust that the Master’s generosity is perfect justice.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) Take that thine is, and go thy way.--The tone of dismissal is natural and intelligible in the parable. The question, What answers to it in God's dealings with men? is not so easy to answer. If the "penny" which each received was the gift of eternal life, did those who answered to the murmuring labourers receive that, or were they excluded by their discontent from all share in it? Was the money which they received as "fairy-gold" that turned to a withered leaf in the hands of its thankless possessor? The answer is, perhaps, to be found in the thought that that reward lies in the presence of God to the soul of the disciple, and that this depends for its blessedness on the harmony between the character of the believer and the mind of God. Heaven is not a place, but a state, its happiness is not sensual but spiritual, and those who are in it share its blessedness in proportion as they are like God and see Him as He is. It is only perfect when their charity is like His.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Take that thine is; thine own. Take your agreed wages, and go; there is nothing more to be said. I will (θέλω δέ) give; but it is my will to give. The lord defends his conduct on the ground that such is his will and pleasure. By it he injures nobody, he benefits many; who should presume to censure him?
