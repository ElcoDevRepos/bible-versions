Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the King will answer
In this passage, "the King" refers to Jesus Christ, who is depicted as the ultimate judge at the end of times. This imagery aligns with the prophetic vision in Daniel 7:13-14, where the "Son of Man" is given authority and sovereign power. The title "King" emphasizes His divine authority and role in the final judgment, a theme consistent throughout the New Testament, particularly in Revelation 19:16, where Jesus is called "King of Kings."

‘Truly I tell you
This phrase is a solemn declaration used by Jesus to emphasize the truth and importance of His words. It is a common expression in the Gospels, often introducing significant teachings or revelations. The use of "truly" (Greek: "amen") underscores the certainty and reliability of what follows, reflecting Jesus' authority as a teacher and prophet.

whatever you did not do for one of the least of these
"The least of these" refers to those who are marginalized, vulnerable, or in need. This phrase highlights the biblical principle of caring for the poor and oppressed, as seen in passages like Proverbs 19:17 and Isaiah 58:6-7. The cultural context of the time placed a strong emphasis on hospitality and charity, especially towards those who could not repay. This teaching challenges believers to see Christ in every person, particularly those who are overlooked by society.

you did not do for Me.’
This statement identifies Jesus with the needy and suffering, illustrating the profound connection between acts of service to others and service to Christ Himself. It echoes the teaching found in Proverbs 14:31, which equates honoring the poor with honoring God. This identification with the "least" is a type of Christ's own humility and incarnation, as described in Philippians 2:5-8, where Jesus took on the form of a servant. The passage calls believers to a life of active compassion, reflecting the love and mercy of Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The speaker of this verse, Jesus is delivering a parable about the final judgment, emphasizing the importance of serving others as a reflection of serving Him.

2. The Least of These
Refers to those in need, such as the hungry, thirsty, strangers, naked, sick, and imprisoned. They symbolize the marginalized and vulnerable in society.

3. The Final Judgment
The event where Jesus separates the righteous from the unrighteous, based on their actions towards "the least of these."

4. Sheep and Goats
Metaphorical groups representing the righteous (sheep) and the unrighteous (goats) in the parable of the final judgment.

5. Disciples/Audience
The immediate audience of Jesus' teaching, which includes His disciples and others who were listening to His teachings.
Teaching Points
The Call to Compassionate Action
Jesus emphasizes that our treatment of the marginalized is a direct reflection of our relationship with Him. We are called to actively engage in acts of compassion and service.

The Importance of Intentional Service
Serving "the least of these" should be intentional and motivated by love for Christ. Our actions should not be for recognition but as a genuine expression of our faith.

Eternal Consequences of Our Actions
The parable highlights that our actions have eternal significance. The way we treat others can impact our standing in the final judgment.

Recognizing Christ in Others
We are encouraged to see Christ in every person, especially those who are suffering or in need, and to serve them as we would serve Christ Himself.

Faith in Action
True faith is demonstrated through our actions. We are called to live out our faith by meeting the practical needs of those around us.

What Does Matthew 25:45 Mean
Then the King will answer

Jesus pictures Himself seated on the throne, responding personally to every life. The scene echoes Revelation 20:11–12, where all stand before the great white throne, and Isaiah 33:22 reminds us, “For the LORD is our Judge, the LORD is our Lawgiver, the LORD is our King; He will save us”. The authority is undeniable: Christ, the King, has the final word on what truly mattered in our earthly choices.

Truly I tell you

Whenever Jesus uses this phrase, He is emphasizing absolute certainty. He cannot lie (Titus 1:2), and “all the promises of God are ‘Yes’ in Christ” (2 Corinthians 1:20). So whatever follows is not suggestion but settled reality—truth that will stand when everything else crumbles (John 14:6).

whatever you did not do

Here the spotlight shifts from overt sins we commit to the good we might neglect. James 4:17 warns, “Whoever knows the right thing to do and fails to do it is guilty of sin” (paraphrased from). Jesus is exposing sins of omission:

• overlooking needs we clearly saw

• leaving ministry to “someone else”

• valuing comfort over compassion (Luke 10:31–32)

for one of the least of these

Christ identifies Himself with the vulnerable: the hungry, the stranger, the sick, the prisoner (Matthew 25:35–36). Proverbs 19:17 affirms, “Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD”. Paul echoes this call: “So then, as we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to the household of faith” (Galatians 6:10). The needy in our path are divinely appointed opportunities to serve Jesus Himself.

you did not do for Me

The punch line lands: neglecting people in need is neglecting Christ. When Saul persecuted believers, the risen Lord asked, “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute Me?” (Acts 9:4). Our treatment of others is inseparable from our relationship with Jesus. Hebrews 6:10 assures that “God is not unjust; He will not forget your work and the love you showed for His name when you served the saints”. Service withheld is love withheld from Him.

summary

Matthew 25:45 teaches that King Jesus takes our acts of mercy—or our lack of them—personally. Genuine faith expresses itself in practical care for the least, proving love for the Lord who first loved us.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 45. - Inasmuch as, etc. The Judge at once disallows all such pleas. He exacts nothing which any good man, Christian or not, might not have done. As before, identifying himself with the human race, he shows that, in neglecting to perform acts of mercifulness and charity to the afflicted, they disregarded him, despised him. dishonoured him. One of the least of these. He adds not "brethren," as above (ver. 40), because the evil acknowledge no such brotherhood; they live for self alone, they own not their real relation to the whole family of man.
