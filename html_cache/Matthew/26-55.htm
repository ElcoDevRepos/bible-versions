Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
At that time Jesus said to the crowd
This phrase sets the scene during the arrest of Jesus in the Garden of Gethsemane. The "crowd" refers to the group sent by the chief priests and elders, including Roman soldiers and temple guards. This moment is pivotal in the Passion narrative, highlighting the transition from Jesus' public ministry to His path to crucifixion. The timing is significant, occurring after the Last Supper and Jesus' prayer in Gethsemane, marking the beginning of His suffering.

Have you come out with swords and clubs to arrest Me as you would an outlaw?
Jesus questions the need for such force, emphasizing the irony and injustice of treating Him like a common criminal. The use of "swords and clubs" indicates the crowd's readiness for violence, reflecting the authorities' fear of Jesus' influence. This phrase underscores the fulfillment of prophecy, as Jesus is portrayed as the suffering servant (Isaiah 53:12), who is "numbered with the transgressors." It also highlights the misunderstanding and rejection of Jesus' peaceful mission.

Every day I sat teaching in the temple courts
Jesus reminds the crowd of His public and peaceful ministry. The "temple courts" were central to Jewish religious life, where Jesus often taught openly, engaging with both followers and critics. This setting underscores His transparency and the acceptance He initially received from the people. His teaching in the temple fulfills prophecies of the Messiah bringing wisdom and truth (Malachi 3:1).

and you did not arrest Me.
This statement points to the authorities' previous reluctance to arrest Jesus publicly due to His popularity among the people (Matthew 21:46). It highlights their hypocrisy and fear, choosing to arrest Him under the cover of night. This action fulfills the prophecy of the Messiah being betrayed and unjustly treated (Psalm 41:9). Jesus' calm demeanor in this moment contrasts with the crowd's aggression, further illustrating His innocence and the injustice of the situation.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is addressing the crowd that has come to arrest Him. He highlights the irony of their approach, given His peaceful and public ministry.

2. The Crowds
These are the people, including the chief priests and elders, who have come to arrest Jesus. They are armed with swords and clubs, treating Jesus as a criminal.

3. Temple Courts
The place where Jesus regularly taught. It was a public and significant location for Jewish teaching and worship, emphasizing the openness of Jesus' ministry.

4. Arrest of Jesus
This event marks the beginning of the Passion account, where Jesus is betrayed, arrested, and eventually crucified.
Teaching Points
The Irony of Fear
Jesus points out the irony of being treated as a criminal despite His peaceful and public ministry. This challenges us to consider how fear and misunderstanding can lead to unjust actions.

Public Ministry and Integrity
Jesus' reference to teaching in the temple courts underscores the importance of living a life of integrity and transparency. Believers are called to be open and honest in their faith.

The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Jesus' arrest fulfills Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah's suffering. This reminds us of God's sovereign plan and the reliability of Scripture.

Responding to Injustice
Jesus' calm response to His unjust arrest serves as a model for how Christians can respond to personal injustice with grace and truth.

What Does Matthew 26:55 Mean
At that time Jesus said to the crowd

• The moment is Gethsemane (Matthew 26:36, 47), a setting of betrayal and darkness, yet Jesus speaks with composure and authority.

• Scripture shows that every detail unfolds “at that time” according to the Father’s sovereign plan (John 18:4; Acts 2:23).

• By addressing “the crowd,” Jesus exposes the collective responsibility of religious leaders, soldiers, and onlookers alike (Luke 22:52).

Have you come out with swords and clubs to arrest Me as you would an outlaw?

• Jesus calls out the excessive force: clubs and swords against the Prince of Peace (Isaiah 9:6; John 18:36).

• Labeling Him “an outlaw” (literally “a robber”) shows the blindness of those treating the sinless Son of God like Barabbas (John 18:40).

• The contrast highlights prophecy: the Messiah would be “numbered with the transgressors” (Isaiah 53:12; Mark 15:28).

Every day I sat teaching in the temple courts

• For an entire week Jesus taught openly—parables, rebukes, and calls to repentance—before the very eyes of His accusers (Matthew 21:12–27:2).

• His posture of sitting underscores recognized rabbinic authority (Luke 4:20–22), fulfilled before “all the people” (Luke 19:47–48).

• This daily ministry fulfills Malachi 3:1, the Lord suddenly coming to His temple, inviting Israel to receive truth.

and you did not arrest Me

• Their restraint earlier was driven by fear of the crowds (Matthew 21:46) and by God’s timing (“His hour had not yet come,” John 7:30).

• The statement exposes their hypocrisy: they feared people more than God, waiting for darkness to hide their deed (Luke 22:53).

• Jesus’ words affirm that even their delayed arrest serves divine purpose; the cross would occur exactly when foretold (Galatians 4:4).

summary

Matthew 26:55 reveals Jesus’ calm authority amid betrayal, contrasts worldly violence with His peaceful ministry, and exposes the hypocrisy of those treating Him like a criminal. By reminding the crowd of His open teaching in the temple and their earlier inaction, He underscores that His arrest is not a triumph of human scheming but the unfolding of God’s sovereign plan, fulfilling prophecy and leading to the redemption accomplished at the cross.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(55) Are ye come out as against a thief?--Better, as against a robber with swords and clubs. The word is the same as that used in John 18:40, of Harabbas, and points to the brigand chieftain of a lawless band as distinct from the petty thief of towns or villages.

I sat daily with you teaching in the temple.--The statement referred primarily, perhaps, to what had passed in the three days immediately preceding, but it looks beyond this in its wide generality, and is important as an indication, occurring in one of the first three Gospels, of a ministry in Jerusalem, which their narratives pass over. The "sitting" in the Temple implied that our Lord took the position of a teacher more or less recognised as such (comp. Note on Matthew 5:1), not that of one who was addressing the multitude without authority.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 55. - The multitudes. St. Luke says that Christ addressed "the chief priests, and captains of the temple, and elders, which were come against him." He submitted to indignities, but he felt them deeply; he allowed himself to be treated as a malefactor, but was not insensible to the shame of being supposed to have been capable of acting as such. A thief; a robber. One at the head of a band of lawless ruffians, who would resist you with arms in their hands - a sicarius, a cutthroat, who lurked in secret places to murder the innocent. I sat daily with you (πρὸς ὑμᾶς, probably an interpolation from Mark). All the past week, at any rate, Christ had taught quietly and openly in the temple. He had none of the habits of the robber; he had not courted secrecy; he had no company of armed men to defend him; why did they not arrest him then? According to St. Luke, Christ adds, "But this is your hour, and the power of darkness."
