At that time
This phrase indicates a specific moment in the timeline of Jesus' life and ministry. It marks the beginning of His public ministry, following His life in Nazareth. The timing is significant as it aligns with the prophetic fulfillment of the coming Messiah and the preparation of the way by John the Baptist.

Jesus came from Galilee
Galilee is a region in northern Israel, known for its diverse population and fertile land. Jesus' coming from Galilee fulfills the prophecy in Isaiah 9:1-2, which speaks of a great light coming from Galilee of the Gentiles. This region was often looked down upon by Judeans, yet it is where Jesus spent much of His early life and ministry, highlighting God's use of humble beginnings.

to the Jordan
The Jordan River holds significant historical and spiritual importance in Israel's history. It was the site where the Israelites crossed into the Promised Land (Joshua 3) and where Elijah was taken up to heaven (2 Kings 2). The Jordan symbolizes a place of transition and new beginnings, making it a fitting location for the commencement of Jesus' ministry.

to be baptized by John
John the Baptist's role was to prepare the way for the Messiah, as prophesied in Isaiah 40:3 and Malachi 3:1. Baptism by John was a sign of repentance and preparation for the coming kingdom. Jesus' baptism, though He was sinless, signifies His identification with humanity and His endorsement of John's ministry. It also prefigures His death, burial, and resurrection, as baptism symbolizes dying to sin and rising to new life.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure of Christianity, the Son of God, who comes to John for baptism, signifying the beginning of His public ministry.

2. John the Baptist

3. Galilee
The region from which Jesus traveled, indicating His humble beginnings and fulfillment of prophecy.

4. The Jordan River
A significant location for Jewish history and symbolism, representing purification and new beginnings.

5. Baptism
A ritual of purification and repentance, which Jesus undergoes to fulfill all righteousness and identify with humanity.
Teaching Points
Humility of Christ
Jesus' willingness to be baptized by John demonstrates His humility and submission to God's plan. We are called to emulate this humility in our own lives.

Fulfillment of Righteousness
Jesus' baptism signifies the fulfillment of all righteousness. As believers, we are called to pursue righteousness in our daily walk with God.

Identification with Humanity
Jesus identifies with sinners through His baptism, showing His solidarity with us. We should strive to identify with and support others in their spiritual journeys.

Obedience to God's Will
Jesus' actions reflect His obedience to the Father's will. We are encouraged to seek and obey God's will in our lives, even when it requires humility and sacrifice.

Symbolism of Baptism
Baptism represents purification and a new beginning. As Christians, we are reminded of our own baptism and the call to live a life transformed by Christ.

What Does Matthew 3:13 Mean
At that time

Matthew pinpoints a real moment in history, not a vague legend. Luke 3:1-3 names rulers like Tiberius and Pilate to anchor John’s ministry in time, showing that God’s plan unfolds in actual dates and places. Galatians 4:4 reminds us that “when the fullness of time had come, God sent His Son,” and this verse in Matthew echoes that precision—Jesus steps onto the stage at the exact moment the Father ordained.

Jesus came

• The eternal Word (John 1:14) moves toward sinners rather than waiting for us to climb to Him.

• His coming fulfills Isaiah 40:3-5, the prophecy of a forerunner preparing the way.

• Mark 10:45 clarifies His motive: “For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve and to give His life as a ransom for many.” His very arrival signals humility and mission.

from Galilee to the Jordan

• Galilee, viewed as spiritually dim (Isaiah 9:1-2), is the launch point—highlighting God’s grace to overlooked places.

• The journey south to the Jordan River mirrors Israel’s earlier crossings (Joshua 3-4); Jesus will lead a new covenant people through the waters, prefiguring deliverance.

• John 1:28 calls the site “Bethany beyond the Jordan,” letting us picture an exact spot where God’s promises meet geography.

to be baptized by John

• Though sinless (2 Corinthians 5:21; Hebrews 4:15), Jesus identifies with repentant Israel, stepping into the same water we need.

• This act inaugurates His public ministry (Acts 10:37-38) and receives the Father’s audible approval (Matthew 3:17).

• By submitting to John, He affirms that John’s message is from God (Matthew 21:25) and models obedience (Philippians 2:8).

• The baptism foreshadows His death and resurrection (Romans 6:3-4)—buried in water, raised to new life—setting the pattern for every believer’s union with Him.

summary

Matthew 3:13 records Jesus deliberately leaving Galilee at the divinely appointed time, traveling to the Jordan, and humbly seeking baptism from John. Each phrase reveals His mission: entering real history, moving toward sinners, bringing light to dark places, fulfilling prophecy, sharing our repentance waters, and previewing the cross and empty tomb. The verse invites trust in a Savior who comes all the way to us and leads us into the life God planned from the beginning.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Then cometh Jesus.--We are brought here face to face with the question which the legend just quoted sought to answer, and cannot altogether turn aside from it: Why did the Lord Jesus come to the baptism of John? The Sinless One had no sin to confess, no need of repentance. We cannot even ascribe to Him that consciousness of evil which weighs upon the hearts of the saints of God almost in exact proportion to their holiness; yet we must believe that His righteousness was essentially human, and therefore capable of increase, even as He increased in wisdom and stature. Holy as He was at every stage of life in proportion to its capacities, there yet rose before Him height upon height of holiness as yet unattained, and after which we may say with reverence He "hungered and thirsted." And for that attainment the baptism, which to others was a stepping-stone out of the slough of despond, might well seem a means, if not a condition. It was meet that He should fill up the full measure of righteousness in all its forms by accepting a divine ordinance, even, perhaps, because it seemed to place Him in fellowship with sinners.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 13-17. - THE BAPTISM OF JESUS. (Parallel passages: Mark 1:9-11; Luke 3:21, 22.) Verse 13. - Then; temporal (ver. 5, note). When John was preaching and baptizing. Cometh (ver. 1, note). From Galilee. Mark adds, "from Nazareth of Galilee" (for this is his first historical mention of our Lord), thereby implying that our Lord had lived in Nazareth since our Matthew 2:22, etc. In contrast to the representative teachers from Jerusalem, and the crowds both from there and from the Jordan valley (ver. 5), this Stranger came from Galilee. To Jordan. It is hard to see why the Revised Version inserts "the" here and leaves the Authorized Version unaltered in ver. 5. To be baptized (τοῦ βαπτισθῆναι); Matthew 2:13, note. By him; and no other. Not mere baptism, but baptism at the hands of John, was our Lord's motive for coming. He would link his own work on to that of John (vide infra) .
