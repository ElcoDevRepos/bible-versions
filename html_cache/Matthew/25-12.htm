Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But he replied
This phrase indicates a response from the bridegroom in the parable of the ten virgins. The context is a wedding feast, a common cultural event in ancient Jewish society, symbolizing the kingdom of heaven. The bridegroom represents Jesus Christ, and His reply signifies a moment of judgment. In biblical times, a reply from a figure of authority, such as a bridegroom, carried significant weight, often determining one's inclusion or exclusion from an event.

Truly I tell you
This phrase is a solemn declaration, often used by Jesus to emphasize the truth and importance of His words. It is akin to an oath, underscoring the certainty and seriousness of the statement. The use of "truly" (Greek: "amen") is a hallmark of Jesus' teaching style, indicating that what follows is of utmost importance and should be heeded carefully.

I do not know you
This statement is a declaration of exclusion and lack of relationship. In the cultural context, knowing someone implied a personal relationship and acceptance. Theologically, this phrase highlights the necessity of a genuine relationship with Christ for entry into the kingdom of heaven. It echoes other scriptural warnings, such as in Matthew 7:21-23, where Jesus speaks of those who perform works in His name but lack a true relationship with Him. This phrase serves as a sobering reminder of the importance of being spiritually prepared and known by Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Bridegroom
Represents Jesus Christ in the parable of the ten virgins. He is the central figure whose arrival is anticipated.

Believers must live in a state of spiritual preparedness, always ready for Christ's return.

Genuine Relationship with Christ
It is not enough to merely profess faith; one must cultivate a true relationship with Jesus.

The parable warns of the eternal consequences of not being ready when Christ returns.

What Does Matthew 25:12 Mean
But he replied

The scene is the closed door of the wedding feast (Matthew 25:10). The bridesmaids who neglected their lamps plead for entry, but the Bridegroom answers.

• His answer shows that Christ is personally involved—He doesn’t delegate this moment to angels (cf. John 10:3, 27).

• A reply implies responsibility: what He says is perfectly just, echoing His earlier warnings about preparedness (Matthew 24:42–44).

• The immediate response underscores that there is no further negotiation once the door is shut, paralleling Noah’s ark once God closed it (Genesis 7:16).

Truly I tell you

Jesus prefaces the verdict with “Truly,” a word He reserves for weighty, irreversible statements (Matthew 5:18; John 3:3).

• It signals absolute authority—He speaks as Judge, not merely as Teacher (John 5:22).

• It confirms the certainty of what follows; the words are not a scare tactic but a settled decree (Revelation 19:11).

• In every use of this formula He draws attention to eternal realities, urging listeners to respond now (John 6:47).

I do not know you

These five chilling words reveal the ultimate issue: relationship, not mere association (Matthew 7:21-23).

• “Know” expresses intimate recognition, as between shepherd and sheep (John 10:14).

• The virgins had proximity, lamps, and the right event, yet lacked genuine connection—mirroring nominal believers who have form without faith (2 Timothy 3:5).

• The Bridegroom’s denial is final, aligning with Luke 13:25-27 where latecomers hear, “I do not know where you are from.”

• The closed door illustrates irreversible judgment (Hebrews 9:27); once Christ returns, opportunity for repentance ends (2 Corinthians 6:2).

summary

Matthew 25:12 warns that when Christ returns, readiness must be relational, not merely external. His authoritative reply, sealed with “Truly,” declares that those without a living relationship with Him will be excluded, no matter their proximity to religious activity. Now is the time to ensure He knows us, because once the door shuts, His verdict is final.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - I know you not. They had not been in the bridal company, nor joined in the festive procession, so the bridegroom could only answer from within that he had no knowledge of them. What is meant spiritually by this rejection is doubtful. This is not a solitary instance of the use of the expression. In the sermon on the mount Christ declared that his sentence on those that professed, but practised not, would be, "I never knew you: depart from me!" (Matthew 7:23). He is said to know those whom he approves and acknowledges to be his (see John 10:14). God says of Abraham, "I know him" (Genesis 18:19) and of Moses, "I know thee by name" (Exodus 33:12). To be known of God is a higher blessing than to know God (Galatians 4:9). Many think that the words of our text imply utter reprobation. So Nosgen; and Chrysostom writes, "When he hath said this, nothing else but hell is left, and that intolerable punishment; or rather, this word is more grievous even than hell. This word he speaks also to them that work iniquity." But we must observe that in the present ease we have not the terrible addition, "Depart from me!" The sentence of exclusion from Christ's presence is not equivalent to that in ver. 41, which dooms souls to the everlasting fire prepared for the devil and his angels. These five virgins had received the grace of God, and used it well for a time, and only failed at the last for lack of care and watchfulness. They had still some love for the Lord, still desired to serve him; it is not conceivable that they should suffer the same punishment as the utterly godless and profane, whose wickedness was perfect and Satanic. Doubtless they were punished; but as there are degrees of happiness in heaven, so there may be gradation of pains and penalties for those debarred from its blessings (see 1 Corinthians 3:15). But it is not improbable that the exclusion in the first place refers to the deprivation of participation in Messiah's future kingdom, whatever that may be, according to the vision in Revelation 20, and that the proceedings at the final judgment are not here intended.
