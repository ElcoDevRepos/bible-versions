Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But he answered one of them
In this parable, the landowner represents God, and the workers symbolize believers. The response is directed to one of the laborers who felt wronged, highlighting the personal nature of God's relationship with each individual. This reflects the biblical theme of God addressing individuals directly, as seen in His interactions with figures like Moses (Exodus 3:4) and Elijah (1 Kings 19:9).

‘Friend, I am not being unfair to you.
The term "friend" is used to address the worker, indicating a relationship that is not hostile despite the complaint. This mirrors Jesus' use of "friend" in other parables (Matthew 22:12, 26:50), suggesting a tone of gentle correction. The concept of fairness is central to the parable, emphasizing God's justice and generosity. The landowner's fairness is rooted in the agreed-upon terms, reflecting God's faithfulness to His promises (Deuteronomy 7:9).

Did you not agree with me on one denarius?
A denarius was a common day's wage for a laborer in Roman times, reflecting the economic context of the period. The agreement on this wage underscores the fairness of the landowner's actions. This phrase highlights the importance of covenant and agreement, echoing the biblical theme of God's covenants with His people (Genesis 15:18, Exodus 19:5). The parable challenges the human tendency to compare and question God's distribution of grace, reminding believers of the sufficiency of God's provision.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Landowner
Represents God, who is sovereign and just in His dealings with humanity.

2. The Workers
Symbolize believers or followers of Christ, each called to work in God's vineyard (the world).

3. The Vineyard
Represents the Kingdom of Heaven, where God's work is to be done.

4. The Denarius
A day's wage, symbolizing the reward of eternal life or God's grace.

5. The Complaint
The grievance of the early workers who felt they deserved more, highlighting human tendencies towards envy and entitlement.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Justice
God, like the landowner, is sovereign and just. He has the right to dispense His grace as He sees fit, and His decisions are always fair, even if they challenge our human sense of fairness.

Grace Over Merit
The parable teaches that God's kingdom operates on grace rather than merit. Our salvation and rewards are not based on the length or nature of our service but on God's generous grace.

Guard Against Envy
The early workers' complaint warns us against envy and entitlement. We should rejoice in the blessings of others and trust in God's perfect provision for us.

Contentment in God's Provision
Believers are called to be content with what God provides, trusting that He knows what is best for each of us.

The Call to Serve
Regardless of when we are called to serve in God's vineyard, our focus should be on faithful service rather than comparison with others.

What Does Matthew 20:13 Mean
But he answered one of them

• Jesus pictures the landowner responding directly to the single laborer who voiced the complaint. The focus shifts from a group grievance to a personal conversation, stressing individual accountability (Romans 14:12).

• This mirrors the father’s appeal to the resentful older brother in Luke 15:28-31, where one-on-one dialogue exposes the heart issue rather than the wage issue.

• God does not ignore grumbling; He lovingly engages it so that motives come to light (Job 38:1-3).

Friend

• The address “Friend” shows kindness, not sarcasm. The landowner invites relationship even while correcting (John 15:15; Matthew 26:50).

• While the worker feels wronged, the master reminds him that they are not adversaries. Graceful confrontation springs from covenant love, not hostility (Proverbs 27:6).

I am not being unfair to you

• The statement asserts the master’s justice. Scripture affirms that God’s ways are perfectly righteous (Deuteronomy 32:4; Psalm 145:17).

• The issue is not injustice but jealousy over generosity. The master’s fairness stands alongside his freedom to be lavish with others (Romans 9:14-16).

• By declaring “I am not being unfair,” the landowner reorients the worker from emotions to facts—exactly what God does when we question His dealings (Malachi 3:13-14).

Did you not agree with me on one denarius?

• The reminder reaches back to the original agreement (Matthew 20:2). Covenant terms were set and honored. God never retracts His promises (Joshua 21:45).

• The question underlines consent: the worker entered the vineyard willingly. Likewise, those who follow Christ accept His terms, trusting His character (Galatians 3:15).

• By highlighting the agreed wage, the master exposes the true complaint: resentment that others received more grace, not that he received less justice (Titus 3:5-7).

summary

Matthew 20:13 reveals a just and gracious Master who personally engages our grumbling, reminds us of our relationship to Him, affirms His flawless fairness, and calls us back to the agreement we made. The verse challenges envy and underscores that God’s generosity toward others never diminishes His faithfulness to us.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Friend.--The word so translated (literally, comrade, companion) always carries, with it in our Lord's lips a tone of reproof. It is addressed to the man who had not on a wedding garment (Matthew 22:12), and to the traitor Judas (Matthew 26:50).

I do thee no wrong.--The answer of the house holder is that of one who is just where claims are urged on the ground of justice, generous where he sees that generosity is right. Had the first-called labourers shared this generosity, they would not have grudged the others the wages that they themselves received, and would have found their own reward in sympathy with their joy. This would be true even in the outer framework of the parable. It is a fortiori true when we pass to its spiritual interpretation. No disciple who had entered into his Master's spirit would grudge the repentant thief his rest in Paradise (Luke 23:43). No consistent Christian thinks that he ought to have some special reward because he sees a death-bed repentance crowned by a peace, the foretaste of eternal life, as full and assured as his own.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - He answered one of them. The Lord condescended to show, not to all the labourers, but to one of them - the ringleader probably - the futility of the ground of his murmur. Christ often explains himself to his friends, while he refuses further elucidation to enemies and the hardened. Friend (ἑταῖρε). Not a term of affection, or special good will, but one of indifference, addressed to an inferior. It was the word used to Judas (Matthew 26:50) when he came to betray his Lord, "Friend, wherefore art thou come?" I do thee no wrong. The labourer had really nothing to complain of in strict justice; he had received the full amount of the stipulated wages. But he very naturally felt that he had not been fairly dealt with. He would say to himself, "If one hour's work, and that in the cool of the evening, is deemed worth a penny, surely a whole day's labour, in the full heat of the sun, ought to deserve a higher remuneration." The difficulty here must be felt by every one. Nor is the master's solution perfect; it would scarcely commend itself to the dissatisfied murmurer. And doubtless it is not intended to be complete.
