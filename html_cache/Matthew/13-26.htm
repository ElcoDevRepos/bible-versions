When the wheat sprouted and bore grain
This phrase indicates the growth and maturation of the wheat, symbolizing the development of true believers in the kingdom of God. In biblical context, wheat often represents the righteous or those who follow God's teachings. The process of sprouting and bearing grain suggests a period of growth and productivity, aligning with the parable's theme of spiritual development. This imagery is consistent with other scriptural references to agricultural growth, such as in John 12:24, where Jesus speaks of a grain of wheat dying to produce much fruit, symbolizing His death and the resulting spiritual harvest.

then the weeds also appeared
The appearance of weeds alongside the wheat highlights the presence of evil or false believers within the kingdom. Historically, the term "weeds" refers to darnel, a plant that closely resembles wheat in its early stages, making it difficult to distinguish between the two until maturity. This reflects the challenge of discerning true believers from false ones within the church. Theologically, this phrase underscores the coexistence of good and evil until the final judgment, as seen in Matthew 13:30, where the separation of wheat and weeds is reserved for the harvest time. This concept is echoed in other scriptures, such as Matthew 25:31-46, where the separation of sheep and goats represents the final judgment.

Teaching Points
Coexistence of Good and Evil
In this world, believers and non-believers live side by side. We must be aware of this reality and remain steadfast in our faith.

Patience and Trust in God's Timing
Just as the harvest is awaited, we must trust in God's perfect timing for justice and judgment.

Bearing Fruit
As wheat bears grain, Christians are called to bear spiritual fruit, demonstrating their faith through actions and character.

Discernment and Vigilance
Be discerning of influences and teachings, ensuring they align with Scripture, as weeds can appear similar to wheat.

Hope in Final Redemption
The promise of a future separation and redemption should encourage believers to persevere in righteousness.

What Does Matthew 13:26 Mean
When the wheat sprouted

• “Wheat” pictures genuine believers, sown by “the Son of Man” (Matthew 13:37-38).

• For a season the crop lay hidden, yet life was already present—just as believers are alive in Christ before their outward testimony is obvious (Colossians 3:3; Mark 4:26-27).

• Sprouting signals the moment God’s work becomes visible: “He who began a good work in you will carry it on to completion” (Philippians 1:6).

• The scene reminds us to wait patiently; growth takes time (James 5:7-8).

and bore grain

• Grain is the proof of true wheat—tangible fruit that confirms life within (John 15:5).

• Jesus’ standard is not mere profession but productivity: “By their fruit you will recognize them” (Matthew 7:20).

• Mature grain benefits others—just as Spirit-produced love, joy, peace, and the rest nourish the church and witness to the world (Galatians 5:22-23).

• Bearing grain also hints at harvest readiness; the Master does not gather until the kernels are full (Mark 4:28-29).

then the weeds also appeared

• Weeds (darnel) were sown by “the enemy” (Matthew 13:25); they look like wheat until heads form. Counterfeit disciples often resemble the real until fruit-testing time arrives (2 Corinthians 11:13-15).

• Their emergence “after” the wheat’s fruit signals that evil reacts to good. Wherever God plants truth, Satan plants deception (Acts 20:29-30; 1 John 2:19).

• The coexistence lasts until harvest; premature uprooting would damage the wheat (Matthew 13:29). God’s patience protects His people while ensuring ultimate justice (2 Peter 3:9-10).

summary

Matthew 13:26 shows a three-stage drama: genuine believers become visible, their spiritual fruit confirms their identity, and only then are counterfeit followers exposed. The verse reassures us that God oversees growth, discerns authenticity, and will, in His perfect time, separate wheat from weeds.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 26. - But when the blade was sprung up (ἐβλάστησεν ὁ χόρτος: cf. Mark 4:27), and brought forth fruit. Observe that there is no thought of the tares injuring the wheat (contrast vers. 7, 22). Then appeared the tares also.
