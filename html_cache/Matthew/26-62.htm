So the high priest stood up and asked Him
The high priest at this time was Caiaphas, a significant figure in the Sanhedrin, the Jewish ruling council. His standing up indicates a moment of heightened tension and authority. In Jewish trials, the high priest played a crucial role in maintaining order and ensuring the proceedings adhered to religious law. This moment is pivotal as it reflects the seriousness of the accusations against Jesus. Caiaphas's actions fulfill the prophecy of Psalm 2:2, where rulers take counsel against the Lord and His Anointed.

“Have You no answer?
This question highlights the silence of Jesus, which fulfills the prophecy in Isaiah 53:7, where the suffering servant is described as being oppressed and afflicted, yet not opening His mouth. Jesus's silence can be seen as a demonstration of His submission to God's will and His awareness of the necessity of His sacrifice. In the cultural context, remaining silent in the face of accusations could be seen as an admission of guilt, yet Jesus's silence is a profound statement of His innocence and divine purpose.

What are these men testifying against You?”
The testimony against Jesus was based on false accusations, as seen earlier in the chapter. The Jewish leaders sought false witnesses to justify their predetermined decision to condemn Him. This reflects the fulfillment of Psalm 27:12, where David speaks of false witnesses rising against him. The trial of Jesus was marked by legal irregularities, as Jewish law required at least two witnesses to agree for a conviction, and their testimonies did not align. This moment underscores the injustice of the trial and the fulfillment of God's redemptive plan through Jesus's suffering.

Persons / Places / Events
1. High Priest
The high priest at this time was Caiaphas, who played a significant role in the trial of Jesus. He was the chief religious authority among the Jews and presided over the Sanhedrin, the Jewish ruling council.

2. Jesus
Central to this passage, Jesus is being questioned during His trial before the Sanhedrin. This event is part of the Passion account, leading to His crucifixion.

3. Sanhedrin
The Jewish council responsible for religious and legal matters. They were the ones conducting the trial of Jesus, seeking evidence to justify a death sentence.

4. False Witnesses
Individuals who were brought forward to testify against Jesus, often providing conflicting and false testimonies.

5. Jerusalem
The city where these events took place, specifically in the high priest's courtyard, which was a significant location for Jewish religious and political activity.
Teaching Points

Jesus' silence in the face of false accusations fulfills prophecy and demonstrates His submission to God's will. It teaches us the power of restraint and trust in God's justice.

The Role of False Witnesses
The presence of false witnesses highlights the reality of injustice in the world. Believers are called to stand for truth and integrity, even when faced with opposition.

Authority and Responsibility
The high priest's role in this event reminds us of the responsibility that comes with authority. Leaders are called to seek truth and justice, not personal or political gain.

Prophecy Fulfilled
This event is a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies, reinforcing the reliability of Scripture and God's sovereign plan.

Responding to Accusations

What Does Matthew 26:62 Mean
So the high priest stood up

The scene shifts from a seated, seemingly orderly gathering to a moment charged with urgency. Caiaphas, the high priest, rises—an action that signals authority and intensifies the drama of this midnight trial (see Mark 14:60). His posture underscores the seriousness of the proceeding and the resolve to condemn Jesus. Throughout Scripture, when leaders stand to address an assembly, it often marks a pivotal turning point (Acts 7:54-56). Here, that turning point is the push to secure an accusation that will justify sentencing the Son of God.

and asked Him,

Caiaphas engages Jesus directly, assuming the role of interrogator. By stepping out of impartial adjudication into aggressive questioning, the high priest abandons any pretense of fairness. Similar confrontations occur when religious leaders challenge Jesus in John 8:3-6 and Luke 20:1-2, yet each time He exposes their hypocrisy. The high priest’s question intends to corner Jesus, but it ultimately highlights the contrast between earthly authority and divine authority standing before him.

“Have You no answer?”

Silence can be a powerful declaration. Jesus’ restraint fulfills Isaiah 53:7—“He was oppressed and afflicted, yet He did not open His mouth”. By refusing to defend Himself, Jesus demonstrates submission to the Father’s redemptive plan (John 10:18). His silence also exposes the weakness of the case against Him; if convincing evidence existed, no response would be necessary. Compare Paul’s later example before Festus in Acts 25:8-9, where a defense is offered, showing that silence here is a deliberate, prophetic choice, not inability.

“What are these men testifying against You?”

Caiaphas refers to earlier false witnesses whose testimonies failed to agree (Mark 14:56-59). The question tries to pressure Jesus into self-incrimination because the accusers cannot supply a consistent charge. Deuteronomy 19:15 requires two or three witnesses for a capital conviction, underscoring the illegitimacy of this trial. The high priest’s frustration reveals that no truthful accusation can stand against the sinless Messiah (Hebrews 4:15). This moment accentuates the contrast between human schemes and God’s unassailable righteousness.

summary

Matthew 26:62 portrays a desperate religious court confronting the silent, sovereign Savior. The high priest’s authoritative stance, probing questions, and reliance on shaky testimonies cannot eclipse Jesus’ quiet fulfillment of prophecy and unwavering submission to the Father’s will. The verse highlights both the miscarriage of human justice and the perfection of God’s redemptive plan—echoing through the cross to assure believers that Christ’s innocence secures our salvation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(62) Answerest thou nothing?--A different punctuation gives, Answerest Thou nothing to what these witness against Thee? as one question. The question implies a long-continued silence, while witness after witness were uttering their clumsy falsehoods, the effect of which it is not easy to realise without a more than common exercise of what may be called dramatic imagination. I remember hearing from a distinguished scholar who had seen the Ammergau Passion-mystery, that, as represented there, it came upon him with a force which he had never felt before. In the silence itself we may perhaps trace a deliberate fulfilment of the prophecy of Isaiah 53:7. In 1Peter 2:23 we find a record of the impression which that fulfilment made on the disciples.

What is it . . .?--The question was clearly put, as it had been before Annas (John 18:19), with the intention of drawing out something that would ensure condemnation.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 62. - The high priest [Caiaphas] arose. As if in indignation at the outrage offered by this vaunt to Jehovah and the sanctuary. But the indignation was assumed and theatrical; for even this charge had broken down, owing to the disagreement of the two witnesses (Mark 14:59). Something more definite must be secured before any formal appeal could be made to the Sanhedrin or the procurator. Answerest thou nothing? The angry president endeavours to browbeat the Prisoner, and to make him criminate himself by intemperate language or indiscreet admission. What is it which these witness against thee? The Received Text (followed here by Westcott and Hort) divides the high priest's words into two questions, as in the Authorized Version. The Vulgate unites the two into one, Nihil respondes ad ea quae isti adversum te testificantur? Alford, Tischendorf, etc., print, Οὐδὲν ἀποκρίνῃ τί οῦτοί σου καταμαρτυροῦσιν; "Answerest thou not what it is which these witness against thee?" Caiaphas professes a desire to hear Christ's explanation of the words just alleged against him.
