So Jesus got up and went with him
This phrase highlights Jesus' readiness to respond to the needs of others, demonstrating His compassion and willingness to help. The context here is that a synagogue leader, Jairus, has approached Jesus, pleading for the healing of his daughter. Jesus' immediate response to get up and go with Jairus shows His authority and willingness to engage with those in need, regardless of their social status. This action reflects the fulfillment of prophecies about the Messiah being a shepherd to His people, as seen in passages like Isaiah 40:11. It also illustrates the incarnational aspect of Jesus' ministry, where He physically moves towards those who seek Him, embodying the presence of God among His people.

along with His disciples
The presence of the disciples underscores the communal aspect of Jesus' ministry. They are witnesses to His miracles and teachings, learning directly from His actions and words. This journey with Jesus is part of their discipleship training, preparing them for their future roles in spreading the Gospel. The disciples' presence also serves to authenticate the events that occur, as they later become the primary sources of the Gospel accounts. This phrase connects to the broader theme of discipleship in the New Testament, where followers of Christ are called to walk with Him, learn from Him, and eventually carry on His mission, as seen in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20).

Teaching Points
Faith in Action
The ruler's approach to Jesus exemplifies active faith. He not only believes in Jesus' power but also takes steps to seek His intervention.

Jesus' Compassion
Jesus' immediate response to the ruler's request shows His compassion and readiness to help those in need. We are reminded of His love and care for us.

The Role of Discipleship
The disciples accompany Jesus, learning from His example. As modern-day disciples, we are called to follow Jesus closely and learn from His teachings and actions.

The Power of Presence
Jesus' willingness to go with the ruler highlights the importance of being present with those who are suffering. We are encouraged to be present and supportive in the lives of others.

What Does Matthew 9:19 Mean
So Jesus got up

“Jesus got up” captures His immediate, willing response.

• He does not hesitate; His readiness mirrors earlier moments, such as when He “reached out His hand and touched” (Matthew 8:3).

• Readiness to move toward need is consistent with John 10:11—“I am the good shepherd.”

• The action follows the synagogue leader’s plea (Matthew 9:18), showing that earnest faith moves the Lord to act (Hebrews 11:6).

and went with him

Jesus physically accompanies the grieving father.

• This personal presence recalls Matthew 8:7, where He declares, “I will go and heal him.”

• Mark 5:24 records the same scene: “So Jesus went with him”, confirming that this was not symbolic but literal movement.

• Luke 8:41-42 emphasizes the urgency—“the crowds were pressing against Him”—yet He still chooses to go.

• His willingness to enter a private home foreshadows the intimacy of Revelation 3:20, where He promises to “come in and dine.”

along with His disciples

The disciples walk the road too.

• Their presence turns the journey into a living lesson on compassion and obedience (Matthew 5:16).

• It prepares them for future ministry patterns—going where need exists, as later in Acts 3:6-7 when Peter lifts the lame man.

• Being eyewitnesses strengthens their testimony (1 John 1:1-3).

• Crowds will soon witness two miracles—the woman healed of bleeding and the girl raised (Matthew 9:20-26)—showing the disciples that no situation lies outside Christ’s authority.

summary

Matthew 9:19 highlights Christ’s prompt compassion, His willingness to step into personal grief, and the disciples’ call to follow His example. Together, these elements affirm that when genuine faith appeals to Jesus, He rises, moves, and brings His followers along to witness His saving power.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - And Jesus arose, Matthew only. From the table, if Matthew's connexion is to be followed; from his seat by the seashore, if Mark's. And followed him. As he led the way to his house. The tense (ἠκολούθει) shows that our Lord had already started when the next incident took place. And so did his disciples. Mark substitutes "a great multitude," and adds that "they thronged him" (cf. also Luke).
