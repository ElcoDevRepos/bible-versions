Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the disciples came to Him and said,
This phrase indicates a private moment between Jesus and His disciples, suggesting a close relationship where the disciples felt comfortable approaching Jesus with their concerns. The disciples often sought clarification from Jesus, showing their role as learners. This setting highlights the importance of direct communication with Christ, a model for prayer and seeking understanding.

“Are You aware that the Pharisees were offended
The Pharisees were a prominent religious group in Jewish society, known for their strict adherence to the Law and traditions. Their offense at Jesus' teachings reflects the tension between Jesus' message and the established religious norms. This moment underscores the growing conflict between Jesus and the religious leaders, which is a recurring theme in the Gospels. The Pharisees' offense also illustrates the challenge of confronting deeply held beliefs with the truth of the Gospel.

when they heard this?”
The specific teaching that offended the Pharisees was Jesus' statement about what defiles a person, emphasizing internal purity over external rituals. This teaching challenged the Pharisaic focus on ceremonial cleanliness and dietary laws. The reaction of the Pharisees is a reminder of the resistance that often accompanies transformative truth. This moment foreshadows the increasing opposition Jesus would face, ultimately leading to His crucifixion. It also serves as a call for believers to prioritize inner righteousness over mere outward conformity.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
- The central figure of the New Testament, the Son of God, who is teaching and confronting the religious leaders of His time.

2. The Disciples
- Followers of Jesus who are learning from His teachings and observing His interactions with others.

3. The Pharisees
- A religious group in Judaism known for their strict adherence to the Law and traditions, often in conflict with Jesus' teachings.

4. The Event
- This verse occurs after Jesus rebukes the Pharisees for their hypocrisy, specifically regarding their traditions that nullify God's commandments.
Teaching Points
Understanding Offense in Truth
Jesus' teachings often challenge established norms and can offend those who are more concerned with tradition than truth. We must prioritize God's truth over human traditions.

Fear of Man vs. Fear of God
The disciples' concern about the Pharisees' offense highlights the tension between fearing human opinion and fearing God. Believers are called to stand firm in truth, even when it offends others.

Hypocrisy and True Worship
Jesus' rebuke of the Pharisees serves as a warning against hypocrisy. True worship involves aligning our hearts with God's commandments, not merely external compliance.

The Role of Tradition
While traditions can be valuable, they should never supersede or contradict God's Word. Believers must discern and evaluate traditions in light of Scripture.

What Does Matthew 15:12 Mean
Then the disciples came to Him

– The disciples draw near privately after Jesus’ public confrontation with the Pharisees (Matthew 15:3-11).

– Their coming shows they recognize Jesus as the authoritative Teacher (John 6:68).

– Like earlier moments (Matthew 13:36) they wait until the crowd disperses, modeling a heart that seeks clarity rather than resisting truth (Proverbs 2:1-5).

and said

– Speaking up demonstrates growing boldness, contrasting with the silence of the crowd (Matthew 14:33).

– Genuine disciples engage the Lord with their concerns, echoing Psalm 62:8, “Pour out your hearts before Him.”

– Their words reveal they still filter events through human expectations, something Jesus will patiently reshape (Luke 24:27).

“Are You aware”

– They assume Jesus might have missed something, forgetting His omniscience revealed in John 1:48 and Matthew 9:4.

– This gentle question underscores human tendency to instruct the Lord instead of trusting His perfect knowledge (Job 38:2-4).

– Christ allows the question, inviting deeper understanding rather than rebuking the ignorance (James 1:5).

that the Pharisees were offended

– The Greek term translated “offended” speaks of stumbling; Jesus’ truth exposes hypocrisy, creating either repentance or resentment (Isaiah 8:14; 1 Peter 2:7-8).

– The Pharisees’ offense fulfills Jesus’ earlier warning that tradition-bound leaders “honor Me with their lips, but their hearts are far from Me” (Matthew 15:8-9).

– Their reaction anticipates the broader rejection He will face (John 15:18-20).

when they heard this?

– “This” refers to Jesus’ declaration that defilement comes from the heart, not from unwashed hands (Matthew 15:11).

– His teaching overturns centuries of man-made regulations, mirroring His Sabbath pronouncements (Mark 2:27-28).

– Hearing truth forces a decision: embrace God’s word or cling to tradition (Joshua 24:15; Hebrews 3:15).

summary

Matthew 15:12 records the disciples’ concern that Jesus has offended powerful religious leaders. Their inquiry exposes limited understanding but also a desire to learn. Jesus will use the moment to reinforce that divine truth, not human tradition, governs righteousness. Offense is the predictable response of prideful hearts confronted with the light, yet disciples are called to stand with Christ, confident that His authoritative word remains unshaken.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) Then came his disciples.--The sequence of events appears in Mark 7:17. The Pharisees drew back as in holy horror at the boldness with which the new Teacher set Himself, not only above their traditions, but above laws which they looked on as divine, and therefore permanent. The multitude heard in silence a teaching so unlike that with which they had been familiar from their youth. Even the disciples were half perplexed at the teaching itself, half afraid of what might be its immediate consequences. They came with their question, "Knowest thou not that the Pharisees were offended?" Had their Master calculated the consequences of thus attacking, not individual members or individual traditions of the party, but its fundamental principle, that which was, so to speak, its very raison d'etre?

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - Then came his disciples. Jesus had been speaking in some open spot; he now leaves the crowd, and, entering a house with his disciples, instructs them further in private (Mark 7:17). These had been greatly alarmed at their Master's antagonism to the popular party, and, on the first occasion that presented itself, expostulated with him on the danger incurred by this hostile attitude. This saying (τὸν λόγον); the word. What he had said to the multitude (ver. 11). The Pharisees had cared less for the denunciation addressed to themselves (vers. 3-9), but when he interfered with their doctrinal supremacy over the people, they were offended, they took exception to p the teaching, believing that they detected therein an insidious attack on the Law. In their view, spiritualization of any of its enactments was equivalent to its subversion. But, as St. Gregory observes, "If offence arises from the statement of the truth, it is more expedient that offence be permitted to arise than that the truth should be abandoned" ('Hom. 7. in Ezek.').
