So the LORD said to Moses
This phrase highlights the direct communication between God and Moses, a unique relationship in the Old Testament. Moses is often seen as a mediator between God and the Israelites, prefiguring Christ as the ultimate mediator (1 Timothy 2:5). The LORD speaking to Moses emphasizes the personal nature of God's relationship with His chosen leaders.

I will do this very thing you have asked
Moses had requested that God's presence continue with the Israelites as they journeyed to the Promised Land. This assurance from God underscores His faithfulness and willingness to respond to the intercession of His servants. It reflects the biblical principle that God hears and answers prayers according to His will (1 John 5:14-15).

for you have found favor in My sight
Finding favor in God's sight is a recurring theme in Scripture, often associated with righteousness and obedience. This favor is not earned by human merit but is a result of God's grace. It parallels the New Testament concept of grace through faith (Ephesians 2:8-9), showing that God's favor is a gift.

and I know you by name
This phrase signifies an intimate and personal relationship. In ancient Near Eastern culture, knowing someone's name implied a deep understanding and connection. It foreshadows the New Testament promise that believers are known by God (John 10:3, 2 Timothy 2:19). This personal knowledge is a source of comfort and assurance for God's people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who is speaking directly to Moses. His name, Yahweh, signifies His eternal, self-existent nature.

2. Moses
The leader of the Israelites, chosen by God to lead His people out of Egypt. Moses is in a unique relationship with God, characterized by direct communication and intercession.

3. The Tent of Meeting
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of Exodus 33 involves Moses meeting with God in the Tent of Meeting, a place of divine encounter and revelation.

4. The Israelites
The people of God, whom Moses is leading through the wilderness toward the Promised Land. Their relationship with God is mediated through Moses.

5. The Favor of God
A central theme in this passage, indicating God's grace and approval towards Moses, which is foundational for the unfolding of His plans.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Favor with God
God's favor is not earned but granted by His grace. Like Moses, believers are called to seek a relationship with God where His favor is evident in their lives.

Intimacy with God
Knowing God and being known by Him is a profound privilege. This relationship is characterized by communication, obedience, and trust.

Intercession and Leadership
Moses' role as an intercessor for Israel highlights the power of prayer and the responsibility of leaders to seek God’s will for those they lead.

God’s Personal Knowledge of Us
God’s knowledge of Moses by name signifies a deep, personal relationship. Believers can take comfort in knowing that God knows them personally and intimately.

The Role of Obedience in Favor
While God’s favor is a gift, Moses’ obedience and faithfulness positioned him to receive and walk in that favor. Believers are encouraged to live lives of obedience to experience God’s favor.

What Does Exodus 33:17 Mean
So the LORD said to Moses

Exodus 33:17 opens with, “So the LORD said to Moses…”.

• The living God speaks directly—personally—to His servant (Genesis 3:9; Exodus 3:4).

• This conversation flows out of Moses’ intercession in vv. 12-16. God’s willingness to talk underscores His covenant faithfulness (Psalm 103:7; John 10:27).

• Because Scripture is literal and accurate, we take this as a real, historical dialogue, not a metaphor or myth (Numbers 12:6-8).

I will do this very thing you have asked

The LORD continues, “I will do this very thing you have asked…”.

• Moses had pleaded that God’s presence would go with Israel (v. 15). God answers exactly, illustrating:

– Prayer that aligns with God’s purposes is powerful (1 John 5:14-15).

– God delights to grant requests that honor His name (John 14:13-14; Psalm 37:4).

• This assurance follows a pattern of God responding to faithful intercession (Genesis 18:23-33; James 5:16-18).

for you have found favor in My sight

God explains the reason: “for you have found favor in My sight”.

• “Favor” is grace—unearned kindness—which originates in God, not in Moses’ merit (Genesis 6:8; Ephesians 2:8-9).

• Still, Moses’ obedience and humility positioned him to receive that grace (Exodus 32:31-32; Hebrews 11:24-26).

• God’s favor distinguishes His people from all others (v. 16; Luke 1:30).

and I know you by name

The verse ends, “and I know you by name”.

• To know someone “by name” is to know them intimately and personally (Isaiah 43:1; John 10:3).

• God’s omniscience is not cold data but warm relationship (Psalm 139:1-4).

• Those who belong to Him are sealed and identified individually (2 Timothy 2:19; Revelation 2:17).

summary

Exodus 33:17 shows a covenant God who speaks, grants specific requests, bestows grace, and knows His people personally. Moses’ experience reassures every believer that the LORD listens, answers, favors, and intimately knows those who trust Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) I will do this thing also that thou hast spoken.--At length the promise is unambiguously given. Moses is rewarded for his importunity. God's people have found grace in His sight. He will "go up" with them, and so "separate them," or distinguish them, from "all the people that are on the face of the earth." Now at last Moses is satisfied.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - I will do this thing also. "I will extend my favour to thy people also, and distinguish them, as well as thee, by going up with them. I will do this for thy sake, because thou hast found grace in my sight." Moses' petition is at last fully granted - the threat of withdrawal cancelled - the promise of Divine guidance and protection renewed I know thee by name. It is a supreme favour for God to know us by name. It marks "a specifically personal relation to God" (Keil). The expression is perhaps taken from the phraseology of Oriental Courts, where not one in a hundred of the courtiers is known to the monarch by name.
