He made six frames
The construction of the tabernacle was a significant event in the history of Israel, as it represented God's dwelling among His people. The frames, or boards, were part of the tabernacle's structure, providing stability and form. The number six may symbolize human effort, as it is one less than seven, which often represents completeness or divine perfection in the Bible. The use of frames indicates a portable structure, suitable for the Israelites' nomadic lifestyle during their wilderness journey.

for the rear of the tabernacle
The rear of the tabernacle was the western side, which was considered the back. This orientation is significant because the entrance faced east, a direction often associated with the presence of God (as seen in the Garden of Eden and the direction of the rising sun). The rear would be the most protected part of the tabernacle, symbolizing the security and sanctity of God's dwelling place.

the west side
In ancient Near Eastern culture, the west was often associated with the end of the day and rest. For the Israelites, the west side of the tabernacle would be the final point of their journey, symbolizing the ultimate rest and peace found in God's presence. The west side's construction with six frames highlights the importance of stability and protection for the most sacred area of the tabernacle, where the Holy of Holies was located. This can also be seen as a type of Christ, who provides ultimate rest and security for believers.

Teaching Points
God's Detailed Instructions
The specific instructions for the Tabernacle's construction highlight God's attention to detail and His desire for order and beauty in worship. This teaches us the importance of following God's guidance in our lives.

Community Contribution
The construction of the Tabernacle involved the entire community of Israel, emphasizing the value of collective effort and unity in fulfilling God's purposes.

Symbolism of the Tabernacle
The Tabernacle serves as a symbol of God's presence among His people. As believers, we are called to be living temples, reflecting God's presence in our lives.

Obedience and Faithfulness
The Israelites' obedience in constructing the Tabernacle according to God's specifications demonstrates the importance of faithfulness in carrying out God's commands.

Spiritual Foundations
Just as the frames provided structural support for the Tabernacle, our lives need a strong spiritual foundation built on God's Word and principles.

What Does Exodus 36:27 Mean
He made

- The verse begins by noting deliberate action. Moses records that skilled artisans actually “made” what God had commanded (Exodus 36:1–2).

- This hands-on obedience flows from God’s prior call in Exodus 35:30–35, where Bezalel and Oholiab are “filled with the Spirit of God, with wisdom… to work in all kinds of craftsmanship.”

- Every blow of the hammer reflects faithful response to God’s Word, mirroring Noah’s obedience in Genesis 6:22 and pointing forward to James 1:22, which urges believers to be “doers of the word.”

six frames

- Exodus 26:22 first gave the pattern: “For the rear of the tabernacle you are to make six frames.” Exodus 36:27 shows that the builders followed the blueprint exactly, displaying the sufficiency and clarity of God’s instructions.

- Six frames—neither five nor seven—underscore precision; God’s design is neither random nor negotiable, echoing the “exact pattern” language of Hebrews 8:5.

- Practical benefit: sturdy support for the coverings (Exodus 26:1–6), reminding us that every detail, however small, contributes to the larger work of worship (1 Corinthians 14:40).

for the rear of the tabernacle

- The tabernacle had a defined front (east) and back (west). These frames specifically braced the rear, ensuring balance and symmetry with the sides (Exodus 26:15–20).

- In the wilderness setting, Israel pitched camp with the tabernacle at the center (Numbers 2:17). Strength at the rear protected the Most Holy Place where the ark rested (Exodus 26:33).

- God’s care for the “hidden” back wall teaches that unseen parts of ministry matter (1 Corinthians 12:22-24).

the west side

- Israel consistently set the entrance on the east (Exodus 27:13-16). Thus, the west wall symbolized the innermost, most secure side.

- Throughout Scripture, westward movement often pictures moving toward God’s presence; the priests entered from east to west, ending before the veil (Leviticus 16:14-15).

- The future New Jerusalem also features a completed orientation with gates on every side, west included (Revelation 21:13), highlighting God’s ordered dwelling.

summary

Exodus 36:27 shows craftsmen obeying God’s specific plan by building six sturdy frames for the tabernacle’s west rear wall. Their meticulous work demonstrates that every command—down to exact numbers and placement—carries purpose: providing strength, maintaining order, and guarding the holy presence of God. Our response today mirrors theirs: faithful, detailed obedience that trusts God’s design in both visible and hidden service.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - For the sides of the tabernacle westward. Literally correct; but it would be more intelligible to render "for the side," or "for the back."
