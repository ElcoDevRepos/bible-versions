The whole congregation of Israel
This phrase emphasizes the collective responsibility and unity of the Israelites. The term "congregation" refers to the assembly of the people of Israel, highlighting their identity as a chosen nation. This collective identity is significant throughout the Old Testament, as seen in passages like Exodus 19:6, where Israel is called a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation." The unity of the congregation is also a precursor to the New Testament concept of the Church as the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12:12-27).

must celebrate it
The command to celebrate refers to the observance of the Passover, a pivotal event in Israel's history marking their deliverance from Egyptian bondage. This celebration is not optional but a divine mandate, underscoring its importance in the life of the Israelites. The Passover is a type of Christ, as it foreshadows Jesus as the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world (John 1:29). The requirement for all to participate points to the inclusivity of God's salvation plan, later expanded to all nations through Christ (Galatians 3:28). The celebration involves specific rituals and practices, as detailed in Exodus 12, which serve as a remembrance of God's deliverance and faithfulness.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Congregation of Israel
Refers to the entire community of the Israelites, God's chosen people, who were living in Egypt at the time of the Exodus. This term emphasizes the collective identity and unity of the Israelites as they prepare to leave Egypt.

2. The Passover
A pivotal event in the history of Israel, instituted by God as a commemoration of their deliverance from slavery in Egypt. It involves the sacrifice of a lamb and the marking of doorposts with its blood, symbolizing God's protection and deliverance.

3. Egypt
The land where the Israelites were enslaved for 400 years. It serves as the backdrop for the events of the Exodus, representing bondage and oppression.

4. Moses
The leader chosen by God to deliver the Israelites from Egyptian bondage. He is the mediator of God's instructions regarding the Passover.

5. The Lord (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who commands the observance of the Passover as a perpetual ordinance for His people.
Teaching Points
Unity in Worship
The command for the "whole congregation" to celebrate the Passover highlights the importance of communal worship and unity among God's people. In the church today, believers are called to gather together in worship and remembrance of Christ's sacrifice.

Obedience to God's Commands
The Israelites were instructed to observe the Passover as a perpetual ordinance. This teaches the importance of obedience to God's commands, which are given for our good and His glory.

Remembrance and Thanksgiving
The Passover serves as a reminder of God's deliverance and faithfulness. Similarly, Christians are called to remember and give thanks for the salvation provided through Jesus Christ.

Symbolism of the Lamb
The Passover lamb is a foreshadowing of Christ, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world. This connection deepens our understanding of the sacrificial system and its fulfillment in Jesus.

Faith in Action
The observance of the Passover required faith in God's promise of deliverance. Believers today are called to live out their faith through obedience and trust in God's promises.

What Does Exodus 12:47 Mean
The whole congregation

- Every household and individual was included—no exceptions (Exodus 12:3–4).

- God did not intend the Passover to be an optional ritual for the devout few but a national act of worship (Deuteronomy 16:1–8).

- The emphasis on unity prefigures the oneness Christ prays for in John 17:20–23 and that Paul urges in 1 Corinthians 10:16–17.

of Israel

- The command is addressed to those in covenant with the LORD through Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (Genesis 17:7–8).

- Outsiders could join, but only by full identification with God’s people (Exodus 12:48–49).

- This anticipates the future gathering of believing Gentiles into the “Israel of God” through faith in Christ (Ephesians 2:11–19).

must celebrate

- “Must” shows divine mandate, not human preference (Numbers 9:13).

- To celebrate is to remember God’s salvation with joy, a pattern carried into the Lord’s Supper (Luke 22:14–20; 1 Corinthians 11:23–26).

- Neglecting the feast equaled rebellion; observing it affirmed trust in the blood of the lamb (Hebrews 11:28).

it.

- “It” points back to the Passover, the night God spared Israel through the substitutionary lamb (Exodus 12:13).

- The singular pronoun keeps focus on God’s redemptive act, not on Israel’s performance.

- Christ fulfills “it” as our Passover Lamb, sacrificed once for all (1 Corinthians 5:7; John 1:29).

summary

Exodus 12:47 mandates that every covenant member participate in the Passover. God called the entire nation—no one left out—to commemorate His saving work. The verse highlights community, covenant identity, obedience, and the central role of substitutionary sacrifice, all of which find their ultimate fulfillment in Jesus, the Lamb who secures eternal redemption for all who believe.
