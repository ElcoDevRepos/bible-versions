Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For when Pharaoh’s horses, chariots, and horsemen went into the sea
This phrase highlights the might of the Egyptian military, which was one of the most powerful forces in the ancient world. The mention of horses and chariots underscores the technological and tactical superiority of Egypt at the time. Chariots were a symbol of military strength and were used effectively in warfare. The historical context here is the pursuit of the Israelites by Pharaoh's army after their departure from Egypt, as described in Exodus 14. This event is a pivotal moment in the Exodus narrative, demonstrating God's power over the mightiest empire of the time.

the LORD brought the waters of the sea back over them
This phrase emphasizes the miraculous intervention of God in the deliverance of the Israelites. The parting and subsequent closing of the Red Sea is a central miracle in the Old Testament, showcasing God's sovereignty over nature. This act of divine judgment against Pharaoh's army is a fulfillment of God's promise to protect His people. It also serves as a typology of salvation, where God delivers His people from bondage and destroys their enemies. This event is often referenced in other parts of Scripture, such as in Psalm 136:15 and Nehemiah 9:11, as a testament to God's enduring faithfulness and power.

But the Israelites walked through the sea on dry ground
This phrase highlights the miraculous nature of the Israelites' escape. Walking on dry ground in the midst of the sea signifies God's provision and protection. It is a demonstration of His ability to make a way where there seems to be none. This event is a foreshadowing of the salvation offered through Jesus Christ, who provides a way for believers to pass from death to life. The dry ground can also symbolize the firm foundation of faith that believers are called to walk upon. This miraculous crossing is a defining moment for the Israelites, marking their transition from slavery to freedom and serving as a powerful reminder of God's deliverance.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Pharaoh
The ruler of Egypt who pursued the Israelites after initially allowing them to leave. His hardened heart led to the ultimate downfall of his army.

2. Horses, Chariots, and Horsemen
Represent the military might of Egypt, which was considered one of the most powerful armies of the ancient world. Their destruction signifies God's power over earthly forces.

3. The Sea
Refers to the Red Sea, which God miraculously parted to allow the Israelites to escape from the Egyptians. It symbolizes both deliverance and judgment.

4. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who demonstrates His sovereignty and faithfulness to His people by delivering them from their enemies.

5. The Israelites
God's chosen people, who were delivered from slavery in Egypt and led through the sea on dry ground, signifying their liberation and God's provision.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Creation
The parting and closing of the sea demonstrate God's control over nature. Believers can trust in God's power to intervene in seemingly impossible situations.

Deliverance and Judgment
The same waters that delivered the Israelites brought judgment upon the Egyptians. This duality serves as a reminder of God's justice and mercy.

Faith in Action
The Israelites' walk through the sea on dry ground is an act of faith. Believers are called to step out in faith, trusting God's promises even when the path seems uncertain.

God's Faithfulness
The event underscores God's faithfulness to His covenant promises. Christians can find assurance in God's unchanging nature and His commitment to His people.

Victory Over Enemies
The destruction of Pharaoh's army symbolizes God's ultimate victory over the forces that oppose His people. Believers can find hope in God's promise of victory over spiritual adversaries.

What Does Exodus 15:19 Mean
For when Pharaoh’s horses

Pharaoh had amassed the best cavalry in the ancient world, symbols of might and speed (Exodus 14:6-7). Scripture often shows rulers trusting in horses rather than in God (Psalm 20:7; Isaiah 31:1), and this verse reminds us that any power opposing the LORD’s purposes will ultimately fail.

Key takeaways:

• Egypt’s horses represent human confidence.

• God allowed them to enter the same path He opened for Israel, demonstrating He controls both the route and the timing (Proverbs 21:30-31).

Chariots

The chariot was the tank of its day—fast, intimidating, and lethal (Exodus 14:17). Yet these impressive war machines could do nothing against the Creator who splits seas (Joshua 17:18).

Consider:

• God often uses the very weapons of the enemy to showcase His supremacy (Psalm 76:6).

• The inclusion of chariots underlines total Egyptian commitment to destroy Israel—making their defeat complete proof of divine deliverance (Deuteronomy 11:4).

And horsemen went into the sea

Egypt’s elite troops followed their king’s command right into a miraculous corridor. Their obedience to Pharaoh contrasted sharply with Israel’s obedience to God (Exodus 14:31).

Notice:

• Blind allegiance to a rebellious leader brings ruin (Proverbs 11:21).

• The scene fulfills God’s promise to gain glory over Pharaoh and all his army (Exodus 14:17-18).

The LORD brought the waters of the sea back over them

At God’s word, the walls of water that had stood like sentinels collapsed (Exodus 14:26-28). Judgment was swift, final, and inescapable.

Points to ponder:

• The same sea that saved Israel destroyed Egypt—grace and judgment from the same hand (Romans 11:22).

• God’s timing was perfect: He waited until every Egyptian was inside (Psalm 136:15).

But the Israelites walked through the sea on dry ground

Dry ground in the middle of the sea highlights a miracle that is both physical and spiritual (Hebrews 11:29). Israel’s walk was an act of faith, made possible solely by divine intervention (Exodus 14:21-22).

Implications:

• God makes a way where none exists (Isaiah 43:16).

• His people experience deliverance, not by their strength, but by trusting His word (2 Chronicles 20:17).

• The contrast between drowning Egyptians and dry-footed Israelites underscores the covenant difference (Psalm 66:5-6).

summary

Exodus 15:19 celebrates the moment when God’s people were forever separated from their oppressors. Pharaoh’s finest forces—horses, chariots, and horsemen—charged into a path God carved, only to be swallowed when the waters returned. Meanwhile, Israel safely crossed on dry ground, proving that the LORD defends His covenant people, overturns human pride, and turns the instruments of oppression into scenes of divine victory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) This verse is parenthetic. It forms no part of the "Song of Moses." Originally, perhaps, when that song was a separate document, it was appended as an historical comment, showing the occasion on which the poem was composed. When the records of Moses were collected--either by himself, towards the close of his life, or by Joshua--the addition was kept, although it had become unnecessary for the original purpose. As it stands, it emphasises the great fact of Israel's final deliverance--the nucleus around which Exodus gathers itself.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 19-21. - Sequel to the Song. The "sequel" treats of two quite separate masters.

1. It asserts, in verse 19, the historic groundwork of the song, reiterating in a condensed form the three principal facts of the presage - already recorded in ch. 14. -

(a) Israel's safe transit across the sea-bed;

(b) the pursuit attempted by the Egyptian chariot-force; end . . .
