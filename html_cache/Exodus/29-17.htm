Cut the ram into pieces
This phrase refers to the specific instructions given to the priests during the consecration ceremony. The act of cutting the ram into pieces symbolizes the thoroughness and completeness required in the sacrificial system. It reflects the seriousness of sin and the need for a complete offering to God. This practice is consistent with other sacrificial rituals in the Old Testament, such as those found in Leviticus 1:6, where the burnt offering is also cut into pieces. The division of the animal can be seen as a type of Christ, whose body was broken for the sins of humanity, fulfilling the sacrificial system once and for all (Hebrews 10:10).

Wash the entrails and legs
Washing the entrails and legs signifies purification and cleanliness, which are essential themes in the Levitical laws. The entrails and legs, being parts of the animal that come into contact with impurities, needed to be cleansed before being offered to God. This act of washing can be seen as a metaphor for spiritual cleansing, emphasizing the need for purity in worship and service to God. In the New Testament, this concept is echoed in passages like John 13:10, where Jesus speaks of the need for His disciples to be spiritually clean.

And place them with its head and other pieces
The placement of the washed parts with the head and other pieces indicates the reassembly of the animal on the altar, symbolizing unity and completeness in the offering. This act ensures that the entire animal is presented to God, signifying total dedication and surrender. The head, representing leadership and authority, is placed with the other parts, highlighting the importance of offering every aspect of one's life to God. This comprehensive offering prefigures the total sacrifice of Christ, who gave Himself wholly for the redemption of humanity (Ephesians 5:2).

Teaching Points
Holiness and Preparation
The washing of the entrails and legs signifies the importance of purity and preparation in approaching God. Believers are called to examine their hearts and lives, seeking holiness in their walk with God.

Symbolism of Sacrifice
The cutting and arranging of the ram parts symbolize the completeness of the sacrifice. In our lives, we are called to offer every part of ourselves to God, holding nothing back.

Priestly Role of Believers
Just as Aaron and his sons were set apart for service, believers today are called to be a royal priesthood, serving God and others with dedication and love.

Obedience to God's Instructions
The detailed instructions given to Moses highlight the importance of obedience in worship. Believers are encouraged to follow God's Word carefully and faithfully.

What Does Exodus 29:17 Mean
Cut the ram into pieces

• Exodus 29:17 opens with the directive: “Cut the ram into pieces”.

• The ram is the second of two offered in the priests’ ordination (see Exodus 29:15-18).

• Dividing the animal pictures total consecration—every part is surrendered to God, echoing the way a burnt offering was prepared in Leviticus 1:6-9.

• Nothing remains withheld; it foreshadows the believer’s call to present his “whole body as a living sacrifice” (Romans 12:1).

• The cutting also prefigures Christ’s complete self-offering, later affirmed in Hebrews 10:10-14.

Wash the entrails and legs

• Next, Moses is told to “wash the entrails and legs” (Exodus 29:17).

• Internal organs and the parts that touched the earth needed cleansing, underscoring God’s demand for inward and outward purity (Psalm 24:3-4; Matthew 23:26).

• This act anticipates the spiritual washing believers receive—“our bodies washed with pure water” (Hebrews 10:22), and reminds us to “cleanse ourselves from all filthiness of flesh and spirit” (2 Corinthians 7:1).

• The priests could not skip this step; holiness is never optional (Leviticus 8:20-21).

Place them with its head and other pieces

• After washing, the instructions continue: “place them with its head and other pieces” (Exodus 29:17).

• Reassembling all parts on the altar ensures a complete offering. It reflects unity—head and body together—hinting at the later truth that Christ is the head and we are His body (Ephesians 1:22-23).

• Every cleansed piece is ready for the fire, symbolizing wholehearted devotion that rises as “a pleasing aroma to the LORD” (Leviticus 9:13-14; Philippians 4:18).

• The order—cut, wash, reunite—mirrors the gospel pattern: conviction, cleansing, and communion.

summary

Exodus 29:17 gives a tangible picture of consecration. The cutting shows total surrender, the washing highlights holiness, and the reassembling points to unified, wholehearted worship. As the priests followed these steps, they modeled the life God still desires—people fully offered, cleansed inside and out, and gathered under their Head for His glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) Thou shalt cut the ram in pieces.--This was the ordinary practice, not only among the Hebrews, but also among other nations, as the Egyptians (Herod. ii. 40), the Greeks, the Romans, and others. It was probably found to facilitate the burning of the animal, which was with difficulty consumed entire. The shoulder, thigh, head, ribs, rump, heart, and kidneys appear separate in the representations of sacrifices on Egyptian altars.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - Thou shalt cut the ram in pieces. Literally, "into its pieces," which Kalisch supposes to mean "into its natural limbs." Egyptian sculptures show us animals thus cut up, and offered at sacrificial feasts to ancestors. Wash its inwards - i.e., its "intestines" - probably the stomach and bowels only. Its legs. The lower joints of the leg, with the foot, to which it was likely that dust might attach. Put them unto his pieces - i.e., "replace them after washing with the other pieces," or joints, into which the animal had been cut.
