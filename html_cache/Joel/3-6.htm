You sold the people of Judah and Jerusalem
This phrase highlights the betrayal and exploitation of the people of Judah and Jerusalem. Historically, the selling of people into slavery was a common practice among conquering nations. The people of Judah and Jerusalem were often caught in the crossfire of larger empires vying for power. This selling into slavery can be seen as a direct violation of the covenant relationship that God had with His people, where they were to be a distinct and protected nation. The selling of Israelites into slavery is reminiscent of the earlier Egyptian bondage, which God had delivered them from, and it underscores the severity of their current plight.

to the Greeks
The mention of the Greeks is significant as it indicates the far-reaching consequences of the people's disobedience and the extent of their dispersion. During the time of Joel, the Greeks were emerging as a significant power in the Mediterranean world. This reference may also foreshadow the later Hellenistic influence that would come to dominate the region after the conquests of Alexander the Great. The Greeks, known for their trade and expansive reach, would have been a likely destination for slaves, indicating the widespread nature of the Israelites' dispersion.

to send them far from their homeland
This phrase emphasizes the complete removal and alienation of the people from their land, which was central to their identity and covenant with God. The land of Israel was not just a physical territory but a symbol of God's promise and blessing. Being sent far from their homeland signifies a loss of identity, heritage, and spiritual connection. This dispersion can be seen as a form of judgment for the people's sins, yet it also sets the stage for future restoration, as prophesied in other parts of Scripture. The theme of exile and return is a recurring motif in the Bible, pointing to the ultimate redemption and gathering of God's people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. People of Judah and Jerusalem
The inhabitants of the southern kingdom of Judah, including the city of Jerusalem, who were God's chosen people and often faced judgment and exile due to their disobedience.

2. The Greeks
Refers to the people of Greece, representing foreign nations to whom the people of Judah and Jerusalem were sold. This indicates a historical context of trade and slavery.

3. Exile
The event of being forcibly removed from one's homeland, which was a common consequence of disobedience and sin for the Israelites, as seen throughout the Old Testament.
Teaching Points
The Consequences of Disobedience
The selling of Judah and Jerusalem's people is a direct result of their disobedience to God. This serves as a reminder of the serious consequences that come from turning away from God's commands.

God's Sovereignty Over Nations
Despite the actions of foreign nations, God remains sovereign. He uses these events to fulfill His purposes and bring His people back to repentance.

The Call to Repentance
The exile and suffering of God's people are not the end of the account. They are a call to repentance and a return to God, who is always ready to restore those who turn back to Him.

The Importance of Justice
The selling of people into slavery is a grave injustice. As Christians, we are called to stand against such injustices and advocate for the oppressed, reflecting God's heart for justice.

What Does Joel 3:6 Mean
You sold

- God confronts the nations around Judah (cf. Joel 3:4) for treating His people as merchandise, a grave violation of His covenant promise: “They cast lots for My people” (Joel 3:3).

- Selling a human being as property is condemned throughout Scripture; compare Amos 2:6, where Israel’s own leaders are rebuked for having “sold the righteous for silver.”

- The Lord underscores His ownership of His people (Isaiah 43:1), so any attempt to traffic them is a direct affront to Him.

the people of Judah and Jerusalem

- This phrase identifies the covenant community—those living in the southern kingdom, anchored by the holy city, Jerusalem (cf. Zechariah 2:8).

- Their unique status:

• Chosen by God (Deuteronomy 7:6)

• Custodians of the Temple (1 Kings 9:3)

• Recipients of messianic promises (Micah 5:2)

- Harming Judah is equated with touching “the apple of His eye” (Zechariah 2:8), ensuring divine response.

to the Greeks

- The offenders (Tyre, Sidon, Philistia; Joel 3:4) ship captives westward, likely through established Mediterranean trade routes (Ezekiel 27:13).

- “Greeks” (literally, “Yavan”) points to coastal Ionia and the wider Hellenic world, foreshadowing later interactions (Daniel 8:21).

- The transaction highlights:

• Profiteering over people (Obadiah 11)

• Alliance with pagan powers against God’s plan (Psalm 83:5–8)

to send them far from their homeland

- Distance compounds the cruelty—removal from the Promised Land severed worship at the Temple (Psalm 137:1–4).

- Exile fulfilled covenant warnings: “The LORD will drive you to a nation unknown to you” (Deuteronomy 28:36, 64).

- God, however, pledges reversal: “I will bring Judah and Jerusalem back from captivity” (Joel 3:1), echoing earlier rescues (Jeremiah 30:10).

summary

Joel 3:6 records a historical injustice—Judah’s neighbors selling God’s people to distant Greeks. The verse underscores the sanctity of God’s covenant community, exposes the sin of human trafficking for profit, and anticipates divine restitution. What men meant for oppression, the Lord promises to judge and ultimately overturn, vindicating His people and His name.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Unto the Grecians.--Javan, the Hebrew name for Greece, is mentioned in Ezekiel as one of the representatives of the heathen nations who "traded (with Tyre) the persons of men and vessels in her market" (Ezekiel 27:13). The Grecian traffic in slaves was enormous.
