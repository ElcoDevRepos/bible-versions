Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Do not be afraid, O land;
This phrase addresses the land of Judah, which had suffered devastation from a locust plague and drought. The command to not be afraid is a common biblical exhortation, often given by God or His messengers to reassure His people in times of distress (e.g., Isaiah 41:10, Joshua 1:9). The land itself is personified, highlighting the extent of the calamity and the promise of restoration. The fear mentioned here is not just physical but also spiritual, as the land's desolation was seen as a sign of divine displeasure.

rejoice and be glad,
The call to rejoice and be glad is a response to the anticipated reversal of fortune. In the Hebrew context, joy and gladness are often associated with God's deliverance and blessings (e.g., Psalm 126:3, Isaiah 35:10). This joy is not merely emotional but is rooted in the covenant relationship with God, who promises to restore and bless His people. The dual command to rejoice and be glad emphasizes the completeness of the restoration.

for the LORD has done great things.
This phrase points to the mighty acts of God, both in the past and in the anticipated future. The "great things" can refer to God's historical acts of deliverance, such as the Exodus (Exodus 14:31), and also to the future restoration and blessings promised to Judah. Theologically, it underscores God's sovereignty and faithfulness. The phrase also has eschatological overtones, pointing to the ultimate redemption and restoration through Christ, who fulfills God's promises (Luke 1:49, Ephesians 3:20). The great things done by the LORD serve as a reminder of His power and His commitment to His people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joel
The prophet who authored the Book of Joel. He is known for his prophecies concerning the Day of the Lord and the call to repentance.

2. The Land
Refers to the land of Judah, which had experienced devastation due to locust plagues and drought, symbolizing God's judgment.

3. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who is the central figure in the prophecy, bringing both judgment and restoration.

4. The Locust Plague
A significant event in the Book of Joel, symbolizing both literal and metaphorical devastation, which serves as a backdrop for the call to repentance and promise of restoration.

5. The Day of the LORD
A recurring theme in Joel, representing a time of both judgment and salvation, when God intervenes in human history.
Teaching Points
Fear Not, Trust in God's Sovereignty
Despite circumstances that may seem dire, believers are called to trust in God's control and His ability to bring about good from difficult situations.

Rejoice in God's Faithfulness
The call to rejoice is rooted in the recognition of God's past faithfulness and His promises for the future. Reflect on how God has been faithful in your life and let that be a source of joy.

Recognize God's Great Works
Acknowledge and celebrate the great things God has done, both in biblical history and in personal experiences. This recognition strengthens faith and encourages a hopeful outlook.

Restoration and Renewal
God's promise of restoration is not just for the land but for our lives as well. Consider areas in your life that need God's renewing touch and seek His intervention.

The Call to Repentance and Hope
Joel's message includes a call to repentance, which precedes the promise of restoration. Reflect on areas where repentance is needed and embrace the hope that follows genuine repentance.

What Does Joel 2:21 Mean
Do not be afraid

The command is immediate and compassionate. God addresses fear before anything else, reminding His people that He is fully aware of their circumstances.

• Similar reassurances appear in Isaiah 41:10—“Do not fear, for I am with you”—and in Matthew 14:27, where Jesus tells the frightened disciples, “Take courage! It is I; do not be afraid.”

• Fear dissipates when faith recognizes the nearness of the Lord (Psalm 46:1-2).

• In Joel’s context, locust devastation had stripped hope, yet God’s first word is that dread is unnecessary because He is intervening.

O land

The address broadens from individuals to creation itself. The soil that had been ravaged is told not to fear because its future rests in the Creator’s hands (Psalm 24:1).

• God speaks to the land in Leviticus 26:4-5, promising abundance when His people walk faithfully.

• Romans 8:19-22 notes that creation groans under corruption but looks forward to restoration—a theme previewed here.

• The land’s healing signals God’s holistic redemption plan: people and environment together find renewal under His rule.

rejoice and be glad

Joy replaces fear, showing the expected response to divine rescue.

• Psalm 118:24 captures the same switch: “This is the day the LORD has made; let us rejoice and be glad in it.”

• Philippians 4:4 urges continual rejoicing “in the Lord,” anchoring happiness not in circumstances but in God’s character.

• Joel outlines a pattern: God speaks, fear subsides, joy erupts—revealing that true gladness is always grounded in His word and works.

for the LORD has done great things

The reason for fearless joy rests on God’s proven track record, not wishful thinking.

• Psalm 126:3 echoes, “The LORD has done great things for us; we are glad.”

• 1 Samuel 12:24 calls Israel to “consider what great things He has done for you,” tying past deliverance to present obedience.

• These “great things” include reversing the locust plague (Joel 2:25), restoring harvests (v. 24), and ultimately pouring out His Spirit (v. 28). The covenant-keeping God acts decisively, and His mighty deeds invite confident celebration.

summary

Joel 2:21 moves from fear to overflowing joy because God Himself intervenes. He commands creation and His people alike to live unafraid, rejoice, and remember His mighty acts. When we reflect on His past faithfulness and present power, bold confidence replaces anxiety, and worship springs naturally from grateful hearts.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Fear not, O land . . .--The sentence of the reversal of judgment has gone forth, and all nature--animate and inanimate, rational and irrational--which had been included in the curse is summoned to rejoice in the blessing vouchsafed by the Lord.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 21-23. - In these verses the land and beasts and men are addressed respectively. Thus the promise is fully developed. In ver. 21 the prophet summons the earth; in ver. 22 the beasts of the field; and in ver. 23 the sons of Zion; all are called to joy and gladness on account of the great deliverance from destruction which the Lord had wrought for them. They are all called on to rejoice in the great deliverance; the land, personified, is summoned to exult and rejoice for the great things God now promises to do or is doing to it. If the locusts had done great things in destruction, God will do great things in deliverance. The beasts are also personified, and forbidden to be afraid; for whereas they had groaned and cried for want of herbage when the pastures were burnt up, those pastures are now beginning to spring, and the fruit trees yield their strength. The children of Zion are invited to rejoice, not only in the delivered land, or springing pastures, or fruitful figs, or blooming vines, or other trees however useful or ornamental; but, as became them with their superior intelligence, in the Lord their God, as the Father of mercies and the Giver of every good and perfect gift, whether temporal or spiritual. At the same time, their temporal wants would be attended to, and their land fertilized by the suitable and sustaining shower. The prophet individualizes the earth, the beasts of the field, and the sons of Zion. Verse 21. - Fear not, O land; be glad and rejoice: for the Lord will do great things. The land had suffered severely from the drought connected with the locusts; but is now summoned to joy and gladness. The prophet assigns for this an appropriate reason: the locusts had done great things in damaging it; Jehovah now does great things in their destruction. When the earth clothes itself with verdure, and brings forth its fruits and flowers and various products, it is said, by a bold but beautiful personification, to rejoice and even exult. Thus the Latins said in like manner, Rident arva, ridet ager. Things are now reversed. Instead of mourning, is exultation; instead of mourning and its visible emblem in girding with sadness, there is joy and gladness; instead of the day of the Lord: very great and terrible or fearful, is "Fear not." Se-machi is fem. imper. Qal in pause for the ordinary simchi.
