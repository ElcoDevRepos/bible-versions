Bakbukiah and Unni
Bakbukiah and Unni are Levites mentioned in the context of the dedication of the wall of Jerusalem. The Levites were a tribe set apart for religious duties, and their role was crucial in the worship and spiritual life of Israel. Bakbukiah is noted elsewhere in Nehemiah as a leader among the Levites, indicating his significant role in the community. Unni, though less frequently mentioned, shares in this important service. Their inclusion highlights the importance of leadership and service in worship.

their associates
The term "associates" refers to other Levites who were involved in the temple services. This collective effort underscores the communal nature of worship and the importance of teamwork in fulfilling religious duties. It reflects the organized structure of Levitical service, as seen in other parts of the Old Testament, such as in 1 Chronicles, where the divisions of the Levites are detailed.

stood across from them
This phrase suggests a specific arrangement or positioning during the service, possibly indicating a form of antiphonal singing or responsive worship, a practice where groups sing or chant in response to each other. This method of worship is seen in other parts of scripture, such as in the Psalms, and emphasizes the participatory and dynamic nature of worship in ancient Israel.

in the services
The services refer to the religious duties and ceremonies conducted by the Levites, which included singing, playing instruments, and other acts of worship. These services were integral to the spiritual life of the community and were conducted according to the instructions given in the Law of Moses. The dedication of the wall was a significant event, symbolizing the restoration and renewal of the community, and the Levites' role in these services was essential in leading the people in worship and thanksgiving.

Teaching Points

Worship leaders like Bakbukiah and Unni play a crucial role in guiding the congregation in worship. Their dedication and service set a standard for those involved in church music and worship today.

The mention of "associates" emphasizes the importance of working together in ministry. Effective worship and service require collaboration and unity among believers.

What Does Nehemiah 12:9 Mean
Bakbukiah and Unni

• Scripture names these two Levites among the singers: “Bakbukiah and Unni… stood across from them” (Nehemiah 12:9).

• Earlier, Bakbukiah is called “the second among his brothers” who led thanksgiving (Nehemiah 11:17), signaling a leadership role in worship.

• Unni appears only here, yet his inclusion with Bakbukiah links him to the same sacred task (compare Nehemiah 12:8, where Mattaniah, Bakbukiah, and others are listed among the chief singers).

• Their prominence underscores how God records and values every servant who preserves pure worship (see 1 Chronicles 9:15 for similar named Levites).

their associates

• The phrase widens the scene from two individuals to an entire team of Levites dedicated to music and praise (Nehemiah 12:8, 24).

• “Associates” signals cooperation—no one leads worship alone. Just as Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun served with their families in David’s day (1 Chronicles 25:1), these men minister with a supportive network.

• The verse reminds us that obedient community service pleases the Lord (Psalm 133:1; Philippians 1:27).

stood across from them

• The singers took positions opposite one another, forming two choirs responding antiphonally. Nehemiah later describes this same pattern during the wall-dedication: “One thanksgiving choir proceeded to the right… the other choir went to the left” (Nehemiah 12:31, 38).

• This setup echoes earlier temple practice where Levites “stood… with instruments of David” while priests blew trumpets “opposite them” (2 Chronicles 29:26).

• The posture conveys vigilance and readiness, mirroring our call to “stand firm” in worship and warfare alike (Ephesians 6:13).

in the services

• “Services” (or “service of praise”) points to organized, ongoing ministry in the temple courts—not a one-time performance (Nehemiah 10:39; 1 Chronicles 16:37).

• Their duties included:

– Leading congregational singing (Ezra 3:11).

– Offering thanksgiving at prescribed times (Nehemiah 11:17).

– Guarding purity of doctrine through song, since lyrics reinforced covenant truth (Colossians 3:16).

• By stationing teams “across from” each other, the Levites ensured unbroken praise, reflecting the heavenly pattern where choirs answer one another day and night (Revelation 4:8).

summary

Nehemiah 12:9 shows two leading Levites—Bakbukiah and Unni—joined by fellow singers, stationed opposite another choir to provide continuous, responsive praise during temple services. The verse highlights organized, cooperative worship, the value God places on every faithful servant, and the call for believers to stand alert in unified adoration.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - Bakbukiah and Unni. Bakbukiah's position with respect to Mattaniah has been already mentioned (Nehemiah 11:17). "Unni" appears, in this place only, as a Levite of Zerubbabel's time. Were over against them in the watches. i.e. "ministered in their courses, as the others did, and kept their stations over against them in their turns of attendance, which are called their 'watches' or wards" (Bp. Patrick). LIST OF THE HIGH PRIESTS FROM JESHUA TO JADDUA (Nehemiah 12:10, 11). That this is the line of descent in the high priestly family of the time sufficiently appears both from the names themselves, and from the position assigned to those who bore them in vers. 22, 23, 26. Whether all of them actually exercised the high priest's office is left uncertain in Scripture, but satisfactorily established by Josephus. The six names cover a space of at least 205 years - from the conquest of Babylon by Cyrus, B.C. 538, to the submission of Jerusalem to Alexander the Great, B.C. 333, which gives very long generations, but still such as are historically possible. Jeshua was certainly high priest from B.C. 538 to B.C. 516. He may have been succeeded by his son, Joiakim, about B.C. 490. Joiakim had certainly been succeeded by his son, Eliashib, before B.C. 444 (Nehemiah 3:1); and Eliashib was probably succeeded by Joiada about B.C. 420. Joiada's high priesthood may be assigned to the period between B.C. 420 and 380; Jonathan's to that between B.C. 380 and 350. Jaddua might then hold the dignity from B.C. 350 to 330, or later, and so be brought into contact with Alexander the Great. It is questioned whether in that case Nehemiah can have written the present passage, and certain that he cannot have done so unless he lived to be at least 131 years of age. As this is exceedingly improbable, it is best to suppose, either that the whole list was placed here by Malachi, or at any rate that that prophet added the clause, "and Jonathan begat Jaddua."
