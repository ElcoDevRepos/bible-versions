Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
These are the words of Nehemiah son of Hacaliah:
Nehemiah is introduced as the son of Hacaliah, a name not mentioned elsewhere in the Bible, indicating Nehemiah's humble origins. Nehemiah's role as a cupbearer to the Persian king suggests a position of trust and influence, which God uses for His purposes. The phrase "the words of Nehemiah" indicates a personal account, providing authenticity and a direct perspective on the events described. Nehemiah's leadership and dedication to God's work are central themes, reflecting the qualities of a godly leader.

In the month of Chislev:
Chislev corresponds to November-December in the Gregorian calendar, marking the beginning of winter. This timing is significant as it sets the stage for the events that follow, including Nehemiah's journey to Jerusalem. The mention of Chislev provides a chronological anchor, situating Nehemiah's narrative within the Jewish calendar, which is important for understanding the sequence of events in the book.

In the twentieth year:
This refers to the twentieth year of King Artaxerxes I's reign, around 445 BC. The historical context is crucial, as it situates Nehemiah's mission within the broader narrative of the Persian Empire's rule over Judah. This period follows the return of the exiles under Zerubbabel and Ezra, highlighting the ongoing process of restoration and rebuilding in Jerusalem.

While I was in the citadel of Susa:
Susa, one of the capitals of the Persian Empire, was a significant administrative center. Archaeological findings confirm its grandeur and importance. Nehemiah's presence in Susa indicates his role within the Persian court, providing him with access to the king. This setting underscores the providential placement of Nehemiah in a position to influence the king's decisions regarding Jerusalem. Susa's mention also connects Nehemiah's story to that of Esther, who lived in the same city, illustrating God's continued work through His people in foreign lands.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Nehemiah
The central figure in the book, Nehemiah is a Jewish cupbearer to the Persian king Artaxerxes. His name means "Yahweh comforts," reflecting his role in the restoration of Jerusalem.

2. Hacaliah
Nehemiah's father, mentioned to establish Nehemiah's lineage and identity within the Jewish community.

3. Chislev
The ninth month of the Hebrew calendar, corresponding to November-December. This timing is significant for the events that unfold in Nehemiah's account.

4. Twentieth Year
Refers to the twentieth year of King Artaxerxes' reign, providing a historical context for the account.

5. Citadel of Susa
A fortified palace complex in the Persian Empire, indicating Nehemiah's position of influence and proximity to the king.

What Does Nehemiah 1:1 Mean
These are the words of Nehemiah son of Hacaliah

- The opening phrase mirrors introductions like “The words of Jeremiah” (Jeremiah 1:1) and “The words of Amos” (Amos 1:1), signaling a first-person, eyewitness record that the Spirit intends for instruction (2 Peter 1:21).

- Naming his father grounds Nehemiah in real history and covenant lineage, just as genealogies in Ezra 2:62 and Matthew 1:1–17 establish authenticity.

- Scripture’s accuracy is underscored: what follows is not legend but reliable testimony that will reveal God’s faithfulness to His promises (Psalm 119:89).

In the month of Chislev

- Chislev is the ninth month (late November/December). Zechariah 7:1 uses the same marker, anchoring prophetic events to an exact date.

- The timing—approaching winter—heightens the urgency of Jerusalem’s broken walls (Nehemiah 1:3).

- God’s Word consistently ties redemptive acts to the calendar (Luke 2:1–2; Exodus 12:2), reminding us that He works within real time, not myth.

in the twentieth year

- This refers to the twentieth year of Artaxerxes I (445 BC). Nehemiah 2:1 confirms the same regnal year, linking chapters 1–2 into one coherent narrative.

- By mentioning a foreign king, the text shows God steering secular rulers for His purposes (Ezra 1:1; Proverbs 21:1).

- The date also bridges to Ezra 7:7 (seventh year of Artaxerxes), letting us trace God’s multi-stage restoration plan across decades.

while I was in the citadel of Susa

- Susa (Shushan) was the Persian winter palace (Esther 1:2; Daniel 8:2). Its “citadel” was both fortress and administrative hub.

- Nehemiah’s presence there as cupbearer (Nehemiah 1:11) parallels Joseph in Pharaoh’s court (Genesis 41:41), illustrating how God places servants strategically (Esther 4:14; Matthew 5:14-16).

- The contrast between Susa’s luxury and Jerusalem’s ruin sets up the tension of the book and highlights Nehemiah’s God-given burden (Philippians 2:13).

summary

Nehemiah 1:1 is a tightly packed introduction that affirms historical precision, trustworthy authorship, and divine orchestration. Every phrase—who, when, and where—roots the coming story in verifiable reality and reveals a sovereign God who works through specific people, dates, and places to advance His redemptive plan.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
I.

(1) The words of Nehemiah.--Rather, The narrative or record. Both as referring to his affairs and as written by him.

(1-3) Introductory: tidings brought to Nehemiah concerning the sad estate of Jerusalem and the people.

(1) In the month Chisleu.--The names rather than the numbers of the months are generally employed after the captivity: Nisan, Iyar, Sivan, Tammuz, Av, Elul, Tishri, Marchesvan, Chisleu, Tebeth, Shevat, Adar; with an intercalary month, the second Adar. Chisleu answers nearly to our December.

In the twentieth year.--Of the reign of Artaxerxes Longimanus, which began B.C. 465 and ended B.C. 425.

In Shushan the palace.--Susa, the capital of Susiana; where, after the capture of the Babylonian empire, a great palace was built by Darius Hystaspis, the ruins of which are still seen. It was the principal and favourite residence of the Persian court, alternating with Persepolis, the older capital, and Babylon. Shushan was one of the most ancient cities in the world; and is associated with the visions of Daniel, and with the feast of Ahasuerus (Daniel 8:2, Esther 1:3).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - The words of Nehemiah the son of Hachaliah. Compare Jeremiah 1:1; Hosea 1:2; Amos 1:1, etc. No other historical book commences in this manner, and we may best account for the introduction of the clause by the consideration that "Nehemiah" having been originally appended to "Ezra," it marked the point at which a new narrative began by a new author. The month Chisleu. The word Chisleu, or rather Kislev, is probably Persian. It was unknown to the Jews before the captivity, and is found only in this passage and in Zechariah 7:1, where Kislev is said to be "the ninth month," corresponding nearly to our December. The twentieth year. The twentieth regnal year of Artaxerxes (Longimanus) is intended (see ch. 2:1). This began in B.C. 445, and terminated in B.C. 444. Shushan the palace, where Daniel saw the vision of the ram with two horns (Daniel 8:2), and Ahasuerus (Xerxes) made his great feast to all his princes and servants (Esther 1:3), is beyond all doubt Susa, the capital city of Kissia, or Susiana, one of the most ancient cities in the world, and the place which, from the time of Darius Hystaspis was the principal residence of the Persian court. It was situated in the fertile plain east of the Lower Tigris, and lay on or near the river Choaspes, probably at the spot now known as Sus, or Shush. Remains of the palace were discovered by the expedition under Sir Fenwick Williams in the year 1852, and have been graphically described by Mr. Loftus ('Chaldaea and Susiana,' pp. 373-375).
