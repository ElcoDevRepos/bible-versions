Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But they were disobedient and rebelled against You;
This phrase highlights the recurring theme of Israel's rebellion against God. Throughout the Old Testament, Israel's disobedience is a central issue, as seen in the wilderness wanderings (Numbers 14:9) and during the period of the Judges (Judges 2:11-19). This rebellion is not just a failure to follow commands but a deeper spiritual defiance against God's authority and covenant relationship.

they flung Your law behind their backs.
This vivid imagery suggests a deliberate rejection of God's commandments. The law, given through Moses, was meant to guide Israel in righteousness (Deuteronomy 6:1-9). By casting it behind their backs, the people showed contempt and disregard for God's instructions, similar to the actions of King Jehoiakim who burned the scroll of Jeremiah (Jeremiah 36:23).

They killed Your prophets, who had admonished them to return to You.
The killing of prophets is a tragic pattern in Israel's history. Prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Zechariah faced persecution and death for speaking God's truth (2 Chronicles 24:20-21, Jeremiah 26:20-23). This reflects a rejection of God's messengers and a refusal to heed calls for repentance, paralleling Jesus' lament over Jerusalem's treatment of prophets (Matthew 23:37).

They committed terrible blasphemies.
Blasphemy in this context refers to acts of profound disrespect towards God, often involving idolatry and false worship (Ezekiel 20:27-28). Such actions violated the first commandment (Exodus 20:3) and were seen as spiritual adultery. This phrase underscores the severity of Israel's sin, which ultimately led to their exile, yet also points to the need for divine mercy and forgiveness, themes fulfilled in the New Testament through Christ's atoning work.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Israelites
The people of Israel, who are the primary subjects of this verse, are described as disobedient and rebellious against God.

2. God
The divine being against whom the Israelites rebelled, whose laws they disregarded.

3. Prophets
Messengers sent by God to admonish the Israelites and call them back to faithfulness.

4. Law
Refers to the commandments and teachings given by God, which the Israelites rejected.

5. Blasphemies
Acts of disrespect or irreverence towards God, committed by the Israelites.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Disobedience
Disobedience to God leads to spiritual decline and separation from His blessings. We must be vigilant in our obedience to God's Word.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets are God's messengers, and their role is crucial in guiding and correcting God's people. We should heed the warnings and teachings of those who speak God's truth today.

Rejection of God's Law
Disregarding God's commandments is akin to turning our backs on Him. We must prioritize His Word in our lives and not treat it lightly.

Consequences of Blasphemy
Blasphemy and irreverence towards God have serious consequences. We should cultivate a heart of reverence and respect for God in all aspects of our lives.

Repentance and Restoration
Despite Israel's rebellion, God's desire was always for their repentance and restoration. We should be quick to repent and seek God's forgiveness when we stray.

What Does Nehemiah 9:26 Mean
But they were disobedient and rebelled against You

Israel’s long pattern of resistance to God is on display.

• Disobedience is not mere occasional failure; it is willful (Judges 2:11-19).

• Rebellion is pictured as rising up against the rightful King (1 Samuel 8:7-8; Isaiah 1:2).

• This confession in Nehemiah 9 comes from a people freshly reminded of God’s covenant mercy, yet they recall how their fathers “stiffened their necks” (Nehemiah 9:16-17).

The verse reminds us that sin is ultimately personal—directed “against You,” the Lord Himself (Psalm 51:4).

They flung Your law behind their backs

The image is vivid: God’s instruction is treated like garbage tossed over the shoulder.

• Ignoring the Law was not ignorance but contempt (Jeremiah 6:19; Hosea 4:6).

• God had given the Law for their good (Deuteronomy 10:12-13), yet they preferred autonomy (2 Kings 17:14-15).

• “Behind their backs” suggests turning away, the opposite of fixing one’s eyes on God’s Word (Psalm 119:15-16).

The lesson is clear: when people push Scripture aside, moral collapse follows.

They killed Your prophets

Rejecting the message soon leads to silencing the messenger.

• From Zechariah son of Jehoiada (2 Chronicles 24:20-22) to Isaiah and Jeremiah’s persecutions (Hebrews 11:37-38; Jeremiah 26:20-23), history is stained with prophetic blood.

• Jesus later indicts the nation for the same crime (Matthew 23:29-35).

• Murdering prophets exposes a heart hardened beyond mere disobedience; it rages at divine authority itself.

Who had admonished them to return to You

God’s prophets pleaded for repentance, not destruction.

• Calls to “return” echo through Hosea 14:1, Joel 2:12-13, and countless others.

• Admonishment shows God’s patience (2 Peter 3:9). He sends warnings before judgment (Amos 3:7).

• Killing those who offer a path back to life underscores the irrational depth of sin (Acts 7:51-52).

They committed terrible blasphemies

The climax: open, outrageous contempt for God.

• “Blasphemies” includes idolatry (Ezekiel 8:17-18), profaning the temple (2 Chronicles 36:14), and attributing God’s works to false gods (1 Kings 12:28-30).

• Such acts invite covenant curses (Leviticus 26:14-17) yet God still pursued restoration (Nehemiah 9:27-31).

• The severity of blasphemy reveals the holiness of God and the seriousness of sin.

summary

Nehemiah 9:26 recounts Israel’s history of hardened rebellion: disobedience, contempt for God’s Law, persecution of His prophets, rejection of gracious calls to repent, and gross blasphemy. The verse exposes the downward spiral that begins when God’s Word is tossed aside and culminates in violent opposition to Him. Yet the larger context of Nehemiah 9 highlights God’s enduring mercy, underscoring both the gravity of sin and the greatness of divine grace.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 26. - They... slew thy prophets. Compare Matthew 23:37; Luke 11:47. Jewish tradition states that Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel were murdered. Many prophets were slain by Jezebel, with Ahab's sanction (1 Kings 18:4). Zechariah, the son of Jehoiada, was put to death by Joash (2 Chronicles 24:22).
