Then the Levites—Jeshua, Kadmiel, Bani, Hashabneiah, Sherebiah, Hodiah, Shebaniah, and Pethahiah—said
The Levites were members of the tribe of Levi, set apart for religious duties and temple service. This group of Levites, including Jeshua, Kadmiel, and others, played a significant role in leading the people in worship and confession. Their leadership in this context highlights the importance of spiritual guidance and the role of the Levites in Israel's religious life. The mention of specific names underscores the personal involvement and responsibility of these individuals in the spiritual renewal of the community.

Stand up and bless the LORD your God from everlasting to everlasting
The call to "stand up" signifies an active participation in worship, indicating reverence and readiness to honor God. The phrase "from everlasting to everlasting" emphasizes God's eternal nature, a theme prevalent throughout Scripture (e.g., Psalm 90:2). This acknowledgment of God's eternal existence serves as a reminder of His unchanging nature and faithfulness, which is foundational to the faith of Israel.

Blessed be Your glorious name
The focus on God's "glorious name" reflects the biblical understanding of God's name as a representation of His character and deeds. In the ancient Near Eastern context, a name was more than a label; it conveyed the essence of a person. Thus, blessing God's name is an act of worship that acknowledges His holiness, majesty, and the honor due to Him. This is consistent with the Third Commandment, which instructs not to take the Lord's name in vain (Exodus 20:7).

and may it be exalted above all blessing and praise
This phrase underscores the transcendence of God, whose worthiness surpasses all human expressions of worship. It echoes the sentiment found in passages like Psalm 145:3, where God's greatness is described as unsearchable. The call for God's name to be exalted above all suggests that no amount of human praise can fully capture His glory, pointing to the infinite nature of His majesty and the inadequacy of human language to fully express His greatness.

What Does Nehemiah 9:5 Mean
Then the Levites—Jeshua, Kadmiel, Bani, Hashabneiah, Sherebiah, Hodiah, Shebaniah, and Pethahiah—said

• The scene unfolds during the great assembly in Jerusalem after the wall is rebuilt (Nehemiah 8–9).

• These named Levites stand as worship leaders, guiding the people in corporate confession and praise—just as the priests did in Deuteronomy 27:14 when Israel first entered the land.

• Their leadership underlines God’s design that spiritual servants direct His people toward wholehearted devotion (Malachi 2:6-7).

Stand up

• A call to rise signals respect and readiness. Standing highlights active engagement rather than passive listening (Exodus 33:8-10; Luke 4:16).

• Worship is never a spectator sport; God’s people physically and spiritually align themselves to honor Him.

and bless the LORD your God

• To “bless” the LORD is to speak well of His character and works (Psalm 34:1; Ephesians 1:3).

• “Your God” personalizes the covenant relationship—He is not a distant deity but the One who rescued them from exile, just as He once redeemed Israel from Egypt (Nehemiah 9:9-12).

• Praise flows naturally when we recall specific acts of God’s faithfulness in our own lives (Psalm 103:2).

from everlasting to everlasting

• The Levites anchor praise in God’s eternal nature: He had no beginning and will never end (Psalm 90:2).

• This phrase assures the returned exiles—and believers today—that the same God who kept His promises before exile will keep them forever (Isaiah 40:28).

• Because He is eternal, His covenant mercy is likewise unending (Jeremiah 31:3).

Blessed be Your glorious name

• In Scripture, “name” represents the full revelation of God’s attributes (Exodus 34:5-7).

• His name is “glorious”—weighty, magnificent, and worthy of honor (Psalm 72:19).

• The Levites echo David’s prayer: “May the whole earth be filled with His glory” (Psalm 72:19).

and may it be exalted above all blessing and praise

• Human praise, even at its best, cannot match God’s greatness (1 Kings 8:27; Psalm 145:3).

• By asking that His name be exalted above every blessing and act of praise, they confess that worship must always reach beyond human limits toward God’s infinite worth.

• This upward gaze guards against worship that centers on feelings or rituals rather than on the LORD Himself (John 4:24).

summary

Nehemiah 9:5 records Levites summoning God’s people to an active, heartfelt, humble celebration of the LORD’s eternal greatness. They call everyone to rise, remember God’s faithfulness, and declare that His majestic name towers above every possible expression of human worship. Here we learn that true praise is led by God-appointed servants, involves the whole person, rests on God’s everlasting nature, and constantly reaches higher than our highest words, because He is infinitely worthy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Hashabniah.--Not found elsewhere. No reason is given why this company is somewhat different from the former; the LXX. arbitrarily omit all names after Kadmiel. Similarly, they insert "and Ezra said" before Nehemiah 9:6. The psalm was perhaps composed by Ezra, but uttered by the Levites in the name of the congregation.

Stand up and bless . . . Blessed be.--Or, let them bless.

Thou, even thou, art Lord alone.--The three phrases mark how the address to the people glides into direct adoration of God.

Thy glorious name.--Literally found again in Psalm 72:19 alone.
