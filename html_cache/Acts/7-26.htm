Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The next day he came upon two Israelites who were fighting
This phrase refers to Moses, who, after killing an Egyptian taskmaster, encounters two Hebrew men in conflict. The timing, "the next day," suggests a continuation of Moses' concern for his people. The Israelites, or Hebrews, were enslaved in Egypt, and internal strife among them highlights the stress and oppression they faced. This incident is a precursor to Moses' future role as a mediator and leader. The fighting among the Israelites can be seen as a microcosm of the larger disunity and struggles that Moses would later address during the Exodus.

and he tried to reconcile them
Moses' attempt to reconcile the two men demonstrates his emerging role as a peacemaker and leader. This act foreshadows his future as a judge and lawgiver for Israel. The concept of reconciliation is central to biblical teaching, reflecting God's desire for peace and unity among His people. Moses' intervention is an early indication of his character and his concern for justice and harmony, qualities that would define his leadership.

saying, ‘Men, you are brothers.
By addressing them as "brothers," Moses emphasizes their shared identity and common heritage as descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. This appeal to brotherhood is a call to unity and mutual respect, which is a recurring theme throughout Scripture. The notion of brotherhood is significant in the biblical narrative, as it underscores the importance of community and the familial bonds that should transcend personal conflicts.

Why are you mistreating each other?’
Moses' question highlights the senselessness of their conflict, especially in light of their shared suffering under Egyptian oppression. The term "mistreating" suggests a deeper moral and ethical failing, as it implies a violation of the command to love one's neighbor. This question echoes throughout the Bible, challenging believers to examine their relationships and to seek reconciliation and peace. It also prefigures the teachings of Jesus, who emphasized love and forgiveness among His followers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
A central figure in the Old Testament, chosen by God to lead the Israelites out of Egypt. In this passage, he attempts to mediate a conflict between two Israelites.

2. Two Israelites
These are the individuals involved in a dispute. They represent the broader community of Israelites who were enslaved in Egypt.

3. Egypt
The land where the Israelites were enslaved. It serves as the backdrop for Moses' early life and his initial attempts to connect with his people.

4. Conflict
The event of two Israelites fighting, which Moses tries to resolve. This reflects the internal strife among the Israelites even while under external oppression.

5. Reconciliation Attempt
Moses' effort to bring peace between the two men, highlighting his emerging role as a leader and peacemaker.
Teaching Points
The Role of a Peacemaker
Moses exemplifies the role of a peacemaker, a calling that is echoed in the teachings of Jesus. As Christians, we are called to be peacemakers in our communities, seeking to resolve conflicts and promote harmony.

Recognizing Brotherhood
Moses' question, "Men, you are brothers," reminds us of the importance of recognizing our shared identity in Christ. As believers, we are part of one family and should treat each other with love and respect.

Courage to Confront
Moses demonstrates courage by stepping into a conflict. Similarly, we are called to confront issues within our communities with wisdom and love, rather than ignoring them.

The Cost of Leadership
Moses' actions foreshadow the challenges of leadership. True leadership often involves stepping into difficult situations and making tough decisions for the greater good.

God's Preparation
This incident is part of God's preparation of Moses for his future role. God often uses our experiences, even challenging ones, to prepare us for His purposes.

What Does Acts 7:26 Mean
The next day

Acts 7:26 opens with, “The next day….” This simple time marker links the scene directly to Moses’ rescue of the Israelite being beaten by the Egyptian (Exodus 2:11–12; Acts 7:24–25). It shows continuity in Moses’ heart for his people:

• He did not act on a momentary impulse; he stayed engaged.

• The timing underscores God’s orderly unfolding plan (Psalm 37:23).

• Moses expected his brothers to recognize God’s deliverance through him (Acts 7:25), yet he still approached them patiently “the next day.”

he came upon two Israelites who were fighting

Now the conflict is not with an oppressor but between fellow Hebrews, exposing sin inside the community. Other Scriptures highlight internal strife: Genesis 13:7–8 (Abram and Lot’s herdsmen), Numbers 12:1–2 (Miriam and Aaron against Moses), 1 Corinthians 3:3 (quarreling in the church). God’s people are called to peace, so internal fighting signals a deeper spiritual issue.

and he tried to reconcile them

Moses “tried to reconcile” (literally “urged them to peace”). Even before Sinai, God raises up a mediator. Moses pictures the coming Mediator, Jesus (1 Timothy 2:5; Hebrews 3:5–6). Key truths:

• Reconciliation is active; Moses “tried.”

• Peacemaking is a divine priority (Matthew 5:9; Romans 12:18).

• Leadership means stepping into messy situations rather than avoiding them.

saying, ‘Men, you are brothers.

Moses appeals to covenant identity: “brothers.” Scripture repeatedly grounds ethics in family relationship:

• Leviticus 19:17–18 commands loving one’s neighbor “as yourself.”

• Psalm 133:1 celebrates “How good and pleasant it is when brothers live together in harmony!”

• In Christ, believers are “all brothers” (Matthew 23:8; Galatians 3:28).

Recognizing shared identity is the first step toward unity.

Why are you mistreating each other?

The question exposes heart motives. James 4:1 asks similarly, “What causes fights and quarrels among you?” Moses’ rebuke:

• Confronts sin directly, not passively.

• Calls for self-examination (2 Corinthians 13:5).

• Reminds that wrongdoing against a brother is ultimately against God (1 Samuel 12:23; Acts 9:4).

Galatians 5:15 warns, “If you keep on biting and devouring one another, watch out or you will be consumed by one another.” Moses’ question aims to halt that destructive path.

summary

Acts 7:26 shows Moses stepping in as a peacemaker among his own people. The verse teaches:

• God’s servants persist in concern for His people.

• Internal conflict grieves the Lord and hinders His purposes.

• Peacemaking requires active, compassionate intervention grounded in shared identity.

• Confrontation, when done in love, exposes sin and invites repentance.

Ultimately, Moses foreshadows Jesus, the perfect Mediator who reconciles us to God and calls us to live as true brothers and sisters in peace.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers

Sirs.--Literally, Ye are brethren, without any word of address. The phrase is the same as "we be brethren" in Genesis 13:8.
