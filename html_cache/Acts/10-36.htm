Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He has sent this message to the people of Israel
This phrase highlights God's initiative in sending a message specifically to the people of Israel. Historically, Israel was chosen as God's covenant people, as seen in Genesis 12:1-3, where God promises Abraham that through his descendants all nations will be blessed. The message sent to Israel is consistent with the Old Testament prophecies, such as Isaiah 52:7, which speaks of the good news of peace and salvation. The focus on Israel underscores the fulfillment of God's promises to His chosen people, yet it also sets the stage for the expansion of the gospel to the Gentiles, as seen later in Acts 10.

proclaiming the gospel of peace
The "gospel of peace" refers to the good news that brings reconciliation between God and humanity, as well as among people themselves. This peace is a fulfillment of prophecies like Isaiah 9:6, where the Messiah is called the "Prince of Peace." In the New Testament, Ephesians 2:14-17 elaborates on how Jesus Himself is our peace, breaking down the dividing wall between Jews and Gentiles. The peace offered through the gospel is not merely the absence of conflict but a holistic restoration of relationships, both vertically with God and horizontally with others.

through Jesus Christ
The centrality of Jesus Christ in the proclamation of the gospel is emphasized here. Jesus is the fulfillment of the Messianic prophecies, such as those found in Isaiah 53, which speak of a suffering servant who would bear the sins of many. The New Testament consistently presents Jesus as the only way to salvation (John 14:6), and His life, death, and resurrection are the core of the gospel message. The phrase underscores that peace with God is made possible only through the atoning work of Jesus Christ.

who is Lord of all
This declaration affirms the universal lordship of Jesus Christ. It echoes Philippians 2:9-11, where every knee will bow and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord. The title "Lord" signifies authority and divinity, aligning with Old Testament references to Yahweh. By stating that Jesus is "Lord of all," the text emphasizes His sovereignty over all creation, both Jews and Gentiles, and His ultimate authority in heaven and on earth. This universal lordship is a key theme in the book of Acts, as the gospel spreads beyond Israel to all nations.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Peter
The apostle who is speaking in this passage. He is delivering a message to Cornelius and his household, emphasizing the universality of the gospel.

2. Cornelius
A Roman centurion who is devout and God-fearing. He receives a vision from God to send for Peter, leading to this pivotal moment in the early church.

3. Israel
The nation to whom the message of peace was first sent. This highlights the fulfillment of God's promises to Israel through Jesus Christ.

4. Jesus Christ
Central to the message, He is proclaimed as the source of peace and the Lord of all, emphasizing His divine authority and universal lordship.

5. Caesarea
The location where this event takes place, a significant city in the Roman Empire, symbolizing the spread of the gospel beyond Jewish boundaries.
Teaching Points
The Universality of the Gospel
The message of peace through Jesus Christ is for all people, transcending ethnic and cultural boundaries. This calls us to share the gospel with everyone, regardless of their background.

Jesus as Lord of All
Recognizing Jesus as Lord of all means submitting every area of our lives to His authority. This challenges us to examine areas where we may not be fully surrendered to His lordship.

Peace through Christ
True peace is found only through a relationship with Jesus Christ. In a world filled with conflict and division, believers are called to be ambassadors of this peace.

Fulfillment of Prophecy
The gospel message fulfills the promises made to Israel, showing God's faithfulness. This encourages us to trust in God's promises in our own lives.

Breaking Down Barriers
Just as Peter's message broke down barriers between Jews and Gentiles, we are called to break down barriers of prejudice and division in our communities.

What Does Acts 10:36 Mean
He has sent this message

• God Himself is the sender; the initiative starts with Him, just as in John 3:16 He “so loved the world that He gave His one and only Son.”

• The “message” is divine revelation, not human speculation (2 Peter 1:21).

• This underscores that every word in Scripture carries God’s authority (2 Timothy 3:16), so we receive it with confidence and obedience.

to the people of Israel

• Israel is the first audience, fulfilling promises made to Abraham and his descendants (Genesis 12:3; Romans 9:4-5).

• God’s covenant faithfulness shines: He never forgets His people (Jeremiah 31:35-37).

• Yet this same message will soon reach Gentiles, as Peter is discovering in Cornelius’s house (Acts 10:34-35), showing God’s plan was always global (Isaiah 49:6).

proclaiming the gospel of peace

• “Gospel” means good news; the core good news is peace—reconciliation with God (Romans 5:1).

• Peace here is not mere absence of conflict but wholeness and restored relationship (Ephesians 2:13-17).

• This peace replaces alienation caused by sin (Isaiah 59:2) and brings fellowship with God and unity among believers (Colossians 3:15).

through Jesus Christ

• The channel of peace is a Person, not a program (John 14:6).

• His atoning death and resurrection secure forgiveness and new life (1 Corinthians 15:3-4).

• All God’s promises find their “Yes” in Him (2 Corinthians 1:20), confirming there is no other mediator (1 Timothy 2:5).

who is Lord of all

• Jesus’ lordship is universal: over Israel and every nation (Matthew 28:18-20).

• He rules creation (Colossians 1:16-18), kings and kingdoms (Revelation 19:16), and every individual heart that yields to Him (Philippians 2:9-11).

• Because He is Lord of all, the gospel invitation extends to all (Romans 10:12-13), breaking down every ethnic, social, and cultural barrier.

summary

Acts 10:36 declares that God Himself sent a divinely authoritative message first to Israel, announcing the good news of restored peace with Him, achieved exclusively through Jesus Christ, who reigns as sovereign over every person and place. The verse anchors our confidence in God’s faithfulness, centers our hope on Christ’s finished work, and widens our vision to a gospel that embraces the whole world.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) The word which God sent . . .--The structure of the sentence, beginning with the object and carried on though a series of clauses, is both in the Greek and English somewhat complicated, but it is characteristically like that of St. Peter's speech in Acts 2:22-24, whether the actual form in which both now appear is due to the speaker or the reporter. It is possible, though the construction is less natural, that "the word which God sent" may look backward to the verb "I perceive" and not to the "ye know" of Acts 10:37.

Preaching peace.--Better, as reproducing with the Greek the thought and language of Isaiah 52:7, preaching glad tidings of peace.

He is Lord of all.--The parenthesis is significant as guarding against the thought which Cornelius might have entertained, that the Jesus of whom he heard as the Christ was only a Prophet and a Teacher. Peter, still holding the truth which had been revealed to him, not by flesh and blood, but by his Father in heaven (Matthew 16:17), proclaims that He was none other than the "Lord of all," of all men, and of all things.
