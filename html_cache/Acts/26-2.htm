Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
King Agrippa
King Agrippa refers to Herod Agrippa II, the last of the Herodian dynasty, who ruled over parts of Judea and surrounding regions. He was the great-grandson of Herod the Great, who was king during Jesus' birth. Agrippa II was known for his knowledge of Jewish customs and laws, which is why Paul addresses him directly. His presence in this trial highlights the political and religious complexities of the time, as the Herodian kings were often seen as Roman puppets by the Jewish people.

I consider myself fortunate
Paul expresses a sense of gratitude and opportunity, which is significant given his circumstances. Despite being a prisoner, Paul sees this as a chance to witness about his faith. This reflects his unwavering commitment to his mission and his belief in God's providence. It also demonstrates Paul's strategic approach to sharing the Gospel, using every situation to further his testimony.

to stand before you today
Paul's trial before Agrippa is a pivotal moment in his ministry. Standing before a king fulfills Jesus' prophecy that His followers would testify before rulers (Matthew 10:18). This setting underscores the fulfillment of God's plan for Paul to spread the Gospel to both Jews and Gentiles, including those in positions of power.

to defend myself
Paul's defense is not just a legal argument but a testimony of his faith and calling. Throughout Acts, Paul often uses his trials as platforms to preach the Gospel. His defense is rooted in his personal conversion experience and the truth of the resurrection, which is central to Christian doctrine. This aligns with 1 Peter 3:15, which encourages believers to always be ready to give a defense for their hope.

against all the accusations of the Jews
The accusations against Paul stem from his preaching of Jesus as the Messiah and his mission to the Gentiles, which some Jews saw as a threat to their traditions and beliefs. This reflects the tension between early Christians and Jewish authorities, a recurring theme in Acts. Paul's defense often involves clarifying misunderstandings about his teachings and affirming his respect for Jewish law, while also proclaiming the fulfillment of the law through Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul
The apostle who is defending himself before King Agrippa. He is a central figure in the New Testament, known for his missionary journeys and epistles.

2. King Agrippa
Herod Agrippa II, the ruler before whom Paul is making his defense. He is knowledgeable about Jewish customs and controversies.

3. The Jews
Refers to the Jewish leaders and accusers who have brought charges against Paul, primarily due to his preaching of the Gospel.

4. Caesarea
The location where Paul is being held and where this defense takes place. It was a significant city in Roman Judea.

5. Festus
The Roman governor who has brought Paul before King Agrippa for this hearing.
Teaching Points
Courage in Defense of Faith
Paul demonstrates courage and confidence in defending his faith. Believers are encouraged to stand firm and articulate their beliefs, even in challenging situations.

Providence in Trials
Paul's situation shows God's providence, as he is given the opportunity to witness before a king. Believers can trust that God can use their trials for His purposes.

Respectful Engagement
Paul addresses King Agrippa with respect, setting an example for how Christians should engage with authority figures and those who may oppose them.

Preparedness to Witness
Like Paul, Christians should be prepared to share their testimony and the reason for their hope, as instructed in 1 Peter 3:15.

Understanding the Audience
Paul tailors his defense to his audience, King Agrippa, who is familiar with Jewish customs. This highlights the importance of understanding and connecting with those we witness to.

What Does Acts 26:2 Mean
King Agrippa

- Paul addresses Herod Agrippa II by name, showing respect for the God-ordained authority before him (Romans 13:1; 1 Peter 2:13).

- This moment fulfills Jesus’ promise that Paul would “carry My name before … kings” (Acts 9:15).

- By starting with the king’s name, Paul signals a personal, courteous approach that invites Agrippa to listen rather than react defensively.

I consider myself fortunate

- Instead of resentment, Paul expresses gratitude for the hearing, trusting God’s providence in every circumstance (Philippians 1:12-14).

- His optimism rests on the help God has already given him “to this day” (Acts 26:22).

- Paul sees opposition as opportunity, echoing James 1:2-3, where trials mature faith.

to stand before you today

- “Today” highlights Paul’s awareness that the Lord has arranged this exact moment (Acts 23:11).

- Standing, not chained to a guard in the background, underscores the dignity God grants His servant when bearing witness (Psalm 119:46).

- The setting—Caesarea’s royal audience hall—places the gospel in front of both Jewish and Gentile leaders, previewing its spread “to the ends of the earth” (Acts 1:8).

to defend myself

- The word Paul uses elsewhere for “defense” (apologia) reflects the biblical call to “always be prepared to give a defense” (1 Peter 3:15).

- Previous defenses (Acts 22:1; Acts 24:10) trained him for this higher-stakes occasion.

- Even so, Paul knows his ultimate Defender is the Lord, who “stood by me and strengthened me” (2 Timothy 4:16-17).

against all the accusations of the Jews

- Since his arrest in Jerusalem (Acts 21:27-28), Paul has faced charges of temple desecration and sedition—claims “they could not prove” (Acts 25:7-8).

- By openly answering every accusation, Paul seeks not only personal vindication but the salvation of his accusers (Romans 10:1).

- He will soon pivot from legal rebuttal to gospel proclamation, showing that the “hope of the promise made by God to our fathers” (Acts 26:6-7) is fulfilled in Christ, thereby turning the focus from himself to Jesus.

summary

Acts 26:2 models a believer’s mindset under pressure: respect for authority, gratitude for God-given opportunities, confidence in divine timing, readiness to give a reasoned defense, and a heart that longs for opponents to know Christ. Paul’s opening words set the stage for a testimony that will echo through history, reminding us that every accusation is a fresh platform to proclaim the gospel.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) I think myself happy, king Agrippa.--We note the characteristic union of frankness and courtesy. He will not flatter a prince whose character, he must have known, did not deserve praise, but he recognises that it was well for him that he stood before one who was not ignorant of the relations of Sadducees and Pharisees on the great question of the Resurrection, and of the expectations which both parties alike cherished as to the coming of a Messiah, and the belief, which some at least of the latter cherished (Acts 15:5; Acts 21:20), that their hopes had been fulfilled in Christ.

Because I shall answer.--Strictly, because I am about to make my defence, or apologia.
