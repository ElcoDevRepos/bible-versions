As they went from town to town
This phrase indicates the missionary journey of Paul and his companions, likely including Silas, Timothy, and possibly others. The journey reflects the early church's commitment to spreading the Gospel beyond Jerusalem, Judea, and Samaria, fulfilling the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). The towns mentioned would have been part of the Roman Empire, showcasing the spread of Christianity into Gentile regions. This movement from town to town also highlights the itinerant nature of early Christian ministry, similar to Jesus' own ministry (Luke 8:1).

they delivered the decisions
The decisions refer to the decrees from the Jerusalem Council, as recorded in Acts 15. This council addressed the issue of whether Gentile converts needed to follow Jewish law, particularly circumcision. The decisions were crucial for maintaining unity between Jewish and Gentile believers and for clarifying the requirements for Gentile inclusion in the church. This reflects the early church's struggle with maintaining doctrinal purity while embracing cultural diversity.

handed down by the apostles and elders in Jerusalem
The apostles and elders in Jerusalem were the recognized leaders of the early church, including figures like Peter, James, and John. Their authority was rooted in their direct relationship with Jesus and their role in the foundational events of the church. The decisions handed down were not merely human opinions but were seen as guided by the Holy Spirit (Acts 15:28). This underscores the importance of apostolic authority and the role of church leadership in doctrinal matters.

for the people to obey
The emphasis on obedience highlights the expectation that the early Christians would adhere to the teachings and decisions of the church leaders. This obedience was not just a matter of legalism but was seen as a response to the grace of God and the guidance of the Holy Spirit. It reflects the New Testament theme of living a life worthy of the calling received (Ephesians 4:1) and the importance of unity and order within the church community.

Teaching Points
Unity in Doctrine
The decisions from the Jerusalem Council highlight the importance of unity in essential doctrines within the church. This unity is crucial for maintaining the integrity and witness of the church.

Authority of Church Leadership
The apostles and elders in Jerusalem held a position of authority, and their decisions were to be obeyed by the churches. This underscores the importance of respecting and adhering to godly leadership within the church.

Cultural Sensitivity and Inclusion
The decisions made at the Jerusalem Council reflect a sensitivity to cultural differences and a desire to include Gentiles without unnecessary burdens. This teaches us to be considerate and inclusive in our ministry efforts.

Role of Discipleship
Timothy’s involvement in delivering the decisions shows the importance of discipleship and mentoring in the church. Investing in younger believers is crucial for the growth and continuity of the faith.

What Does Acts 16:4 Mean
As they went from town to town

Paul, Silas, and the newly added Timothy (Acts 16:1–3) did not stay put; they moved. Their pattern mirrors Paul’s earlier journey where “he traveled throughout Syria and Cilicia, strengthening the churches” (Acts 15:41).

• Movement was intentional, fulfilling Jesus’ “Go therefore and make disciples of all nations” (Matthew 28:19).

• Each stop strengthened existing believers, much like Acts 14:22, where Paul revisited churches “encouraging them to continue in the faith.”

The verse reminds us that the gospel is meant to spread, not stagnate.

they delivered the decisions

What they carried was not personal opinion but a concrete message: the rulings of the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:23–29).

• These decisions clarified that salvation is by grace through faith apart from law-keeping (Acts 15:11; Ephesians 2:8–9).

• They also included practical guidelines—abstaining from idolatry, sexual immorality, and blood—so Gentile and Jewish believers could fellowship without offense (Acts 15:20).

This delivery shows how doctrine and practice travel together.

handed down by the apostles and elders in Jerusalem

The source matters. Authority rested in leadership Christ appointed (Acts 1:2; Ephesians 2:20).

• Apostles provided doctrinal foundation (1 Corinthians 14:37).

• Elders gave pastoral oversight (1 Peter 5:1–3).

• Unity resulted when local congregations recognized this shared authority (Acts 2:42; Philippians 2:2).

By submitting to Jerusalem’s guidance, the growing Gentile churches affirmed their oneness with the original Jewish believers, fulfilling Jesus’ prayer for unity (John 17:21).

for the people to obey

The final purpose was action. Information without obedience is empty (James 1:22).

• Obedience safeguards liberty; clear boundaries prevent stumbling (Romans 14:13).

• It also guards doctrine; deviating from apostolic teaching invites confusion (2 Thessalonians 2:15).

• True faith expresses itself “through love” (Galatians 5:6) and willingly aligns with God-given instruction.

Thus, even grace-saved believers are called to heed God’s directives (Titus 2:11–12).

summary

Acts 16:4 shows a missionary team carrying authoritative, grace-centered guidance from Jerusalem to scattered congregations. Their constant travel spreads truth; the delivered decisions preserve gospel purity; apostolic authority secures unity; and the call to obey turns doctrine into daily life. The verse invites every generation of believers to keep moving with the gospel, receive apostolic teaching, and live it out in obedient faith.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) They delivered them the decrees.--The number of copies which the process implies is in itself a sufficient guarantee that that which St. Luke gives is a faithful transcript. The decrees were clearly still regarded by the Gentile converts as being the charter on which they might take their stand in any dispute with the Judaisers, and doubtless helped to determine many who had previously hesitated, to seek admission into the Church.
