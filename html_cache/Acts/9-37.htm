Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
At that time, however, she became sick and died
This phrase refers to the events surrounding Tabitha, also known as Dorcas, a disciple in Joppa. The timing is significant as it occurs during the early growth of the Christian church. The mention of her sickness and death highlights the reality of human mortality, even among the faithful. In the cultural context of the time, illness and death were common, and medical knowledge was limited. This event sets the stage for a miraculous intervention, reminiscent of Jesus' own miracles, such as the raising of Jairus' daughter (Mark 5:21-43).

and her body was washed
The washing of the body was a customary Jewish practice, signifying respect and preparation for burial. This act reflects the cultural and religious traditions of the time, emphasizing the community's care for the deceased. It also underscores the finality of death, as preparations for burial were typically made when there was no expectation of recovery.

and placed in an upper room
The upper room was often used for important gatherings and events, as seen in the Last Supper (Luke 22:12-13). In this context, placing Tabitha's body in an upper room may indicate a place of honor or a space large enough to accommodate mourners. It also sets the scene for Peter's arrival and the subsequent miracle, drawing a parallel to the resurrection power demonstrated by Jesus. The use of an upper room connects to themes of hope and divine intervention, as it becomes a place where life is restored.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Tabitha (also known as Dorcas)
A disciple in Joppa known for her good works and acts of charity. Her life and death are central to this passage.

2. Joppa
A coastal city in ancient Israel, significant as a setting for this event. It was a hub of early Christian activity and a place where Peter performed a miracle.

3. The Upper Room
A place where Tabitha's body was laid after her death. This setting is reminiscent of other significant biblical events, such as the Last Supper and the gathering of the disciples at Pentecost.

4. The Disciples in Joppa
Fellow believers who were deeply affected by Tabitha's death and took action by sending for Peter.

5. Peter
One of Jesus' apostles, who was called to Joppa to address the situation. His role in this event highlights the power of faith and the continuation of Jesus' ministry through His apostles.
Teaching Points
The Power of Community
The disciples in Joppa exemplify the strength of Christian community. In times of crisis, they come together to support one another and seek divine intervention.

Faith in Action
Tabitha's life was marked by good works and charity. Her example encourages believers to live out their faith through tangible acts of kindness and service.

The Continuation of Jesus' Ministry
Peter's role in this event shows that the apostles carried on Jesus' work, empowered by the Holy Spirit. Believers today are called to continue this mission.

Hope Beyond Death
The resurrection of Tabitha serves as a reminder of the Christian hope in eternal life and the power of God to overcome death.

The Importance of Prayer and Seeking God
The disciples' decision to send for Peter reflects their reliance on God and the importance of seeking His guidance and intervention in difficult times.

What Does Acts 9:37 Mean
At that time

• The phrase anchors the account in a real moment of history, immediately after verse 36 celebrates Tabitha’s “acts of kindness and charity.”

• God’s providence governs timing (Psalm 31:15; Romans 8:28). Even seasons of loss arrive within His wise calendar, just as Esther discovered “for such a time as this” (Esther 4:14).

• The church in Joppa was thriving (Acts 9:31), yet God allows a crisis to deepen faith and display His power.

she became sick

• Followers of Jesus still experience frailty (2 Corinthians 4:16). Sickness is part of life in a fallen world, touching even the most devoted servants like Elisha (2 Kings 13:14) and Lazarus (John 11:3).

• The text offers no hint of sin causing her illness; suffering is not always disciplinary (John 9:3).

• Believers are invited to care, pray, and anoint the sick (James 5:14), which the widows around Tabitha would soon exemplify.

and died

• “The wages of sin is death” (Romans 6:23). Until Jesus returns, every believer faces this appointment (Hebrews 9:27).

• Scripture reports the event plainly, affirming its historicity. This is no parable—it really happened.

• Yet death does not have the final word (John 11:25–26; 1 Thessalonians 4:13–14). In Acts 9 the stage is set for resurrection power to vindicate that promise.

her body was washed

• First-century funeral customs included washing and wrapping the deceased (John 19:40; Luke 23:55–56).

• Respect for the body flows from the truth that it is “fearfully and wonderfully made” (Psalm 139:14) and destined for future glorification (1 Corinthians 15:42–44).

• Preparing Tabitha’s body also testifies that her friends accepted the reality of her death; they were not concocting a hoax.

and placed in an upper room

• An upper room was spacious and private, ideal for gatherings (Acts 1:13; Luke 22:12).

• Elijah and Elisha both laid dead children in upper rooms before God raised them (1 Kings 17:19–23; 2 Kings 4:32–36). The believers in Joppa may have remembered those accounts and prepared the setting in faith.

• Positioning the body upstairs kept it accessible for visitation and, providentially, for Peter’s arrival (Acts 9:38–39).

summary

Acts 9:37 records a real, sorrow-filled turn in the life of a beloved disciple. The Spirit highlights each step—timing, sickness, death, preparation, placement—to show that nothing escapes God’s notice or plan. The verse sets a stark backdrop against which the Lord will soon display resurrection power, reinforcing that in Christ even the darkest moments become opportunities for His glory and His people’s good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) They laid her in an upper chamber.--This implies some little delay in the usual rapidity of Eastern funerals. As Lydda was only nine miles from Joppa, the report of 'neas's recovery might well have travelled from the one city to the other, and led to the hope that the power which St. Peter had thus put forth might extend even to the farther work of raising from the dead.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 37. - Fell sick for was sick, A.V.; and when they had washed her for whom when they had washed, A.V. For the phrase in those days, comp. Acts 6:1. The days here meant are those while Peter was in those parts. An upper chamber (ὑπερώον), as in Acts 1:13. The upper chamber was much more private and quiet than a room on the ground floor (see 2 Kings 4:10, 11).
