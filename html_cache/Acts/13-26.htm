Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Brothers, children of Abraham,
This phrase addresses the Jewish audience, emphasizing their shared heritage as descendants of Abraham. It highlights the covenant relationship God established with Abraham, which is foundational to Jewish identity (Genesis 12:1-3). The term "brothers" signifies a familial bond, underscoring the unity among the Jewish people.

and you Gentiles who fear God,
This refers to the God-fearing Gentiles, non-Jews who worshiped the God of Israel and adhered to certain Jewish practices without full conversion. This inclusion reflects the early church's mission to reach both Jews and Gentiles, as seen in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). It also fulfills the prophecy of Isaiah 49:6, where the Messiah is a light to the Gentiles.

it is to us that this message of salvation has been sent.
The "message of salvation" refers to the gospel of Jesus Christ, which offers redemption and reconciliation with God. This message was first delivered to the Jews, as seen in Romans 1:16, but is also extended to the Gentiles, fulfilling God's promise to bless all nations through Abraham's seed (Genesis 22:18). The phrase "has been sent" indicates divine initiative and purpose, aligning with God's redemptive plan throughout history.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul
The apostle who is speaking in this passage, delivering a sermon in the synagogue in Pisidian Antioch.

2. Children of Abraham
Refers to the Jewish people, the descendants of Abraham, who are part of the audience.

3. Gentiles who fear God
Non-Jewish individuals who worship the God of Israel and are present in the synagogue.

4. Pisidian Antioch
A city in the region of Galatia where Paul and Barnabas are preaching.

5. Message of Salvation
The gospel of Jesus Christ, which Paul is proclaiming to both Jews and Gentiles.
Teaching Points
Inclusivity of the Gospel
The message of salvation is for everyone, regardless of ethnic or cultural background. This challenges us to share the gospel with all people, without prejudice.

Fulfillment of God's Promises
The inclusion of Gentiles in the message of salvation demonstrates God's faithfulness to His promises. We can trust that God will fulfill His promises in our lives as well.

Unity in Christ
Both Jews and Gentiles are united in the message of salvation. As believers, we should strive for unity within the body of Christ, transcending cultural and ethnic divisions.

Fear of God
The Gentiles who fear God are acknowledged in this passage. A reverent fear of God is essential for both understanding and receiving the message of salvation.

Responsibility to Share the Gospel
Paul’s example shows the importance of proclaiming the message of salvation. We are called to be active participants in sharing the gospel with others.

What Does Acts 13:26 Mean
Brothers

Paul opens with a family word that immediately draws everyone close. By saying “Brothers,” he:

• Places himself on the same level as his hearers (Acts 13:15).

• Echoes Jesus, who “is not ashamed to call them brothers” (Hebrews 2:11).

• Models the unity Christ prayed for (John 17:20–21).

The greeting reminds us that Christian fellowship rests on shared faith, not social rank or background.

children of Abraham

Now Paul narrows in on ethnic Israel. “Children of Abraham” are the physical descendants through whom God first unfolded His promises:

• Genesis 12:2–3—“I will make you into a great nation… and in you all the families of the earth will be blessed.”

• Romans 9:4–5—Israel “theirs are the covenants, the giving of the Law, the worship, and the promises.”

• John 8:39—Jesus acknowledges Abrahamic lineage yet calls for true, living faith.

Paul honors the covenant line while preparing to show how those promises reach their fulfillment in Jesus.

and you Gentiles who fear God

The circle widens again to embrace “God-fearers”—non-Jewish worshipers who revered Israel’s God but had not fully converted. Luke highlights them elsewhere:

• Cornelius, “a devout man and God-fearing” (Acts 10:2).

• Lydia, “a worshiper of God” (Acts 16:14).

• Many in Thessalonica who believed (Acts 17:4).

By naming them, Paul shows the door of salvation already swinging open to the nations, just as Isaiah foresaw (Isaiah 49:6).

it is to us

Notice the inclusive pronoun. Jew and Gentile together stand in the spotlight of God’s gracious plan:

• Acts 3:25–26—The promise is “to you first,” yet intended for “all peoples.”

• Romans 1:16—“It is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, first to the Jew, and then to the Greek.”

• Ephesians 2:14—Christ “has made the two one.”

The gospel is not a relic reserved for a select few; it is a present-tense gift for all who will receive it.

that this message of salvation has been sent

The climax: God has taken the initiative. “Message” (literally “word”) underscores proclamation; “salvation” identifies the content. Key echoes:

• John 3:17—“For God did not send His Son into the world to condemn the world, but to save the world through Him.”

• Acts 4:12—“There is no other name under heaven… by which we must be saved.”

• 1 John 4:14—“The Father sent His Son to be the Savior of the world.”

To “send” implies divine mission, authority, and urgency. Refusing or neglecting such a message is therefore unthinkable.

summary

Acts 13:26 is a beautifully layered address. Paul:

• Welcomes his listeners as family.

• Honors the Abrahamic line while extending hope to the Gentile seeker.

• Declares that the long-promised rescue has arrived—sent by God Himself—and belongs to “us,” Jew and Gentile alike.

The verse invites every reader today to stand under that same gracious announcement and trust the Savior who was sent for all.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(26) Children of the stock of Abraham, and whosoever among you feareth God.--The two classes are, as before (see Note on Acts 13:16), again pointedly contrasted with each other.

To you is the word of this salvation sent.--The demonstrative pronoun implies that the salvation which St. Paul proclaimed rested on the work of Jesus the Saviour (Acts 13:23), and was found in union with Him. (Comp. "this life" in Acts 5:20.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 26. - Brethren for men and brethren, A.V., as ver. 15; those among you that fear for whosoever among you feareth, A.V.; to us for to you, A.V. and T.R.; sent forth for sent, A.V. and T.R. The same address in substance as that in ver. 16, comprising the Jews and the devout heathen. To us; see ver. 33; but on the other hand (ver. 38), "to you," seems preferable. This salvation proceeding from the Savior, mentioned in ver. 23 (comp. Acts 10:36, "The word which God sent").
