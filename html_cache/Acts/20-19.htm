Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I served the Lord with great humility
Paul's service to the Lord is characterized by humility, a key Christian virtue. This humility is reflective of Christ's own example (Philippians 2:5-8). Paul's ministry was not about self-promotion but about glorifying God and serving others (1 Corinthians 9:19). His humility is evident in his willingness to endure hardships and his focus on the needs of the church rather than his own.

and with tears
The mention of tears indicates Paul's deep emotional investment in his ministry and the people he served. It reflects his compassion and the burdens he carried for the churches (2 Corinthians 2:4). Tears also signify the genuine love and concern he had for the spiritual well-being of others, echoing Jesus' own weeping over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41).

especially in the trials that came upon me
Paul faced numerous trials throughout his ministry, including physical hardships, persecution, and opposition (2 Corinthians 11:23-28). These trials were a testament to his commitment to the gospel and his willingness to suffer for Christ's sake (Philippians 1:29). The trials also served to strengthen his faith and reliance on God (2 Corinthians 12:9-10).

through the plots of the Jews
Paul often encountered opposition from Jewish leaders who saw his message as a threat to their religious authority and traditions (Acts 9:23, Acts 13:45). This opposition was rooted in a misunderstanding of the fulfillment of the Law through Christ (Romans 10:4). The plots against Paul highlight the tension between the early Christian movement and traditional Jewish beliefs, as well as the fulfillment of Jesus' prophecy that His followers would face persecution (John 15:20).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The primary speaker in this passage, Paul is addressing the Ephesian elders, recounting his ministry and the challenges he faced.

2. Ephesus
A significant city in Asia Minor where Paul spent considerable time preaching and teaching, establishing a strong Christian community.

3. The Jews
Refers to certain Jewish individuals or groups who opposed Paul's message and sought to undermine his ministry.

4. The Ephesian Elders
Leaders of the church in Ephesus, whom Paul is addressing in this farewell speech.

5. Trials and Plots
The various challenges and conspiracies Paul faced, particularly from those who opposed his mission to spread the Gospel.
Teaching Points
Humility in Service
Paul exemplifies serving the Lord with humility. As believers, we are called to serve without seeking personal glory, following Christ's example of humility.

Perseverance Through Trials
Despite facing opposition and plots, Paul remained steadfast. Christians are encouraged to persevere in their faith and mission, trusting in God's strength.

Emotional Investment in Ministry
Paul's tears indicate his deep care for those he ministered to. We should be emotionally invested in our ministry, showing genuine love and concern for others.

Facing Opposition
Opposition is a reality in the Christian walk. Like Paul, we should be prepared to face challenges with faith and courage, relying on God's guidance.

Community and Leadership
Paul's address to the Ephesian elders highlights the importance of strong, humble leadership within the church community.

What Does Acts 20:19 Mean
I served the Lord

Acts 20:19 opens with Paul’s simple testimony: “I served the Lord.” Service was the defining note of his life.

• Serving the Lord, not himself or any agenda, gave Paul clarity and courage (cf. Romans 1:9; Acts 27:23).

• His ministry sprang from gratitude for salvation (1 Timothy 1:12–14) and from a sense of stewardship (1 Corinthians 9:16–17).

• True service puts the Master first, people next, self last (Mark 10:45). Paul models that order here.

with great humility

Paul adds the manner of his service: “with great humility.”

• He always remembered the grace shown him as a former persecutor (1 Corinthians 15:9; Ephesians 3:8).

• Humility guarded him from boasting in results and kept him dependent on the Spirit (2 Corinthians 4:7).

• His attitude mirrored Christ, “who humbled Himself” (Philippians 2:5-8), reminding us that lowliness is kingdom greatness (Matthew 23:11-12).

and with tears

Next, Paul notes the emotion involved: “with tears.”

• Ministry touched his heart; he wept over sin, suffering, and the spiritual peril of others (Acts 20:31; 2 Corinthians 2:4).

• Tears show compassion, not weakness (John 11:35). They water the seeds of gospel fruitfulness (Psalm 126:5-6).

• A dry-eyed servant may reveal a hard heart; Paul’s tears call us to tenderhearted engagement.

especially in the trials

Paul served this way “especially in the trials.”

• Trials were not interruptions but arenas for faithfulness (Acts 14:22).

• Hardship refined his motives and proved the gospel’s power (2 Corinthians 11:23-28).

• He could rejoice because suffering with Christ leads to sharing His glory (Romans 8:17-18).

that came upon me through the plots of the Jews

Finally, Paul identifies the source of many trials: “the plots of the Jews.”

• From Damascus onward, Jewish opposition followed him (Acts 9:23; 13:45; 23:12).

• Yet he kept loving them, yearning for their salvation (Romans 9:1-3; 10:1).

• Resistance never silenced him; it spread the gospel farther (Acts 28:28). Faithfulness in persecution remains a hallmark of true discipleship (2 Timothy 3:12).

summary

Acts 20:19 paints a portrait of authentic Christian ministry—serving the Lord first, clothed in humility, moved by compassion, steadfast in trials, and undeterred by opposition. Paul’s example invites us to the same wholehearted, tear-stained, humble service, confident that the Lord whom we serve will sustain, reward, and use every act done for His glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) Serving the Lord with all humility of mind . . .--The participle exactly answers to the epithet of the "servant" or "slave" of Christ which St. Paul so often uses of himself (Romans 1:1; Galatians 1:10; Philippians 1:1; Titus 1:1). The "tears," too, are characteristic of the Apostle, whose intense sensitiveness and sympathy had not been hardened into a Stoic apathy, and therefore found vent in a form which the Stoic would have scorned as unmanly. (Comp. Acts 20:31; 2Corinthians 2:4.) Epictetus (Enchirid. c. 2) barely allowed a follower of wisdom to mourn outwardly with those who mourned, and added the warning: "Take heed that thou mourn not inwardly." . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - Lowliness for humility, A.V.; tears for many tears, A.V. and T.R.; with trials for temptations, A.V.; plots for lying in wait, A.V. Plots (ἐπιβουλαῖς); comp. ver. 3, and note. There is no special account of Jewish plots in St. Luke's narrative of St. Paul's sojourn at Ephesus. But from Acts 19:9, 13, and probably 33, we may gather how hostile the unbelieving Jews were to him.
