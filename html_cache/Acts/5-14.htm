Yet more and more believers were brought to the Lord
This phrase highlights the rapid growth of the early Christian church. The apostles' teachings and the signs and wonders performed among the people (Acts 5:12) contributed to this increase. The phrase "brought to the Lord" indicates a conversion experience, where individuals accepted Jesus Christ as their Savior. This growth fulfills Jesus' command in Acts 1:8 to be His witnesses "in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth." The early church's expansion is a testament to the power of the Holy Spirit working through the apostles.

large numbers of both men and women
The inclusion of both men and women signifies the breaking of social and cultural barriers in the early church. In a patriarchal society, the mention of women as part of the growing number of believers is significant. It reflects the inclusive nature of the Gospel, as seen in Galatians 3:28, where Paul states, "There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." This growth also fulfills the prophecy of Joel 2:28-29, which Peter referenced in Acts 2:17-18, indicating that God's Spirit would be poured out on all people, regardless of gender. The large numbers suggest a movement that was gaining momentum and influence, setting the stage for the spread of Christianity beyond Jerusalem.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Believers
Individuals who accepted the message of Jesus Christ and committed their lives to follow Him. In the context of Acts, these were primarily Jewish converts who recognized Jesus as the Messiah.

2. The Lord
Refers to Jesus Christ, whom the early Christians recognized as the risen Savior and the Son of God. The phrase "brought to the Lord" indicates a personal relationship and commitment to Jesus.

3. Men and Women
This highlights the inclusive nature of the early Christian movement, where both genders were equally welcomed and valued in the community of believers.

4. Jerusalem
The city where these events took place, serving as the epicenter of early Christian activity and the location of the early church's growth.

5. Apostles
The primary leaders and witnesses of Jesus' resurrection, who were instrumental in preaching the gospel and performing signs and wonders that attracted new believers.
Teaching Points
The Power of the Gospel
The message of Jesus Christ has the power to transform lives and draw people to God. As believers, we should be confident in sharing the gospel, knowing that it is God who brings people to Himself.

Inclusive Community
The early church was marked by its inclusivity, welcoming both men and women. Our churches today should reflect this same openness and value for all individuals, regardless of gender or background.

Role of the Holy Spirit
The growth of the church in Acts is attributed to the work of the Holy Spirit. We should rely on the Holy Spirit for guidance and empowerment in our evangelistic efforts.

Faithfulness in Witnessing
The apostles were faithful in their witness despite opposition. We are called to be steadfast in sharing our faith, trusting that God will use our efforts to bring others to Him.

Growth through Discipleship
The increase in believers was not just about numbers but about making disciples. We should focus on nurturing new believers in their faith and helping them grow in their relationship with Christ.

What Does Acts 5:14 Mean
Yet more and more

Luke notes a continued swell of spiritual momentum: “Yet more and more…” The gospel was not plateauing but expanding. Earlier, 3,000 were added in one day (Acts 2:41), then about 5,000 men (Acts 4:4). Now the pace keeps accelerating. God’s pattern is clear: wherever Christ is faithfully preached and the church walks in holiness (Acts 5:1-11) and power (Acts 5:12-13), growth follows. Just as Jesus promised, the mustard seed keeps spreading its branches (Matthew 13:31-32), and the gates of Hades cannot prevail (Matthew 16:18).

believers

Those being added are expressly called “believers,” identical to the description in Acts 4:32: “All the believers were one in heart and mind.” Salvation is not mere interest or attendance; it is personal trust in the risen Christ (John 1:12; 3:16). Luke stresses authentic conversion, echoing the call Peter gave at Pentecost: “Repent and be baptized…” (Acts 2:38). God is growing a community defined by faith, not by heritage or ritual (Galatians 3:26-29).

were brought to the Lord

No one drifts into the kingdom by accident; they are “brought.” God draws people (John 6:44) through Spirit-empowered witness (Acts 1:8). The miracles in Solomon’s Colonnade (Acts 5:12) verified the apostles’ message, but it was the Lord Himself who saved (Acts 2:47). The wording reminds us the church’s task is to lead people to Christ, not simply to religion (2 Corinthians 5:20).

large numbers

Luke loves to highlight God’s arithmetic: added (Acts 2:47), multiplied (Acts 6:7), increased greatly (Acts 12:24). “Large numbers” underscores that the gospel is sufficient for crowds, not just individuals (Romans 1:16). While each soul matters, Scripture also celebrates sweeping harvests (John 4:35-38). This verse invites us to expect God-sized results when He moves.

both men and women

In a culture often dominated by male headcounts, Luke is careful to honor the women God is calling. He did the same at Pentecost (Acts 1:14; 2:17-18) and will again with Lydia (Acts 16:13-15) and many others. The Spirit is poured out “on all people,” fulfilling Joel’s prophecy. Salvation levels the ground at the cross (Galatians 3:28), welcoming every believer into full participation and gifting.

summary

Acts 5:14 paints a vibrant picture of a holy, Spirit-filled church: momentum building, genuine faith spreading, Christ Himself drawing multitudes, and both men and women embraced in equal measure. When God’s people honor His Word and rely on His power, He still delights to add “yet more and more” to His family.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) Added to the Lord.--Here, probably, the word is used in its definite New Testament sense for the Lord Jesus.

Both of men and women.--The mention of the latter forms an introduction to the dissensions connected with the "widows" in Acts 6, and is itself characteristic of St. Luke as a writer who had seen and known the effect of the new Religion in raising women to a higher life, and whose knowledge of its history was in great measure derived from them. (See Introduction to St. Luke's Gospel.) So in Acts 8:3 women are named as prominent among the sufferers in the first general persecution.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Added to the Lord; as in Acts 11:24, not as in margin. Multitudes; πλήθη, found in the plural nowhere else in the New Testament.
