Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
When they arrived at Salamis
Salamis was a major city on the eastern coast of Cyprus, an island in the Mediterranean Sea. It was a significant commercial hub, which made it a strategic location for spreading the Gospel. The choice of Salamis as a starting point for the mission reflects the early church's strategy of targeting urban centers where the message could spread rapidly. The presence of a large Jewish community in Salamis is evidenced by the mention of synagogues, indicating a well-established Jewish population.

they proclaimed the word of God
The proclamation of the word of God refers to the preaching of the Gospel, the good news of Jesus Christ's death and resurrection. This was the central mission of Paul and Barnabas, as they sought to fulfill the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). The focus on the word of God underscores the importance of Scripture and the message of salvation as the foundation of their ministry.

in the Jewish synagogues
The synagogue was the center of Jewish religious life and a natural starting point for Paul and Barnabas, who were both Jewish. By beginning their ministry in the synagogues, they were able to connect with those who were already familiar with the Scriptures and the prophecies concerning the Messiah. This approach is consistent with Paul's later practice of going "to the Jew first" (Romans 1:16). The synagogue setting provided a platform for dialogue and debate, allowing the apostles to present Jesus as the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies.

And John was with them as their helper
John, also known as John Mark, was a relative of Barnabas (Colossians 4:10) and is traditionally identified as the author of the Gospel of Mark. His role as a helper suggests he assisted in logistical and practical matters, supporting the mission of Paul and Barnabas. This partnership highlights the importance of teamwork and the diverse roles within the early church. John's presence also foreshadows later events, as he would eventually part ways with Paul, leading to a significant moment of contention (Acts 15:37-39).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Salamis
A city on the eastern coast of Cyprus, significant as the first location where Paul and Barnabas preached during their missionary journey. It was a major city with a substantial Jewish population, which is why they began their ministry in the synagogues there.

2. Paul and Barnabas
Key figures in the early Christian church, sent out by the Holy Spirit from the church in Antioch to spread the Gospel. Their mission marks the beginning of Paul's first missionary journey.

3. John (Mark)
Also known as John Mark, he accompanied Paul and Barnabas as a helper. He is traditionally believed to be the author of the Gospel of Mark and is a relative of Barnabas.

4. Jewish Synagogues
The initial focus of Paul and Barnabas's ministry in each city they visited. Synagogues were central to Jewish community life and served as a strategic starting point for spreading the Gospel to both Jews and God-fearing Gentiles.

5. Proclamation of the Word of God
The central activity of Paul and Barnabas's mission, emphasizing the importance of preaching and teaching as foundational to the spread of Christianity.
Teaching Points
Strategic Evangelism
Paul and Barnabas's approach to begin their ministry in synagogues teaches us the importance of starting where people are already gathered and open to spiritual discussions.

Teamwork in Ministry
The inclusion of John Mark as a helper highlights the value of teamwork and the support roles that are crucial in ministry efforts.

Cultural Sensitivity
By starting in the synagogues, Paul and Barnabas show respect for Jewish customs and traditions, which can be a model for engaging with different cultures today.

Faithful Proclamation
The focus on proclaiming the Word of God underscores the centrality of Scripture in evangelism and discipleship.

Perseverance in Mission
Despite challenges, Paul and Barnabas's commitment to their mission encourages believers to remain steadfast in their calling.

What Does Acts 13:5 Mean
Arrival in Salamis

“When they arrived at Salamis” (Acts 13:5a)

• Paul and Barnabas have just been “sent out by the Holy Spirit” (Acts 13:4), sailing from Seleucia to the eastern port city of Cyprus.

• Luke records their landing point to underline that this journey is real history, rooted in geography we can still identify today.

• Salamis was a commercial hub; beginning here put the gospel in front of travelers who could carry it farther.

• Cross references: Acts 11:19 shows other believers preaching on Cyprus; Acts 15:39 notes Barnabas’s later return, highlighting ongoing ministry to the island.

Proclaiming the word of God

“they proclaimed the word of God” (Acts 13:5b)

• Their first priority on arrival is speaking, not sightseeing. The message—“the word of God”—is presented without alteration or apology (Acts 13:32–33; Romans 10:17).

• This proclaiming is continuous; Luke’s verb choice pictures a repeated, habitual action.

• Wherever the Spirit places us, verbal witness remains essential (Acts 20:27; 2 Corinthians 4:13).

In the Jewish synagogues

“in the Jewish synagogues” (Acts 13:5c)

• Paul follows the pattern he will repeat across the empire: start with the synagogue (Acts 17:1–2; 18:4).

• He honors God’s covenant order—“to the Jew first and also to the Greek” (Romans 1:16).

• Synagogues provided:

– an audience already prepared by Scripture (Acts 13:15),

– a platform open to visiting teachers, and

– a network that could spread news quickly through the diaspora.

• Some Jews would believe, others would reject, but the gospel is offered genuinely to all.

And John was with them as their helper

“And John was with them as their helper” (Acts 13:5d)

• John Mark, introduced in Acts 12:12, assists with practical needs—arranging lodgings, handling scrolls, perhaps translating.

• His presence illustrates that ministry often requires supportive roles, not only upfront preaching (1 Corinthians 12:28).

• Mark’s later departure (Acts 13:13) will test the team, yet God will still use him mightily (2 Timothy 4:11; Colossians 4:10).

• Even helpers have lasting impact: Mark eventually writes the Gospel that bears his name.

summary

Acts 13:5 shows a Spirit-led team landing in a strategic city, immediately teaching God’s unchanging word in the familiar setting of local synagogues, and relying on a trusted helper to make the work possible. The verse models purposeful arrival, clear proclamation, wise strategy, and the value of every member of the mission—principles still vital for faithful ministry today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) When they were at Salamis.--The city was, as stated above, at the east end of Cyprus. The mention of "synagogues" implies a considerable Jewish population, and to these the Apostles, following the general rule announced in Acts 13:46, naturally, in the first instance, turned.

They had also John to their minister.--The noun so rendered is not that commonly used for the "deacons" or "ministers" of the Church, but implies rather the attendance of personal service. It is probable, however, that he was employed in baptising converts, and, where a church was founded, in preparing for the Supper of the Lord. Looking to the after-work of Mark, it would hardly, perhaps, be too much to say that he was, more than any other disciple, the courier of the Apostolic Church.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Proclaimed for preached, A.V.; as their attendant for to their minister, A.V. (ὑπηρέτην). It is a word taken from the synagogue, where it denotes an inferior minister (see Luke 4:20). In Acts 5:22 the ὑπηρέται are the apparitors of the high priest. Here it is synonymous with διάκονος, a deacon. John was to Barnabas and Saul what Joshua was to Moses, Elisha to Elijah, etc. Peter, when he went to Caesarea, was accompanied by six brethren (Acts 11:12).
