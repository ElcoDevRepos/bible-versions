Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But our fathers refused to obey him.
This phrase refers to the Israelites' disobedience to Moses, who was chosen by God to lead them out of Egypt. The refusal to obey highlights a recurring theme of rebellion against divinely appointed leadership, seen throughout the Old Testament. This disobedience is a reflection of the human tendency to resist God's commands, as seen in other instances such as the rebellion against God in the wilderness (Numbers 14:1-4). The Israelites' refusal to obey Moses can be seen as a type of humanity's broader rebellion against God, which is ultimately addressed through the obedience of Jesus Christ (Philippians 2:8).

Instead, they rejected him
The rejection of Moses by the Israelites is symbolic of the rejection of God's messengers throughout biblical history. This rejection is paralleled in the New Testament with the rejection of Jesus Christ by the Jewish leaders and people (John 1:11). Moses, as a type of Christ, foreshadows the ultimate rejection and crucifixion of Jesus. The rejection of divine leadership is a recurring theme, emphasizing the need for repentance and acceptance of God's chosen deliverer.

and in their hearts turned back to Egypt.
Turning back to Egypt in their hearts signifies a longing for the past and a lack of faith in God's promises. Egypt represents bondage and slavery, both physically and spiritually. This longing for Egypt is a metaphor for the temptation to return to a life of sin and bondage rather than embracing the freedom offered by God. The Israelites' desire to return to Egypt reflects a spiritual condition that is addressed in the New Testament, where believers are called to leave behind their old lives and follow Christ (Galatians 5:1). Egypt, in this context, serves as a symbol of the world and its enticements, which believers are urged to forsake in pursuit of the promised land, representing eternal life with God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Stephen
The speaker in Acts 7, Stephen is one of the first deacons of the early church and the first Christian martyr. He is addressing the Sanhedrin, recounting Israel's history to illustrate their resistance to God's messengers.

2. Moses
The leader whom God appointed to deliver the Israelites from Egyptian bondage. In this verse, Moses is the one whom the "fathers" refused to obey.

3. The Fathers (Israelites)
Refers to the ancestors of the Jewish people who were delivered from Egypt. They are depicted as disobedient and rebellious in their journey through the wilderness.

4. Egypt
Symbolizes bondage and the past life of slavery for the Israelites. In this context, it represents a place of spiritual regression and disobedience.

5. The Wilderness
The setting for the Israelites' journey after leaving Egypt, where they frequently displayed a lack of faith and obedience to God.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Disobedience
Disobedience to God's appointed leaders and commands can lead to spiritual regression. Just as the Israelites turned back to Egypt in their hearts, Christians must guard against returning to old ways of sin.

The Heart's True Desires
The Israelites' desire to return to Egypt reveals that physical liberation is not enough; the heart must also be transformed. Believers should examine their hearts to ensure their desires align with God's will.

The Importance of Faithfulness
Despite the challenges and uncertainties, faithfulness to God is crucial. The Israelites' lack of faith led to their downfall, serving as a warning for believers to trust in God's promises.

Learning from the Past
The history of Israel serves as a lesson for Christians today. Reflecting on past mistakes can help prevent future disobedience and encourage a closer walk with God.

Spiritual Leadership
Just as Moses was appointed to lead the Israelites, God places leaders in the church to guide His people. Respect and obedience to spiritual leadership are vital for community growth and unity.

What Does Acts 7:39 Mean
But our fathers refused to obey him

• “Him” is Moses, God’s chosen deliverer (Exodus 3:10).

• Their refusal was deliberate; they had witnessed miracles yet still hardened their hearts (Psalm 106:7–13; Hebrews 3:16).

• Stephen links their disobedience to the current council’s resistance to the Holy Spirit (Acts 7:51).

• Obedience is inseparable from faith; ignoring God-appointed authority is ignoring God Himself (Romans 13:1–2).

Instead, they rejected him

• Rejection of Moses began early—“Who made you ruler and judge over us?” (Exodus 2:14).

• Complaints in the wilderness echoed that same spirit (Numbers 14:2; Exodus 16:2).

• To reject the mediator is to reject the LORD who sent him (1 Samuel 8:7; John 12:48).

• Stephen uses this to show the Sanhedrin their pattern of rejecting every messenger, culminating in the Messiah (Acts 7:52).

In their hearts turned back to Egypt

• Outwardly they walked toward Canaan; inwardly they craved Egypt’s comforts (Exodus 16:3; Numbers 11:4–6).

• The “heart turn” preceded the golden calf episode (Acts 7:40–41), proving idolatry starts within (James 1:14–15).

• Egypt represents bondage to sin; longing for it denies the freedom God provides (Galatians 5:1; 2 Peter 2:20–22).

• Followers of Christ must guard against nostalgic attraction to the old life (Luke 9:62; Philippians 3:13–14).

summary

Acts 7:39 highlights Israel’s history of hard-hearted rebellion: they refused obedience, rejected God’s chosen leader, and inwardly yearned for the bondage they had been rescued from. Stephen uses their ancestors’ example to convict his listeners—and all readers—of the danger of resisting God’s gracious deliverance and turning back to worldly slavery instead of moving forward in faithful obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(39) To whom our fathers would not obey.--The historical parallelism is continued. The people rejected Moses then (the same word is used as in Acts 7:27) as they were rejecting Christ now, even after He had shown Himself to be their redeemer from a worse than Egyptian bondage.

In their hearts turned back again into Egypt.--The sin was one often repeated, but the history referred to is probably that in Exodus 16:3. For a later example see Numbers 11:5.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 39. - Obedient for obey, A.V.; turned back in their hearts unto Egypt for in their hearts turned back again into Egypt, A.V. Our fathers would not be obedient, though God had bestowed such signal marks of favor upon them. Turned back in their hearts. A striking instance of their rejection of God's chiefest mercies.
