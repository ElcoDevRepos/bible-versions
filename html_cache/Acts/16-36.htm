Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The jailer informed Paul:
In the Roman world, jailers were often retired soldiers or local officials responsible for maintaining order in the prison. The jailer here is likely the same one who witnessed the miraculous events of the previous night, including the earthquake and the conversion of his household. His role as a messenger indicates a shift in his relationship with Paul, from captor to one who respects and perhaps even admires him. This reflects the transformative power of the Gospel, as seen in Acts 16:31-34, where the jailer and his family come to faith.

The magistrates have sent orders to release you:
The magistrates, or "praetors," were the chief officials in a Roman colony like Philippi. Their decision to release Paul and Silas may have been influenced by the realization of their Roman citizenship, which they had violated by beating and imprisoning them without trial (Acts 16:37-38). This reflects the legal protections afforded to Roman citizens, highlighting the importance of citizenship in the Roman Empire. The magistrates' change of heart could also be seen as a divine intervention, ensuring the spread of the Gospel.

Now you may go on your way in peace:
The phrase "in peace" suggests a formal dismissal, allowing Paul and Silas to leave without further harassment. This peace is not just a cessation of conflict but also a deeper spiritual peace that comes from God, as seen in John 14:27. The release signifies a victory for the early church, as the Gospel continues to spread despite opposition. It also prefigures the peace that believers have in Christ, who is the ultimate source of peace (Isaiah 9:6). The peaceful departure underscores the theme of divine providence and protection throughout the book of Acts.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul
An apostle and missionary, Paul is a central figure in the New Testament, known for his missionary journeys and epistles. In Acts 16, he is imprisoned in Philippi.

2. Silas
A leader in the early Christian church and companion of Paul on his missionary journeys. He was also imprisoned with Paul in Philippi.

3. The Jailer
The Philippian jailer who was responsible for guarding Paul and Silas. After witnessing a miraculous earthquake and the faith of Paul and Silas, he converts to Christianity.

4. Magistrates
The Roman officials in Philippi who ordered the imprisonment of Paul and Silas and later sent orders for their release.

5. Philippi
A Roman colony in Macedonia where Paul and Silas were imprisoned. It was the site of the first Christian church in Europe.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Trials
Even in imprisonment, God was at work. The release of Paul and Silas was part of His divine plan.

The Power of Witness
The faith and conduct of Paul and Silas led to the conversion of the jailer and his household. Our actions can be a powerful testimony to others.

Peace in Obedience
The phrase "go on your way in peace" reflects the peace that comes from following God's will, even in difficult circumstances.

The Role of Authority
The magistrates' decision to release Paul and Silas shows that God can use earthly authorities to accomplish His purposes.

The Importance of Community
The early church in Philippi was strengthened through these events, emphasizing the importance of community in the Christian faith.

What Does Acts 16:36 Mean
The jailer informed Paul

• This Roman jailer, newly converted after the miraculous earthquake (Acts 16:25-34), becomes a messenger rather than a captor.

• His respectful approach shows the swift change Christ brings—“he washed their wounds” earlier, and now he brings good news (Acts 16:33-34; cf. Luke 3:12-14 where transformed lives follow repentance).

• God often turns former adversaries into allies, as seen when the commander protected Paul in Jerusalem (Acts 23:16-24) and when Joseph’s jailer favored him (Genesis 39:21-23).

“The magistrates have sent orders to release you”

• The city officials, alarmed by Paul’s Roman citizenship (Acts 16:37-38), reverse their unjust sentence, fulfilling Proverbs 21:1—“The king’s heart is a watercourse in the hand of the LORD; He directs it wherever He pleases.”

• Their command highlights the sovereignty of God over legal systems, echoing Daniel 6:22-23 when divine intervention led to release.

• This moment answers prayer for deliverance (Psalm 34:17) and mirrors earlier angelic releases in Acts 5:19 and 12:7-11, underscoring that no prison bars can hinder the gospel.

Now you may go on your way in peace

• “Peace” (shalom-like wholeness) signals not only freedom from chains but God-granted well-being (John 14:27; Isaiah 26:3).

• Paul and Silas’s calm refusal to slip away secretly (Acts 16:37) teaches integrity: they want public vindication for the gospel’s sake (2 Corinthians 8:21).

• The instruction to leave peacefully parallels Jesus’ charge to the healed demoniac to “go home to your own people” (Mark 5:19), showing that liberation leads to mission, not aimless wandering.

summary

Acts 16:36 records God’s immediate, tangible answer to suffering saints. The jailer’s announcement shows how the Lord transforms hearts, moves authorities, and grants peace-filled release. Believers can trust that He orchestrates every detail for the advance of His gospel and the vindication of His servants.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) Go in peace.--The few hours which the gaoler had spent with his new teacher had probably taught him to use the phrase in the fulness of its meaning (see Notes on Luke 7:50; Luke 8:48), and not as a mere conventional formula. He naturally looks on the offer--securing, as it did, safety for his new friend--as one that should be accepted.
