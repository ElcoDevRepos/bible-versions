Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
keep yourselves in the love of God
This phrase emphasizes the believer's responsibility to remain within the sphere of God's love. It suggests an active, ongoing effort to live in a way that aligns with God's commandments and teachings. The concept of "keeping" oneself can be linked to John 15:9-10, where Jesus instructs His disciples to remain in His love by keeping His commandments. The love of God is both a gift and a command, requiring believers to cultivate their relationship with Him through prayer, obedience, and community with other believers. This aligns with the broader biblical theme of perseverance in faith, as seen in Philippians 2:12-13, where believers are encouraged to work out their salvation with fear and trembling, knowing that God works in them.

as you await the mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ
This phrase points to the eschatological hope that Christians hold, looking forward to the return of Jesus Christ. The "mercy" mentioned here is a reminder of the grace that believers will fully experience at Christ's second coming. This expectation is rooted in the teachings of Jesus and the apostles, such as in Titus 2:13, which speaks of the "blessed hope" of Christ's appearing. The early church lived with a strong sense of anticipation for Christ's return, which influenced their daily lives and decisions. This waiting is not passive but involves active faith and hope, as seen in 1 Thessalonians 1:10, where believers are described as waiting for God's Son from heaven.

to bring you eternal life
Eternal life is a central promise of the Christian faith, assured through the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ. This life is not only a future reality but also a present possession for believers, as indicated in John 17:3, where Jesus defines eternal life as knowing God and Jesus Christ. The assurance of eternal life is a source of comfort and motivation for believers to live holy and godly lives. The concept of eternal life is deeply rooted in the Jewish understanding of the age to come, which Jesus expanded to include both Jews and Gentiles who believe in Him. This promise is a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies, such as those found in Isaiah 25:8, which speaks of God swallowing up death forever.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jude
The author of the epistle, traditionally understood to be Jude, the brother of James and half-brother of Jesus. He writes to warn believers against false teachers and to encourage them to remain faithful.

2. God
The central figure in this verse, whose love believers are instructed to remain in. This reflects the relational aspect of faith, emphasizing God's enduring love and mercy.

3. Jesus Christ
Referred to as "our Lord," highlighting His authority and role in providing eternal life through His mercy.

4. Believers
The recipients of Jude's letter, who are encouraged to actively maintain their relationship with God and anticipate Christ's return.

5. Eternal Life
The ultimate promise and hope for believers, which is to be fully realized through the mercy of Jesus Christ.

What Does Jude 1:21 Mean
Keep yourselves in the love of God

• Jude is urging believers to stay within the sphere of God’s active, covenant love. Jesus said, “Just as the Father has loved Me, I also have loved you. Remain in My love” (John 15:9–10).

• Remaining involves conscious choices:

– Obedience to His commands (1 John 5:3).

– Ongoing fellowship with other believers (Hebrews 10:24–25).

– Guarding the truth of the gospel (2 Timothy 1:13–14).

• God’s love toward His children is unchanging, yet Jude reminds us that we can drift from a vibrant enjoyment of it through sin or neglect (Revelation 2:4–5).

• The call is personal—“keep yourselves.” The Spirit empowers, but we actively cooperate (Philippians 2:12–13).

As you await

• The Christian life is lived in a tension of present faithfulness and future expectation (Titus 2:11–13).

• “Await” is not passive; it shapes priorities. We invest in things that last (Matthew 6:19–21), remain alert (1 Thessalonians 5:6), and cultivate endurance (James 5:7–8).

• Hope fuels love. Looking forward keeps us from growing cold or discouraged (Romans 8:24–25).

The mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ

• Jude shifts focus from our effort to Christ’s compassion. At the cross we received mercy once for all (Ephesians 2:4–5), yet we still “approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy” daily (Hebrews 4:16).

• Jesus’ mercy is personal—“our Lord.” It is certain—rooted in His finished work (1 Peter 1:3–5).

• Anticipating His mercy guards against self-reliance. We stand not on our performance but on His grace (2 Timothy 1:9).

To bring you eternal life

• Eternal life is both present possession and future fullness. Jesus promised, “Whoever hears My word and believes Him who sent Me has eternal life” (John 5:24), yet we still “wait for the blessed hope” when mortality is swallowed up by life (2 Corinthians 5:4).

• Jude links mercy to eternal life to underline assurance. Christ will “keep you from stumbling and present you blameless” (Jude 1:24).

• This future perspective motivates holy living now, knowing “our citizenship is in heaven” (Philippians 3:20–21).

summary

Jude 1:21 calls believers to an active, guarded walk in God’s love, energized by a hopeful anticipation of Christ’s merciful return that consummates the eternal life already begun in us. Living loved, waiting expectantly, leaning on mercy—this is the rhythm of faithful discipleship until we step into glory.
