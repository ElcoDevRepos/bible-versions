1. Balak's sacrifices














Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Balak said to him,
Balak, the king of Moab, is speaking to Balaam, a non-Israelite prophet. Balak's repeated attempts to have Israel cursed highlight his fear and desperation due to Israel's growing power and presence. This reflects the broader biblical theme of opposition to God's chosen people.

“Please come with me to another place
Balak's strategy involves changing locations, hoping a different vantage point might influence Balaam's ability to curse Israel. This reflects ancient beliefs in the power of place and ritual in divination practices. It also underscores Balak's misunderstanding of the true source of Balaam's prophetic words, which is God, not the location.

where you can see them.
Balak desires Balaam to have a visual of the Israelites, believing that seeing them might aid in cursing them. This reflects the ancient Near Eastern practice where sight and presence were thought to enhance the effectiveness of a curse or blessing.

You will only see the outskirts of their camp—not all of them.
Balak's plan is to limit Balaam's view to only a portion of the Israelite camp. This could suggest a tactical move to make the Israelites appear less formidable or to focus the curse on a specific part of the camp. It also highlights the vastness of the Israelite encampment, fulfilling God's promise to Abraham about his descendants being numerous.

And from there, curse them for me.”
Balak's insistence on cursing Israel reveals his ignorance of God's covenant with Israel, which includes blessings and protection. This request is ultimately futile, as God's will cannot be thwarted by human schemes. The narrative foreshadows the ultimate blessing that Balaam will pronounce, which aligns with God's promises to Israel. This also prefigures the New Testament understanding of Christ, who turns curses into blessings for those who are in Him.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty
Despite human intentions, God's will prevails. Balak's attempts to curse Israel are thwarted by God's overarching plan.

Spiritual Warfare
The account illustrates the reality of spiritual battles. Believers must be aware of spiritual opposition but trust in God's protection.

Faithfulness of God
God remains faithful to His promises, protecting His people from harm. This encourages believers to trust in God's promises.

The Power of Blessing and Curse
Words have power, but God's authority supersedes human intentions. Believers should use their words to bless rather than curse.

Perspective and Obedience
Balak's limited perspective leads to misguided actions. Believers should seek God's perspective and obey His guidance.

What Does Numbers 23:13 Mean
Setting the scene

- Israel has camped on the plains of Moab after defeating neighbors (Numbers 22:1–4).

- Balak, king of Moab, fears their numbers and seeks supernatural help, hiring Balaam to curse them (Numbers 22:5–6).

- Balaam has already blessed Israel once instead of cursing (Numbers 23:7–12; cf. Deuteronomy 23:5). Balak is frustrated but hasn’t given up.

Balak’s persistence

- “Then Balak said to him” shows a king who refuses to accept God’s answer.

- Similar stubborn resistance appears in Pharaoh’s repeated hardening of heart (Exodus 8:15, 32).

- Balak believes another attempt might override the blessing; he ignores Balaam’s earlier warning, “Must I not speak what the LORD puts in my mouth?” (Numbers 23:12).

A new vantage point

- “Please come with me to another place” signals a change of strategy, not of heart.

- Moving locations recalls his earlier shift from Bamoth-baal to the field of Zophim (Numbers 23:13–14).

- He hopes environment can influence outcome, yet God’s word stands unchanged (Isaiah 40:8).

Seeing but not seeing

- “Where you can see them. You will only see the outskirts of their camp—not all of them.”

- Balak thinks a limited view might yield a different oracle.

- Human tactics try to diminish God’s people, but the promise to Abraham—“I will bless those who bless you” (Genesis 12:3)—covers the whole nation, not just part.

- Like Elisha’s servant who saw only armies until his eyes were opened (2 Kings 6:15–17), Balak’s perspective is flawed.

Demand for a curse

- “And from there, curse them for me.”

- Balak’s words echo his first request (Numbers 22:6); repetition shows desperation.

- Scripture warns against seeking mediums or curses (Deuteronomy 18:10–12).

- God later reminds Israel, “I would not listen to Balaam. Instead, I blessed you again and again, and I delivered you” (Joshua 24:10).

summary

Balak’s plan in Numbers 23:13 reveals a king doubling down on unbelief. He thinks a new location and a partial view of Israel will sway God’s prophet into pronouncing a curse. Yet the verse underscores a timeless truth: no human scheme can overturn the blessing God has decreed. The scene sets up the next oracle where God will again turn intended curses into blessings, proving His sovereignty and faithfulness to His covenant people.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Thou shalt see but the utmost part of them . . . --If this rendering be correct, it strongly confirms that interpretation of Numbers 22:41 according to which Balaam saw the whole host of Israel from Bamoth-Baal. The words may, however, be rendered thus: Thou seest (i.e., here) but the utmost part of them, and thou dost not see them all. If the interpretation of Numbers 22:41 is adopted, which restricts the view from Bamoth-Baal to the extremity of the host of Israel, the meaning of this verse would seem to be that if Balaam could obtain a full view of the entire army he would not only perceive the ground which existed for Balak's alarm, but would be induced to put forth more strenuous efforts to deliver him from so formidable an invasion. On the other hand, if that interpretation of Numbers 22:41 be adopted, which implies that from Bamoth-Baal Balaam had a view of the whole of the host of Israel from one extremity of their camps to the other, the meaning of this verse would be that although the sight of so vast and orderly a mass produced so powerful an effect upon Balaam that he was unable to utter the curses which he had desired to pronounce upon Israel, such an effect would not be equally likely to be produced if only a portion of the camps was visible at the same time.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Come... unto another place. Balak attributed the miscarriage of his enterprise thus far to something inauspicious in the locality. Thou shalt see but the utmost part of them. אֶפֶס קָצֶהוּ תִרְאֶה. Both the meaning of the nouns and the tense of the verb are disputed. By some "e ephes katsehu" (the end of the last of them) is held equivalent to "the whole of them," which seems to contradict the next clause even if defensible in itself. The ordinary rendering is favoured by the Septuagint (ἀλλ η} μέρος τι αὐτοῦ ὄψει) and by the Targums. On the other hand, some would read the verb in the present tense, and understand Balak's words to refer to the place they were leaving. This is in accordance with the statement in Numbers 22:41, and it would certainly seem as if Balak and Balaam moved each time nearer to that encampment which was for different masons the center of attraction to them both.
