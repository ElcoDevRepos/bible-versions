Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and he lifted up an oracle, saying:
In the ancient Near East, an oracle was a divine message or revelation, often delivered by a prophet or seer. Balaam, though not an Israelite, was recognized as a prophet who could communicate with God. This phrase indicates the solemnity and authority of the message that follows, suggesting it is not merely human insight but a divine revelation.

“This is the prophecy of Balaam son of Beor,
Balaam is introduced with his lineage, emphasizing his identity and the significance of his role. Balaam, though a non-Israelite, is used by God to deliver a message to His people. This highlights God's sovereignty and ability to use anyone to fulfill His purposes. Balaam's father, Beor, is mentioned to establish his prophetic credentials, as lineage was important in establishing authority and credibility in ancient cultures.

the prophecy of a man whose eyes are open,
This phrase suggests that Balaam has received a special revelation or insight from God. In biblical terms, having "open eyes" often refers to spiritual enlightenment or the ability to perceive divine truths. This is significant because it indicates that Balaam's message is not based on his own understanding but on a vision or insight granted by God. This can be connected to other instances in Scripture where prophets receive visions, such as Isaiah or Ezekiel, and it foreshadows the coming of Christ, who is the ultimate revelation of God.

Teaching Points
The Sovereignty of God
God's plans cannot be thwarted by human intentions. Despite Balak's desire to curse Israel, God uses Balaam to bless them instead.

The Danger of Divided Loyalties
Balaam's account warns against the temptation to serve both God and personal gain. His divided loyalties ultimately lead to his downfall.

Spiritual Insight and Obedience
Having spiritual insight, like Balaam's open eyes, is not enough. True obedience to God is required to align with His will.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The account underscores God's unwavering commitment to His promises to Israel, reminding believers of His faithfulness.

The Power of Blessing and Curse
Words have power, and as believers, we are called to speak blessings rather than curses, aligning our speech with God's purposes.

What Does Numbers 24:3 Mean
Lifting Up an Oracle

- Balaam “lifted up an oracle” (Numbers 24:3), meaning he publicly pronounced a divinely given message.

- This moment is not Balaam’s imagination; God directly places words in his mouth (Numbers 23:5, 16).

- Similar prophetic scenes:

• Moses delivering the Law at Sinai (Exodus 19:3–6).

• Samuel revealing God’s word to Saul (1 Samuel 10:1).

- God’s sovereign choice of even a pagan seer underscores His power to use anyone for His redemptive purposes (Isaiah 46:10).

Declaring the Message

- The phrase “saying” introduces inspired speech, setting it apart from Balaam’s earlier attempts to curse Israel for money (Numbers 22:7, 18).

- Prophetic statements carry divine authority; they are not suggestions but settled truth (2 Peter 1:21; Jeremiah 1:7).

- By recording the exact words, Scripture emphasizes verbal inspiration—every word matters (Matthew 5:18).

This Is the Prophecy of Balaam Son of Beor

- Naming the speaker establishes accountability: Balaam cannot claim ignorance or deny authorship.

- His lineage “son of Beor” grounds him in history, showing this event occurred in real time (Numbers 22:5).

- God brings a foreign diviner onto the biblical stage to bless Israel, fulfilling His promise to Abraham that those who bless Israel will be blessed (Genesis 12:3).

- Despite Balaam’s mixed motives (Numbers 31:16; 2 Peter 2:15), God’s word through him is flawless (Psalm 12:6).

The Prophecy of a Man Whose Eyes Are Open

- “Eyes are open” signifies spiritual sight: Balaam now perceives God’s revelation clearly (Numbers 24:4, 16).

- Contrast: earlier, Balaam’s physical eyes failed to see the Angel of the LORD blocking the way (Numbers 22:31).

- True prophetic insight comes from God, not human technique (1 Corinthians 2:10–14).

- Application points:

• Spiritual discernment begins when God opens our eyes (Psalm 119:18).

• Even the unwilling can become instruments of blessing when God intervenes (Proverbs 21:1).

summary

Numbers 24:3 shows God empowering Balaam to deliver a genuine, God-breathed oracle of blessing over Israel. By identifying Balaam, stressing the divine origin of the message, and affirming that his “eyes are open,” the verse highlights God’s sovereignty, the trustworthiness of prophecy, and the necessity of spiritual sight granted by the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Balaam the son of Beor hath said.--The Hebrew word (neum) is imperfectly rendered by hath said. It is the word which is commonly used in the prophetical books of Scripture to denote a Divine saying, and is rarely used when a human author is named. It occurs in the Pentateuch only in Genesis 22:16, Numbers 14:28, and in this chapter, where it is found in Numbers 24:3-4; Numbers 24:15-16.

The man whose eyes are open.--There is great diversity of opinion respecting the meaning of the word which is here rendered open, and which, as it is here written, occurs in no other place. If shatham is identified with satham, it means to close, not to open. The meaning, however, of this verse is sufficiently explained by that which follows, whichever rendering of the word shethum is adopted. Balaam appears to have been thrown into an ecstatic state, as was Saul, and as were many of the ancient prophets; and whilst the eye of the outer senses was closed, the eye of the inner senses was preternaturally opened.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Balaam... hath said. Rather, "the utterance of Balaam." נְאֻם is constantly used, as in Numbers 14:28, for a Divine utterance, effatum Dei, but it does not by itself, apart from the context, claim a superhuman origin. The man whose eyes are open. הַגֶּבֶר שְׁתֻם הָעָיִן. The authorities are divided between the rendering in the text and the opposite rendering given in the margin. סָתַם is used in Daniel 8:26, and שָׂתָם in Lamentations 3:8, in the sense of "shut;" but, on the other hand, a passage in the Mishnah distinctly uses שׁתם and סתם in opposite senses. The Vulgate, on the one hand, has obturatus; the Septuagint, on the other, has ὁ ἀληθινῶς ὁρῶν, and this is the sense given by the Targums. Strange to say, it makes no real difference whether we read "open" or "shut," because in any case it was the inward vision that was quickened, while the outward senses were closed.
