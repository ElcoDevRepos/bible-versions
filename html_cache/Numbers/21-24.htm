And Israel put him to the sword
This phrase refers to the Israelites' military victory over Sihon, king of the Amorites. The Israelites, under God's command, engaged in battle as they journeyed toward the Promised Land. This victory is significant as it demonstrates God's faithfulness in delivering His people and fulfilling His promises. The phrase "put him to the sword" indicates a complete and decisive victory, which was common in ancient warfare narratives. This event is a fulfillment of God's promise to give the Israelites the land of the Amorites (Deuteronomy 2:24-36).

and took possession of his land
The Israelites' taking possession of the land signifies the beginning of the fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham regarding the land (Genesis 15:18-21). This act of taking possession is not just a military conquest but also a divine mandate, as the land was part of the inheritance promised to the descendants of Abraham. The possession of the land also symbolizes God's provision and the establishment of Israel as a nation.

from the Arnon to the Jabbok
The Arnon and the Jabbok are rivers that define the geographical boundaries of the land taken from Sihon. The Arnon River marked the southern boundary, while the Jabbok River marked the northern boundary. This territory was strategically important, providing control over key trade routes and fertile land. The mention of these rivers highlights the specific fulfillment of God's promise and the tangible nature of the Israelites' inheritance.

but only up to the border of the Ammonites
The Israelites respected the border of the Ammonites, as God had instructed them not to harass or provoke the Ammonites (Deuteronomy 2:19). This respect for boundaries demonstrates Israel's obedience to God's commands and the recognition of God's sovereignty over the nations. The Ammonites were descendants of Lot, Abraham's nephew, and thus had a distinct relationship with Israel.

because it was fortified
The fortification of the Ammonite border indicates the presence of strong defenses, which were common in ancient Near Eastern cities. These fortifications served as protection against invasions and were a testament to the military and political strength of the Ammonites. The Israelites' decision not to engage with the Ammonites reflects a strategic choice to avoid unnecessary conflict and adhere to God's specific instructions regarding their conquests.

Persons / Places / Events

2. Sihon
The Amorite king who opposed Israel and was defeated by them.

3. Arnon

4. Jabbok

5. Ammonites
A neighboring people whose border was respected by Israel due to its fortification and possibly divine instruction.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Battles
This passage illustrates God's control over the outcomes of battles. Israel's victory over Sihon was not merely a military success but a fulfillment of God's promise.

Respect for Boundaries
Israel's decision to not encroach upon the Ammonite territory demonstrates a respect for boundaries, which can be seen as an acknowledgment of God's sovereignty over nations and their lands.

Faith and Obedience
Israel's willingness to engage in battle against Sihon, despite the potential risks, reflects their faith in God's promises and their obedience to His commands.

Divine Provision
The acquisition of land signifies God's provision for His people, reminding believers that God provides for our needs in His timing and way.

Historical Validation of Faith
The historical accounts of Israel's conquests serve to validate the faith of believers, showing that God's promises are reliable and His power is unmatched.

What Does Numbers 21:24 Mean
And Israel put him to the sword

- Sihon, king of the Amorites, had just refused Israel peaceful passage and came out against them (Numbers 21:21-23).

- Acting on God’s clear command, Israel fought: “The LORD said to me, ‘See, I have begun to deliver Sihon and his land over to you’” (Deuteronomy 2:31-33).

- The victory shows that God’s promises are literal and certain, just as He earlier pledged to defeat every foe in Canaan (Exodus 23:27-28).

- God’s judgment on the Amorites had reached its fullness (Genesis 15:16). Israel became the instrument, not an aggressor seeking plunder.

and took possession of his land

- Possession fulfilled God’s covenant pledge: “To your offspring I will give this land” (Genesis 12:7; 15:18-21).

- This is Israel’s first actual territory, east of the Jordan, later allotted to Reuben, Gad, and half-Manasseh (Numbers 32:33; Joshua 13:8-12).

- The transfer underscores:

• God keeps His word.

• Obedience opens the way for inheritance (Deuteronomy 11:22-25).

• Former strongholds of pagan worship are now dedicated to the LORD (Deuteronomy 12:2-5).

from the Arnon to the Jabbok

- The Arnon River marked Moab’s northern border; the Jabbok River lay south of Ammon. This stretch covers all of Sihon’s domain (Deuteronomy 2:36).

- Geographically it includes Aroer, Medeba, and up through Gilead (Joshua 12:2-6).

- Spiritually it illustrates complete victory; nothing God gives remains out of reach when His people trust Him (Psalm 44:3).

—but only up to the border of the Ammonites, because it was fortified.

- Israel deliberately stopped where God said stop. “Do not harass the Ammonites or provoke them to war” (Deuteronomy 2:19, 37).

- The Ammonites were relatives through Lot (Genesis 19:38); their land was not part of Israel’s promise.

- “Fortified” points to strong defenses, yet the primary reason Israel refrained was obedience, not intimidation. Respecting God-set boundaries matters as much as taking God-given territory (Proverbs 30:5-6).

- The scene highlights restrained power—victory under control, governed by divine directive.

summary

Numbers 21:24 records more than a military success; it displays God’s faithful fulfillment of promise, His righteous judgment on hardened opposition, and the necessity of precise obedience. Israel wins where God says fight, inherits what God says possess, and halts where God says halt. The verse invites confidence that every word God speaks—whether promise, warning, or boundary—is trustworthy and good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) For the border of the children of Ammon was strong.--These words assign the reason why the conquests of the Amorites were arrested, not why the children of Israel did not take possession of the land of the Ammonites, with whom they were forbidden to meddle, and whose land they were not to occupy. (See Deuteronomy 2:19.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - And Israel smote him with the edge of the sword. This was the first time that generation had seen war, if we except the uncertain episode of the king of Arad, and they could have had no weapons but such as their fathers had brought out of Egypt. It was, therefore, a critical moment in their history when they met the forces of Sihon, confident from their recent victory over Moab. We may suppose that Joshua was their military leader now, as before and after. From Arnon unto Jabbok. The Jabbok, which formed the boundary of Sihon on the north towards the kingdom of Og, and on the east towards the Ammonites, is the modern Zerka: it runs in a large curve northeast, north-west, and west, until it fails into Jordan, forty-five miles north of the mouth of the Arnon. Even unto the children of Ammon: for the border of the children of Ammon was strong. This is perhaps intended to explain rather why the Amorites had not extended their conquests any further, than why the Israelites made no attempt to cross the border of Ammon; they had another and more sufficient reason (see Deuteronomy 2:19). Rabbah of Ammon, which stood upon the right (here the eastern) bank of the Upper Jabbok, was an extremely strong place which effectually protected the country behind it, even until the reign of David (see on 2 Samuel 11, 12).
