Now the daughters of Zelophehad son of Hepher
Zelophehad is introduced as a descendant of Hepher, indicating his lineage within the tribe of Manasseh. This genealogy is significant as it establishes the daughters' rightful claim to their father's inheritance. In the patriarchal society of ancient Israel, lineage and inheritance were typically passed through male descendants, making the mention of daughters noteworthy. This sets the stage for a legal and cultural shift regarding inheritance rights.

the son of Gilead, the son of Machir, the son of Manasseh
This lineage traces back to Manasseh, one of Joseph's sons, who was born in Egypt. Manasseh was one of the two tribes that descended from Joseph, the other being Ephraim. The mention of Gilead and Machir highlights the family's connection to the land east of the Jordan River, which was allocated to the half-tribe of Manasseh. This geographical context is important as it underscores the daughters' claim to land that was part of their ancestral heritage.

belonged to the clans of Manasseh son of Joseph
The reference to the clans of Manasseh son of Joseph emphasizes the tribal structure of Israelite society. Each tribe was divided into clans, which were further divided into families. This structure was crucial for maintaining order and distributing land among the Israelites. The daughters' association with the clan of Manasseh underscores their legitimate place within the tribal system and their right to seek an inheritance.

These were the names of his daughters: Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah
The naming of Zelophehad's daughters is significant as it personalizes their story and highlights their individual identities. In biblical narratives, the naming of women is often associated with important events or roles they play. The inclusion of their names in the text underscores the importance of their legal case and the precedent it sets for future generations. Each name carries its own meaning and cultural significance, reflecting the values and hopes of their family.

They approached
The act of approaching indicates a bold and proactive step taken by the daughters. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, women typically did not have the same legal standing as men, especially in matters of inheritance. Their approach to the leaders of Israel demonstrates courage and a desire for justice. This action sets a precedent for addressing legal and social issues within the community, highlighting the importance of advocacy and the pursuit of equity.

What Does Numbers 27:1 Mean
Now the daughters of Zelophehad

“Now the daughters of Zelophehad…” (Numbers 27:1) begins with a surprising focus on women in a male-centered society. God is highlighting:

• The reality that these five women truly existed in Israel’s camp (Numbers 26:33; Joshua 17:3).

• His willingness to involve women in covenant matters, anticipating later passages such as Proverbs 31:10–31 and Luke 8:1–3, where women’s faithfulness is again spotlighted.

This opening clause sets the stage for a moment that will reshape Israel’s inheritance customs (Numbers 27:7).

son of Hepher, the son of Gilead, the son of Machir, the son of Manasseh

The Holy Spirit traces the family line four generations back:

• Hepher ➔ Gilead ➔ Machir ➔ Manasseh (compare Numbers 26:29–34; Genesis 50:23).

• By rooting these daughters in Manasseh, God links their plea to Joseph’s promise of future blessing (Genesis 48:20).

• This genealogy underscores covenant continuity; inheritance flows through specific, historic families (Ruth 4:18–22 shows a similar pattern).

belonged to the clans of Manasseh son of Joseph

Their tribal identity matters because land was parceled by tribe (Numbers 26:55). If left unresolved, the absence of a male heir could scatter their allotment into other tribes. This brief phrase therefore foreshadows:

• God’s later directive that the daughters marry within Manasseh to keep land secure (Numbers 36:6–9).

• A broader biblical theme: God safeguards His people’s inheritance (1 Peter 1:4).

These were the names of his daughters: Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah

Listing all five names personalizes the account:

• God knows each by name, much like He knows believers individually (Isaiah 43:1; John 10:3).

• Repeating the names in later passages (Numbers 36:11; Joshua 17:3; 1 Chronicles 7:15) reinforces their importance to Israel’s story.

• The number five—often signaling grace—reminds us that God graciously includes unexpected participants in His plans (Genesis 6:8; Ephesians 2:8-9).

They approached

The verse ends with movement: “They approached.” In context (Numbers 27:2), they come before Moses, Eleazar, the leaders, and the entire congregation. Their action models:

• Bold faith that steps forward rather than remaining silent (Hebrews 4:16).

• Respect for God-ordained leadership while seeking justice under His law (Deuteronomy 17:9-10).

• Confidence that God listens to the cries of the marginalized (James 4:8).

summary

Numbers 27:1 foregrounds five sisters whose courageous approach will prompt new inheritance legislation. By tracing their lineage, naming each daughter, and noting their bold advance, the verse affirms God’s detailed care for His people and His commitment to just, orderly, and inclusive distribution of covenant blessings within the family of faith.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - The daughters of Zelophehad. The genealogy here given agrees with those in Numbers 26:29-33 and in Joshua 17:3. These women would appear to have been in the eighth generation from Jacob, which hardly accords with the 470 years required by the narrative; some links, however, may have been dropped.
