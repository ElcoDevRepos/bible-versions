In the fourth year of King Darius
This phrase situates the prophecy within a specific historical context. King Darius I of Persia reigned from 522 to 486 BC, and the fourth year of his reign would be around 518 BC. This period is significant as it follows the return of the Jewish exiles from Babylon, which began in 538 BC under the decree of Cyrus. The rebuilding of the temple in Jerusalem was underway, and the Jewish community was re-establishing itself in the land. Darius was known for his administrative skills and efforts to consolidate the Persian Empire, which provided a relatively stable environment for the Jews to rebuild their society and religious practices.

the word of the LORD came to Zechariah
This indicates divine revelation, a common occurrence in the prophetic books of the Old Testament. Zechariah, a prophet and priest, was a contemporary of Haggai and played a crucial role in encouraging the Jewish people during the post-exilic period. The phrase underscores the authority and divine origin of the message, emphasizing that it is not merely human wisdom but a communication from God. Prophets like Zechariah were instrumental in guiding the people back to covenant faithfulness and in providing hope for future restoration.

on the fourth day of the ninth month
The specificity of the date highlights the importance of the message and its historical accuracy. The fourth day of the ninth month corresponds to a time in the Jewish calendar that would be late November or early December. This precision in dating is typical of prophetic literature, which often records the exact timing of divine revelations to underscore their authenticity and urgency.

the month of Chislev
Chislev is the ninth month in the Hebrew calendar, which corresponds to November-December in the Gregorian calendar. This month is significant in Jewish history, as it is the time when the festival of Hanukkah is celebrated, commemorating the rededication of the Second Temple. Although Hanukkah was established later, the mention of Chislev in Zechariah's time reflects the ongoing process of religious and cultural restoration among the Jewish people. The timing of this prophecy during Chislev may also suggest themes of renewal and dedication, aligning with the broader message of Zechariah's ministry.

What Does Zechariah 7:1 Mean
In the fourth year of King Darius

• Zechariah anchors his prophecy to a specific point in history—about 518 BC—during the reign of the Persian ruler Darius I.

• This precision echoes Zechariah 1:1, Haggai 1:1, and Ezra 6:14–15, underscoring that God acts in real time and space, not in myth or legend.

• Setting the message during Darius’s reign reminds the remnant that, though they live under a foreign king, the true King of kings still directs events (Proverbs 21:1).

the word of the LORD came to Zechariah

• The phrase signals divine initiative: the prophet is merely the messenger, not the source (Jeremiah 1:4–9).

• Scripture affirms this dynamic—“All Scripture is God-breathed” (2 Timothy 3:16) and “men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit” (2 Peter 1:21).

• Because it is God’s word, the message carries absolute authority; the people must heed it just as surely as if God spoke audibly from heaven (Isaiah 55:10–11).

on the fourth day of the ninth month

• A precise calendar date—“the fourth day”—demonstrates God’s detailed oversight. Nothing is random or approximate with Him (Psalm 139:16).

• The ninth month falls in late autumn, when the initial fervor of temple rebuilding (cf. Haggai 2:18) may have waned. God steps in to re-energize His people.

• Such timestamping shows that prophetic fulfillment can be verified; the Bible invites historical scrutiny because it is true (Luke 1:1–4).

the month of Chislev

• “Chislev” corresponds roughly to late November–December. By naming the month, Zechariah aligns the Jewish calendar with the Gentile dating system under Darius—illustrating God’s sovereignty over both sacred and secular timeframes.

• Nehemiah 1:1 opens with the same month, linking the rebuilding eras of temple and walls. God weaves separate stories into one redemptive tapestry (Romans 8:28).

• The wintry season hints at hardship and dormancy, yet God’s word arrives, warming hearts and calling for renewed obedience (Psalm 74:17; Songs 2:11–12).

summary

Zechariah 7:1 is more than a timestamp; it is a four-fold reminder that God speaks precise, authoritative truth into concrete history. Under a pagan king, on a specific day, in a particular month, the Lord’s unchanging word came with clarity and purpose. The verse assures us that the God who governs calendars and kingdoms still addresses His people today—and His word remains utterly reliable and ready to be obeyed.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1-ch. 8:23. - Part II. THE ANSWER TO A QUESTION CONCERNING THE OBSERVANCE OF CERTAIN FASTS. Verses 1-3. - § 1. A deputation comes from Bethel to ask whether a fast instituted in memory of the calamity of Jerusalem was still to be observed. Verse 1. - In the fourth year of King Darius. This happened, then, B.C. 518, nearly two years after the visions had occurred (Zechariah 1:7). In two years more the temple was finished (Ezra 6:15), and the work of rebuilding was now proceeding vigorously; it seemed a fit opportunity for inquiring whether, in this period of comparative prosperity and success, it behoved the people to continue the fast appointed in sadder times. The word of the Lord came. This is the usual formula for introducing a revelation (Zechariah 1:1), but it is here placed in a peculiar position, dividing the date into two parts. Keil connects the last clause, which gives the day of the month, with the next verse; but this is against the traditional accentuation, and is not required by the wording of ver. 2. The prophet first gives the date generally when the word came to him, and then defines it more accurately. Chisleu; Chislev (Nehemiah 1:1). This month corresponded to parts of November and December.
