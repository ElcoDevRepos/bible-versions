Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Awake, O sword, against My Shepherd
This phrase signifies a divine command for judgment. The "sword" is often a symbol of divine justice or judgment in the Bible. The "Shepherd" refers to a leader or guide, often used to describe kings or spiritual leaders. In the context of prophecy, this Shepherd is understood to be the Messiah, Jesus Christ. The call for the sword to "awake" suggests an impending action ordained by God, highlighting the seriousness and inevitability of the event.

against the man who is My Companion
The term "Companion" indicates a close relationship, suggesting equality or partnership. This phrase underscores the unique relationship between God and the Messiah, affirming the divinity and humanity of Jesus. The use of "man" emphasizes the incarnation of Christ, who is both fully God and fully man. This relationship is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus is described as being one with the Father (John 10:30).

declares the LORD of Hosts
This declaration comes from the "LORD of Hosts," a title that emphasizes God's sovereignty and command over the heavenly armies. It underscores the authority and power behind the prophecy, indicating that the events to follow are part of God's divine plan. This title is frequently used in the Old Testament to assert God's supreme authority and control over all creation.

Strike the Shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered
This prophecy is directly referenced in the New Testament, specifically in Matthew 26:31 and Mark 14:27, where Jesus predicts His disciples will abandon Him after His arrest. The "Shepherd" being struck refers to the crucifixion of Christ, and the "sheep" symbolize His followers, who would be disoriented and dispersed without their leader. This scattering fulfills the prophecy and highlights the human tendency to falter in faith during trials.

and I will turn My hand against the little ones
The "little ones" likely refer to the followers of the Shepherd, possibly indicating the disciples or the early Christian community. The phrase "turn My hand against" suggests a period of trial or testing. This can be seen in the persecution faced by the early church, which, while difficult, ultimately led to the spread of the Gospel. It reflects the refining process God often uses to strengthen and purify His people, as seen throughout biblical history.

What Does Zechariah 13:7 Mean
Awake, O sword

God summons the sword—His chosen instrument of judgment—to action.

• Isaiah 34:5 and Jeremiah 47:6 show the “sword” as a vivid picture of divine justice coming fully awake at God’s command.

• Romans 13:4 reminds us that God does not wield the sword aimlessly; it is a purposeful, righteous tool in His hand.

• The call to “awake” signals a decisive, divinely timed moment, not a random burst of violence.

against My Shepherd

The target is God’s own Shepherd—ultimately Jesus, the Good Shepherd.

• Psalm 23:1 identifies the LORD as Shepherd; Ezekiel 34:23 promises a singular, future Shepherd.

• Jesus applies this title to Himself in John 10:11, drawing a straight line from Zechariah’s prophecy to His mission.

• The Shepherd’s unique role underscores that what follows is both personal to God and essential to His redemptive plan.

against the man who is My Companion

“Companion” speaks of shared nature and intimate fellowship.

• John 1:1 says, “the Word was with God, and the Word was God,” matching the closeness pictured here.

• Philippians 2:6 affirms Christ’s equality with God, while John 10:30 records Jesus saying, “I and the Father are one.”

• The Shepherd is not a distant servant; He is God’s own equal, willingly stepping into harm’s way.

declares the LORD of Hosts

The LORD of armies stakes His name on this prophecy.

• Isaiah 6:3 extols His holiness; Revelation 1:8 calls Him “the Almighty,” grounding every promise in omnipotence.

• This divine declaration guarantees fulfillment; human schemes cannot derail what He proclaims.

Strike the Shepherd

The sword falls. Jesus is crucified.

• Isaiah 53:4-6 foretells the suffering Servant pierced for our transgressions.

• Jesus cites this very line in Matthew 26:31 just before His arrest, identifying Himself as the Shepherd who will be struck.

• Acts 2:23 notes He was “handed over by God’s set purpose,” confirming the strike was foreseen and foreordained.

and the sheep will be scattered

When the Shepherd is struck, the flock panics and flees.

• Fulfilled literally in Matthew 26:56 and John 16:32 as the disciples abandon Jesus.

• Scattering also portrays Israel’s broader dispersion (Zechariah 11:17) and the vulnerability of people without godly leadership.

• Yet even in scattering, God’s eye remains on His sheep, preparing to regather them (John 10:16).

and I will turn My hand against the little ones

God’s hand moves toward the “little ones”—the humble, the remnant—for refining, not destruction.

• Zechariah 13:8-9 continues with a purifying fire that yields a faithful third.

• Hebrews 12:6 assures that “the Lord disciplines the one He loves,” while Luke 12:32 calls believers a “little flock.”

• Trials that follow the Shepherd’s striking become divine instruments shaping steadfast, purified followers.

summary

Zechariah 13:7 is a precise prophecy of Christ’s crucifixion and its immediate aftermath. God awakens His judgment, directs it at His own equal—the Shepherd—then allows temporary scattering and refining of the flock. Every detail unfolds under the absolute sovereignty of the LORD of Hosts, confirming both the reliability of Scripture and the central place of the cross in God’s plan to save and purify His people.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 7-9. - § 4. For the smiting of the good Shepherd Israel is punished, passes through much tribulation, by which it is refined, and in the end (though reduced to a mere remnant) is saved. Verse 7. - Awake, O sword. Zechariah proceeds to show the course of the purification of the people. The mention of the false prophet and the shameful wounds in his flesh leads him to the contrast of the true Prophet and the effects of his "piercing." The abruptness of the commencement of the verse is dramatic, and gives no sufficient cause for supposing that this paragraph ought to be transferred (as Ewald and others desire) to the end of ch. 11. (For a similar apostrophe, comp. Jeremiah 47:6.) It is introduced here to show that all that happened to the Shepherd was done after the determinate counsel and foreknowledge of God; and as if the sword could never have dared to act thus except it were permitted by the Divine will. The "sword" represents any kind of instrument that inflicts death (comp. Exodus 5:21; 2 Samuel 12:9; Isaiah 27:1). My Shepherd. The Shepherd of Jehovah, who is speaking. He is the good Shepherd, the Representative of Jehovah, mentioned in Zechariah 11:4, etc., the Messiah, who is identified with Jehovah in Zechariah 12:10. The Septuagint has, τοὺς ποιμένας μου, "my shepherds" (Vatican), as if no particular person was indicated, but rather all the leaders of the people of God; but the next clause seems to render the reference definite. The man that is my fellow. The word rendered "man" means rather "mighty man;" that rendered "fellow" occurs often in Leviticus, but nowhere else (Leviticus 5:21; 6:2; 19:11, 15, 17, etc.), and is usually translated "neighbour;" it implies one united to another by the possession of common nature, rights, and privileges. God could speak only of One thus associated with himself, that is, of him who could say, "I and my Father are One" (John 10:30). The term is variously translated by the versions. Septuagint, Ανδρα = πολίτην μου: Aquila, Ανδρα σύμφυλον μου: Vulgate, Virum cohaerentem mihi. That the Shepherd is Messiah is proved by Christ's application of the following clause to himself (Matthew 26:31). Smite the Shepherd, and the sheep shall be scattered. When Christ was apprehended, all the disciples forsook him and fled (Matthew 26:56); and what they did was done by others. Even the faithful few were scandalized at the cross The command, "Smite the Shepherd," like the apostrophe, "Awake, O sword," shows that it was God's purpose that was being there executed (see John 19:11; Acts 2:23). It is also thus intimated that the dispersion of the Jews, and their denationalizing, were results of this rejection and smiting of the Shepherd. This dispersion is farther explained in vers. 8, 9, where it is shown that to some it will be ruin, to others salvation. I will turn mine hand. "To turn," or "bring back the hand over," is used in a good and a bad sense (comp. Isaiah 1:25; Amos 1:8). There is a promise of comfort in the use of the phrase here. God's hand shall cover and protect some, while he punishes the others. Those thus protected are called the little ones, the humble and meek. This recalls Christ's words to his disciples, "Fear not, little flock; for it is your Father's good pleasure to give you the kingdom" (Luke 12:32).
