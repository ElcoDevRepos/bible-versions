Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And the LORD said to me:
This phrase indicates direct communication from God to the prophet Zechariah. In the context of the Old Testament, God often spoke to His prophets to deliver messages to His people. Zechariah, a post-exilic prophet, was tasked with conveying God's words to the Israelites who had returned from Babylonian captivity. This communication underscores the authority and divine origin of the message.

Take up once more:
The phrase "once more" suggests a return to a previous action or role. Earlier in Zechariah 11, the prophet had symbolically acted out the role of a shepherd, representing God's leadership over Israel. This repetition emphasizes the cyclical nature of Israel's history, where periods of faithfulness are followed by rebellion and judgment. It also highlights the patience and persistence of God in dealing with His people.

the equipment of a foolish shepherd:
The "equipment" refers to the tools or symbols of a shepherd's trade, such as a staff or rod. In biblical times, shepherds were responsible for the care and protection of their flocks, a common metaphor for leadership. However, the term "foolish shepherd" indicates a leader who is neglectful or harmful, contrasting with the ideal shepherd who is wise and caring. This imagery is prophetic, pointing to future leaders of Israel who would fail in their duties. It also serves as a type of Christ, the Good Shepherd, who contrasts with these foolish leaders by perfectly caring for His flock. The foolish shepherd can be seen as a warning against false leaders and a call to recognize the true Shepherd.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Zechariah
A prophet of the post-exilic period, Zechariah is the one receiving the message from the LORD. His role is to convey God's messages to the people of Israel.

2. The LORD
The covenant God of Israel, who communicates His will and plans through His prophets. In this context, He instructs Zechariah to take symbolic action.

3. Foolish Shepherd
Represents leaders who fail to care for their flock, symbolizing irresponsible and self-serving leadership. The term "foolish" in Hebrew ('ewil) implies moral deficiency and lack of wisdom.

4. Israel
The nation to whom Zechariah is prophesying. They are the recipients of God's messages and are often depicted as sheep in need of a shepherd.

5. Prophetic Symbolism
The act of taking up the equipment of a foolish shepherd is a symbolic gesture meant to convey a deeper spiritual truth about leadership and responsibility.
Teaching Points
The Responsibility of Leadership
Leaders are accountable to God for the care and guidance of those they lead. Neglect and self-interest are condemned by God.

Symbolism in Prophecy
Prophetic actions often carry deep spiritual meanings. Understanding these symbols helps us grasp the seriousness of God's messages.

The Danger of Foolishness
Foolishness, especially in leadership, leads to destruction and harm. Wisdom and moral integrity are essential for those in authority.

God's Sovereignty in Judgment
God allows certain leaders to rise as a form of judgment, but He also promises redemption and restoration through righteous leadership.

Christ as the Ultimate Shepherd
Jesus fulfills the role of the Good Shepherd, providing the perfect example of leadership and care for His people.

What Does Zechariah 11:15 Mean
And the LORD said to me:

- God Himself initiates the message, underscoring His absolute authority (Isaiah 1:2; Revelation 22:6).

- Zechariah is reminded of his prophetic role; like Samuel who replied, “Speak, for Your servant is listening” (1 Samuel 3:10), he stands ready to obey.

- The statement also signals a serious shift in the prophecy, comparable to when the Lord said to Ezekiel, “Son of man, prophesy” (Ezekiel 6:2), introducing words of warning and judgment.

Take up once more

- “Once more” reaches back to Zechariah 11:4–14, when the prophet acted out shepherding Israel with two staffs, Favor and Union. God now calls him to repeat a symbolic enactment.

- This repetition highlights Israel’s persistent rejection of true guidance, much as Jesus later lamented, “O Jerusalem… how often I wanted to gather your children” (Matthew 23:37).

- Prophetic drama is God’s teaching tool: Hosea married Gomer (Hosea 1:2) and Ezekiel lay on his side (Ezekiel 4:4–6); here Zechariah must once again embody God’s message so no one can miss it.

the equipment of a foolish shepherd.

- “Equipment” points to the visible tools—staff, cloak, pouch—identifying a shepherd. By picking them up, Zechariah impersonates the kind of leader Israel will choose after rejecting the Good Shepherd (Zechariah 11:12–13; John 19:15).

- “Foolish” in Scripture describes moral and spiritual blindness (Psalm 14:1). Such a shepherd:

• Neglects the flock (Ezekiel 34:2–4).

• Abandons the sheep in danger (John 10:12–13).

• Seeks personal gain and power (Jeremiah 23:1–2).

- Verse 17 will pronounce “Woe to the worthless shepherd,” linking this figure to end-time deception (2 Thessalonians 2:3–4), but the warning also applied immediately to Israel’s leaders who paved the way for Rome’s devastation (Luke 19:41–44).

- By ordering Zechariah to wield these props, God exposes both the folly of ungodly leadership and the tragedy awaiting any people who prefer it (Proverbs 29:18).

summary

The Lord commands Zechariah to act out a vivid lesson: after spurning the Good Shepherd, Israel will experience the rule of a foolish, self-serving shepherd. Every phrase underscores divine authority, the repetition of prophetic warning, and the stark contrast between faithful and faithless leadership. The verse therefore calls readers to embrace the true Shepherd and reject every counterfeit, for only in Him is safety, guidance, and life.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 15-17. - § 10. In retribution for their rejection of the good Shepherd the people are given over to a foolish shepherd, who shall destroy them, but shall himself, in turn, perish miserably. Verse 15. - Take unto thee yet (yet again) the instruments of a foolish shepherd (comp. Hosea 3:1). The prophet, in vision, is directed to do as he had done before (ver. 4, etc,), and enact the part of a shepherd, taking the dress, scrip, and crook, which were appropriate to the character; but this time he was to represent "a foolish," i.e. an evil, shepherd; for sin is constantly denoted by "folly" in the Old Testament; e.g. Job 5:2, 3; Psalm 14:1; Psalm 107:17; Proverbs 1:7; Proverbs 7:22; Proverbs 14:9, etc. (comp. ver. 17).
