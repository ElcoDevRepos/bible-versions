Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
All the land from Geba to Rimmon south of Jerusalem will be turned into a plain
This phrase describes a significant topographical transformation. Geba, located in the territory of Benjamin, and Rimmon, situated in the southern part of Judah, mark the northern and southern boundaries of this transformation. The leveling of the land into a plain symbolizes a divine intervention, often associated with the preparation for the establishment of God's kingdom. This imagery echoes prophetic themes found in Isaiah 40:4, where valleys are raised and mountains made low, signifying the removal of obstacles for God's people.

but Jerusalem will be raised up and will remain in her place
Jerusalem's elevation signifies its exaltation and centrality in God's plan. This imagery of being "raised up" can be seen as a metaphor for spiritual prominence and divine favor. The city remaining in its place suggests stability and permanence, contrasting with the upheaval of the surrounding regions. This aligns with prophecies like Isaiah 2:2, where Jerusalem is depicted as the highest of the mountains, drawing nations to it.

from the Benjamin Gate to the site of the First Gate to the Corner Gate
The mention of specific gates highlights the historical and geographical context of ancient Jerusalem. The Benjamin Gate, likely located on the northern side, indicates the city's connection to the tribe of Benjamin. The First Gate and the Corner Gate, though their exact locations are debated, suggest a comprehensive inclusion of the city's boundaries. These gates symbolize access and protection, reflecting the city's fortified nature as seen in Nehemiah's rebuilding efforts (Nehemiah 3:1-32).

and from the Tower of Hananel to the royal winepresses
The Tower of Hananel, mentioned in Nehemiah 3:1 and Jeremiah 31:38, was part of Jerusalem's fortifications, representing strength and vigilance. The royal winepresses indicate the city's prosperity and agricultural abundance, essential for its economy and sustenance. This combination of defense and provision underscores Jerusalem's role as a secure and thriving center in the messianic age, resonating with the imagery of peace and prosperity in Zechariah 8:12.

Teaching Points
The Elevation of Jerusalem
The prophecy of Jerusalem being raised signifies God's intention to establish His presence and authority. It reminds us of the importance of spiritual elevation in our lives, seeking to be closer to God and His purposes.

God's Sovereignty Over Geography
The transformation of the land into a plain and the elevation of Jerusalem demonstrate God's control over creation. This encourages believers to trust in God's power to change circumstances and landscapes in our lives.

Symbolism of Gates and Landmarks
The specific mention of gates and landmarks in Jerusalem highlights the importance of spiritual fortifications and boundaries. Believers are called to establish and maintain spiritual boundaries that protect their faith and integrity.

Unity and Inclusivity in God's Plan
The leveling of the land from Geba to Rimmon suggests a unification under God's rule. This points to the inclusivity of God's kingdom, where all are invited to come and worship.

Hope for Restoration
The prophecy offers hope for restoration and renewal. In times of personal or communal desolation, believers can hold onto the promise of God's restorative power.

What Does Zechariah 14:10 Mean
All the land from Geba to Rimmon south of Jerusalem will be turned into a plain

“From Geba to Rimmon” stretches the territory north to south of Jerusalem (cf. Joshua 18:24; Joshua 15:32). The verse pictures a vast topographical upheaval when the Lord comes (Zechariah 14:4–5).

• Literally, the hill country surrounding the capital will be leveled, forming an expansive plain. Isaiah 40:4 anticipates this: “Every valley shall be lifted up and every mountain and hill made low.”

• The flattening clears the way for nations to approach Jerusalem unhindered (Isaiah 2:2–3; Micah 4:1–2).

• It also echoes Revelation 16:18–20, where earthquake and upheaval accompany the final judgment.

but Jerusalem will be raised up and will remain in her place

While everything else sinks to a level plain, Jerusalem itself is lifted—physically elevated and permanently secure.

• Psalm 48:1–2 calls Zion “the joy of all the earth… Mount Zion in the farthest north.” The imagery now becomes literal.

• Zechariah 8:3 promised, “I will return to Zion and dwell in Jerusalem.” Here that promise reaches full realization: the city stands immovable, the center of Messiah’s kingdom (Zechariah 14:9).

• Isaiah 2:2 foresees the “mountain of the house of the LORD” as chief among the mountains—now fulfilled by the city’s exaltation.

from the Benjamin Gate to the site of the First Gate to the Corner Gate

Three historic gates mark the northern and western stretches of ancient Jerusalem (Jeremiah 37:13; 2 Kings 14:13). Mentioning them underscores that every part of the rebuilt city will remain intact.

• No quarter is abandoned or in ruins; the populace lives securely throughout (Zechariah 8:4–5).

• The precise boundaries highlight continuity with the old city yet affirm its glorified, secure future.

and from the Tower of Hananel to the royal winepresses

The Tower of Hananel stood at the northeast corner of the walls (Nehemiah 3:1; Jeremiah 31:38), while the royal winepresses lay in the southern sector near the king’s gardens. Together they encompass the full north–south axis.

• Protection: a fortified tower signifies watchfulness; winepresses speak of abundance (Joel 3:18).

• Every segment, from defense to sustenance, prospers under Messiah’s rule (Isaiah 62:8–9).

summary

Zechariah 14:10 promises a literal, dramatic reshaping of the land around Jerusalem at the Lord’s return. The surrounding hills flatten into a plain, granting unhindered access for the nations, while Jerusalem itself is physically and spiritually exalted, firmly established within its historic boundaries. The city becomes the unshakable, flourishing center of Messiah’s kingdom, fulfilling earlier prophetic hopes and showcasing God’s faithfulness to His covenant people.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - All the land shall be turned as a plain. To indicate the exaltation and stability of the centre of the new theocracy, the prophet announces that all the country round Jerusalem shall be turned into a plain, dominated by the metropolis, which stands sublime on a lofty mountain. The Revised Version renders, "shall be turned as the Arabah," i.e. as the Jordan ghor, a valley of abnormal fertility. From Geba to Rimmon south of Jerusalem; i.e. from the north of Judah to its southern boundary. Geba was a town and district on the edge of the great Wady Suweinit, five miles north of Jerusalem. It is identified with Jeba (1 Samuel 13:3), and it formed the northern boundary of the kingdom of Judah (Joshua 18:24). Rimmon is described as "south of Jerusalem," to distinguish it from a town of the same name in Galilee (Joshua 19:13), and from the famous rock Rimmon, to which the Benjamites fled (Judges 20:45, 47). It was situated in the territory of Simeon (Joshua 15:32; Joshua 19:7), and has been identified with Umm-er-Rummamin, a town ten miles north of Beersheba. It shall be lifted up. Jerusalem shall remain exalted on its hill, while all the country around sinks into a plain - a figure representing the spiritual exaltation of the new theocracy. Inhabited in her place; or, shall dwell in her place. Shall occupy her ancient limits, and abide there safely without fear (comp. Jeremiah 31:38-40; Ezekiel 48:15, etc.). From Benjamin's gate, etc. (Jeremiah 37:13). It is difficult to define the given boundaries with certainty in every particular. Benjamin's gate is the same as the gate of Ephraim (2 Kings 14:13; Nehemiah 8:16), so called as leading to the territory of Benjamin, and beyond again to that of Ephraim. It was situated in the north or second wall. From this point the course of the wall is followed, first to the west, and then to the east. The first gate. This was in the eastern part at this wall, and is the same as "the old gate," or "gate of the old town," of Nehemiah 12:39. The corner gate (2 Kings 14:13; Jeremiah 31:38) was at the northwest corner, west of the gate of Benjamin, at the angle where the first and second walls approached each other. These dimensions would give the breadth of the city from east to west. The tower of Hananeel (Nehemiah 3:l) was at the northeast corner of the north wall, where the citadel Basis or Antonia afterwards stood. The king's wine presses were probably near "the king's garden" (Nehemiah 3:15), at the southeast extremity of the city. They may have been cut out of the rock, as was often the case. This description gives the extent of the city from north to south. Thus Zechariah illustrates the growth and stability of the Church of God by the figure of the earthly city Jerusalem, firmly and orderly built, and inhabited by a teeming population, as the following verse shows. There is no ground for expecting the literal fulfilment of this prediction.
