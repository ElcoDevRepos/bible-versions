Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
This is what the LORD of Hosts says:
This phrase emphasizes the authority and sovereignty of God, often used by prophets to introduce divine declarations. The title "LORD of Hosts" underscores God's command over heavenly armies, reflecting His power and majesty. This introduction assures the reader that the message is not of human origin but a divine proclamation, reinforcing the certainty and importance of the prophecy.

Peoples will yet come—
This indicates a future event, suggesting hope and restoration. The term "peoples" refers to various ethnic groups and nations, highlighting the inclusivity of God's plan. This prophecy points to a time when diverse groups will seek the God of Israel, foreshadowing the New Testament's message of the Gospel reaching all nations (Matthew 28:19). It also echoes the Abrahamic covenant, where all nations would be blessed through Abraham's descendants (Genesis 12:3).

the residents of many cities—
This phrase suggests a widespread movement, not limited to a single location. In the historical context, cities were centers of culture and influence, and their residents coming to seek God indicates a significant shift in spiritual focus. This can be seen as a precursor to the spread of Christianity, where urban centers played a crucial role in the dissemination of the Gospel (Acts 19:10). The mention of "many cities" also implies a reversal of the dispersion and division seen at the Tower of Babel (Genesis 11:9), pointing towards unity under God's reign.

What Does Zechariah 8:20 Mean
This is what the LORD of Hosts says

- The passage opens with God’s own declaration, reminding us that what follows carries His full authority and certainty (cf. Isaiah 45:5-7, Jeremiah 1:12).

- “LORD of Hosts” emphasizes His command over angelic armies, assuring the reader that the promise is backed by infinite power (cf. Psalm 46:7, Haggai 2:4-5).

- Because God Himself is speaking, the prophecy is not wishful thinking; it is an unshakeable certainty that will unfold exactly as stated (cf. Numbers 23:19).

Peoples will yet come

- The word “yet” signals a future fulfillment beyond Zechariah’s immediate day, pointing ahead to a time when nations previously indifferent to Israel will be drawn in (cf. Isaiah 60:1-3; Micah 4:1-2).

- God’s plan has always included the gathering of Gentile nations to Himself, a theme fulfilled partially in the church age (Acts 15:14-18) and fully in the Millennial Kingdom (Revelation 15:4).

- Bullet points for clarity:

• God’s faithfulness ensures this future ingathering.

• The prophecy anticipates worldwide recognition of the LORD.

• Mission focus: God’s heart beats for all peoples (Matthew 28:19-20).

the residents of many cities

- This phrase underscores breadth: whole populations, not just isolated individuals, will respond (cf. Zechariah 2:11; Isaiah 19:23-25).

- “Many cities” highlights urban centers where culture and influence concentrate; God intends His glory to permeate every sphere of society (cf. Psalm 72:8-11).

- Practical implications:

• Expect global, civic-scale revival in God’s timing.

• Pray and labor for urban evangelism now, trusting the larger promise.

• Recognize that God’s redemptive reach is bigger than any single locale (Acts 1:8).

summary

Zechariah 8:20 presents a divine pledge: the Almighty Commander guarantees that multitudes from numerous nations and cities will one day stream to Him. The verse anchors hope in God’s unchanging character, forecasts a sweeping international turning to the LORD, and assures believers that the gospel’s ultimate success is certain.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - It shall yet come to pass, that there shall come people; peoples. The sight of the prosperity of the Jews shall induce surrounding nations to join in the worship of Jehovah. The same truth is expressed in Psalm 126:1-3. Perowne thinks that vers. 20, 21 refer to the tribes of Israel; but it seems unnatural to suppose the prophet asserting that it will yet happen that Israelites will seek the Lord, when there is no reason to think that they had not done so in some fashion, or that they would need the previous deliberation mentioned in the next verse. Many cities. So the LXX. and Vulgate. Others translate, "great, or, populous cities;" but this is less suitable.
