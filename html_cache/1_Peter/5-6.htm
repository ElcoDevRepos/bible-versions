Humble yourselves
This phrase calls for a voluntary act of humility. In the biblical context, humility is a recurring theme, especially in the teachings of Jesus (Matthew 23:12). The Greek word used here, "tapeinoō," implies a lowering of oneself in terms of pride and self-importance. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative that emphasizes the importance of humility before God and others (Philippians 2:3-8).

therefore
The word "therefore" connects this instruction to the preceding verses, where Peter discusses the need for humility among church leaders and members. It serves as a conclusion to the exhortations given earlier in the chapter, emphasizing that humility is a response to God's grace and care (1 Peter 5:5).

under God’s mighty hand
This phrase evokes imagery of God's power and sovereignty. The "mighty hand" of God is a motif found throughout the Old Testament, often associated with deliverance and protection (Exodus 13:9, Deuteronomy 5:15). It suggests that believers should submit to God's authority, trusting in His ability to guide and protect them.

so that in due time
The concept of "due time" indicates God's perfect timing. It reflects the biblical principle that God operates according to His own schedule, not human expectations (Ecclesiastes 3:1). This phrase reassures believers that their humility and submission will be recognized and rewarded at the appropriate time, according to God's plan.

He may exalt you
Exaltation here refers to being lifted up or honored by God. This promise of exaltation is contingent upon humility and submission to God's will. It echoes the teachings of Jesus, who stated that those who humble themselves will be exalted (Luke 14:11). This future exaltation can be understood both in terms of spiritual growth in this life and ultimate glorification in the life to come.

What Does 1 Peter 5:6 Mean
Humble yourselves

• The command is deliberate and personal: we choose to bow our wills before the Lord.

• True humility imitates Christ (Philippians 2:5–7) and places others first (Philippians 2:3–4).

• God “mocked” the proud but “gives grace to the humble” (James 4:6; Proverbs 3:34), so lowering ourselves opens the floodgates of His favor.

• Practical outworking includes confessing sin (1 John 1:9), serving quietly (Matthew 6:1–4), and yielding rights (1 Corinthians 9:19).

therefore

• This word ties the verse to the context: “Clothe yourselves with humility toward one another” (1 Peter 5:5). Because humility is the posture God honors, we respond accordingly.

• Peter is summing up the entire letter’s call to suffer faithfully (1 Peter 2:19–21; 4:12–13).

under God’s mighty hand

• “Mighty hand” recalls His powerful deliverance of Israel (Exodus 13:3; Deuteronomy 26:8). We submit beneath the same sovereign strength.

• His hand both protects (John 10:28–29; Isaiah 41:10) and disciplines (Hebrews 12:6). Bowing under it signals trust in His perfect control.

so that

• The phrase reveals purpose. Humility is not aimless self-denial but the God-ordained route to blessing (Luke 14:11).

• God orchestrates every trial “so that the proven character of your faith” shines (1 Peter 1:7; Romans 8:28).

in due time

• The Lord acts according to His timetable (Ecclesiastes 3:11; Habakkuk 2:3). Waiting refines us (James 5:7–8).

• “Due time” encourages perseverance: suffering is temporary, glory is certain (2 Corinthians 4:17; 1 Peter 1:6).

He may exalt you

• Exaltation comes from God alone (Psalm 75:6–7). Any self-promotion is counterfeit (Jeremiah 45:5).

• The pattern is Christ’s: humiliation followed by exaltation (Philippians 2:8–9).

• Exaltation may be present—peace, usefulness, spiritual authority—or future, when Christ rewards the faithful (2 Timothy 4:8; 1 Peter 5:4).

summary

Choosing humility under God’s powerful hand aligns us with His redemptive purposes. While we entrust ourselves to His timing, He promises final and faithful exaltation, turning present submission into everlasting honor.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Humble yourselves therefore.--This, too, looks an amplification of a proverb, when we compare it with James 4:10. The humility here recommended is not merely a submissive bearing of the strokes which it pleased God to let fall upon them, but it was to be shown, as we see in the former verse, in their bearing toward one another. And "the mighty hand of God" is not to be regarded as that which is chastising them, but as the protecting shelter which they are humbly to seek.

In due time.--St. Peter probably means, in the day of judgment, which seemed so instant.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Humble yourselves therefore under the mighty hand of God, that he may exalt you in due time. The Alexandrine Manuscript and some ancient versions add ἐπισκοπῆς, "in the time of visitation," probably from Luke 19:44. For "the mighty hand of God," comp. Deuteronomy 3:24; Luke 1:51. St. Peter was doubtless thinking of the well-remembered words of the Lord, "He that humbleth himself shall be exalted."
