Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For I have chosen him
This phrase indicates God's sovereign election of Abraham. The concept of divine choice is central to the narrative of the Old Testament, where God selects individuals and nations for specific purposes. Abraham's selection is foundational for the establishment of Israel as God's chosen people. This choice is not based on Abraham's merit but on God's grace and purpose, as seen in Deuteronomy 7:7-8, where God explains His choice of Israel.

so that he will command his children and his household after him
Abraham's role as a patriarch involves instructing his family in the ways of God. This reflects the cultural context of the ancient Near East, where the family unit was central, and the patriarch held significant authority and responsibility. The emphasis on teaching future generations is echoed in Deuteronomy 6:6-7, where parents are instructed to diligently teach God's commandments to their children.

to keep the way of the LORD
The "way of the LORD" refers to living according to God's commandments and statutes. This phrase underscores the covenant relationship between God and His people, where obedience is a response to God's grace. The concept of "the way" is further developed in the New Testament, where Jesus refers to Himself as "the way" (John 14:6), indicating that following Him is the fulfillment of living in God's way.

by doing what is right and just
Righteousness and justice are central themes in the Bible, reflecting God's character. Abraham is called to model these attributes, which are essential for maintaining a covenant relationship with God. The prophets frequently call Israel back to righteousness and justice, as seen in Micah 6:8. These qualities are also embodied in Jesus Christ, who perfectly fulfills God's standards of righteousness and justice.

in order that the LORD may bring upon Abraham what He has promised.
This phrase highlights the conditional aspect of God's promises, which often require human cooperation through obedience. God's promises to Abraham include making him a great nation and blessing all nations through him (Genesis 12:2-3). The ultimate fulfillment of these promises is found in Jesus Christ, a descendant of Abraham, through whom all nations are blessed (Galatians 3:16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Abraham
The central figure in this passage, chosen by God to be the father of many nations. His role is pivotal in the unfolding of God's covenant and promises.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God who has chosen Abraham to lead his family in righteousness and justice.

3. Abraham's Household
This includes his immediate family and servants, who are to be instructed in the ways of the LORD.

4. The Promised Land
The land that God has promised to Abraham and his descendants, a key element of God's covenant with him.

5. The Covenant
The divine promise made by God to Abraham, which includes blessings, land, and numerous descendants.
Teaching Points
Chosen for a Purpose
God chooses individuals for specific purposes. Abraham was chosen to lead his family in righteousness, which is a calling for all believers to influence their families and communities positively.

The Importance of Family Leadership
Abraham's role as a leader in his household underscores the importance of spiritual leadership within the family. Parents and guardians are called to guide their families in the ways of the LORD.

Righteousness and Justice
These are central themes in God's expectations for Abraham. Believers are called to live out these principles in their daily lives, reflecting God's character to the world.

Faithfulness to God's Promises
God's promises to Abraham are contingent upon his obedience and leadership. This teaches us that while God's promises are sure, our participation through obedience is crucial.

Generational Impact
Abraham's instruction to his household has a lasting impact, highlighting the importance of passing down faith and values to future generations.

What Does Genesis 18:19 Mean
For I have chosen him

• God’s initiative is front-and-center. Abraham did not stumble into blessing; the Lord deliberately selected him. “You did not choose Me, but I chose you” (John 15:16).

• The choice is rooted in grace, not Abraham’s résumé (Genesis 12:1-3; Deuteronomy 7:6-8).

• Election carries responsibility. Being chosen is never an end in itself but a launchpad for God’s wider plan (Romans 4:1-3).

so that he will command his children and his household after him

• God’s purpose is generational. Faith is to flow from Abraham to his “children and household.”

• Spiritual leadership begins at home:

– Impress God’s words on your children (Deuteronomy 6:6-7).

– “As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15).

– Fathers are to bring children up “in the discipline and admonition of the Lord” (Ephesians 6:4).

• Abraham’s obedience sets the pattern for every parent and household head who desires covenant blessing to continue.

to keep the way of the LORD

• “The way of the LORD” is a lifestyle aligned with His character (Psalm 25:4; Proverbs 10:29).

• Jesus later identifies Himself as that Way (John 14:6), showing the continuity between Abraham’s faith walk and New-Testament discipleship.

• Obedience is not optional add-on; it is the road God lays out for His people.

by doing what is right and just

• The “way” is defined by practical ethics—righteousness and justice.

• God’s throne is founded on these same qualities (Psalm 89:14).

• True religion always weds devotion and equity (Micah 6:8; Matthew 23:23).

• Faith that does not act is dead (James 2:17); Abraham’s descendants are to mirror God’s heart in daily dealings.

in order that the LORD may bring upon Abraham what He has promised.

• Promises abound: a great nation, worldwide blessing, and ultimately Messiah (Genesis 12:2-3; 22:16-18).

• Obedience is the appointed channel through which the covenant flows, yet the certainty rests on God’s unchangeable oath (Hebrews 6:13-15).

• God links promise and practice so that His people experience the fullness of what He swore to give.

summary

Genesis 18:19 reveals a beautiful chain: God elects Abraham, equips him to instruct his household, expects them to walk in His ways through righteous and just living, and thereby unfolds every promise He made. Election, instruction, obedience, and fulfillment interlock, showing how divine grace and human responsibility harmonize to advance God’s redemptive plan from one generation to the next.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(19) For I know him, that he will.--This translation has most of the Versions in its favour, and means that Abraham's good conduct earns for him the Divine condescension. But the Hebrew is, For I have known him in order that he may command his sons, &c. It gives God foreknowledge of the purpose for which He had called Abraham as the reason for thus revealing to him the method of the Divine justice. And this purpose was, that from Abraham should spring a nation whose institutions were to be fraught with Divine truth, whose prophets were to be the means of revealing God's will to man, and of whom, as concerning the flesh, the Messiah should come. What more fitting than that one appointed to fill so noble a calling should also be raised to the rank of a prophet, and be permitted to share in the Divine counsels? This rendering closely agrees with what is said in Genesis 18:18 about Abraham growing into a mighty nation; and it was the unique and high purpose for which this nation was to be called into being which brought Abraham into so close a relation to Jehovah.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - For I know him, that - literally, for I have known (or chosen, יָדַע being - dilexi, as in Amos 3:2) him to the end that (לְמַעַן conveying the idea of purpose; vide Ewald, § 357), the language expressing the idea that Abraham had been the object of Divine foreknowledge and election (Gesenius, Rosenmüller, Delitzsch, Keil, Oehler, Kalisch, Lange), although the reading of the text is substantially adopted by many (LXX., Vulgate, Targums, Luther, Calvin, Dathe, et alii). The latter interpretation assigns as the reason of the Divine communication the knowledge which Jehovah then possessed of Abraham's piety; the former grounds the Divine resolution on the prior fact that Divine grace had elected him to the high destiny described in the language following. It is generally agreed that this clause connects with Ver. 17; Bush regards it as exhibiting the means by which the future promised to Abraham in Ver. 18 should be realized - he will (rather, may) command his children and his household after him (by parental authority as well as by personal example), and they shall keep (rather, that they may keep) the way of the Lord, - i.e. the religion of Jehovah (cf. Judges 2:22; 2 Kings 21:22; Psalm 119:1; Acts 18:25), of which the practical outcome is - to do justice and judgment; - or righteousness and judgment, that which accords with right or the sense of oughtness in intelligent and moral beings, and that which harmonizes with the Divine law (cf. Ezekiel 18:5) - that (literally, to the end that, in order that, לְמַעַן, ut supra) the Lord may bring upon Abraham that which he hath spoken of him.
