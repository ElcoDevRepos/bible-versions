When Pharaoh summons you and asks,
This phrase sets the scene for a significant encounter between Joseph's family and Pharaoh, the ruler of Egypt. The summoning by Pharaoh indicates the importance of the meeting, as Pharaoh was the most powerful figure in Egypt. This reflects the high status Joseph had achieved, allowing his family to have an audience with the king. Historically, Egypt was a powerful and advanced civilization, and Pharaohs were considered divine figures. This context highlights the gravity of the situation for Jacob's family, who were shepherds from Canaan.

‘What is your occupation?’
The question about occupation is crucial in the context of ancient Egypt. Shepherds were often looked down upon by Egyptians, as seen in Genesis 46:34, where Joseph advises his brothers to say they are keepers of livestock rather than shepherds. This distinction was important because Egyptians considered shepherds to be an abomination (Genesis 46:34). The occupation of Joseph's family as shepherds also connects to their identity as Hebrews, a nomadic people with a pastoral lifestyle. This question foreshadows the separation of the Israelites in the land of Goshen, which would allow them to maintain their distinct cultural and religious identity. The theme of occupation also ties into the broader biblical narrative of God using humble beginnings and professions to fulfill His purposes, as seen in the lives of other biblical figures like David, who was a shepherd before becoming king.

What Does Genesis 46:33 Mean
When

• The word “When” signals the certainty of the moment God has arranged. Just as the LORD promised Joseph in Genesis 45:9-11 that his family would be preserved in Egypt, so the timing here is no accident.

• Scripture repeatedly shows that God sets appointments for His people—Genesis 22:1 (“after these things”), Esther 4:14 (“for such a time as this”). We can trust that divine timing stands behind the events that bring Jacob’s family before Pharaoh.

• Acts 17:26 reminds us that God “determined their appointed times and the boundaries of their lands,” reassuring us that every step into Egypt unfolds under His sovereign calendar.

Pharaoh summons you

• “Pharaoh” represents the highest earthly authority in Egypt, yet even he is subject to God’s larger plan (Proverbs 21:1).

• Joseph’s instruction anticipates royal protocol: a summons, not an informal invitation. Genesis 41:14 records how Joseph himself was “sent for” out of prison—again showing God uses summonses to advance His purpose.

• Exodus 3:10 later echoes this dynamic when God sends Moses back to Pharaoh, proving that God often arranges encounters with rulers to fulfill covenant promises (Genesis 15:13-14).

and asks

• Joseph prepares his brothers to give an answer, emphasizing integrity and clarity (Genesis 46:34). In 1 Peter 3:15 believers are similarly told to be “ready to give a defense.”

• This moment also protects the family: Joseph anticipates the inquiry so that their response aligns with God’s provision for them in Goshen (Genesis 47:6).

• Proverbs 16:1 says, “The preparations of the heart belong to man, but the reply of the tongue is from the LORD.” Their words will ultimately be guided by God to secure their place.

‘What is your occupation?’

• Occupation determines status in Egyptian culture; shepherds are “detestable to Egyptians” (Genesis 46:34). By openly identifying as shepherds, the brothers ensure separation from pagan society, preserving their distinct identity (Leviticus 20:24-26).

• The question also ties back to their ancestry—Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob were shepherds (Genesis 13:2-5; 26:12-14). Their honest answer affirms covenant continuity even in a foreign land.

• Genesis 47:3-4 shows the brothers obeying Joseph’s instruction word-for-word, and Pharaoh then grants them Goshen. God uses their humble vocation to fulfill His promise of provision, echoing Psalm 23:1, “The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want.”

summary

Genesis 46:33 highlights Joseph’s careful preparation of his family for a divinely appointed audience with Pharaoh. Each phrase underscores God’s sovereign timing, the authority structures He ordains, the importance of truthful speech, and the role of humble obedience in securing His blessings. The verse teaches that when God orchestrates a meeting—even with the most powerful earthly ruler—He equips His people to speak faithfully, preserving both their identity and His redemptive plan.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 33, 34. - And it shall come to pass, when Pharaoh shall call you, and shall say, What is your occupation? Pharaoh's inquiry was characteristically Egyptian, being rendered necessary by the strict distinction of castes that then prevailed. According to a law promulgated by Amasis, a monarch of the 26th dynasty, every Egyptian was obliged to give a yearly account to the monarch or State governor of how he lived, with the certification that if he failed to show that he possessed an honorable calling (δικαίην ζόην) he should be put to death (Herod., 2:177). That ye shall say, Thy servants' trade hath been about cattle (literally, men of cattle arc thy servants) from our youth even until now, both we, and also our fathers: that ye may dwell in the land of Goshen. Joseph probably desired his brethren to settle in Goshen for three reasons.

(1) It was suitable for their flocks and herds;

(2) it would secure their isolation from the Egyptians; and

(3) it was contiguous to Canaan, and would be easier vacated when the time arrived for their return. For every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians. These are obviously the words not of Joseph, but of the historian, and their accuracy is strikingly corroborated by Herodotus (2. 47, 164), who affirms that the swine-herds, one of the seven castes, classes, or guilds into which the Egyptians were divided, were regarded with such abhorrence that they were not allowed to enter a temple or contract marriage with any others of their countrymen; and by existing monuments, which show that though the statement of Josephus ('Ant.,' 2:07, 5) is incorrect that "the Egyptians were prohibited from meddling with the keeping of sheep,' yet those, who tended cattle were greatly despised, Egyptian artists evincing the contempt in which they were held by frequently representing them as either lame or deformed, dirty and unshaven, and sometimes of a most ludicrous appearance (vide Wilkinson, 'Ancient Egyptians,' vol. 2. p. 444, ed. 1878).
