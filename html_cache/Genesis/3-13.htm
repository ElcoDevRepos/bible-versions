Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the LORD God said to the woman,
This phrase introduces the direct involvement of God in the aftermath of the Fall. The use of "LORD God" emphasizes the covenantal relationship between God and humanity. In the context of Genesis, God is portrayed as both Creator and Judge. The direct address to the woman highlights her role in the narrative and God's personal engagement with each individual involved in the transgression.

“What is this you have done?”
This question is not for God's information but serves as a means for the woman to acknowledge her actions. It echoes God's earlier question to Adam, emphasizing personal responsibility. The question is reminiscent of God's inquiry to Cain in Genesis 4:10, "What have you done?" It underscores the seriousness of sin and the need for confession and accountability.

“The serpent deceived me,” she replied,
The woman's response points to the serpent's role in the deception. This reflects the theme of temptation and the cunning nature of the serpent, which is later identified as Satan in Revelation 12:9. The act of deception is central to the narrative, illustrating the introduction of sin into the world through lies and manipulation. This also connects to 2 Corinthians 11:3, where Paul warns of being led astray as Eve was.

“and I ate.”
The admission "and I ate" is a straightforward acknowledgment of her disobedience. Eating the forbidden fruit represents the act of sin, which brought about spiritual death and separation from God. This act is pivotal in the biblical narrative, as it sets the stage for the need for redemption and the eventual coming of Jesus Christ, the "second Adam" (1 Corinthians 15:45), who would restore what was lost through His obedience and sacrifice.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD God
The Creator, who is omniscient and omnipotent, questioning Eve to bring her to a realization of her actions.

2. The Woman (Eve)
The first woman, created by God, who was deceived by the serpent and ate the forbidden fruit.

3. The Serpent
A creature used by Satan to deceive Eve, representing cunning and deceit.

4. The Garden of Eden
The perfect place created by God for Adam and Eve, where the fall of man occurred.

5. The Deception and Fall
The event where Eve was deceived by the serpent, leading to the disobedience of God's command and the subsequent fall of humanity.
Teaching Points
The Nature of Deception
Deception often begins with questioning God's word and intentions. We must be vigilant and grounded in Scripture to recognize and resist deceit.

Personal Responsibility
While Eve was deceived, she still bore responsibility for her actions. We must acknowledge our own choices and their consequences, rather than shifting blame.

The Importance of Obedience
Disobedience to God's commands leads to separation from Him. Obedience is crucial for maintaining a right relationship with God.

The Role of Confession
God's question to Eve was an opportunity for confession. We should be quick to confess our sins and seek God's forgiveness.

The Consequences of Sin
Sin has far-reaching consequences, affecting not only the individual but also the broader community and creation. Understanding this can motivate us to pursue righteousness.

What Does Genesis 3:13 Mean
Then the LORD God said to the woman

• God Himself confronts the woman, underscoring that every person answers directly to Him (Genesis 3:9; Romans 14:12).

• His personal address shows that He takes sin seriously and does not overlook it (Psalm 50:21; Job 38:1–3).

• By speaking to the woman after addressing Adam, the Lord confirms shared responsibility within the human family (1 Peter 3:7).

“What is this you have done?”

• The question is not for God’s information but for the woman’s self-examination (Joshua 7:19; 2 Samuel 12:9).

• It highlights the gravity of a single act of disobedience in a perfect environment (James 2:10).

• The inquiry invites confession, paving the way for God’s righteous judgment mingled with grace (Proverbs 28:13; 1 John 1:9).

“The serpent deceived me,”

• The woman points to an external tempter, acknowledging real spiritual deception (2 Corinthians 11:3; Revelation 12:9).

• While identifying the serpent’s role, she exposes the age-old strategy of Satan: lies that twist God’s word (John 8:44).

• Her words reveal the danger of dialoguing with temptation rather than resisting it (Ephesians 6:11; James 4:7).

“and I ate.”

• Despite shifting blame, she confesses the act itself—personal choice remains at the core (James 1:14–15).

• The simple phrase links temptation to concrete rebellion, the entry point of sin into human history (Romans 5:12).

• By stating the fact without excuse, she unknowingly foreshadows the need for a Redeemer who will one day undo the damage (Genesis 3:15; Galatians 4:4–5).

summary

Genesis 3:13 shows a holy God confronting human sin, drawing honest confession, and exposing both the deceiver’s craft and the sinner’s choice. The woman’s admission, “The serpent deceived me, and I ate,” unites external temptation with personal responsibility, highlighting humanity’s need for divine rescue—fulfilled in Christ, the promised Seed who crushes the serpent’s head.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - And the Lord said unto the woman - without noticing the excuses, but simply accepting the admission, and passing on, "following up the transgression, even to the root - not the psychological merely, but the historical (Lange): What is this that thou hast done? Or, "Why hast thou done this?" (LXX., Vulgate, Luther, De Wette). "But the Hebrew phrase has more vehemence; it is the language of one who wonders as at something prodigious, and ought rather to be rendered, ' How hast thou done this?'" (Calvin). And the woman said (following the example of her guilty, husband, omitting any notice of her sin in tempting Adam, and transferring the blame of her own disobedience to the reptile), The serpent beguiled me. Literally, caused me to forget, hence beguiled, from נָשָׁה, to forget a thing (Lamentations 3:17), or person (Jeremiah 23:39; Stanley Loathes, 'Gram.,' App. 197); or, caused me to go astray, from נָשָׁא (unused in Kal), kindred to כָשָׁה, perhaps to err, to go astray (Gesenius, Furst); ἠπατήσε (LXX.), ἐξαπάτησεν (2 Corinthians 11:3). And I did eat. "A forced confession, but no appearance of contrition. 'It's true I did eat, but it was not my fault'" (Hughes).
