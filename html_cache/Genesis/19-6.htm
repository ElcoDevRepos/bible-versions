Lot went outside to meet them
Lot's action of going outside to meet the men at his door reflects the ancient Near Eastern custom of hospitality, which was highly valued and often involved protecting guests at all costs. This act of stepping outside indicates Lot's willingness to confront the mob and protect his visitors, who were actually angels. The cultural expectation of hospitality is seen throughout Scripture, such as in Genesis 18 with Abraham's hospitality to the same visitors. Lot's actions can be compared to the protective nature of Christ, who intercedes on behalf of humanity.

shutting the door behind him
By shutting the door behind him, Lot creates a barrier between the men of Sodom and his guests, symbolizing a separation between righteousness and wickedness. This act of closing the door can be seen as a protective measure, emphasizing the urgency and danger of the situation. It also foreshadows the divine judgment that will soon befall Sodom, as the door represents a boundary that the wicked cannot cross. This imagery of separation is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus speaks of the narrow door in Luke 13:24, highlighting the distinction between those who are saved and those who are not.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Lot
Nephew of Abraham, Lot is living in Sodom, a city known for its wickedness. He is a central figure in this chapter, demonstrating hospitality and protection for his guests.

2. Sodom
A city infamous for its sinfulness and eventual destruction by God. It serves as a backdrop for the events in Genesis 19.

3. The Men of Sodom
The inhabitants of the city who surround Lot's house, demanding to have relations with his guests, illustrating the city's moral depravity.

4. The Angels
Visitors in the form of men who come to Sodom to assess its wickedness and ultimately deliver Lot and his family from the impending destruction.

5. The Door
Symbolic of protection and separation, the door represents Lot's attempt to shield his guests from the wickedness outside.
Teaching Points
Hospitality and Protection
Lot's actions demonstrate the importance of hospitality and the lengths one should go to protect those under their care, even in the face of danger.

Moral Courage
Lot's decision to confront the men of Sodom shows the necessity of standing up for righteousness, even when surrounded by wickedness.

Separation from Sin
The act of shutting the door symbolizes the need to separate oneself from sinful influences and protect one's household from moral corruption.

Divine Intervention
The presence of the angels highlights God's active role in delivering the righteous from judgment, reminding believers of His sovereignty and care.

Judgment and Mercy
The events in Sodom serve as a reminder of God's judgment on sin and His mercy towards those who seek righteousness.

What Does Genesis 19:6 Mean
Lot went outside

• Lot literally steps out of the safety of his house (Genesis 19:6), placing himself between his guests and the violent crowd.

• This choice echoes Abraham’s earlier action of going out to meet the three visitors (Genesis 18:2), showing a continuity of righteous hospitality.

• His movement mirrors Christ’s later example of leaving heaven’s glory to rescue sinners (Philippians 2:5-8).

• Stepping over the threshold also recalls Moses going out to meet Pharaoh (Exodus 7:15)—a confrontation for the sake of others.

to meet them

• “Them” refers to the men of Sodom demanding to harm Lot’s guests (Genesis 19:4-5).

• Lot initiates face-to-face engagement rather than hurling words from behind a barrier, reflecting Proverbs 15:1, “A gentle answer turns away wrath.”

• Hebrews 13:2 reminds believers that extending hospitality may entertain angels unawares—exactly Lot’s circumstance with the two heavenly messengers inside.

• His meeting the mob shows courage akin to David meeting Goliath (1 Samuel 17:48), confronting evil head-on.

shutting the door

• Lot “shut the door” (Genesis 19:6), creating a clear line between righteousness and wickedness.

• Like the Lord shutting Noah into the ark (Genesis 7:16), this act seals the protected inside from judgment outside.

• Jesus employs the same imagery: “When once the Master of the house has risen and shut the door…” (Luke 13:25). A closed door indicates finality and urgency.

• It also prefigures Revelation 3:7 where Christ alone “opens and no one will shut, and shuts and no one opens.”

behind him

• Lot places the barrier “behind him,” meaning he stands outside while his guests remain secure.

• He becomes a living shield, anticipating the intercessory role of the coming Messiah who would “lay down His life for the sheep” (John 10:11).

• Ezekiel 22:30 laments for a man to “stand in the gap before Me for the land”—Lot literally fills that gap.

• The detail underscores personal responsibility: believers are called to risk comfort for others’ safety (Philippians 2:4).

summary

Genesis 19:6 captures a deliberate, courageous sequence: Lot leaves safety, confronts evil, firmly seals off the innocent, and positions himself as their protector. Each movement illustrates sacrificial hospitality, immediate obedience, and clear moral boundaries—principles still vital for believers who trust the literal truth of God’s Word.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 6-8. - And Lot went out at the door unto them, - literally, at the doorway, or opening (pethach, from pathach, to open; cf. pateo, Latin; πρόθυρον, LXX.); in which the gate or hanging door (deleth, from dalai, to be pendulous) swings, and which it closes (vide Gesenius, p. 201) - and shut the door (deleth, ut supra; θύρα, LXX.) after him, - to protect his visitors, which he also sought to accomplish by personal exhortation - and said, I pray you, brethren, do not so wickedly - and also by an infamous proposal which nothing can extenuate and the utmost charity finds difficult to reconcile any pretence of piety on the part cf. Behold now, I have two daughters which have not known man; - i.e. unmarried (cf. Genesis 4:1), though, according to some, already betrothed to two Sodomites (Ver. 14) - let me, I pray you, bring them out unto you, and do ye to them as is good in your eyes. The usual apologies - that in sacrificing his daughters to the Sodomites instead of giving up his guests to their unnatural lust. Lot

(1) selected the lesser of two sins (Ambrose);

(2) thereby protected his guests and discharged the duties of hospitality incumbent on him (Chrysostom);

(3) believed his daughters would not be desired by the Sodomites, either because of their well-known betrothal (Rosenmüller), or because of the unnatural lust of the Sodomites (Lunge); . . .
