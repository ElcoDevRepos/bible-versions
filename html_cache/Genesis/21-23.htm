Now, therefore, swear to me here before God
This phrase highlights the solemnity and seriousness of the oath being requested. In ancient Near Eastern culture, swearing an oath before God was a binding and sacred act, indicating the importance of truthfulness and integrity. The invocation of God in the oath underscores the belief in divine witness and accountability. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God as a witness to covenants, as seen in other scriptures like Genesis 31:50, where Laban and Jacob make a similar agreement.

that you will not deal falsely with me
The request for honesty and integrity is central to maintaining peaceful relations. In the context of Genesis, Abraham had previously deceived Abimelech regarding Sarah (Genesis 20), which may have prompted this request for assurance. The emphasis on not dealing falsely is a recurring biblical principle, as seen in Proverbs 12:22, which states that lying lips are an abomination to the Lord.

or my children or descendants
This phrase extends the request for honesty beyond the immediate parties to future generations, highlighting the importance of long-term peace and trust. In the ancient world, familial and tribal relationships were crucial, and agreements often included provisions for descendants. This reflects the biblical theme of generational blessings and curses, as seen in Exodus 20:5-6.

Show to me and to the country in which you reside
Abimelech's request for kindness extends to the land, indicating the interconnectedness of people and their environment. This reflects the biblical understanding of stewardship and the responsibility to care for the land, as seen in Genesis 2:15. The mention of the country also underscores the importance of hospitality and fair treatment of sojourners, a theme reiterated in Leviticus 19:34.

the same kindness that I have shown to you.
Abimelech appeals to the principle of reciprocity, a common cultural norm in the ancient Near East. This reflects the biblical ethic of treating others as one wishes to be treated, as articulated in Matthew 7:12. The kindness shown by Abimelech likely refers to his previous interactions with Abraham, including the resolution of the conflict over Sarah and the granting of land rights. This principle of reciprocal kindness is foundational to covenant relationships throughout scripture.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Integrity
Abraham is called to live with integrity, reflecting God's character. As Christians, we are to be truthful and honest in all our dealings.

The Role of Covenants
Covenants are serious commitments that require faithfulness. Our relationship with God is based on His covenant promises, and we are called to uphold our commitments to others.

Kindness as a Witness
Abimelech's request for kindness reflects the impact of Abraham's witness. Our actions should demonstrate God's love and kindness to those around us.

The Power of Reconciliation
Despite past deception, Abraham and Abimelech seek reconciliation. We are called to pursue peace and reconciliation in our relationships.

Generational Impact
The covenant extends to future generations, reminding us that our actions and commitments affect those who come after us.

What Does Genesis 21:23 Mean
Now, therefore

• Abimelech is responding to the events of Genesis 20, where Abraham’s half-truth about Sarah endangered Abimelech’s household.

• “Therefore” signals a logical response: having witnessed God’s protection of Abraham (Genesis 20:17–18) and the miraculous birth of Isaac (Genesis 21:1–7), Abimelech seeks a formal agreement.

• Cross references: Genesis 26:26–29 shows a later Abimelech taking the same cautious step with Isaac; Proverbs 16:7 notes that when a man’s ways please the LORD, even his enemies make peace with him.

Swear to me here before God

• The king insists on an oath “before God,” acknowledging the LORD’s supremacy (cf. Genesis 14:22–23; Hebrews 6:16).

• An oath invokes divine witness, making deception a direct offense against God (Exodus 20:7).

• This anticipates Israel’s later covenant ceremonies, e.g., Joshua 24:22, where swearing before God seals commitment.

That you will not deal falsely with me or my children or descendants

• Abimelech desires protection not just for himself but for future generations, illustrating how a single act of faithfulness (or deceit) can echo through families (Exodus 20:5-6).

• Abraham had earlier misled Abimelech (Genesis 20:2). The king wants assurance of transparent dealings going forward (Proverbs 12:22).

• Compare David’s oath to spare Saul’s offspring in 1 Samuel 24:21-22, underscoring the biblical value of generational integrity.

Show to me and to the country in which you reside the same kindness that I have shown to you

• “Kindness” (ḥesed) speaks of covenant loyalty—practical, steadfast love (Ruth 3:10; Micah 6:8).

• Abimelech reminds Abraham of his earlier goodwill: returning Sarah unharmed, granting gifts, and offering land (Genesis 20:14-15).

• The request mirrors Jesus’ principle of reciprocal mercy (Luke 6:31) and Paul’s call to believers to “do good to everyone” (Galatians 6:10).

• Abraham agrees, later formalizing the pact by giving sheep and oxen (Genesis 21:27), modeling tangible grace.

summary

Genesis 21:23 records a pagan king recognizing God’s hand on Abraham and seeking a binding oath of honesty and covenant love. Abimelech’s appeal for enduring kindness highlights the reach of godly testimony, the seriousness of oaths before God, and the call to faithful dealings that bless not only present relationships but generations to come.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Nor with my son, nor with my son's son.--The words are not those commonly used for son and grandson, but a Hebrew phrase signifying my kith and kin. They might be translated, "nor with mine offshoot nor mine offspring." The words occur again in the same proverbial way in Job 18:19; Isaiah 14:22.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - Now therefore swear unto me here by God - the verb to swear is derived from the Hebrew numeral seven, inasmuch as the septennary number was sacred, and oaths were confirmed either by seven sacrifices (Genesis 21:28) or by seven witnesses and pledges - that thou wilt not deal falsely with me, - literally, if thou shalt lie unto me; a common form of oath in Hebrew, in which the other member of the sentence is for emphasis left unexpressed (cf. Ruth 1:17, and vide Genesis 14:23). As a prince, Abimelech was afraid of Abraham's growing power; as a good man, he insures the safety of himself and his dominions not by resorting to war, but by forming an amicable treaty with his neighbor - nor with my son, nor with my son's son: - σπέρμα καὶ ὅνομα (LXX.); posteri et stirps (Vulgate); offspring and progeny (Kalisch); kith and kin (Murphy) - but according to the kindness that I have done unto thee (vide Genesis 20:15), thou shalt do unto me, and to the land wherein thou hast sojourned - the land being put for the people (cf. Numbers 14:13).
