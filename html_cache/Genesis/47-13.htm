Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
There was no food, however, in all that region
This phrase highlights the severity of the famine affecting the entire region. Famines were not uncommon in the ancient Near East due to reliance on seasonal rains and the Nile's inundation. This particular famine was prophesied by Joseph (Genesis 41:30) and serves as a backdrop for God's providential care for His people. The lack of food emphasizes the dire circumstances that led to the centralization of power in Egypt under Joseph's administration.

because the famine was so severe
The severity of the famine underscores the fulfillment of Joseph's interpretation of Pharaoh's dreams (Genesis 41:25-32). It was a divine orchestration to bring Jacob's family to Egypt, setting the stage for the Israelites' eventual growth into a great nation. The famine's intensity also serves as a reminder of human vulnerability and the need for reliance on God's provision.

the lands of Egypt and Canaan had been exhausted by the famine
This phrase indicates the widespread impact of the famine, affecting both Egypt and Canaan. Egypt, typically a breadbasket due to the Nile, was also suffering, which highlights the extraordinary nature of this famine. Canaan's inclusion shows the interconnectedness of the region and sets the stage for the Israelites' migration to Egypt. This movement is significant in biblical history, as it fulfills God's promise to Abraham about his descendants' sojourn in a foreign land (Genesis 15:13). The exhaustion of the lands also prefigures the spiritual barrenness that can occur without God's intervention, pointing to the need for a savior, a role ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joseph
As the governor of Egypt, Joseph was responsible for managing the resources during the famine. His leadership and wisdom were crucial in navigating this crisis.

2. Egypt
The land where Joseph served as governor. Egypt was a powerful nation, but even it was not immune to the severe famine.

3. Canaan
The land where Jacob and his family resided before moving to Egypt. It was also affected by the famine, prompting Jacob's sons to seek help in Egypt.

4. Famine
A severe shortage of food that affected both Egypt and Canaan, leading to widespread hardship and necessitating Joseph's strategic management of resources.

5. Jacob's Family
The family of Israel, who were directly impacted by the famine and eventually moved to Egypt for survival.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Crisis
The famine was severe, yet God had already positioned Joseph to provide for His people. This reminds us that God is in control, even in dire circumstances.

Wise Stewardship
Joseph's management of resources during the famine teaches us the importance of wise stewardship and planning, especially in times of abundance.

Trust in God's Provision
Despite the severity of the famine, God provided for Jacob's family. We can trust that God will meet our needs, even when resources seem scarce.

Interconnectedness of God's Plan
The famine affected both Egypt and Canaan, showing how God's plan often involves multiple people and nations. Our lives are interconnected, and our actions can have far-reaching impacts.

Faithfulness in Adversity
Joseph remained faithful to God throughout his trials, and God used him mightily. We are encouraged to remain steadfast in our faith, trusting that God can use our circumstances for His glory.

What Does Genesis 47:13 Mean
There was no food, however, in all that region

- The verse opens with an unqualified statement of lack. Joseph’s earlier warnings, based on Pharaoh’s dreams (Genesis 41:25–32), are now fulfilled in full view.

- Scripture repeatedly shows that physical scarcity can serve God’s larger redemptive purposes (Deuteronomy 8:3; 1 Kings 17:1–7).

- The absence of food underlines humanity’s dependence on the Lord rather than earthly abundance. As Psalm 104:27 affirms, “These all look to You to give them their food in due season.”

because the famine was so severe

- Genesis 41:56 already described the famine as “severe in all the lands,” and by Genesis 47:13 its intensity peaks.

- Severity emphasizes that no human plan, apart from God’s direction through Joseph, could sustain life (Acts 7:11–12 echoes this).

- The verse calls to mind later crises, such as the drought in Elijah’s day (James 5:17), where God again displayed His sovereignty in the midst of extreme need.

the lands of Egypt and Canaan had been exhausted by the famine

- Both Egypt, the political superpower, and Canaan, the promised homeland, stand emptied—showing the universal reach of judgment and testing (Romans 8:20–22 acknowledges creation’s groaning).

- God positions Joseph to save not only Egypt but also Israel’s family, preserving the covenant line (Genesis 45:7; 50:20).

- This exhaustion paves the way for Jacob’s household to settle in Goshen, fulfilling God’s word to Abraham about sojourning in a foreign land (Genesis 15:13–14).

- The scene anticipates the ultimate Deliverer who will rescue from a far greater deprivation—spiritual death (John 6:35).

summary

Genesis 47:13 records the famine’s climax: total scarcity, escalating severity, and complete exhaustion of Egypt and Canaan. The verse highlights human dependence, divine sovereignty, and God’s unfolding plan to preserve His people through Joseph, foreshadowing the greater salvation to come in Christ.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - And there was no bread in all the land; for the famine was very sore (literally, heavy), so that the land of Egypt and all the land of Canaan fainted (literally, was exhausted, had become languid and spiritless) by reason of the famine. The introduction of the present section, which first depicts the miseries of a starving population, and then circumstantially describes a great political revolution forced upon them by the stern necessity of hunger, may have been due to a desire

(1) to exhibit the extreme urgency which existed for Joseph's care of his father and brethren (Bush),

(2) to show the greatness of the benefit conferred on Joseph's house (Baumgarten, Keil, Lange), and perhaps also

(3) to foreshadow the political constitution afterwards bestowed upon the Israelites (Gerlach).
