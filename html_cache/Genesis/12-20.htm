Then Pharaoh gave his men orders concerning Abram
Pharaoh, the ruler of Egypt, represents the highest authority in the land. His involvement with Abram highlights the significance of Abram's presence in Egypt. This event occurs after Abram's deception about Sarai being his sister, which led to Pharaoh taking her into his palace. The divine intervention through plagues (Genesis 12:17) prompts Pharaoh to act. This phrase underscores the theme of God's protection over Abram despite his shortcomings. It also foreshadows future interactions between the descendants of Abram and Egypt, notably the Exodus.

and they sent him away with his wife
Abram's departure with Sarai intact signifies God's faithfulness in preserving the covenant lineage. Despite Abram's failure to trust fully in God's promise, God ensures Sarai's return to her rightful place. This act of sending away can be seen as a form of divine deliverance, prefiguring later redemptive acts in Scripture. The restoration of Sarai to Abram also maintains the integrity of the promise that through Abram's seed all nations would be blessed (Genesis 12:3).

and all his possessions
Abram leaves Egypt not only with his wife but also with increased wealth, as indicated earlier in Genesis 12:16. This reflects a recurring biblical theme where God's people often leave periods of trial with greater blessings, as seen in the Israelites' exodus from Egypt (Exodus 12:35-36). The possessions symbolize God's provision and blessing, despite human error. This also sets a precedent for the wealth and prosperity that would characterize the patriarchs, pointing to God's faithfulness in fulfilling His promises.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Pharaoh
The ruler of Egypt who interacts with Abram. He is a significant figure in this account as he unknowingly takes Sarai into his house, believing she is Abram's sister.

2. Abram
Later known as Abraham, he is the central figure in this passage. He is called by God to leave his homeland and is promised to be the father of a great nation.

3. Sarai
Abram's wife, who is taken into Pharaoh's house due to Abram's deception about their relationship.

4. Egypt
The land where Abram and Sarai go during a famine. It serves as a place of testing and provision for Abram.

5. Possessions
The wealth and goods that Abram accumulates during his time in Egypt, which he leaves with.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Protection
Despite Abram's deception, God protects Sarai and ensures their safe departure. This demonstrates God's sovereignty over human actions and His ability to protect His promises.

The Consequences of Fear and Deception
Abram's fear leads him to deceive Pharaoh, which could have had dire consequences. This teaches the importance of trusting God rather than resorting to deceit.

God's Provision
Abram leaves Egypt with more than he came with, illustrating how God can provide abundantly even in difficult circumstances.

Faith Amidst Imperfection
Abram's account shows that God can use imperfect people to fulfill His purposes, encouraging believers to trust in God's grace and plan.

What Does Genesis 12:20 Mean
Then Pharaoh gave his men orders

• Pharaoh, the highest civil authority in Egypt, recognizes he must act decisively. God has already struck his household with plagues (Genesis 12:17), so Pharaoh’s command is both an act of self-protection and an acknowledgment of Abram’s God.

• Scripture often shows pagan rulers compelled to respect God’s purposes, as with Nebuchadnezzar honoring Daniel’s God after the fiery furnace (Daniel 3:28-29) and later Pharaoh ordering Israel to leave Egypt (Exodus 12:31-32).

• Romans 13:1 reminds us that “there is no authority except from God,” indicating the Lord can direct even unbelieving leaders to fulfill His will.

concerning Abram

• The focus of Pharaoh’s orders is Abram, the bearer of God’s covenant promise (Genesis 12:2-3). Though Abram’s deception placed him in danger, God intervenes to preserve His chosen servant.

• This protection echoes Psalm 105:15, “Do not touch My anointed ones; do no harm to My prophets,” and anticipates later instances where the Lord safeguards His people despite their failings (Genesis 20:6-7 with Abimelech).

• The episode underscores that God’s faithfulness is anchored in His promise, not Abram’s performance.

and they sent him away

• “Sent away” signals a formal dismissal—Abram is expelled from Egypt. Yet, the forced exit becomes deliverance, turning potential disaster into forward progress toward Canaan.

• Parallels appear when Moses and Israel are driven out of Egypt centuries later (Exodus 12:39) and when Paul and Barnabas are expelled from Pisidian Antioch (Acts 13:50-52), each time advancing God’s redemptive plan.

• What seems like rejection often becomes a divine redirection toward promised blessings.

with his wife

• Sarai is returned unharmed, restoring the marriage bond Abram had jeopardized. God safeguards the lineage through which He will bring forth the Messiah (Galatians 3:16).

• Similar restoration occurs in Genesis 20:14 when Abimelech returns Sarah, and in 1 Peter 3:7 husbands are urged to honor their wives “so that your prayers will not be hindered.”

• The scene upholds the sanctity of marriage and hints at God’s larger purpose of preserving the covenant family.

and all his possessions

• Abram departs not only intact but enriched (Genesis 13:2). Pharaoh’s gifts, meant to placate, become instruments of blessing, illustrating Proverbs 13:22, “the wealth of the sinner is stored up for the righteous.”

• This foreshadows Israel leaving Egypt “with silver and gold” (Psalm 105:37) and Job receiving double after his trial (Job 42:10). God turns worldly wealth into provision for His people’s mission.

summary

Genesis 12:20 shows God overruling Abram’s missteps to protect His covenant plan. Pharaoh’s command, Abram’s expulsion, the restoration of Sarai, and the retention of possessions all reveal a sovereign Lord who keeps His promises, disciplines His servant, and transforms potential ruin into blessing for the unfolding story of redemption.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - And Pharaoh commanded his men (i.e. certain officers designated for the purpose) concerning him (to see to his departure): and they seat him away, and his wife, and all that he had. The partitionists assign this entire section to the Jehovist.
