The next day
This phrase indicates the continuation of events following the miraculous feeding of the 5,000, which occurred the previous day. It sets the stage for the unfolding narrative and highlights the ongoing interest and pursuit of Jesus by the crowd. The timing is significant as it shows the immediacy of the crowd's reaction to Jesus' miracles.

the crowd that had remained on the other side of the sea
The crowd refers to those who had witnessed or heard of the miracle of the loaves and fishes. "The other side of the sea" refers to the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee, near Bethsaida. This area was less populated and served as a retreat for Jesus and His disciples. The crowd's presence indicates their eagerness to follow Jesus, seeking more signs or teachings.

realized that only one boat had been there
This observation by the crowd is crucial as it sets up the miraculous nature of Jesus' actions. The crowd's awareness of the single boat emphasizes their understanding of the logistics involved and their curiosity about Jesus' whereabouts. It also foreshadows the miraculous crossing of Jesus over the sea, which they did not witness.

and that Jesus had not boarded it with His disciples
The crowd's realization that Jesus did not accompany His disciples in the boat highlights their keen observation and interest in Jesus' movements. This detail underscores the mystery surrounding Jesus' actions and sets the stage for the revelation of His divine nature. It also points to the disciples' journey across the sea without Jesus, which is significant in the context of the subsequent miracle of Jesus walking on water.

but they had gone away alone
This phrase emphasizes the separation between Jesus and His disciples at this point in the narrative. The disciples' departure without Jesus is a key detail that adds to the crowd's confusion and anticipation. It also serves to highlight the forthcoming miracle, as Jesus later joins His disciples in a supernatural manner, demonstrating His authority over nature and His divine identity.

What Does John 6:22 Mean
The next day

• John ties this statement to the previous evening’s miracles—the feeding of the five thousand (John 6:1-14) and Jesus’ walk across the water to His disciples (John 6:16-21).

• The phrase signals a literal, 24-hour progression in the narrative, anchoring the events in real time much like Luke 9:37 (“On the next day…”) does after the Transfiguration.

• It reminds us that Jesus’ ministry unfolded in daily, observable steps, confirming the historical trustworthiness of the Gospel record.

the crowd that had remained on the other side of the sea

• These were the same people who had eaten the multiplied loaves and fish (John 6:12-13).

• They had camped on the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee rather than disperse, demonstrating lingering fascination with Jesus (cf. Mark 8:2).

• Their location—physically separated from Capernaum—highlights the determination of those seeking more from Jesus, a pattern repeated later in John 6:24 when they cross over to find Him.

realized that only one boat had been there

• This simple observation underscores eyewitness precision; John notes what the crowd could plainly count.

• It hints at divine orchestration: Jesus would soon reveal Himself as the “bread of life” (John 6:35), and the crowd’s puzzle over the missing boat sets up that teaching.

• Similar narrative details appear in Acts 27:30-32, where specific boats and movements build trust in the inspired account.

and that Jesus had not boarded it with His disciples

• The crowd knew the disciples had departed without Jesus (John 6:17).

• This fact deepens the mystery: How did Jesus travel across? Their curiosity prepares them to encounter a sign they cannot explain away.

• Mark 6:45-47 records the same departure pattern, reinforcing Gospel unity and literal accuracy.

but they had gone away alone

• “They” refers to the disciples, emphasizing that Jesus was physically absent from the boat when it launched.

• The crowd’s realization sets the stage for their search, which culminates in their astonishment when they find Jesus already in Capernaum (John 6:25).

• The scene anticipates Jesus’ later teaching that He, not mere miracles or bread, is what truly satisfies (Isaiah 55:2; John 6:26-27).

summary

John 6:22 records a deliberate, factual observation by the crowd on the morning after the miraculous feeding. They notice one boat, know Jesus never boarded it, and grasp that His disciples left without Him. This small puzzle propels them to seek Jesus across the sea, paving the way for His “bread of life” discourse. The verse affirms the historical reliability of the Gospel, showcases the crowd’s persistent curiosity, and forms a bridge between the preceding miracles and the profound teaching that follows.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) The people.--Better, the multitude. It is the same word which in John 6:5 is rendered "company."

On the other side of the sea--i.e., on the eastern side. The writer's starting-point is now Capernaum. In John 6:25 the same words mean the western side, the starting-point of the multitude being the scene of the miracle.

Save that one whereinto his disciples were entered.--Better, save one, with the best MSS. The addition has arisen from an explanatory gloss.
