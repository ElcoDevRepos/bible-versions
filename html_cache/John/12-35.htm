Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Jesus told them
This phrase indicates a direct communication from Jesus to His audience, which included His disciples and the crowd. It is important to note that this occurs during the final week of Jesus' earthly ministry, shortly before His crucifixion. Jesus often used these moments to impart crucial teachings and prepare His followers for His impending departure.

For a little while longer, the Light will be among you.
Here, "the Light" refers to Jesus Himself, as He previously declared in John 8:12, "I am the light of the world." The phrase "a little while longer" signifies the limited time remaining before His crucifixion. This is a call to urgency, emphasizing the need to embrace His teachings and presence while He is still physically with them. The concept of light is deeply rooted in Jewish tradition, symbolizing God's presence, truth, and guidance, as seen in Psalm 27:1 and Isaiah 60:1-3.

Walk while you have the Light,
"Walk" implies living or conducting one's life. Jesus is urging His listeners to live according to His teachings and example while they have the opportunity. This echoes the biblical theme of walking in the light, which is associated with righteousness and fellowship with God, as seen in 1 John 1:7.

so that darkness will not overtake you.
Darkness symbolizes sin, ignorance, and separation from God. Jesus warns that without His guidance, people are susceptible to being overwhelmed by spiritual darkness. This reflects the broader biblical narrative of light versus darkness, as seen in Ephesians 5:8-14, where believers are called to live as children of light.

The one who walks in the darkness does not know where he is going.
This statement highlights the confusion and lack of direction inherent in a life apart from Christ. It underscores the spiritual blindness that results from rejecting Jesus, as illustrated in John 9:39-41. The imagery of walking in darkness is a powerful metaphor for living without divine truth and guidance, leading to spiritual lostness and peril.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is speaking to the crowd, offering them guidance and a warning about the urgency of following Him, the Light.

2. The Crowd
The audience to whom Jesus is speaking. They are representative of those who are curious about Jesus but have not yet fully committed to following Him.

3. Jerusalem
The setting of this discourse, as Jesus is in the city for the Passover festival, a time of great significance for the Jewish people.

4. The Light
Symbolically represents Jesus Himself, who is the source of truth and guidance.

5. Darkness
Represents ignorance, sin, and separation from God, which overtakes those who do not follow the Light.
Teaching Points
Urgency of Following the Light
Jesus emphasizes the limited time to walk in the light. Believers should recognize the urgency of following Christ and not delay in their commitment.

Awareness of Spiritual Darkness
Understanding that spiritual darkness is a real threat, believers must be vigilant in their walk with Christ to avoid being overtaken by it.

Guidance and Direction
Walking in the light provides clarity and direction in life. Believers should seek Jesus' guidance in all decisions and actions.

Living as Light Bearers
As followers of Christ, believers are called to reflect His light in the world, influencing others towards the truth.

Contrast Between Light and Darkness
Recognize the stark difference between living in the light and walking in darkness, and choose to live in a way that honors God.

What Does John 12:35 Mean
For a little while longer, the Light will be among you

Jesus says, “For a little while longer, the Light will be among you” (John 12:35).

• “The Light” is Jesus Himself, as He has already declared: “I am the Light of the world” (John 8:12; 9:5).

• “A little while longer” points to the brief span between this statement and His crucifixion, echoing John 7:33 and 13:33 where He tells the disciples He will soon leave.

• Because Scripture is true and literal, we understand this as a real, imminent departure—first in death, then ultimately in His ascension. Yet the Light would shine again through His resurrection and indwelling Spirit (John 14:18–20).

Walk while you have the Light

“Walk while you have the Light” (John 12:35).

• “Walk” means to order one’s daily life around Him (Ephesians 5:8–10; 1 John 1:7).

• The verb is urgent: do it now, not later. The crowd must place active faith in Christ before the window closes.

• We too are called to continual, practical obedience—letting His Word guide each step (Psalm 119:105).

So that darkness will not overtake you

“…so that darkness will not overtake you” (John 12:35).

• Darkness represents spiritual blindness and the domain of Satan (Colossians 1:13; Acts 26:18).

• It “overtakes” when a person rejects the Light; like nightfall, it envelops everything (1 Thessalonians 5:4–5).

• The Lord warns that neutrality is impossible: either we move toward the Light or the darkness swallows us.

The one who walks in the darkness does not know where he is going

“The one who walks in the darkness does not know where he is going” (John 12:35).

• Apart from Christ, people stumble through life without purpose or eternal direction (Proverbs 4:19; John 11:9–10).

• They may feel free, yet they wander aimlessly, unaware of looming judgment (John 3:19–20).

• Only by stepping into the Light do we gain clear vision for today and secure hope for eternity.

summary

John 12:35 is a loving but urgent plea. Jesus, the Light, was physically present for only a short time; His hearers needed to believe and live in Him immediately. To “walk” in the Light means practical, obedient faith. Refusal lets darkness engulf the soul, leaving a person lost and directionless. Even now the message stands: embrace and follow Christ today, and His Light will keep you from being overtaken by the darkness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) Then Jesus said unto them, Yet a little while is the light with you.--It is better, as we have often seen, to read Therefore for "Then." The word connects what follows closely with what has gone before. It was because of their question that Jesus said this. And yet it is not said that "He answered them," because what He said was not a direct answer. They are asking questions in which we may trace the spirit, if not the very words, of the formal, literal objectors who had, with like technicalities, stifled the truth whenever it was springing up in their minds. Such questions cannot be really answered, because they are not really questions. And now the day has gone, and the night is at hand. The old thought comes back to Him (John 9:4; John 11:9). The last rays of light are shining. It is but a little while, and He warns them with all the solemnity of this thought.

Walk while ye have the light, lest darkness come upon you.--The better reading is, Walk according as ye have the light--i.e., "Walk as men who are conscious that the light is among them, use your opportunities; do not ask questions to raise objections, but ask them in order that you may know the truth." The man who thus used the light would by no means walk in darkness, but would have the light of life (John 8:12). For him that neglected to use the means and faculty he had, both would cease to exist. (Comp. Note on Romans 1:21.)

The words "come upon," or "overtake," is used of some sudden seizure. There are two parallels in Biblical Greek, "But ye, brethren, are not in darkness that the day should overtake you as a thief" (1Thessalonians 5:4), and "Be sure your sin will find you out" (Numbers 32:23). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - Christ's reply is introduced with a simple εϊπεν. Jesus therefore said to them, not in answer to their question, but by taking up a title of dignity that he had claimed before, he evidently assumes to be the Light of the world (John 8:12), and now the time is almost over when they could see its luster or discern other things, either themselves, or their sins, or this world, or the next world, by that Light. The time for further instruction, or remonstrance, or declarations is at an end. The evangelist sums up, in vers. 44-50, the general substance of our Lord's teaching with reference to himself and his disciples and the world which would not believe; and thus, then, in a wonderful way, justifies, as it were, the non-answer to the captious question, "Who is this Son of man?" Yet a little while is the Light amongst you. The "little while" of our Lord's day of ministry was often upon his lips (John 7:33; John 13:33; John 14:19; John 16:16). Verily to his consciousness it must have been but as the twinkling of an eye, and now it was a very little while even for his hearers. Based on this solemn fact, he makes a last public appeal to individuals, propounding gracious invitation, Divine promise, solemn warning; and so he terminated his public ministry, and vanished from before them. As far as the memory of his living words and deeds might influence them, the Light, though not among them, might still shine, and the glory of Pentecost would renew the appeal. Walk as ye have the Light; make progress in the understanding of self, of duty, of time, of eternity, and act accordingly. The ὡς is the reading preferred to the ἕως of the T.R. in this and the following verse by Tischendorf, Meyer, Westport and Hort, and the Revisers' text. Meyer here differs from Godet and others who, accepting the reading ὡς, give it, in virtue of certain passages in the classics, the sense of quamdiu, and justly maintains the sense "as," "in the measure that." According to the light that you see, walk, lest (ἵνα μὴ, "in order that not") darkness overtake you: and he that walketh in the darkness knoweth not whither he goeth; lest the possibility of seeing the Divine revelation in me be taken from you, and lest there be taken away from you that which you seem to have (cf. Jeremiah 13:16). Then, in harmony with the great sayings of John 9:4, 5 and John 11:9, "In the night no man can work;" "In the night, when men cannot see the light of this world, they stumble over unseen perils and pitfalls;" so here, he says, in the darkness that will come upon men from making no use of the Light of the world, "they will not know whither they are going," they will find no work, have no perception of imminent danger, but, driven on and on by measureless force, they will drift over the fathomless unknown into infinite and endless suspense. When the Light of the world is spurned, and a godless evolution made to supply its place, humanity and the world have no goal set before them; there is no end at which they aim - no mind or will to guide the progress of mankind.
