Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The next day
This phrase indicates a continuation of events from the previous verses, suggesting a sequence of days where John the Baptist is actively ministering. It highlights the ongoing nature of John's testimony about Jesus. The Gospel of John often uses time markers to structure the narrative, emphasizing the unfolding revelation of Jesus as the Messiah.

John was there again
John the Baptist's presence is significant as he consistently points others to Jesus. His role as a forerunner is prophesied in Isaiah 40:3 and Malachi 3:1, preparing the way for the Lord. His steadfastness in his mission underscores his commitment to God's calling, serving as a model of faithfulness.

with two of his disciples
These disciples are later identified as Andrew and, likely, John the Apostle. The presence of disciples indicates that John the Baptist had a following, people who were committed to his message of repentance and preparation for the coming Messiah. This reflects the Jewish tradition of rabbinic discipleship, where students would learn from a teacher. The transition of these disciples from John to Jesus marks the shift from the old covenant to the new, as they recognize Jesus as the fulfillment of John's message.

Persons / Places / Events
1. John the Baptist
A pivotal figure in the New Testament, John the Baptist is known for his role in preparing the way for Jesus Christ. He is a prophet who baptizes with water and bears witness to the coming Messiah.

2. Two Disciples
These are followers of John the Baptist who are present with him. They are significant because they later become followers of Jesus, illustrating the transition from John's ministry to that of Jesus.

3. The Location
While the specific location is not mentioned in this verse, it is understood to be in the region where John was baptizing, likely near the Jordan River. This setting is important as it is a place of spiritual awakening and preparation.

4. The Event
This verse marks a moment of transition and witness, as John the Baptist points his disciples towards Jesus, indicating the shift from his own ministry to that of Jesus.
Teaching Points
Witnessing to Christ
John the Baptist exemplifies the role of a witness, pointing others to Jesus. As believers, we are called to direct others to Christ through our words and actions.

Transition of Discipleship
The movement of John's disciples to Jesus illustrates the importance of recognizing and following the true source of salvation. We must be willing to transition our loyalties to Christ above all else.

Preparation for Ministry
Just as John prepared the way for Jesus, we are called to prepare our hearts and lives for Christ's work in us and through us.

The Role of Humility
John the Baptist's willingness to decrease so that Jesus might increase is a powerful lesson in humility and submission to God's greater plan.

What Does John 1:35 Mean
The timing: “The next day”

- Scripture sets a deliberate pace. After proclaiming Jesus as “the Lamb of God” (John 1:29), John continues his ministry without pause.

- God often works in a series of purposeful days—creation (Genesis 1), Israel’s march around Jericho (Joshua 6:3–4), and Jesus’ own final week (Mark 11–16). Each “next day” signals progress in God’s unfolding plan.

- For the readers of John’s Gospel, this phrase anchors the narrative in real time, inviting us to see each day as an opportunity to point others to Christ (Ephesians 5:15–16).

The steadfast witness: “John was there again”

- John the Baptist remains in the same place of ministry, illustrating perseverance (1 Corinthians 15:58).

- “Again” highlights consistency; yesterday’s faithful stand is repeated today (Galatians 6:9).

- His unwavering presence contrasts with shifting crowds (Luke 7:24–25), underscoring the call to remain steadfast even when attention turns elsewhere.

Investing in disciples: “with two of his disciples”

- John does not minister alone; he’s pouring into future witnesses. One of these is Andrew (John 1:40), who will soon bring Simon Peter to Jesus.

- Mentorship is woven through Scripture: Moses with Joshua (Deuteronomy 31:7), Elijah with Elisha (2 Kings 2:9), Paul with Timothy (2 Timothy 2:2).

- By staying “with” his disciples, John models relational discipleship—life shared, truth explained, hearts prepared for Jesus (Acts 18:26).

- The scene also shows humility; John willingly hands his disciples over to the Messiah when He appears (John 3:30).

summary

John 1:35 captures more than a date stamp; it showcases daily faithfulness, steadfast presence, and intentional disciple-making. Each element—“the next day,” “John was there again,” and “with two of his disciples”—invites believers to embrace consistent witness, remain rooted where God places us, and invest in others who will follow Christ.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) Again the next day after John stood.--Better, The next day again John was standing. The description is of a scene present to the mind, and by one of the two disciples (John 1:40). The "again" refers to John 1:29.

Two of his disciples.--There is no reason for thinking that these were absent on the previous day, and that the testimony is specially repeated for them. Rather it is that, in that band of disciples too, there is an inner circle of those who, because they can receive more, are taught more. They had heard the words before, it may be had talked together about them, at least in individual thought had tried to follow them, and now they have come to the Teacher again. Can we doubt what questions fill the heart or shape themselves in word? He had passed through their struggle from darkness into light. There is a Presence with them which he now knows, and before which his own work must cease. The passing voice is no longer needed now that the abiding Word has come. Can we doubt what his answer is?

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 35-51. - 3. The first disciples, and their testimony. Verses 35-39. -

(1) John directs his own disciples to Jesus. Verse 35. - On the morrow, again John was standing, and two from his disciples; implying that there were many others within hearing of his voice, or, at least, under his influence. The imperfect tense of the verb εἱστήκει suggests the idea that he was waiting for some fresh announcement, some providential event, to determine his course. The "again" refers back to ver. 29. Much must be read between the lines as to these disciples, their excited interest in the words already uttered by their master.
