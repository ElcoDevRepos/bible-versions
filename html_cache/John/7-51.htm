Does our law convict a man
This phrase refers to the Jewish legal system, which was based on the Mosaic Law. The law emphasized justice and fairness, requiring evidence and testimony before a conviction. This reflects the principle found in Deuteronomy 1:16-17, where judges are instructed to hear cases fairly. The question posed here highlights the importance of due process, a concept that is foundational in both ancient and modern legal systems.

without first hearing from him
The requirement to hear from the accused is rooted in the legal traditions of ancient Israel, where a fair trial was essential. This principle is echoed in Exodus 23:1-3, which warns against spreading false reports and emphasizes impartiality. The practice of hearing from the accused ensures that justice is not only done but seen to be done, preventing wrongful convictions.

to determine what he has done?
This part of the verse underscores the need for a thorough investigation to establish the facts of a case. It aligns with the biblical mandate for truth and justice, as seen in Proverbs 18:13, which warns against answering a matter before hearing it. The emphasis on determining the actions of the accused reflects a commitment to truth, ensuring that judgments are based on evidence rather than assumptions or hearsay.

What Does John 7:51 Mean
Does our law

Nicodemus reminds the council that God’s own Torah sets the standard for justice.

• The law was never intended to be wielded as a weapon of convenience; it embodies God’s righteous character (Deuteronomy 1:16-17; Exodus 23:1-3).

• By asking, “Does our law…,” he appeals to an authority everyone in the room claims to obey—highlighting their accountability before God (Psalm 19:7-9).

• The scene exposes a tension: the leaders profess devotion to the Law yet stand poised to sidestep it for the sake of expedience (Matthew 23:27-28).

convict a man

The phrase points to formal judgment—moving from accusation to condemnation.

• Conviction in Israel required careful, lawful procedure (Deuteronomy 17:6; Numbers 35:30).

• Nicodemus is not denying that guilt exists; he is challenging the rush to pronounce it.

• True justice requires more than suspicion or public pressure (Acts 25:16; John 18:31).

without first hearing from him

God’s standard insists on allowing the accused to speak.

• “He who answers a matter before he hears it, it is folly and shame” (Proverbs 18:13).

• “The first to state his case seems right until another comes and cross-examines him” (Proverbs 18:17).

• Jesus had repeatedly testified about Himself (John 5:30-32), yet the leaders refused to listen with open hearts (Isaiah 11:3-4).

to determine what he has done

Evidence, not rumor, must drive any verdict.

• “On the testimony of two or three witnesses a matter shall be confirmed” (Deuteronomy 19:15).

• Jesus’ works authenticated His claims (John 5:36; 10:37-38).

• Even in church life, Paul echoes the same principle: “Do not entertain an accusation… unless it is brought by two or three witnesses” (1 Timothy 5:19).

• Nicodemus challenges the council to pause, examine the facts, and render judgment in line with God’s revealed standard.

summary

John 7:51 is a Spirit-inspired reminder that God’s law demands fair hearing, credible evidence, and righteous judgment. Nicodemus’ question exposes the hypocrisy of leaders ready to condemn while ignoring the very Scriptures they claim to uphold. For every believer, the verse underscores both the reliability of God’s Word and His unchanging call to justice grounded in truth.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(51) Doth our law judge any man?--He identifies Himself with them. He, like they, is an expounder of the Law. The force of the question is in the word "Law," which they had used but the moment before in their scorn for the people who knew not the Law. "Well, this Law, which we do know and understand, doth it judge without open investigation?" Did they in their blind zeal forget such passages as Exodus 23:1; Deuteronomy 1:16-17; Deuteronomy 19:15? They had determined a death, and were seeking to carry their sentence into effect in direct contravention of the Law. This holy people, instructed in the Law--they were the Law-breakers.

Before it hear him.--The better reading is, unless it hear first from him.

And know what he doeth--i.e., know the deed for which he is tried.
