Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
My Father who has given them to Me
This phrase emphasizes the divine relationship between Jesus and God the Father, highlighting the authority and unity within the Trinity. The concept of God giving believers to Jesus is rooted in the idea of divine election and predestination, as seen in passages like Ephesians 1:4-5. This reflects the belief that salvation is initiated by God and that believers are a gift to Christ, underscoring the security and assurance of their salvation. The Father’s role in giving believers to Jesus also points to the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah gathering God’s people, such as in Isaiah 53:10-12.

is greater than all
This phrase asserts the supremacy and omnipotence of God the Father over all creation. It reflects the monotheistic belief central to Jewish and Christian theology that God is the ultimate authority and power. This statement can be connected to passages like Isaiah 40:25-26, which speak of God’s incomparable greatness. The phrase reassures believers of the security found in God’s power, as nothing can surpass or challenge His will. It also serves as a reminder of the divine hierarchy, where Jesus, though equal with God, submits to the Father’s authority, as seen in Philippians 2:6-8.

No one can snatch them out of My Father’s hand
This phrase provides a powerful assurance of the believer’s eternal security. The imagery of being in the Father’s hand suggests protection, care, and possession. It echoes the shepherd imagery found earlier in John 10, where Jesus describes Himself as the Good Shepherd who protects His sheep. The idea of being unable to be snatched away is reminiscent of Psalm 95:7, where God is depicted as the shepherd of His people. This assurance is further supported by Romans 8:38-39, which declares that nothing can separate believers from the love of God. The phrase emphasizes the permanence of salvation and the believer’s safety in God’s sovereign grip.

What Does John 10:29 Mean
My Father

Jesus begins with relationship. He is not speaking of an abstract deity but of “My Father,” the One with whom He shares perfect unity (John 10:30). This personal language reminds us:

• Believers have been brought into the same family (John 1:12).

• The Father’s character shines through the Son (John 14:9).

• Everything Jesus does reflects the Father’s will (John 5:19).

Who has given them to Me

The “them” are the sheep—those who hear Christ’s voice and follow Him (John 10:27). The Father’s gift of people to the Son is a recurring theme:

• “All that the Father gives Me will come to Me” (John 6:37).

• “This is the will of Him who sent Me: that I shall lose none of all He has given Me” (John 6:39).

• “I have revealed Your name to the men You have given Me” (John 17:6).

This speaks of divine initiative and grace; our security rests on God’s choice, not our performance.

Is greater than all

The Father’s supremacy guarantees the promise that follows. Scripture repeatedly affirms His unrivaled greatness:

• “For the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords” (Deuteronomy 10:17).

• “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory” (1 Chronicles 29:11).

• “Great is the LORD and greatly to be praised; He is above all gods” (Psalm 95:3).

With such power behind our salvation, any rival force shrinks to nothing.

No one can snatch them

“Snatch” pictures violent seizure. Yet Jesus insists there is no thief—human, demonic, or circumstantial—strong enough to steal a believer away:

• “I give them eternal life, and they will never perish” (John 10:28).

• “Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? … neither death nor life … nor any other creature” (Romans 8:35-39).

• Believers “are protected by the power of God through faith” (1 Peter 1:5).

Out of My Father’s hand

The “hand” represents authority and protection. Once placed there, we remain:

• “Indeed, I have inscribed you on the palms of My hands” (Isaiah 49:16).

• “No one can deliver out of My hand; when I act, who can reverse it?” (Isaiah 43:13).

• “Your right hand sustains me” (Psalm 18:35).

God’s grip is loving, firm, and eternal.

summary

John 10:29 underscores unbreakable security for every believer: the Father personally gives us to His Son, stands supreme over every power, and holds us in a grasp no enemy can loosen. Resting in that truth, we follow our Shepherd with confidence and joy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(29) My Father, which gave them me (better, hath given them Me), is greater than all.--For the thought that they are given by the Father, comp. Note on John 6:37. Here our version has rightly made no limiting addition to "all" (comp. last verse). In the width of the word, which extends to every creature and to every power, and even to the Son in His subordination to the Father, the Father is thought of as greater than all. Again the thought mounts with each succeeding sentence: (1) "None shall pluck them out of My hand;" (2) "They are My Father's gifts, and He is greater than all;" (3) "None shall pluck them out of My Father's hand."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 29. - The last statement is sustained by a still loftier assumption. Before translating, it is necessary to notice the three readings of the text.

(1) That of the T.R. and the Revisers' Text: My Father who gave (them) to me is greater than all the powers that can possibly be arrayed against them.

(2) The reading of א, D, With reference to that which my Father, One greater than all, gave me, and no one is able to pluck from the hand of the Father. Meyer, however, translates this differently; he supposes the μεῖζον to refer to the Father "a something greater, a greater potence." Westcott and Hort prefer the reading with ὅ and μείζον; and Westcott translates, That which my Father has given me is greater than all, and regards it as a reference to the sheep as a collective unity. The internal reasons compel Luthardt, Godet, and Lange to fall back on T.R., and surely the extraordinary strain of the meaning justifies them. Our Lord would sustain with even stronger assurance the safety of his sheep. The Father's gift to himself, the Father's own eternal love and power, the Divine omnipotence of the Lord God himself, is pledged to their security. "My hand" becomes "my Father's hand." He seems to say, "If you question my capacity, you need not question his power. Sacrilegious violence may apparently nail my hands to the cross; the sword may awake against Jehovah's Shepherd. But none can outwit, surprise, crucify, conquer, my Father, none can invalidate his care."
