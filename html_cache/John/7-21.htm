Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Jesus answered them
In this context, Jesus is responding to the Jewish leaders and the crowd during the Feast of Tabernacles in Jerusalem. This festival was one of the three major Jewish feasts, drawing large crowds to the city. Jesus often used these gatherings to teach and reveal His identity. His response is part of a larger dialogue where He addresses the skepticism and hostility of the religious leaders. This phrase indicates a direct engagement with His audience, emphasizing His role as a teacher and authority.

I did one miracle
The miracle Jesus refers to is likely the healing of the man at the Pool of Bethesda on the Sabbath, as recorded in John 5:1-15. This act of healing was significant because it challenged the Jewish leaders' strict interpretation of the Sabbath laws. The miracle demonstrated Jesus' authority over the Sabbath and His divine power to heal, which was a sign of the coming Messianic age prophesied in Isaiah 35:5-6. This miracle was not just a display of power but a revelation of Jesus' identity as the Son of God.

and you are all amazed
The amazement of the people reflects the impact of Jesus' miraculous works and teachings. In the Jewish context, miracles were seen as signs of divine intervention. However, the amazement also highlights a division among the people: some were open to believing in Jesus as the Messiah, while others, particularly the religious leaders, were skeptical and even hostile. This amazement can be seen as a fulfillment of prophecies like Isaiah 29:14, where God promises to do a marvelous work that would astonish the people. Jesus' miracles were intended to lead people to faith, yet they also exposed the hardness of heart among those who refused to believe.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is addressing the crowd and religious leaders in Jerusalem during the Feast of Tabernacles. He is responding to their amazement and criticism regarding His miraculous works.

2. The Crowd
The people gathered in Jerusalem for the Feast of Tabernacles, including both common people and religious leaders, who are reacting to Jesus' teachings and miracles.

3. The Miracle
Refers to the healing of the man at the Pool of Bethesda on the Sabbath (John 5:1-15), which had previously caused controversy among the Jewish leaders.

4. Jerusalem
The city where this event takes place, significant as the religious and cultural center of Jewish life, especially during the major feasts.

5. Feast of Tabernacles
A Jewish festival commemorating the Israelites' wilderness journey, during which Jesus is teaching and performing miracles.
Teaching Points
Understanding Jesus' Authority
Jesus' miracles demonstrate His divine authority and identity as the Messiah. Believers are called to recognize and submit to His authority in their lives.

The Sabbath and Mercy
Jesus challenges legalistic interpretations of the Sabbath, emphasizing the importance of mercy and compassion. Christians are encouraged to prioritize love and mercy over rigid adherence to rules.

Responding to Miracles
The crowd's amazement at Jesus' miracle invites reflection on how we respond to God's work in our lives. Believers should cultivate a heart of gratitude and faith.

Confronting Legalism
Jesus' interaction with the religious leaders serves as a warning against legalism. Christians should examine their own practices to ensure they align with the spirit of God's law, not just the letter.

Faith in Action
Jesus' miracles call for a response of faith and action. Believers are encouraged to trust in God's power and step out in faith, even when faced with opposition.

What Does John 7:21 Mean
Jesus answered them

- The setting is the Feast of Tabernacles in Jerusalem (John 7:14). Religious leaders are questioning Jesus’ authority, and “Jesus answered them” shows He engages directly, not avoiding confrontation.

- His willingness to respond illustrates His transparency, echoing earlier moments when He “answered and said” to critics (John 5:17; Matthew 22:29).

- Scripture records this dialogue as literal history, reminding us that every word Christ speaks is trustworthy (John 17:17).

I did one miracle

- The “one miracle” points back to the healing of the lame man at Bethesda (John 5:1-9). That single act on the Sabbath sparked ongoing controversy (John 5:16).

- Jesus highlights it to expose the inconsistency of His opponents: they tolerate circumcision on the Sabbath (John 7:22-23) yet condemn Him for restoring a man completely.

- Other passages underline that Jesus’ works validate His divine mission (John 10:25; Acts 2:22). Here He focuses on just one sign to make the leaders face their own hardness of heart.

and you are all amazed

- “Amazed” captures mixed reactions: wonder from the crowd (John 7:31) and irritation from rulers (John 7:32). Their astonishment proves the miracle’s authenticity; no one denied it happened.

- Similar amazement follows many of Jesus’ deeds (Mark 2:12; Matthew 12:23), yet amazement alone is not faith. Some marveled but still plotted against Him (Luke 6:11).

- The verse invites us to move from mere wonder to worship, recognizing the Lord who “does all things well” (Mark 7:37).

summary

John 7:21 captures Jesus calmly confronting His critics: He reminds them of a single, undeniable miracle, exposing their inconsistent judgment and calling them from shallow amazement to genuine belief. The verse underscores Christ’s authority, the reliability of His works, and our need to respond with wholehearted faith rather than conflicted astonishment.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) I have done one work--i.e., the one conspicuous work of healing the infirm man on the Sabbath day, which He did at His last visit to Jerusalem. We have already had a reference to other works in John 2:23, and He Himself refers to His many good works in John 10:32.

Ye all marvel.--This answer is addressed to the multitude who said "Thou hast a devil," when He spoke of the intention to kill Him. This work on the Sabbath day, which provoked the deadly hostility of the hierarchy (John 5:16; John 5:18), was cause of wonder to them all. They, too, though not in the same degree, were led by it to take a hostile position.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - Jesus answered and said to them; i.e. to the multitude wire had so coarsely treated him, and to the "Jews" who were present, who were all marvelling together at the line he was taking. The very interruption was a proof both of the extent and consequence of their wonderment. One work I did, and ye are all marvelling. This one work was a very small fraction of his mighty signs, but it was one which, from its manner of operation, and from the fact that it was immediately brought before the religious authorities as an unlawful act (ch. 5.), and which, moreover, became the occasion for one of the greatest of his discourses, and for his solemn claim to be the Son of God and the Arbiter of life and death, of resurrection and judgment, made the profoundest impression on the Sanhedrin, compelled them to think that he was a Man who must be sooner or later arrested, and who deserved condign punishment. He must be either submitted to, confined as a madman, or killed as a blasphemer.
