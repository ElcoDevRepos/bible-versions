Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Many of them said
This phrase indicates a division among the people who were listening to Jesus. The context is the Feast of Dedication in Jerusalem, where Jesus was teaching. The "many" refers to a significant portion of the Jewish leaders and possibly some of the crowd. This division is a recurring theme in the Gospel of John, highlighting the varied responses to Jesus' ministry.

He is demon-possessed
Accusations of demon possession were not uncommon in the ancient world, especially when someone exhibited behavior or teachings that were considered radical or threatening to the established order. In the Gospels, Jesus is accused multiple times of being under demonic influence (e.g., Matthew 12:24, Mark 3:22). This reflects the spiritual blindness and hardness of heart of those who opposed Him, unable to recognize His divine authority and power.

and insane
The charge of insanity suggests that Jesus' teachings and claims were perceived as irrational or beyond comprehension. In the cultural context, mental illness was often misunderstood and stigmatized. The accusation of insanity also serves to discredit Jesus' message, as it implies that His words should not be taken seriously. This reflects the tension between human understanding and divine revelation.

Why would you listen to Him?
This rhetorical question is meant to dissuade others from following Jesus. It reflects the leaders' fear of losing influence and control over the people. The question also underscores the challenge of faith, as listening to Jesus requires discernment and openness to the truth He reveals. This echoes the broader theme in John of hearing and recognizing the voice of the Good Shepherd (John 10:3-4, 27).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
Central figure in the Gospel of John, whose teachings and miracles are the subject of debate and division among the people.

2. The Jews
Refers to the Jewish leaders and people who were divided in their opinion about Jesus, some accusing Him of being demon-possessed.

3. Jerusalem
The city where these events take place, a significant location for Jewish religious life and the backdrop for much of Jesus' ministry.

4. Feast of Dedication (Hanukkah)
The context in which this discussion occurs, a time when many Jews gathered in Jerusalem.

5. Demon-possession
A term used by some of the Jews to describe Jesus, reflecting their misunderstanding and rejection of His divine nature.
Teaching Points
Misunderstanding of Jesus' Identity
Many people, even today, misunderstand who Jesus is. It's crucial to seek a true understanding of His nature and mission through Scripture.

Spiritual Blindness
The accusations against Jesus reflect a spiritual blindness that can affect anyone. We must pray for discernment and openness to the truth.

The Cost of Following Jesus
Being associated with Jesus may lead to misunderstanding and ridicule. Believers should be prepared to face opposition for their faith.

The Divisive Nature of Truth
Jesus' teachings often cause division. We must be prepared to stand firm in the truth, even when it is unpopular or misunderstood.

Listening to Jesus
Despite accusations, many still chose to listen to Jesus. We should prioritize His voice above all others in our lives.

What Does John 10:20 Mean
Many of them said,

The crowd surrounding Jesus at the Feast of Dedication was not monolithic. John 10:19 already notes, “Again there was division among the Jews because of Jesus’ message”.

• The inspired text accurately captures real voices—neighbors, religious leaders, and bystanders—who had just heard Jesus claim, “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30).

• Scripture repeatedly shows that clear revelation produces either faith or hostility: see John 7:43; Luke 4:28-29; Acts 14:4.

• Taking the passage literally, we recognize that these spoken words are part of God’s record, demonstrating human unbelief in the face of divine truth.

“He is demon-possessed and insane.

The accusation combines spiritual slander (“demon-possessed”) with a personal insult (“insane”).

• Earlier, a similar charge was hurled: “You are a Samaritan and have a demon” (John 8:48). The pattern fulfills Isaiah 53:3—“He was despised and rejected by men.”

• Matthew 12:24 and Mark 3:22 reveal the same tactic: attributing Christ’s works to demonic power rather than acknowledging divine authority.

• Such claims ignore the evident goodness Jesus displayed in healing the blind man (John 9) and in offering eternal life (John 10:28).

• By labeling Him insane, they dismissed both His miracles and His teaching, contrary to the testimony of Psalm 34:8, “Taste and see that the LORD is good.”

The literal wording underscores a tragic irony: the One they call mad is perfect holiness in human flesh.

Why would you listen to Him?”

The scoffers not only reject Jesus for themselves but seek to silence His voice for others.

• Proverbs 1:7 states, “The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge,” yet unbelief treats divine wisdom as folly.

• Jesus had just said, “My sheep listen to My voice; I know them, and they follow Me” (John 10:27). The question “Why listen?” exposes who is—and is not—among His sheep.

• Acts 4:18-19 shows the same pressure later placed on the apostles: “Do not speak or teach at all in the name of Jesus.” True disciples must choose obedience to God over the crowd’s ridicule.

• Despite opposition, the gospel still advances (Romans 1:16). The literal charge to stop listening only highlights the power of Christ’s words that still draw hearts today.

summary

John 10:20 records an authentic moment of resistance: some listeners brand Jesus demon-possessed and insane and urge others to stop listening. Their reaction fulfills Scripture’s portrayal of hardened hearts while contrasting sharply with the promise that Jesus’ true sheep will hear His voice. The verse therefore stands as both a sober warning about unbelief and a reaffirmation of Christ’s unquestioned authority, integrity, and divine mission.
