You will pay them back what they deserve
This phrase reflects the principle of divine retribution, a common theme throughout the Bible. It echoes the concept found in Deuteronomy 32:35, where God declares that vengeance belongs to Him. The idea of God repaying individuals according to their deeds is also seen in the New Testament, such as in Romans 12:19. This reflects the justice of God, who is portrayed as a righteous judge throughout Scripture. In the context of Lamentations, the prophet Jeremiah is lamenting the destruction of Jerusalem and the suffering of the people, and he calls upon God to enact justice upon those who have wronged them.

O LORD
The use of "O LORD" signifies a direct appeal to Yahweh, the covenant name of God, emphasizing a personal relationship and a plea for divine intervention. This invocation is consistent with the Hebrew tradition of calling upon God in times of distress, as seen in the Psalms. It underscores the belief in God's sovereignty and His ability to act on behalf of His people. The name "LORD" in all capitals is a translation of the Tetragrammaton, YHWH, which is considered sacred and is a reminder of God's eternal presence and faithfulness to His covenant promises.

according to the work of their hands
This phrase highlights the biblical principle of sowing and reaping, where individuals are held accountable for their actions. It is reminiscent of passages like Galatians 6:7, which states that a person reaps what they sow. In the historical context of Lamentations, this reflects the consequences faced by the nations and individuals who have acted against God's people. The "work of their hands" can be understood as their deeds and actions, which in the context of the Babylonian conquest, includes acts of violence and idolatry. This phrase serves as a reminder of the moral order established by God, where justice is ultimately served.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
- Traditionally attributed as the author of Lamentations, Jeremiah is known as the "weeping prophet." He laments the destruction of Jerusalem and the suffering of his people.

2. Jerusalem
- The city that has been destroyed by the Babylonians, serving as the backdrop for the book of Lamentations. It represents the consequences of the nation's disobedience to God.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
- The covenant God of Israel, who is appealed to for justice and recompense against the enemies of His people.

4. The Enemies of Israel
- Those who have brought destruction and suffering upon Jerusalem, often seen as instruments of God's judgment but also as subjects of future divine justice.

5. The Babylonian Exile
- The historical event during which the people of Judah were taken captive by Babylon, leading to the lamentations over Jerusalem's fall.
Teaching Points
Divine Justice
God is just and will repay individuals according to their actions. This assurance can bring comfort to believers who face injustice, knowing that God sees and will act in His perfect timing.

Trust in God's Timing
While we may desire immediate justice, God's timing is perfect. We are called to trust in His wisdom and sovereignty, even when we do not see immediate results.

The Consequences of Sin
The destruction of Jerusalem serves as a sobering reminder of the consequences of turning away from God. It calls us to examine our own lives and repent where necessary.

Prayer for Justice
Like Jeremiah, we can bring our cries for justice to God, trusting that He hears and will act. This encourages us to maintain a prayerful attitude in the face of adversity.

Hope in Restoration
Despite the lament, there is hope for restoration. God's justice is not only about punishment but also about restoring what is broken, pointing us to the ultimate restoration through Christ.

What Does Lamentations 3:64 Mean
You will pay them back

The verse begins with confident certainty that God Himself will act. Jeremiah, the author of Lamentations, is not wishing for blind fate to even the score; he is relying on the righteous Judge of all the earth.

• Scripture consistently presents God as the One who personally executes justice (Romans 12:19; Deuteronomy 32:35).

• In a world that often appears unjust, this assurance anchors believers: the Lord is actively involved, not passive or distant (Psalm 94:1).

what they deserve

The phrase speaks to proportional justice—no more, no less. It is the biblical equivalent of “an eye for an eye,” ensuring that recompense matches wrongdoing.

• God’s judgments are neither capricious nor excessive; they are exact (Jeremiah 17:10; Psalm 62:12).

• This reminder guards our hearts from bitterness: we can entrust wrongs to the Lord, knowing He will balance the scales with perfect equity (Proverbs 24:12).

O LORD

By invoking the covenant name, Jeremiah appeals to God’s character—unchanging, holy, and faithful.

• The very name “LORD” recalls His self-revelation as compassionate yet just (Exodus 34:6-7; Psalm 86:15).

• In the flow of Lamentations 3, this address connects with earlier affirmations of God’s steadfast love and mercy that are “new every morning” (verses 22-23). The same faithful God who shows mercy also upholds justice.

according to the work of their hands

Accountability is tied to deeds. Human actions are not forgotten; they are recorded and weighed.

• Scripture repeats this principle: we reap what we sow (Galatians 6:7-8), each will receive “according to what he has done in the body” (2 Corinthians 5:10).

• Final judgment in Revelation also hinges on “what they had done” (Revelation 20:12; Matthew 16:27).

• The phrase underscores human responsibility: hands that build evil will face the consequences their own efforts produced (Jeremiah 25:14).

summary

Lamentations 3:64 affirms God’s personal, precise, and covenant-based justice. The Lord Himself will repay evildoers in exact proportion to their deeds, demonstrating both His faithfulness to His people and His unwavering commitment to righteousness.

(64) Render unto them . . .--The words are noticeable as being taken from Psalm 28:4, and reproduced by St. Paul in 2Timothy 4:14.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 64. - Render unto them, etc. The sacred poet is familiar with the psalms; here we have a condensation of Psalm 28:4. The tone of vers. 64-66 reminds us of passages in the Book of Jeremiah (see Jeremiah 18:23; Jeremiah 20:12);
