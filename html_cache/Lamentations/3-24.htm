The LORD is my portion
In the context of Lamentations, the author, traditionally believed to be Jeremiah, is expressing deep sorrow over the destruction of Jerusalem. The phrase "The LORD is my portion" reflects a profound declaration of faith amidst suffering. In ancient Israel, the term "portion" often referred to an inheritance or share of land, which was a source of security and identity. For the Levites, who had no land inheritance, the LORD Himself was their portion (Numbers 18:20). This signifies a reliance on God as the ultimate source of sustenance and security, transcending material possessions. The psalmist echoes this sentiment in Psalm 73:26, emphasizing God as the strength and portion forever.

says my soul
The use of "soul" indicates a deep, inner conviction. In Hebrew thought, the soul (nephesh) encompasses the whole being, not just the spiritual aspect. This suggests that the declaration is not merely intellectual but involves the entire person—mind, will, and emotions. It reflects a personal and intimate relationship with God, where the soul finds rest and assurance in Him. This internal dialogue is a common biblical motif, seen in passages like Psalm 42:5, where the soul is encouraged to hope in God despite external circumstances.

therefore I will hope in Him
The word "therefore" connects the acknowledgment of God as one's portion to the resulting hope. This hope is not wishful thinking but a confident expectation based on God's character and promises. In the midst of lament, the author finds hope in God's faithfulness and mercy, which are new every morning (Lamentations 3:22-23). This hope is rooted in the covenant relationship between God and His people, as seen throughout the Old Testament. It anticipates the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises in Jesus Christ, who is described as our hope (1 Timothy 1:1) and the anchor for the soul (Hebrews 6:19).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
- Traditionally attributed as the author of Lamentations, Jeremiah is known as the "weeping prophet." He wrote these laments during the destruction of Jerusalem, expressing deep sorrow and hope in God.

2. Jerusalem
- The city that faced destruction by the Babylonians in 586 B.C. This event is the backdrop for the book of Lamentations, highlighting the consequences of Israel's disobedience and the hope for restoration.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
- The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal faithfulness and relationship with Israel. In this verse, He is described as the "portion" of the soul, indicating a personal and sustaining relationship.
Teaching Points
God as Our Portion
The Hebrew word for "portion" (cheleq) signifies an inheritance or share. In times of loss or uncertainty, believers can find comfort in knowing that God Himself is their inheritance, providing all they need.

Hope in God
The declaration "therefore I will hope in Him" emphasizes a conscious decision to trust in God's faithfulness. This hope is not based on circumstances but on the unchanging nature of God.

Soul's Declaration
The phrase "says my soul" indicates a deep, internal conviction. Believers are encouraged to cultivate a heart that consistently affirms God's sufficiency and presence.

Faith Amidst Suffering
Lamentations teaches that even in the midst of suffering and judgment, there is room for hope. This hope is rooted in God's character and promises, not in immediate relief or change in circumstances.

Contentment in Christ
Just as the Levites had no land inheritance but God Himself, Christians are called to find their ultimate satisfaction and contentment in Christ, who is our eternal portion.

What Does Lamentations 3:24 Mean
The LORD

• Scripture names Him “the LORD”—the covenant-keeping God who never changes (Exodus 3:14: “I AM WHO I AM”).

• Jeremiah centers the entire verse on God’s character, not on circumstances. When the prophet earlier said, “Surely my soul remembers and is humbled within me” (Lamentations 3:20), he moves now to the unfailing One.

• Psalm 18:2 echoes the same confidence: “The LORD is my rock, my fortress, and my deliverer.”

is my portion

• “Portion” speaks of inheritance and sufficiency; the Levites heard the same assurance: “You shall have no inheritance in the land, nor shall you have any portion among them; I am your portion” (Numbers 18:20).

• When physical assets crumble, the believer still owns the richest treasure—God Himself.

• Additional echoes:

– Psalm 16:5: “The LORD is my chosen portion and my cup.”

– Psalm 73:26: “God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.”

says my soul

• This is more than an outward motto; it is an inward declaration.

• Jeremiah’s soul, bruised by Jerusalem’s fall, still testifies. Like the psalmist who talks to himself—“Why are you downcast, O my soul?… Put your hope in God” (Psalm 42:11)—the prophet speaks faith to his own heart.

• Genuine assurance must travel from head to heart; the soul must own it personally.

therefore I will hope

• Hope is the logical outcome when the LORD is the portion; anything less would contradict His nature.

• Jeremiah’s choice: “I WILL hope”—a deliberate act of trust, even before relief arrives.

• Scripture reinforces this pattern:

– Jeremiah 17:7: “Blessed is the man who trusts in the LORD, whose confidence is in Him.”

– Romans 5:5: “Hope does not disappoint us, because God’s love has been poured into our hearts.”

in Him

• The preposition matters. Hope is not in plans, people, or possessions but “in Him.”

• Hebrews 6:19 pictures the outcome: “We have this hope as an anchor for the soul, firm and steadfast.”

• Because the Lord Himself is the anchor, hope holds even when waves crash.

summary

Jeremiah strings together five truths: the LORD—unchanging covenant God—is all the inheritance we need; the soul must claim this personally; therefore hope becomes a settled choice, fixed in Him alone. When we make the same confession, we trade fragile earthly securities for an anchor that never fails.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) The Lord.--An inversion of the sentence gives a closer and more emphatic rendering: My portion is Jehovah. The phrase is a reminiscence from Psalm 16:5; Psalm 73:26; Psalm 142:5; Psalm 119:57, the thought resting primarily on Numbers 18:20.
