Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Greet Apelles, who is approved in Christ.
Apelles is mentioned only here in the New Testament, and his name suggests a Greek origin. The phrase "approved in Christ" indicates that Apelles has been tested and found faithful, possibly through trials or persecution. This approval echoes the concept found in James 1:12, where those who persevere under trial receive the crown of life. The commendation "approved in Christ" suggests a deep, personal relationship with Jesus, aligning with the idea of being refined and proven genuine, as seen in 1 Peter 1:7. Apelles' faithfulness serves as a model for believers, emphasizing the importance of steadfastness in the Christian walk.

Greet those who belong to the household of Aristobulus.
The mention of "the household of Aristobulus" rather than Aristobulus himself may imply that Aristobulus was not a believer or was deceased. Aristobulus is a common name, but some scholars suggest he could be connected to the Herodian dynasty, possibly a grandson of Herod the Great. If so, his household might have included both Jews and Gentiles, reflecting the diverse nature of the early church. The greeting to his household indicates that there were believers within it, highlighting the spread of Christianity across different social and familial structures. This aligns with Acts 16:31, which speaks of entire households coming to faith. The inclusion of Aristobulus' household underscores the communal aspect of the early church, where faith often spread through familial and social networks.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Apelles
A Christian in Rome who is commended by Paul as "approved in Christ." The term "approved" (Greek: dokimos) suggests someone who has been tested and found faithful.

2. Household of Aristobulus
Likely refers to the family or servants of Aristobulus, who may have been a prominent figure in Rome. The mention of his household rather than Aristobulus himself might imply that Aristobulus was not a believer, or perhaps he was deceased.
Teaching Points
Approval in Christ
Being "approved in Christ" involves enduring trials and remaining faithful. It is a mark of spiritual maturity and integrity.

The Importance of Community
The mention of the household of Aristobulus highlights the significance of community and family in the early church. Even if the head of the household was not a believer, the faith of the household members was acknowledged.

Recognition and Encouragement
Paul’s greetings serve as a reminder of the importance of recognizing and encouraging fellow believers. Acknowledging others' faithfulness can strengthen the community.

Faithfulness in Testing
Like Apelles, believers today are called to remain steadfast in their faith, especially during trials, to be found approved by God.

What Does Romans 16:10 Mean
Greet Apelles

Paul writes, “Greet Apelles…” (Romans 16:10).

• Paul names Apelles personally, signaling a known, faithful believer whose testimony mattered to the Roman church.

• Individual greetings highlight how the Lord values each member of the body (cf. John 10:3, “He calls His own sheep by name”).

• By instructing the church to greet him, Paul models tangible love and unity (cf. 1 Peter 5:14, “Greet one another with a kiss of love”).

• The verse reminds us to recognize and affirm brothers and sisters who serve quietly yet faithfully, just as Apelles apparently had done.

who is approved in Christ

“…who is approved in Christ.”

• “Approved” points to tested and proven character (cf. James 1:12, “Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has withstood the test, he will receive the crown of life”).

• The approval comes “in Christ,” underscoring that any commendation rests on union with Jesus, not personal merit (cf. Galatians 2:20).

• Apelles’ trials likely refined his faith; Paul publicly acknowledges God’s stamp of approval on that endurance (cf. 2 Timothy 2:15, “Present yourself approved to God”).

• This encourages believers facing hardship: faithfulness under pressure is noticed by God and should be celebrated by the church.

Greet those who belong to the household of Aristobulus

“Greet those who belong to the household of Aristobulus.”

• Paul now greets a group rather than an individual, showing that whole households can become centers of gospel influence (cf. Acts 16:15, 34; 1 Corinthians 1:16).

• Some members of Aristobulus’s household are evidently believers, even if the head himself may not be; the gospel penetrates every social layer (cf. Philippians 4:22, “All the saints greet you, especially those from Caesar’s household”).

• Recognizing believers within a larger secular context encourages courage and witness for those living out their faith in challenging environments.

• This greeting also underscores the early church’s diversity—Jews, Gentiles, freedmen, and slaves worshiping side by side (cf. Colossians 3:11).

summary

Romans 16:10 spotlights Apelles, proven through trials, and the faithful within Aristobulus’s household. Paul’s greetings teach us to honor steadfast believers, to see testing as God’s pathway to approval, and to remember that the gospel flourishes in individuals and households alike.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) Apelles.--This name is also found among the dependents of the emperor. Horace, in the well-known phrase, "Credat Judaeas Apella" (Ep. 1, v. 100) takes it as a typical Jewish name.

Approved in Christ.--Whose fidelity to Christ has been tried, and has stood the test.

Aristobulus' household.--Aristobulus, a grandson of Herod the Great, was educated and lived in a private station at Rome. From the friendly terms on which he stood with the Emperor Claudius, it seems not unlikely that, by a somewhat common custom, his household may have been transferred to the emperor at his death. In that case, his slaves would be designated by a term such as we find in the Greek.
