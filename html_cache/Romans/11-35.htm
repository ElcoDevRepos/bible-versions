Who has first given to God
This phrase emphasizes the sovereignty and self-sufficiency of God. It reflects the biblical truth that God is the Creator and Sustainer of all things, and therefore, He is not dependent on anything outside Himself. This echoes Job 41:11, where God declares His independence and ownership over all creation. The rhetorical question implies that no one can give to God in a way that obligates Him, highlighting His grace and generosity. In the cultural context of the time, gods were often seen as needing offerings to be appeased, but the God of Israel is portrayed as entirely self-sufficient.

that God should repay him?
This part of the verse underscores the concept of grace, which is central to Christian theology. It suggests that human beings cannot place God in their debt through their actions or offerings. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative that salvation and blessings from God are not earned but are gifts of grace (Ephesians 2:8-9). The idea of God repaying someone implies a transactional relationship, which is contrary to the nature of God's covenant with humanity. This also connects to the teachings of Jesus, who emphasized that God's kingdom operates on principles of grace and mercy rather than human merit.

3. God
The central figure in this verse, highlighting His sovereignty and the fact that He is not indebted to anyone.

4. The Jewish People
Throughout Romans 11, Paul discusses the role of Israel in God's salvation plan, emphasizing that God's gifts and calling are irrevocable.

5. Gentiles
Non-Jewish believers who are being grafted into the spiritual blessings of Israel, as discussed in the broader context of Romans 11.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Ownership
Recognize that God is the Creator and Owner of all things. We cannot give to God in a way that places Him in our debt.

Grace and Humility
Understand that our relationship with God is based on His grace, not our merit. This should lead us to humility and gratitude.

Dependence on God
Acknowledge our complete dependence on God for everything we have. This should inspire trust and reliance on His provision.

Generosity as a Response
Our giving should be a response to God's generosity, not an attempt to earn His favor. We give because He first gave to us.

Worship and Praise
Let this truth lead us to worship and praise God for His unsearchable wisdom and riches, as Paul does in the doxology of Romans 11.

What Does Romans 11:35 Mean
Who has first given to God

- Paul echoes Job 41:11 — “Who has given to Me that I should repay him?”—reminding us that every created thing already belongs to the Lord (Psalm 24:1).

- Since God is the Creator (Genesis 1:1) and sustainer of all life (Colossians 1:16-17), no creature can ever place Him in a position of obligation.

- Psalm 50:10-12 tells us He owns “every beast of the forest… the cattle on a thousand hills,” underscoring that even our finest gifts originate in His prior generosity.

- Acts 17:24-25 reinforces this truth: “He is not served by human hands, as if He needed anything.”

- Therefore, any act of worship, service, or giving on our part is always a response to grace already received (1 Chronicles 29:14).

that God should repay him?

- The phrase drives home God’s absolute independence: if no one can give to Him first, no one can rightfully expect repayment on human terms.

- Grace is by definition undeserved (Ephesians 2:8-9). Salvation, blessings, and spiritual gifts flow from God’s mercy, not from a debt He owes (Titus 3:5-7).

- James 1:17 reminds us, “Every good and perfect gift is from above,” highlighting that God remains the sole Source, never the debtor.

- This truth eliminates boasting (Romans 3:27) and fuels humble gratitude, leading believers to serve not for reward but out of love and awe (2 Corinthians 9:15).

summary

Romans 11:35 teaches that God is never indebted to anyone. Because He already owns and supplies all things, any good we experience is a gift of grace, not payment for services rendered. This reality humbles pride, silences boasting, and inspires wholehearted worship of the One from whom, through whom, and to whom are all things (Romans 11:36).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) The depth of God's knowledge none can penetrate, and the counsels of His wisdom admit of no assessor. The means by which God works are not supplied to Him from without, but proceed from the boundless stores of His omnipotence.
