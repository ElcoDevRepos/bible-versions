Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
in purity
Purity in this context refers to moral and spiritual cleanliness. Paul emphasizes living a life free from sin and corruption, aligning with the call to holiness found throughout Scripture (1 Peter 1:16). Purity is essential for maintaining a close relationship with God and is a reflection of the believer's transformation through Christ (2 Corinthians 5:17). The call to purity is also a call to be set apart from the world, echoing the Old Testament requirements for the Israelites to be holy as God is holy (Leviticus 11:44).

knowledge
Knowledge here refers to a deep understanding of God's truth and His will, as revealed through Scripture. This is not merely intellectual knowledge but a spiritual insight that comes from the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 2:10-12). Paul often prays for believers to grow in knowledge and discernment (Philippians 1:9-10), emphasizing that true knowledge leads to a life that pleases God. This knowledge is rooted in the fear of the Lord, which is the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 9:10).

patience
Patience is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22) and is crucial for enduring trials and tribulations. Paul himself faced numerous hardships (2 Corinthians 11:23-28), and his call to patience reflects the endurance required to remain faithful amidst suffering. Patience is also linked to hope, as believers wait for the fulfillment of God's promises (Romans 8:25). It is a characteristic of God's nature, as He is patient with humanity, not wanting anyone to perish (2 Peter 3:9).

and kindness
Kindness is another fruit of the Spirit and is demonstrated through acts of compassion and mercy. It reflects God's own kindness towards humanity, as seen in His grace and forgiveness (Ephesians 4:32). In the cultural context of Corinth, where social divisions and conflicts were prevalent, kindness would have been a powerful testimony of the transformative power of the Gospel. Believers are called to imitate Christ's kindness, which is evident in His interactions with others (Luke 6:35).

in the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit is the source of power and guidance for the believer's life. Paul emphasizes the role of the Spirit in enabling believers to live out these virtues. The Holy Spirit indwells believers (1 Corinthians 6:19) and empowers them to bear fruit that reflects Christ's character. The Spirit also provides wisdom and discernment, helping believers navigate the challenges of life and ministry (John 14:26).

and in sincere love
Sincere love is genuine and without hypocrisy. It is the greatest commandment, as Jesus taught (Matthew 22:37-39), and is the hallmark of true discipleship (John 13:35). This love is selfless and sacrificial, modeled after Christ's love for the church (Ephesians 5:25). In the context of the Corinthian church, where divisions and conflicts were common, sincere love was essential for unity and effective ministry. It is a love that seeks the best for others and is patient and kind (1 Corinthians 13:4-7).

Teaching Points
Pursuing Purity
As believers, we are called to live lives of purity, both in our actions and thoughts. This involves a conscious effort to avoid sin and seek holiness.

Growing in Knowledge
Knowledge of God and His Word is essential for spiritual growth. We should be diligent in studying the Scriptures and seeking wisdom from the Holy Spirit.

Exercising Patience
Patience is a virtue that reflects our trust in God's timing and sovereignty. We should practice patience in our interactions with others and in our personal spiritual journey.

Demonstrating Kindness
Kindness is an outward expression of the love of Christ within us. We are called to show kindness to all, reflecting God's grace and mercy.

Living in the Spirit
The Holy Spirit empowers us to live out these virtues. We must rely on His guidance and strength to exhibit sincere love and other godly attributes.

What Does 2 Corinthians 6:6 Mean
purity

“in purity” (2 Corinthians 6:6)

•Paul is reminding believers that gospel ministry stands or falls on moral integrity. Like clean vessels (2 Timothy 2:21), our lives must match our message.

•Purity begins in the heart (Matthew 5:8) and flows outward to actions (1 Peter 1:15–16).

•It protects fellowship with God (Psalm 24:3–4) and guards the church’s witness (Philippians 2:15).

•Practical steps: flee what defiles (1 Timothy 5:22), fill the mind with what is true (Philippians 4:8), and walk transparently with trusted believers (James 5:16).

knowledge

“in … knowledge”

•This is not mere information but a growing, personal grasp of God’s truth (Colossians 1:9–10).

•Scripture supplies that knowledge (Psalm 119:99), and the Spirit illuminates it (John 16:13).

•It equips us to refute error (Titus 1:9) and to guide others toward Christ (2 Peter 3:18).

•Cultivated through daily reading, meditation, and obedient application (Joshua 1:8).

patience

“in … patience”

•The term pictures long-suffering endurance when ministry is slow or opposition is fierce (James 1:2–4).

•God models this patience toward us (2 Peter 3:9); we extend it to others (Ephesians 4:2).

•It is forged through trials (Romans 5:3–4) and fueled by hope that God’s timing is perfect (Hebrews 10:36).

•Practical habits: pause before reacting, pray for those who test you, remember eternity.

kindness

“and kindness”

•Love in action—gentle, considerate deeds that mirror the Lord’s tenderness (Luke 6:35).

•God’s kindness led us to repentance (Romans 2:4), so ours should point others to Him.

•Part of the believer’s wardrobe (Colossians 3:12) and a powerful testimony in a harsh world (Ephesians 4:32).

•Expressed through encouraging words, generous help, and forgiving hearts.

the Holy Spirit

“in the Holy Spirit”

•All the previous qualities are impossible without Him (Galatians 5:22–23).

•He indwells every believer (Romans 8:9), empowers ministry (Acts 1:8), and seals our adoption (Ephesians 1:13–14).

•Walking by the Spirit means daily surrender to His leading (Romans 8:14) and active resistance to the flesh (Galatians 5:16).

•The Spirit supplies discernment, boldness, and supernatural love that natural effort cannot produce.

sincere love

“and in sincere love”

•Authentic, unhypocritical affection (Romans 12:9) that seeks another’s highest good.

•Rooted in God’s own love poured into our hearts by the Spirit (Romans 5:5).

•Seen in sacrificial service (1 John 3:18), honest speech (Ephesians 4:15), and steadfast commitment (1 Corinthians 13:4–7).

•Binds all virtues together in perfect unity (Colossians 3:14), proving we are Christ’s disciples (John 13:35).

summary

2 Corinthians 6:6 sketches a portrait of credible Christian ministry: morally clean, saturated with truth, enduring under pressure, gentle toward people, Spirit-empowered, and driven by unfeigned love. These traits are not optional extras; they are the Spirit’s standard equipment for every believer who wants to represent Christ faithfully in a watching world.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) By pureness . . .--The word may possibly mean "purity of motive" in its widest sense, but the use of the corresponding adjective in 2Corinthians 11:2; 1Timothy 5:22; Titus 2:3; 1Peter 3:2, and, indeed, its general sense elsewhere, is decisive in favour of "purity from sensual sin"--personal chastity. In the general state of morals throughout the empire, and especially in writing to such a city as Corinth, it was natural to dwell on this aspect of the Christian character. (Comp. 1Corinthians 7:7.) The "knowledge" is obviously not that of earthly things, but of the mysteries of God (Ephesians 3:4). In "kindness" we trace the consciousness of an effort to reproduce the graciousness which he looked on as a characteristic attribute of God and Christ (Ephesians 2:7; Titus 3:4). In the "Holy Ghost" we may see a reference both to spiritual gifts, such as those of tongues and prophecy (1Corinthians 14:18-19), and to the impulses and promptings in which he traced the general guidance of the Spirit (Acts 16:6-7). "Love unfeigned" (i.e., without hypocrisy) presents the same combination as in Romans 12:9 ("without dissimulation" in the English version). . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - By pureness; rather, in pureness, as the preposition is the same. He now gives six instances of special gifts and virtues. The "pureness" is not only "chastity," but absolute sincerity (1 John 3:3; 2 Corinthians 4:2; 1 Thessalonians 2:10). By knowledge. The knowledge is the true knowledge of the gospel in its fulness (Ephesians 3:4). In his depth of insight into the truth St. Paul was specially gifted. The word gnosis had not yet acquired the fatal connotations which afterwards discredited it. By long suffering (2 Timothy 3:10; 2 Timothy 4:2). The patient endurance of insults, of which St. Paul shows a practical specimen in this Epistle, and still more in Philippians 1:15-18. By kindness. "Love suffereth long, and is kind" (1 Corinthians 13:4); "Long suffering, kindness" (Galatians 5:22). By the Holy Ghost. To the special gift of the Spirit St. Paul attributed all his success (1 Thessalonians 1:5; Romans 15:18, 19). By love unfeigned; which is the surest fruit of the Spirit, and the best of all spiritual gifts (2 Corinthians 12:15; 1 Corinthians 8:1; 1 Corinthians 13; Romans 12:9, etc.).
