Therefore come out from among them and be separate, says the Lord.
This phrase calls for a distinct separation from the practices and influences of the world that are contrary to God's holiness. The context of 2 Corinthians 6 involves Paul addressing the Corinthian church, urging them to avoid being unequally yoked with unbelievers. This echoes the Old Testament command to Israel to remain distinct from the pagan nations around them (Isaiah 52:11). The call to separation is not about physical withdrawal but a spiritual and moral distinction. The phrase "says the Lord" emphasizes divine authority, reminding believers that this command is not merely apostolic advice but a directive from God Himself. The concept of separation is also seen in the life of Jesus, who, while engaging with sinners, remained untainted by sin (Hebrews 7:26).

Touch no unclean thing,
This part of the verse refers to the Old Testament laws concerning ritual purity, where touching unclean things would make one ceremonially unclean (Leviticus 11). In the New Testament context, it symbolizes avoiding sin and anything that defiles the believer's spiritual purity. The idea of cleanliness is deeply rooted in Jewish tradition, where physical cleanliness often represented spiritual purity. For Christians, this extends to avoiding moral and spiritual contamination. The call to avoid "unclean things" is a reminder of the need for holiness, as God is holy (1 Peter 1:16). It also reflects the separation from idolatry and pagan practices prevalent in Corinth, a city known for its diverse religious practices.

and I will receive you.
This promise of acceptance by God is conditional upon obedience to His command to separate from unclean things. It reflects the covenant relationship God desires with His people, where obedience leads to blessing and fellowship. The assurance "I will receive you" is reminiscent of God's promises to Israel, where obedience led to His presence and favor (Exodus 19:5-6). In the New Testament, this reception is through Christ, who reconciles believers to God (2 Corinthians 5:18-19). The phrase underscores the relational aspect of faith, where separation from sin leads to a closer relationship with God. It also points to the ultimate reception into God's eternal kingdom, where purity and holiness are prerequisites (Revelation 21:27).

The recipients of Paul's letter, struggling with issues of idolatry, immorality, and integration with pagan practices.

Symbolic of sin, idolatry, and anything that defiles a believer's relationship with God.
Teaching Points
Call to Holiness
Believers are called to live distinct lives, set apart from the world's sinful practices, reflecting God's holiness.

Separation from Sin
Christians must actively avoid and reject anything that compromises their spiritual integrity and relationship with God.

God's Promise of Acceptance
When believers separate themselves from sin, God promises to receive them, indicating a restored and intimate relationship.

Cultural Discernment
In a world filled with moral and spiritual compromise, Christians must discern and reject influences that lead away from God's standards.

Active Obedience
The call to "come out" requires intentional action and commitment to live according to God's will, not merely passive avoidance.

What Does 2 Corinthians 6:17 Mean
Therefore

Paul has just argued that “we are the temple of the living God” (2 Corinthians 6:16), so a response is mandatory.

• “Therefore” links the command to the truth that God now indwells His people, just as Romans 12:1 urges believers to act “in view of God’s mercy.”

• The same pattern appears in 1 Peter 2:9–11—identity first, then conduct.

The verse opens by reminding us that obedience flows from what God has already done.

come out from among them

The Holy Spirit echoes Isaiah 52:11: “Depart, depart, go out from there….”

• “Them” refers to the unbelieving world (2 Corinthians 6:14), those tied to idolatry and darkness.

• Revelation 18:4 repeats the call: “Come out of her, My people, lest you share in her sins.”

• Abraham’s obedience in Genesis 12:1 models this separation—leaving the familiar to follow God.

Leaving is more than relocation; it is a decisive break with systems and relationships that oppose Christ.

and be separate

Separation is not isolation but distinction.

• Leviticus 20:24, 26: God set Israel apart to display His holiness.

• 1 Peter 1:15–16 echoes the same command for believers: “Be holy, for I am holy.”

Practical outworking:

– Different values (Philippians 4:8)

– Different speech (Ephesians 4:29)

– Different priorities (Matthew 6:33)

says the Lord

The authority behind the command is God Himself.

• “The word of our God stands forever” (Isaiah 40:8).

• Jesus affirmed, “Scripture cannot be broken” (John 10:35).

Because the speaker is the sovereign Lord, obedience is not optional or negotiable.

Touch no unclean thing

“Touch” pictures personal involvement and approval.

• Under the Law, contact with the unclean brought defilement (Leviticus 5:2).

• Isaiah 52:11 couples separation with purity: “Touch no unclean thing.”

• Ephesians 5:11 tells believers to “have no fellowship with the fruitless deeds of darkness.”

Modern parallels include entertainment, business practices, and relationships that entice us to sin.

and I will receive you

God’s promise crowns the command.

• “Draw near to God and He will draw near to you” (James 4:8).

• Jesus said, “If anyone loves Me…My Father will love him, and We will come to him” (John 14:23).

• Hebrews 4:16 invites believers to “approach the throne of grace with confidence.”

The Father’s open arms motivate holiness; separation deepens fellowship rather than earning it.

summary

2 Corinthians 6:17 calls believers to decisive, practical holiness grounded in their identity as God’s dwelling place. Because the Lord Himself commands it, we leave compromising alliances, live distinctly, refuse contaminating influences, and enjoy the welcome of our Heavenly Father.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) Wherefore come out from among them.--Another composite quotation follows, beginning with Isaiah 52:11. In their primary historical sense, the words were addressed as to the priests and Levites who were to return from Babylon. They were not to bring back with them any symbol of that "unclean" ritual which they had witnessed there. The local and historical meaning has for the Apostle passed away, and the "unclean thing" is identified with the whole system of heathenism. The close connection of this verse with the great prophecy of the atoning work makes it probable that, in writing of that work, St. Paul had remembered, or, perhaps, actually turned to Isaiah 53, as it stood in the LXX. version, and so was led on to the verse which almost immediately preceded it. "I will receive you" comes, in lieu of the ending of Isaiah, from the Greek of Ezekiel 11:17; Jeremiah 24:5.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - From among them; i.e. from among the unbelievers. Touch not the unclean thing (Leviticus 11:8, etc.; Isaiah 52:11). I will receive you (comp. Ezekiel 20:34). These promises to Israel are naturally transferred to the ideal Israel, the Christian Church.
