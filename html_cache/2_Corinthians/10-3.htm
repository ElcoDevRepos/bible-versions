For though we live in the flesh
This phrase acknowledges the human condition of living in a physical body. In biblical context, "flesh" often refers to human nature and its limitations. The Apostle Paul, who authored 2 Corinthians, frequently contrasts the flesh with the spirit, emphasizing the struggle between earthly desires and spiritual aspirations (Galatians 5:16-17). The acknowledgment of living in the flesh also reflects the reality of human existence, subject to weakness and mortality (Romans 7:18).

we do not wage war according to the flesh
Here, Paul introduces the concept of spiritual warfare, indicating that the battles Christians face are not fought with physical weapons or human strategies. This aligns with Ephesians 6:12, where Paul explains that the struggle is not against flesh and blood but against spiritual forces. The phrase suggests a reliance on divine power rather than human strength, echoing the Old Testament narrative of David and Goliath, where David's victory was attributed to God's intervention rather than physical might (1 Samuel 17:45-47). This perspective encourages believers to seek spiritual solutions through prayer, faith, and the Word of God, rather than relying on worldly methods.

Teaching Points
Understanding Spiritual Warfare
The Christian life involves a spiritual battle, not a physical one. Recognize that our true enemies are spiritual forces, not people or circumstances.

Living in the Flesh vs. Waging War in the Spirit
While we live in a physical world, our methods and strategies must be spiritual. This requires reliance on God's power and wisdom rather than human strength.

The Role of Faith and Prayer
Engage in spiritual warfare through faith and prayer. These are our primary weapons against spiritual opposition.

Discernment and Wisdom
Seek God's wisdom to discern the spiritual nature of challenges and respond appropriately. This involves understanding the difference between fleshly and spiritual responses.

The Importance of Spiritual Armor
Equip yourself with the full armor of God, as described in Ephesians 6, to stand firm against spiritual attacks.

What Does 2 Corinthians 10:3 Mean
For though we live in the flesh

Paul starts by acknowledging the obvious: we inhabit real, mortal bodies and navigate daily routines that feel very earthy—work, meals, relationships, traffic. Scripture never minimizes this reality. Jesus Himself shared it (John 1:14).

• Living in the flesh reminds us of human limitations (Psalm 103:14) and the pull of sinful desires (Galatians 5:17).

• Yet our core identity has shifted. “I no longer live, but Christ lives in me” (Galatians 2:20).

• We remain in the world but are not defined by it, echoing Jesus’ prayer, “They are not of the world, just as I am not of the world” (John 17:16).

Recognizing where we physically stand prepares us for how we spiritually stand.

we do not wage war

The Christian life involves conflict. Paul uses military language to jolt us awake: faith is not a spectator sport.

• Throughout his letters he urges, “Fight the good fight of the faith” (1 Timothy 6:12) and “Run in such a way as to take the prize” (1 Corinthians 9:24).

• The battle is ongoing; no believer is exempt (1 Peter 5:8–9).

• Because the struggle is real, God supplies full armor (Ephesians 6:11).

Knowing we are at war prevents complacency and pushes us toward continual dependence on Christ.

according to the flesh

Here’s the contrast: while we live in fleshly bodies, we refuse fleshly tactics.

• Worldly warfare relies on persuasive personalities, intimidation, or manipulative marketing. Paul rejects all of it. “The weapons of our warfare are not the weapons of the world” (2 Corinthians 10:4).

• Instead, we depend on divine power—truth, righteousness, faith, salvation, Scripture, and prayer, the very items listed in Ephesians 6:14-18.

• Victory comes “not by might nor by power, but by My Spirit,” says the LORD (Zechariah 4:6).

• Walking by the Spirit produces fruit (Galatians 5:22-23) that no earthly strategy can manufacture.

So the verse calls us to renounce self-reliance and embrace Spirit-reliance in every skirmish, whether battling personal temptation or confronting spiritual strongholds around us.

summary

2 Corinthians 10:3 reminds believers that while we still move in physical bodies within a very physical world, our real battles are spiritual and must be fought with God-provided, Spirit-empowered weapons. Living in the flesh is unavoidable; fighting in the flesh is optional—and disastrous. Victory belongs to those who rely on the Lord’s strength, armor, and methods rather than their own.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) For though we walk in the flesh.--The phrase is generally used by St. Paul for the simple fact of bodily existence, with all its incidental infirmities and trials, but, commonly, without implying sin, as "after the flesh" does (Galatians 2:20; Philippians 1:22-24; 1Timothy 3:16). The thought of participating in the sin of which the body is the occasion is, however, very close to that of sharing its weakness; and the phrase appears with this sense in Romans 8:8-9.

We do not war after the flesh.--Strictly, we are not carrying on our campaign. See Note on Luke 3:14, where the same word is used. As so often in St. Paul's style, the word--especially any word like this, connected with the soldier's life--becomes the germ of an elaborate figurative imagery, almost of a parable. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - We walk in the flesh. St. Paul does not disclaim the possession of human infirmities, but maintains that such trials and temptations were not the guiding force of his life. We do not war after the flesh. His campaigns (Luke 3:14) were fought with spiritual weapons. The metaphor is a constant one with St. Paul (2 Corinthians 2:14-16; 1 Corinthians 9:26; Ephesians 6:10-17, etc.).
