You will sow much seed in the field
This phrase reflects the agrarian society of ancient Israel, where agriculture was a primary means of sustenance and economic stability. Sowing seed in abundance indicates a significant investment of labor and resources, expecting a proportional return. In the biblical context, sowing is often used metaphorically to represent actions and their consequences (Galatians 6:7-8). The act of sowing much seed suggests diligence and hope for a fruitful harvest, which is a common theme in Scripture, symbolizing spiritual and material blessings (Psalm 126:5-6).

but harvest little
The expectation of a bountiful harvest is thwarted, highlighting the futility of human effort without divine blessing. This outcome serves as a warning of the consequences of disobedience to God's commandments, as outlined in the covenantal blessings and curses in Deuteronomy. The concept of harvesting little despite much effort is echoed in Haggai 1:6, where the people experience scarcity due to neglecting God's house. This phrase underscores the principle that true prosperity is contingent upon faithfulness to God.

because the locusts will consume it
Locusts are a recurring symbol of divine judgment and devastation in the Bible (Joel 1:4, Exodus 10:12-15). They represent natural disasters that can decimate crops, leading to famine and economic hardship. The mention of locusts consuming the harvest serves as a tangible expression of the curses for disobedience, emphasizing the direct impact of sin on the land and its produce. In a broader theological context, locusts can be seen as instruments of God's discipline, calling His people to repentance and reliance on Him for restoration and protection.

Teaching Points
The Consequences of Disobedience
Deuteronomy 28:38 serves as a stark reminder of the consequences of turning away from God's commandments. The Israelites are warned that their efforts will be in vain if they do not remain faithful to God.

The Symbolism of Locusts
Locusts are a powerful symbol of destruction and judgment. They remind us of the fragility of human efforts without God's blessing and protection.

The Principle of Sowing and Reaping
This verse illustrates the broader biblical principle that what we sow, we will reap. It challenges us to consider the spiritual seeds we are planting in our lives.

Dependence on God for Provision
The futility of the Israelites' labor without God's blessing underscores our need to rely on God for provision and success in all our endeavors.

The Importance of Covenant Faithfulness
The verse highlights the importance of remaining faithful to God's covenant. It calls us to examine our own faithfulness to God's commands and promises.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:38 Mean
context

Deuteronomy 28 records the covenant blessings for obedience (vv. 1-14) and curses for disobedience (vv. 15-68). Verse 38 belongs to the second list, underscoring a literal consequence Israel would face if it ignored God’s commands (cf. Deuteronomy 28:15; Leviticus 26:14-20). The land—normally God’s good gift (Genesis 8:22; Psalm 65:9-13)—would stop cooperating, turning abundant labor into futility.

sow much seed

“You will sow much seed in the field…”

• Israel would still plow, plant, and invest considerable effort (Isaiah 28:24-26).

• The wording assumes normal agricultural diligence; the problem would not be laziness (Proverbs 20:4) but spiritual rebellion (Hosea 10:12-13).

• God’s covenant expected Israel to work the land faithfully (Deuteronomy 11:13-15). Here He warns that even wholehearted labor cannot outpace divine discipline.

but harvest little

“…but harvest little…”

• The promised yield would collapse (Haggai 1:6); barns that should be full (Proverbs 3:9-10) would echo empty.

• This reverses earlier covenant blessings such as Deuteronomy 7:13 where God pledged, “He will bless the fruit of your womb and the produce of your land.”

• The lesson: material results ultimately hinge on obedience, not merely on industry (Psalm 127:1-2).

because the locusts will consume it

“…because the locusts will consume it.”

• Locusts are a familiar instrument of God’s judgment (Exodus 10:12-15; Joel 1:4; Amos 4:9).

• Their arrival is sudden, overwhelming, and uncontrollable, illustrating the inescapable nature of divine chastening (2 Chronicles 7:13-14).

• Natural forces that normally serve human flourishing bow to the Creator’s purposes, reminding Israel—and us—that the LORD “does whatever He pleases” (Psalm 135:6).

application

• Hard work is necessary, yet futile apart from faithful submission to God (Luke 12:16-21; James 4:13-16).

• National or personal prosperity cannot be secured by technique alone; righteousness remains the decisive factor (Proverbs 14:34).

• Believers today should heed the warning: habitual disobedience invites God’s loving but severe discipline (Hebrews 12:5-11; Galatians 6:7-8).

summary

Deuteronomy 28:38 teaches that when God’s people persist in disobedience, He can turn even their greatest efforts into disappointment. Abundant sowing cannot guarantee a bountiful harvest if the LORD sends locusts. The verse calls believers to wholehearted obedience, trusting that true fruitfulness flows from walking in covenant faithfulness with God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(38-42) These are the contrary to Deuteronomy 28:11; and Deuteronomy 28:44 is the contrary to Deuteronomy 28:12-13. From the order of the passage it might seem that these particular troubles were to come on Israel after their captivity. And perhaps it is not accidental that something very like a fulfilment of Deuteronomy 28:38-40 is found in Haggai 1:6-11. (Comp. also Isaiah 5:10, "Ten acres of vineyard shall yield one bath, and the seed of an homer shall yield an ephah.") . . .
