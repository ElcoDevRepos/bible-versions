Cursed is he who sleeps with his sister, the daughter of his father or the daughter of his mother.
This phrase addresses a specific prohibition against incestuous relationships, which were strictly forbidden in the Mosaic Law. The curse here is a pronouncement of divine disfavor and judgment upon those who violate this command. In the ancient Near Eastern context, such relationships were considered taboo and were often associated with pagan practices. The law serves to maintain the sanctity and purity of the family unit, which is foundational to Israelite society. This command is reiterated in Leviticus 18:9 and 20:17, emphasizing its importance. The prohibition reflects God's desire for moral purity and the protection of familial relationships. The mention of both "the daughter of his father" and "the daughter of his mother" ensures that all potential incestuous relationships are covered, regardless of whether the siblings share both parents or just one.

And let all the people say, ‘Amen!’
The response of "Amen" by the people signifies their agreement and acceptance of the law. It is a communal affirmation of the covenant between God and Israel, indicating that the people collectively endorse the curse and the moral standards it upholds. This practice of communal affirmation is seen throughout Deuteronomy 27, where the Israelites are called to confirm their commitment to God's laws. The use of "Amen" is a solemn acknowledgment of the truth and seriousness of the command, and it binds the community to uphold these standards. This practice of public affirmation can be seen as a precursor to the New Testament's emphasis on the church as a community of believers who hold each other accountable to God's standards.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Moses
The leader of the Israelites who delivered God's laws to the people. He is the central figure in the book of Deuteronomy, which is a series of speeches given to the Israelites before they enter the Promised Land.

2. The Israelites
The chosen people of God, who are receiving the laws and commandments as they prepare to enter Canaan, the land promised to them by God.

3. Mount Ebal and Mount Gerizim
The locations where the blessings and curses were to be pronounced. Mount Ebal was the mountain of curses, and Mount Gerizim was the mountain of blessings.

4. The Levites
The tribe of Israel responsible for the religious and priestly duties, including the pronouncement of blessings and curses.

5. Canaan
The Promised Land that the Israelites are about to enter, where they are to live according to God's laws.
Teaching Points
The Sanctity of Family Relationships
God's laws are designed to protect the integrity and sanctity of family relationships. Incestuous relationships violate the natural order and lead to familial and societal breakdown.

The Seriousness of Sin
The pronouncement of a curse underscores the seriousness with which God views sin. It serves as a warning to the community to uphold God's standards.

Community Responsibility
The communal response of "Amen" signifies collective agreement and responsibility. The community is called to uphold God's laws and hold each other accountable.

Holiness and Purity
God's people are called to live lives of holiness and purity, reflecting His character. This includes maintaining moral and ethical standards in all relationships.

The Role of the Law
The law serves as a guide to righteous living and reveals the need for God's grace. It points to the necessity of a Savior who fulfills the law on our behalf.

What Does Deuteronomy 27:22 Mean
Cursed is he

- The verse opens with a solemn declaration of divine judgment. A “curse” is the opposite of God’s blessing; it signals separation from His favor (Deuteronomy 28:15–20; Galatians 3:10).

- By placing this statement on Mount Ebal with the other covenant curses (Deuteronomy 27:13), the Lord publicly binds Israel to uphold His moral law.

- The curse is not merely social disapproval—God Himself enforces it. This underscores His holy character and His right to define right and wrong (Leviticus 19:2; Isaiah 5:20).

who sleeps with his sister

- The act described is sexual intercourse with one’s sister, a clear violation of God’s created order for family relationships (Leviticus 18:6–9; 20:17).

- Incest strikes at the heart of family integrity, which is designed to mirror God’s covenant faithfulness (Ephesians 5:25, 32).

- Scripture records tragic fallout when this line is crossed, such as Amnon’s sin against his sister Tamar and its devastating aftermath (2 Samuel 13:11–15, 28-29).

the daughter of his father or the daughter of his mother

- This phrase removes any loophole: whether the sister is full, half, or step, the prohibition stands (Genesis 20:12 highlights why this needed to be clarified).

- God intends marriage to exist outside the immediate family, preserving genetic health and social stability. Violating that boundary invites judgment (Leviticus 20:17, “They shall be cut off in the sight of their people”).

- The family is the fundamental training ground for love, respect, and protection. Turning it into a context for sexual gratification corrupts that purpose (1 Timothy 5:1-2).

And let all the people say, ‘Amen!’

- “Amen” means “so be it,” signaling entire-community agreement with God’s standard (Nehemiah 8:6; 1 Chronicles 16:36).

- Public assent makes each person accountable, reinforcing that righteousness is not optional but required (Romans 1:32, “they know God’s righteous decree”).

- By responding together, Israel affirms God’s authority and pledges mutual responsibility to uphold His commands (Hebrews 3:13 urges believers to exhort one another daily).

summary

Deuteronomy 27:22 warns that incest brings divine curse, not obscure cultural taboo. God protects the sanctity of family, demands holiness, and calls His people to agree with Him openly. Obedience secures blessing; defiance invites judgment—truths as relevant today as when Moses first spoke them.
