The LORD will bring you and the king you appoint
This phrase indicates a divine action where God is actively involved in the destiny of Israel. The mention of "the king you appoint" suggests a future time when Israel would have a monarchy, which was not yet established during Moses' time. This foreshadows the period of the kings, beginning with Saul, as seen in 1 Samuel 8. The people's desire for a king was a departure from God's original plan of direct theocracy, and this phrase warns of the consequences of such a choice.

to a nation neither you nor your fathers have known
This refers to exile, a punishment for disobedience. Historically, this prophecy was fulfilled when Israel was taken captive by Assyria and later Judah by Babylon. These nations were foreign and unfamiliar to the Israelites, emphasizing the severity of the judgment. The phrase underscores the loss of the Promised Land and the severing of the covenant blessings due to disobedience.

and there you will worship other gods—gods of wood and stone
This highlights the spiritual consequences of exile, where the Israelites would be surrounded by idolatry. The "gods of wood and stone" are lifeless idols, contrasting with the living God of Israel. This phrase serves as a warning against idolatry, a recurring sin in Israel's history, as seen in the worship of the golden calf (Exodus 32) and the high places (2 Kings 17:10-12). It also reflects the futility of idol worship, as these gods cannot save or deliver. Theologically, it points to the need for true worship of God, as emphasized by Jesus in John 4:24.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is both the giver of blessings and the enforcer of curses based on Israel's obedience or disobedience.

2. The King
Refers to the leader appointed by the Israelites, indicating a future time when Israel would have a monarchy.

3. A Nation Neither You Nor Your Fathers Have Known
This represents foreign nations, often seen as a place of exile and judgment for Israel's disobedience.

4. Gods of Wood and Stone
Idols worshipped by pagan nations, symbolizing false worship and the spiritual decline of Israel when they turn away from Yahweh.

5. Exile
The event of being taken away from the Promised Land, a consequence of Israel's disobedience and idolatry.
Teaching Points
The Seriousness of Disobedience
The verse highlights the severe consequences of turning away from God. It serves as a reminder of the importance of faithfulness to God's commandments.

The Danger of Idolatry
Worshipping "gods of wood and stone" symbolizes the futility and danger of idolatry. Believers are called to examine their lives for modern-day idols that may lead them away from God.

The Sovereignty of God
God's control over nations and kings is evident. He uses even foreign nations to accomplish His purposes, reminding us of His ultimate authority.

The Call to Repentance
The warning of exile serves as a call to repentance. Believers are encouraged to turn back to God and seek His forgiveness and restoration.

The Hope of Restoration
While the verse speaks of judgment, it also implies the possibility of return and restoration if the people repent, as seen in other parts of Scripture.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:36 Mean
The LORD will bring you

• God Himself initiates the judgment; exile is not random but the covenant consequence for unrepentant disobedience (Deuteronomy 28:15; 29:27; 2 Kings 17:18).

• His sovereign action shows that every word He speaks comes to pass (Numbers 23:19).

and the king you appoint

• Even the future monarch is swept up in the nation’s sin; leadership offers no immunity (Deuteronomy 17:14-20; 2 Kings 24:12-15).

• The phrase highlights corporate accountability: the people and their ruler rise or fall together (Hosea 10:3, 7).

to a nation neither you nor your fathers have known

• Captivity will be in a foreign, unfamiliar land (Jeremiah 5:15; 16:13), fulfilled in Assyria and Babylon.

• Removal from the promised land strips away covenant blessings and temple worship (Leviticus 26:33; Psalm 137:1-4).

and there you will worship other gods

• The people who once chased idols freely will face the bitter irony of enforced idolatry (Jeremiah 44:8, 17-18; Ezekiel 20:32).

• Physical bondage mirrors the spiritual bondage they had already chosen.

—gods of wood and stone

• Scripture mocks the lifelessness of idols (Psalm 115:4-8; Isaiah 44:9-20); exile will expose their impotence.

• The contrast between mute statues and the living LORD calls Israel—and us—to exclusive loyalty.

summary

Deuteronomy 28:36 foretells a literal exile executed by the LORD, sweeping away both people and king to an unknown land where empty idols reign. Historically fulfilled, the verse validates God’s Word, warns of the cost of covenant breach, and urges wholehearted obedience and worship of the one true, living God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) Thee, and thy king that thou shalt set over thee.--Comp. Deuteronomy 17:14. The former passage is not the only one in which Moses shows his fore knowledge that Israel would have a king. But could any later writer have concealed his knowledge that there were two kingdoms, or have avoided all allusion to the throne of David in passages like these?

Several kings went into captivity. Jehoahaz was taken to Egypt; Jeconiah and Zedekiah to Babylon. Hoshea's fate is not recorded in Scripture; but he was taken (apparently) with Samaria by the Assyrians.

Shalt thou serve other gods, wood and stone.--See Note on Deuteronomy 4:28.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 36, 37. - As a consequence, God would bring them under subjection to a foreign power, and they should be made to serve other gods, wood and stone (Deuteronomy 4:28), and would become an object of horror, a proverb, and a byword among the nations (cf. 1 Kings 9:7; Jeremiah 24:9). Yen. 38. - Even in their own land the curse would overtake them and rest upon them in all their interests and relations.
