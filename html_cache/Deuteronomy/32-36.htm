Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For the LORD will vindicate His people
This phrase emphasizes God's role as a just judge who will defend and uphold His people. In the context of Deuteronomy, this is a promise to Israel, reflecting God's covenant relationship with them. The term "vindicate" suggests a legal setting where God acts as an advocate for His people, ensuring justice is served. This is consistent with God's character as seen throughout the Old Testament, where He often intervenes on behalf of Israel against their enemies (e.g., Exodus 14:14). It also foreshadows the ultimate vindication through Christ, who intercedes for believers (Romans 8:34).

and have compassion on His servants
God's compassion is a recurring theme in Scripture, highlighting His mercy and love. The term "servants" refers to those who are faithful to Him, indicating a personal relationship. This compassion is not just emotional but leads to action, as seen in God's deliverance of Israel from Egypt (Exodus 3:7-8). It also points to the New Testament, where Jesus embodies God's compassion, healing the sick and forgiving sins (Matthew 9:36).

when He sees that their strength is gone
This phrase indicates a moment of complete human weakness and helplessness. It reflects the times when Israel was overwhelmed by enemies or internal strife, such as during the Babylonian exile. Biblically, God often acts when human strength fails, demonstrating His power and sovereignty (2 Corinthians 12:9). This reliance on divine strength is a key theme in both the Old and New Testaments.

and no one remains, slave or free.
This phrase underscores the totality of the situation, where all human resources are exhausted. The mention of "slave or free" indicates that God's concern and action are not limited by social status, reflecting the inclusive nature of His care. This is echoed in the New Testament, where Paul writes that in Christ, there is neither slave nor free (Galatians 3:28), emphasizing the universal reach of God's salvation and justice.

Teaching Points
God's Justice and Compassion
God is both just and compassionate. He will vindicate His people, showing mercy when they are at their weakest.

Dependence on God
Recognize that human strength is limited. True strength and deliverance come from God, especially when we are at our lowest.

God's Timing
Trust in God's perfect timing. He acts when He sees that our strength is gone, reminding us to rely on Him rather than our own abilities.

Servanthood and Obedience
As God's servants, we are called to live in obedience and trust, knowing that He will act on our behalf.

Hope in God's Promises
Hold onto the hope that God will fulfill His promises. His vindication and compassion are assured for those who remain faithful.

What Does Deuteronomy 32:36 Mean
For the LORD will vindicate His people

- “Vindicate” promises real, decisive action. As in Deuteronomy 32:41-43, the same LORD who judges the nations also “avenges the blood of His servants.”

- Psalm 135:14 echoes the line verbatim, showing that later worshippers relied on this assurance.

- Romans 12:19 and Hebrews 10:30 quote the surrounding verses (“Vengeance is Mine”), proving that God Himself reserves the right to set things right.

- Because the statement is future-oriented, it anchors hope: whatever injustice Israel faces, God personally steps in as their defender.

and have compassion on His servants

- The verse shifts from courtroom to family room; the Judge is also a Father. Psalm 103:13 says, “As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear Him.”

- Compassion here is not abstract pity. In 2 Kings 13:23, the LORD “was gracious to them and had compassion and showed concern,” sparing Israel in spite of their failure.

- The servants receive mercy because they belong to Him. Isaiah 49:13 celebrates this same tenderness: “For the LORD comforts His people and will have compassion on His afflicted ones.”

when He sees that their strength is gone

- God’s intervention is triggered by Israel’s utter helplessness. Judges 10:16 records a similar moment: when Israel could do nothing, “His soul could no longer endure the misery of Israel.”

- Psalm 73:26 voices the personal experience: “My flesh and my heart fail, but God is the strength of my heart.”

- In the New Testament, 2 Corinthians 12:9 affirms the pattern: divine power shines when human strength collapses. The principle spans both covenants—God waits until every prop is removed so His glory alone sustains.

and no one remains, slave or free.

- The phrase pictures total depletion. In 1 Kings 14:10, judgment “cuts off every male, both bond and free,” showing how thorough a crisis can become.

- Yet even in such desolation, the LORD’s eye remains on His own. 2 Chronicles 16:9 reminds us, “The eyes of the LORD roam to and fro throughout the whole earth to show Himself strong on behalf of those whose hearts are fully His.”

- Whether high-born or bond-servant, every category is empty; only then does God’s exclusive sufficiency appear.

summary

Deuteronomy 32:36 guarantees that when God’s people reach rock bottom—no resources, no distinctions, no hope—He steps in. He vindicates wrongs, shows fatherly compassion, and proves that human weakness is the very stage on which His strength shines.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(36) For the Lord shall judge His people.--Quoted in Hebrews 10:30, in connection with the previous verse. According to this view "shall judge" means "shall punish," not "shall defend."

And repent Himself for His servants.--Or, and will be comforted over His servants. Comp. Ezekiel 5:13, "I will cause my fury to rest upon them, and I will be comforted;" and also Isaiah 1:24, &c.

None shut up, or left.--Comp. 1Kings 14:10; 1Kings 21:21; 2Kings 9:8, and especially Deuteronomy 14:26. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 36. - The Lord shall judge his people (cf. Psalm 135:14; 1 Peter 4:17). And repent himself for his servants; rather, and have compassion upon his servants. And there is none shut up, or left. The words rendered "shut up or left" are a proverbial expression for "every one, men of all sorts" (cf. 1 Kings 14:10; 1 Kings 21:21; 2 Kings 9:8; 2 Kings 14:26); but how the words are to be rendered or explained is uncertain. Rosenmüller renders as in the Authorized Version; Gesenius has, "the shut up and the let go free, the bond and the flee;" so also Furst and De Wette; De Dieu, "married and single, conjugatus et coelebs," referring to the Arabic usage in support of his conclusion ('Animad. in Ver. Test.,' p. 114), and this Keil approves. Ewald has "kept in (by legal impurity) or at large." The explanation of Gesenius and Furst seems best.
