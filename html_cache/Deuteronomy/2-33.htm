And the LORD our God delivered him over to us
This phrase emphasizes the divine intervention and sovereignty of God in the battles of Israel. The "him" refers to Sihon, king of the Amorites, whose defeat was crucial for Israel's journey to the Promised Land. This act of God delivering enemies into the hands of His people is a recurring theme in the Old Testament, illustrating God's faithfulness to His covenant promises. It echoes the assurance given in Deuteronomy 1:30, where God promises to fight for Israel. This divine deliverance prefigures the ultimate victory over sin and death through Jesus Christ, who is often seen as the fulfillment of God's promises.

and we defeated him and his sons and his whole army
The defeat of Sihon and his forces signifies a complete victory granted by God. This victory was not just over Sihon himself but extended to his sons and entire army, indicating the totality of the conquest. Historically, this event marked a significant turning point for the Israelites, as it opened the way for further conquests in the Transjordan region. The comprehensive nature of the victory is reminiscent of other biblical battles where God grants His people triumph over seemingly insurmountable odds, such as the battle of Jericho (Joshua 6). This phrase also underscores the importance of obedience and reliance on God for success, a principle that is echoed throughout Scripture.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Battle
The victory over Sihon underscores God's control over nations and leaders. Believers can trust in God's sovereignty in their own battles, knowing He is able to deliver them.

Faithfulness to Promises
God's deliverance of Sihon into Israel's hands is a fulfillment of His promise to give them the land. This encourages believers to hold fast to God's promises, assured of His faithfulness.

Divine Assistance in Challenges
Just as God delivered Sihon to Israel, He provides assistance in our challenges. Believers are reminded to seek God's help and guidance in their struggles.

The Role of Obedience
The Israelites' victory was contingent on their obedience to God's command to engage Sihon. Obedience to God's directives is crucial for experiencing His blessings and victories.

God's Power Over Adversaries
The defeat of Sihon illustrates God's power over any adversary. Believers can find comfort in knowing that no enemy is too great for God to overcome.

What Does Deuteronomy 2:33 Mean
And the LORD our God delivered him over to us

– The sentence opens by crediting victory entirely to the Lord. Israel did not chalk up the win to superior tactics; rather, God actively “delivered” Sihon into their hands (see Deuteronomy 1:30; Exodus 14:14).

– God’s faithfulness keeps His covenant promises to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and reassures the people that every step into the promised land comes under divine oversight.

– Notice the personal pronoun “our”: this is the God who has entered relationship with His people (Exodus 6:7; Romans 8:31).

– When God delivers, the outcome is settled before the battle begins—an encouraging truth for believers facing spiritual opposition today (2 Corinthians 10:4).

and we defeated him and his sons

– Human responsibility follows divine initiative. Israel still fought, but victory flowed from reliance on the Lord (Joshua 10:25; Psalm 44:3).

– Mentioning Sihon’s sons underscores the finality of the triumph; the entire royal line fell, eliminating any future threat (Numbers 21:24).

– This mirrors how Christ’s finished work disarms rulers and authorities (Colossians 2:15), guaranteeing lasting freedom for God’s people.

and his whole army

– Complete victory means no lingering pockets of resistance (Deuteronomy 7:2). God’s deliverance is thorough, not partial.

– The phrase underlines corporate solidarity: when the leader opposes God, the people who follow share in his downfall (Proverbs 11:21).

– For believers, this anticipates the ultimate eradication of evil when Christ returns (Revelation 19:11-16).

summary

Deuteronomy 2:33 portrays a seamless partnership: God initiates deliverance, and His people respond in obedient action. The verse assures us that the Lord’s intervention is decisive and comprehensive—overcoming leaders, heirs, and forces arrayed against His purposes. That same covenant-keeping God still grants victory to those who trust Him and walk forward in faith.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 33, 34 (cf. Numbers 22:24, 25; Numbers 32:34, 35, etc.). - And utterly destroyed the men, and the women, and the little ones.... we left none to remain. As the Amorites came out of Canaan, they belonged to the race which God had doomed to destruction. The Israelites, therefore, had a commission to extirpate them. Utterly destroyed; literally, devoted or placed under a ban, which of course implied utter destruction. The men, and the women, and the little ones, of every city; literally, every city of men and women and little children. The phrase "city of men" can hardly mean, as Rosenmüller affirms, "men of a city;" the hypallage here would be too violent. It rather means "a peopled city," "a city inhabited by men." The word rendered "men" (מְתִים) does not designate males as opposed to females, but is a designation of human beings in general (cf. Job 11:3; Job 24:12 [Hebrews 20:48]; 31:31; Psalm 26:4, "vain persons," Authorized Version, literally, men of emptiness or of falsehood, etc.). The passage might be rendered, every inhabited city, even the women and the little children.
