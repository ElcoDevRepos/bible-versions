Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You will be blessed when you come in
This phrase signifies the comprehensive nature of God's blessings upon His people. In the context of ancient Israel, "coming in" often referred to entering one's home or city, symbolizing safety and provision. The promise of blessing upon entering suggests a life filled with God's favor in daily activities and personal endeavors. This idea is echoed in Psalm 121:8, which assures God's protection in all aspects of life. The concept of blessing upon entering can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate source of spiritual blessing and security for believers, as He is the door through which one enters into eternal life (John 10:9).

and blessed when you go out.
The phrase "going out" typically refers to leaving one's home or city, often for work, travel, or battle. In the historical context of Deuteronomy, this would include agricultural activities, trade, and military campaigns. The assurance of blessing in these endeavors highlights God's provision and protection in all external activities. This promise is a reminder of God's omnipresence and His involvement in every aspect of life. The dual promise of blessing both in coming and going underscores the totality of God's care, reminiscent of the Great Commission in Matthew 28:19-20, where Jesus promises His presence with His disciples as they go out into the world.

Teaching Points
Understanding Blessings
Recognize that blessings are tied to obedience to God's commands. Reflect on how obedience in daily life can lead to experiencing God's favor.

God's Omnipresence
Acknowledge that God's presence and blessings are not confined to specific locations or situations. He is with us in all our comings and goings.

Covenant Relationship
Emphasize the importance of maintaining a covenant relationship with God, which involves both privileges and responsibilities.

Daily Walk with God
Encourage believers to seek God's guidance and blessing in every aspect of their daily lives, trusting Him in both routine and significant decisions.

Faith and Trust
Cultivate a lifestyle of faith and trust in God's promises, knowing that He is faithful to bless those who follow Him.

What Does Deuteronomy 28:6 Mean
You will be blessed

Moses is speaking God’s promise to Israel: “You will be blessed…” (Deuteronomy 28:6). The blessing is not vague goodwill; it is the concrete favor of the covenant-keeping Lord who delights to pour out goodness on His obedient people (cf. Numbers 6:24-26; Psalm 5:12). He is the source, the guarantee, and the sustainer of every benefit mentioned in this chapter.

When you come in

Daily life begins at the threshold. “Coming in” pictures returning home from work or travel, re-entering family life, worship, or rest. God promises that every return—whether from the fields (Ruth 2:12; Psalm 121:8) or from battle (1 Samuel 18:6)—will be accompanied by His favor. The home front is secure because the Lord Himself watches over it (Psalm 127:1), granting peace, provision, and joy in the ordinary rhythms of life.

And blessed

The repetition underscores certainty. The blessing is not occasional or partial; it is steady, comprehensive, and covenant-wide (Genesis 12:2-3; Ephesians 1:3). God’s people can count on His constancy because His character never changes (Malachi 3:6; James 1:17). He wants them to feel doubly assured that His goodness covers both ends of every journey and everything in between.

When you go out

Leaving home for labor, leadership, or warfare carries risks, yet God promises favor “when you go out.” He shields and prospers His people in the marketplace (Deuteronomy 28:8), the public square (Proverbs 3:5-6), and even hostile territory (Joshua 1:9). The same Lord who blessed Abraham’s journeys (Genesis 24:1) and guarded David’s campaigns (2 Samuel 8:6) pledges His protective presence to all who follow His commands.

summary

Deuteronomy 28:6 reassures obedient believers that God’s covenant blessing rests on every entrance and every exit—home or away, starting or finishing. Because He is faithful, we can step out with courage and return with peace, confident that His favor encloses our entire way.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) When thou comest in . . . and when thou goest out.--These words may apply to the details of life, or they may have a further meaning, as the eisodus of Christ was His entrance into this world's labour, and His exodus His departure (Acts 13:24; Luke 9:31). Rashi says, "So that thy departure from the world shall be like thine entrance into it, sinless." (The Jews, as a whole, do not believe in original sin.)
