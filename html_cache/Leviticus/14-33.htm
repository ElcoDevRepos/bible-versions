Then the LORD said to Moses and Aaron,
This phrase indicates direct divine communication, emphasizing the authority and importance of the instructions that follow. In the context of Leviticus, God often speaks directly to Moses, who serves as the mediator between God and the Israelites. Aaron, as the high priest, is included here, highlighting the priestly role in the purification process. This divine instruction underscores the holiness and purity required in the community, reflecting God's desire for His people to be set apart. The inclusion of both Moses and Aaron signifies the unity of leadership in guiding the Israelites according to God's commands. This phrase also connects to other instances in the Pentateuch where God provides specific instructions for maintaining holiness, such as in Exodus 25:1 and Numbers 4:1.

Teaching Points
God's Holiness and Purity
God's instructions regarding leprosy and mold reflect His desire for His people to be holy and pure, both physically and spiritually. We are called to examine our lives for any "unclean" areas and seek God's cleansing.

Obedience to God's Commands
The detailed laws given to Moses and Aaron show the importance of obedience to God's commands. As believers, we are to follow God's Word diligently, trusting in His wisdom and guidance.

Community Responsibility
The laws concerning leprosy and mold involve the entire community, emphasizing that holiness is a collective responsibility. We are to support one another in maintaining purity and righteousness.

God's Provision and Care
God's instructions for dealing with leprosy and mold demonstrate His care for the health and well-being of His people. We can trust God to provide for our needs and guide us in times of difficulty.

Spiritual Cleansing through Christ
The cleansing rituals in Leviticus point to the ultimate cleansing available through Jesus Christ. As believers, we are cleansed from sin through His sacrifice and are called to live in the freedom and purity He provides.

What Does Leviticus 14:33 Mean
Then

• The word “Then” signals a new moment in the flow of Leviticus. God has just laid out the priestly rituals for cleansing a person healed from skin disease (Leviticus 14:1–32). Now He moves straight into instructions about houses, showing that holiness must permeate every sphere—people and property alike (Leviticus 11:44; 1 Corinthians 10:31).

• It reminds us that God’s revelation is orderly. Each step builds on the previous one, just as the covenant people were to build their daily lives on every word that proceeds from God’s mouth (Deuteronomy 8:3).

the LORD

• The covenant name underscores divine authority. These directions do not arise from human opinion but from the God who redeemed Israel (Exodus 6:2-7). Therefore they carry the same weight as the Ten Commandments (Leviticus 26:1).

• Because “the LORD” speaks, His words are entirely trustworthy and binding, encouraging us to receive even small details—such as mildew in plaster—as expressions of His care for a holy people (Psalm 19:7-11; 2 Timothy 3:16).

said to

• Speech is God’s chosen vehicle for covenant relationship. From “Let there be light” (Genesis 1:3) to “Come to Me” (Matthew 11:28), He engages His people by speaking.

• The phrase links Leviticus 14:33 to a pattern: “The LORD spoke to Moses” (Leviticus 12:1; 13:1; 15:1). Repetition reinforces that the priestly code is divine, not merely ceremonial tradition (Hebrews 12:25).

Moses and Aaron

• God addresses both the lawgiver and the high priest. Moses will record the statute; Aaron and his sons will apply it. Together they picture Christ, who perfectly unites lawgiver and high priest (John 1:17; Hebrews 4:14-16).

• Their joint reception of this word stresses that community leaders stand under Scripture just as much as the people do (Numbers 20:7-12; James 3:1).

summary

Leviticus 14:33, though brief, establishes the unbroken chain of divine authority: at a specific moment (“Then”), the covenant God (“the LORD”) deliberately communicated (“said to”) His appointed servants (“Moses and Aaron”). The verse is a doorway, assuring us that the forthcoming instructions about diseased houses originate from the same holy, faithful God who redeems, commands, and provides. When God speaks, even in the smallest details of life, His people are called to listen, trust, and obey.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(33) And the Lord spake unto Moses and unto Aaron.--Whilst the law about the cleansing of restored lepers was addressed to Moses alone (see Leviticus 14:1), the regulations about leprous houses, like those with regard to leprous garments and persons, are for the same reason delivered to Moses and Aaron conjointly. (See Leviticus 13:1.)

OF A HOUSE, AND ITS CLEANSING (verses 33-53). The subject of leprosy in houses must be regarded from the same point of view as that of leprosy in clothes. The regulations respecting it are not sanitary laws, as Lange represents them, but rest, as Keil argues, upon an ideal or symbolical basis. The same thought is attached to all species of uncleanness. Something - it matters not what - produces a foul and repulsive appearance in the walls of a house. That is in itself sufficient to make that house unclean; for whatever is foul and repulsive is representative of moral and spiritual defilement, and therefore is itself symbolically defiling and defiled. It has been suggested that the special cause of the affection of the houses in Canaan was saltpetre exuding from the materials employed in their building, or iron pyrites in the stone used. This may have been so, or more probably it was the growth of some fungus. Whatever it was, the appearance created by it was so similar to that of leprosy in the human body, as to derive its name from the latter by analogy.
