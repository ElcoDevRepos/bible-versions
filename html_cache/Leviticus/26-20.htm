Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
and your strength will be spent in vain.
This phrase highlights the futility of human effort without divine blessing. In the context of Leviticus 26, God is warning the Israelites of the consequences of disobedience. The strength spent in vain can be seen as a reversal of the blessing promised in Deuteronomy 28:12, where God assures that the work of their hands will be blessed. Theologically, this reflects the principle that human labor, apart from God, is ultimately unproductive, echoing the sentiment found in Psalm 127:1, "Unless the LORD builds the house, the builders labor in vain." This futility is a direct result of breaking the covenant, emphasizing the importance of obedience to God's commands.

For your land will not yield its produce,
The land's failure to yield produce is a direct consequence of the Israelites' disobedience, as outlined in the covenant curses. This is a stark contrast to the blessings of a fruitful land promised in Leviticus 26:4 for obedience. The agricultural context is significant, as the Israelites were primarily an agrarian society, and their livelihood depended heavily on the land's productivity. This curse would have been particularly devastating, as it threatened their survival and prosperity. Theologically, this reflects the idea that the land itself is under God's control and responds to the spiritual state of the people, a theme also seen in Haggai 1:10-11.

and the trees of the land will not bear their fruit.
The failure of the trees to bear fruit further emphasizes the severity of the curse. Fruit-bearing trees were a symbol of blessing and abundance, as seen in Psalm 1:3, where the righteous are likened to a tree that yields fruit in season. The lack of fruit is a sign of divine displeasure and a withdrawal of God's provision. This imagery is also used in the New Testament, where Jesus curses the barren fig tree in Mark 11:12-14, symbolizing judgment on unfruitfulness. Theologically, this can be seen as a call to spiritual fruitfulness, as emphasized in John 15:5, where Jesus states that apart from Him, we can do nothing.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Israelites
The primary audience of Leviticus, the Israelites were God's chosen people, receiving the Law through Moses.

2. The Promised Land
The land of Canaan, promised to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, where the Israelites were to live and prosper under God's covenant.

3. Moses
The prophet and leader who delivered God's laws and commandments to the Israelites, including the blessings and curses outlined in Leviticus 26.

4. God's Covenant
The agreement between God and the Israelites, which included blessings for obedience and curses for disobedience.

5. Agricultural Imagery
The use of land and produce as symbols of God's provision and the consequences of disobedience.
Teaching Points
The Consequences of Disobedience
Disobedience to God's commands leads to futility and unfruitfulness, both physically and spiritually.

The Importance of Covenant Faithfulness
God's covenant with His people requires faithfulness; breaking it results in loss and hardship.

Spiritual Fruitfulness
Just as the land was to yield produce, our lives should yield spiritual fruit through obedience to God.

Dependence on God for Provision
True provision and success come from God; without Him, our efforts are in vain.

The Call to Repentance
When facing the consequences of disobedience, God calls us to repentance and restoration.

What Does Leviticus 26:20 Mean
Setting the scene

Leviticus 26 lays out two divergent paths for Israel: blessings tied to obedience (vv. 3-13) and consequences tied to disobedience (vv. 14-39). Verse 20 sits inside the second path, detailing what happens when the covenant people insist on ignoring God’s statutes. Similar warning passages appear in Deuteronomy 28:15-24 and Amos 4:7-9, reinforcing that God, not nature or human effort, controls harvest outcomes.

When strength runs empty

“Your strength will be spent in vain.”

• Israel might plow, sow, weed, and water—but the return on labor would be nil.

• Psalm 127:1-2 reminds us, “Unless the LORD builds the house, the builders labor in vain.” The futility described here echoes that truth on a national scale.

• Haggai 1:5-6 pictures the same principle after the exile: the people worked hard yet “earned wages to put into a bag with holes.”

Fields that refuse to respond

“For your land will not yield its produce.”

• The soil that once flowed with milk and honey (Exodus 3:8) could tighten like iron (Leviticus 26:19).

• Deuteronomy 28:38-39 forecasts insects eating grain and vines failing despite cultivation, underscoring that God’s favor is the decisive factor.

• Lack of produce affected worship as well; no grain meant no grain offerings (Leviticus 2), cutting off fellowship with God.

Silent trees

“And the trees of the land will not bear their fruit.”

• Orchards take years to mature; fruitlessness wastes past investment and erodes future hope.

• Joel 1:12 laments a similar scene: “The fig tree and the vine have withered.”

• The warning anticipates Jesus cursing the barren fig tree (Mark 11:12-14), a living parable of judgment on unfruitful covenant people.

The heart issue at stake

• God was not merely punishing crops; He was addressing covenant unfaithfulness (Leviticus 26:14-15).

• Galatians 6:7 echoes the principle: “Do not be deceived: God is not mocked. For whatever a man sows, he will reap.”

• Physical barrenness mirrored spiritual barrenness. Without repentance, further escalations would follow (Leviticus 26:21-24).

Living this out today

• While modern believers may not farm, we still invest energy in careers, relationships, and ministries. John 15:5 says, “Apart from Me you can do nothing.” Fruitfulness hinges on abiding in Christ.

• Obedience invites God’s blessing (Proverbs 3:9-10). Persistent rebellion invites wasted effort, anxiety, and emptiness, even in seasons of apparent abundance (Revelation 3:17).

• The passage encourages self-examination: Are we laboring in step with God or striving independently?

summary

Leviticus 26:20 warns that labor divorced from covenant loyalty collapses into futility: strength drains away, fields stop yielding, and trees refuse fruit. God uses material setbacks to call His people back to Himself, highlighting that true productivity flows from obedient reliance on Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) And your strength shall be spent in vain.--That is, with the heaven over them as metal, their labour expended in ploughing, digging, and sowing will be perfectly useless.

Your land shall not yield her increase, as no amount of human labour will make up for the want of rain. In Deuteronomy 11:17, where the same punishment is threatened, and the same phrase is used, the Authorised Version unnecessarily obliterates the identity of the words in the original by rendering them "the land yield not her fruit."
