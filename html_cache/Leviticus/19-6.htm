It shall be eaten on the day you sacrifice it, or on the next day;
This instruction pertains to the peace offerings, which were a type of fellowship offering in the Levitical sacrificial system. The immediate consumption of the sacrifice underscores the importance of maintaining the sanctity and freshness of the offering, symbolizing the immediacy and sincerity of the worshiper's devotion. In the ancient Near Eastern context, sharing a meal was a sign of fellowship and covenant, and this practice reinforced the communal aspect of worship. The restriction to consume the offering within a short time frame also prevented decay, which would render the sacrifice impure. This reflects the broader biblical theme of holiness and purity, as seen in other scriptures such as Exodus 12:10 regarding the Passover lamb, which also had to be consumed quickly.

but what remains on the third day must be burned up.
The command to burn any leftovers on the third day emphasizes the importance of holiness and the avoidance of corruption. In the biblical context, the number three often signifies completeness or divine perfection, and the third day here marks the limit of the offering's acceptability. This practice ensured that the offering remained a symbol of purity and dedication to God, free from decay or defilement. The burning of the remains can be seen as a type of purification, removing any potential for impurity. This concept of burning as a means of purification is echoed in other parts of scripture, such as in Numbers 19:9, where the ashes of a burnt offering are used for purification. Additionally, this can be seen as a type of Christ, who was resurrected on the third day, symbolizing the ultimate purification and victory over corruption and death.

Teaching Points
Holiness in Worship
The requirement to consume the offering within a specific time frame underscores the importance of approaching God with reverence and purity. Our worship should be intentional and respectful, reflecting God's holiness.

Obedience to God's Commands
The detailed instructions for offerings remind us of the importance of obedience in our relationship with God. We are called to follow His commands fully, trusting in His wisdom and sovereignty.

The Temporal Nature of Sacrifices
The time-bound nature of the offerings points to the temporary nature of the Old Testament sacrificial system, which finds its fulfillment in Christ, the ultimate sacrifice. This encourages us to live in the light of Christ's completed work.

Community and Fellowship
The communal aspect of the peace offerings, often shared among family and community, highlights the importance of fellowship and unity in the body of Christ. We are called to build each other up in love and unity.

What Does Leviticus 19:6 Mean
It shall be eaten on the day you sacrifice it

“ ‘It shall be eaten on the day you sacrifice it…’ ” (Leviticus 19:6)

• God commands immediacy: the worshiper is to enjoy the fellowship offering the very day it is presented.

• This underscores the freshness of devotion—worship is meant to be living, vibrant, and current (Exodus 12:10; Leviticus 7:15).

• The worshiper shares a meal that celebrates peace with God, reminding us that reconciliation is personal and timely (Romans 5:1).

or on the next day

“… or on the next day…”

• God grants a brief extension, showing practical kindness for families and communities who traveled to the sanctuary (Deuteronomy 12:17-18).

• Yet the window is small: gratitude and obedience should never drift into delay (Psalm 119:60; Proverbs 3:28).

• The permitted second day hints at Christ’s burial period, pointing forward to the third-day resurrection that ends all hint of decay (Luke 24:7).

but what remains on the third day

“… but what remains on the third day…”

• Anything left signals neglect; the sacrifice is no longer associated with fresh worship (Leviticus 7:17-18).

• Leftover portions picture how spiritual complacency can creep in when we postpone devotion (Hebrews 3:7-8).

• God warns that even well-intended offerings lose value if we let them sit unused.

must be burned up

“… must be burned up.”

• Burning removes impurity and prevents corruption, keeping the camp holy (Leviticus 6:30; Deuteronomy 23:14).

• Judgment falls on what is stale, foreshadowing how God will ultimately purge all that is defiled (2 Peter 3:10).

• The fire also reassures Israel that sin will not linger; God decisively deals with any threat to holiness (Hebrews 12:29).

summary

Leviticus 19:6 teaches that worship is to be immediate, heartfelt, and undefiled. God invites His people to celebrate peace with Him right away, allows a short grace period, but refuses to let devotion turn stale. Anything left unoffered by the third day must be consumed by fire, reminding us that our relationship with the Lord must remain fresh, pure, and prompt—never postponed or polluted.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) It shall be eaten the same day . . . and on the morrow.--The fact that the flesh of the animal might be eaten both on the day on which it was offered and on the following day, according to the authorities during the second Temple, shows that the second class of peace-offering is here meant, described in Leviticus 8:16, since the flesh of the first class of peace-offerings had to be eaten on the same day. (See Leviticus 7:15).

Until the third day.--See Leviticus 7:17.
