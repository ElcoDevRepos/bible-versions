Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And the angel of God said to him
The "angel of God" is often understood as a theophany, a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ. This divine messenger's presence signifies God's direct intervention in Israel's affairs. The angel's instructions to Gideon are part of a divine test and revelation, emphasizing the sacred nature of the encounter.

Take the meat and the unleavened bread
The offering of meat and unleavened bread is reminiscent of sacrificial practices in Israelite worship, symbolizing sustenance and purity. Unleavened bread, used during Passover, signifies haste and purity, connecting Gideon's actions to Israel's deliverance history. The use of these elements underscores the sacredness of the offering and Gideon's role as a deliverer.

lay them on this rock
The rock serves as an altar, a common practice in ancient Israel where natural formations were used for worship. This act of laying the offering on the rock signifies consecration and dedication to God. Rocks as altars are seen throughout the Old Testament, symbolizing God's strength and permanence.

and pour out the broth
Pouring out the broth is an act of libation, a form of offering that signifies the complete dedication of the sacrifice to God. This act can be seen as a type of Christ, who poured out His life for humanity. The broth, representing life and sustenance, being poured out, prefigures the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus.

And Gideon did so
Gideon's obedience is crucial, demonstrating his faith and submission to God's will. This act of compliance is a turning point, marking Gideon's transformation from a hesitant individual to a leader chosen by God. His obedience foreshadows the obedience of Christ, who submitted to the Father's will.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Gideon
A judge of Israel, chosen by God to deliver the Israelites from the oppression of the Midianites. He is known for his initial hesitance and need for reassurance from God.

2. The Angel of God
A divine messenger who appears to Gideon, delivering God's instructions and confirming His presence and promise to Gideon.

3. The Rock
The place where Gideon is instructed to place the meat and unleavened bread. It serves as an altar for the offering to God.

4. The Offering
Consists of meat, unleavened bread, and broth, symbolizing a sacrifice and an act of worship and obedience to God.

5. Midianites
The oppressors of Israel during this time, whose dominance over Israel sets the stage for Gideon's calling and mission.
Teaching Points
Obedience to Divine Instruction
Gideon's compliance with the angel's command demonstrates the importance of following God's instructions, even when they seem unusual or challenging.

Faith in God's Presence
The encounter reassures us that God is present and active in our lives, especially when we face overwhelming challenges.

Symbolism of Sacrifice
The offering on the rock symbolizes surrender and dedication to God, reminding us of the need for personal sacrifice in our spiritual journey.

God's Patience with Doubt
Gideon's account shows that God is patient with our doubts and questions, providing reassurance and guidance as we grow in faith.

Preparation for Service
Before Gideon could lead Israel, he needed to experience God's power personally. This teaches us that personal encounters with God prepare us for greater service.

What Does Judges 6:20 Mean
And the angel of God said to him

• Scripture treats this “angel” as a visible manifestation of God Himself (see v. 14; also Exodus 3:2–6; Judges 13:21–22).

• The initiative is God’s; He meets Gideon in fear and calls him to faith, just as He did Moses (Exodus 3:10-12).

• The authoritative word shows that deliverance begins with listening to God’s voice (Romans 10:17).

Take the meat and the unleavened bread

• Gideon had prepared “a young goat and an ephah of flour” (Judges 6:19), a substantial, costly gift during a famine caused by Midianite oppression.

• Unleavened bread recalls Israel’s first deliverance at Passover—“They are to eat the meat that night… with unleavened bread” (Exodus 12:8). God links past salvation to present need.

• Meat and bread together picture complete sustenance, hinting ahead to Christ who feeds multitudes (Mark 6:41) and calls Himself “the bread of life” (John 6:35).

Lay them on this rock

• The rock becomes an impromptu altar, a place of meeting between heaven and earth (compare Genesis 28:18; Judges 6:24).

• Rock imagery throughout Scripture points to God’s strength and permanence—“The LORD is my rock” (Psalm 18:2) and ultimately to Christ, “the spiritual Rock that accompanied them” (1 Corinthians 10:4).

• By placing the offering on the rock, Gideon acknowledges God as the foundation of Israel’s hope.

and pour out the broth

• Pouring liquid on a sacrifice renders human fire-lighting impossible; acceptance must come supernaturally. Elijah later does the same at Carmel (1 Kings 18:33-35).

• A poured-out offering foreshadows drink offerings prescribed in the Law (Numbers 15:5-10) and anticipates Paul’s testimony, “I am already being poured out like a drink offering” (2 Timothy 4:6).

• The act symbolizes surrender—nothing held back, everything yielded to God’s consuming presence.

And Gideon did so

• Immediate obedience turns Gideon from fearful to faithful. James 1:22 urges, “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only”.

• Obedience precedes revelation; fire falls in the next verse (Judges 6:21), confirming that the messenger truly is of God.

• Gideon’s simple compliance models the attitude Jesus commends: “Blessed… are those who hear the word of God and obey it” (Luke 11:28).

summary

Judges 6:20 records a divine directive and Gideon’s prompt response. God Himself instructs the offering, ties it to Israel’s redemption history, sets it upon a rock of refuge, drenches it to exclude human effort, and receives it when Gideon obeys. The verse teaches that deliverance starts with hearing God’s voice and acting in wholehearted trust, resting on the Rock who secures every sacrifice we bring.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) The angel of God.--Here alone in the chapter called "the angel of Elohim" and not "of Jehovah."

Upon this rock.--Rather, upon yonder crag. The living rock (Exodus 20:22) served well as an altar.

Pour out the broth.--Comp. Genesis 35:14; Exodus 30:9; 1Kings 18:34. In the first of these instances the "drink offering" is used as a libation; in the last Elijah pours the sea-water on the sacrifice, to show the impossibility of any deception. In 2 Maccabees 1:20-36 Nehemiah pours the "thick water," called "Naphthai," on the sacrifice, and when the sun shone "there was a great fire kindled, so that every man marvelled." . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - Lay them upon this rock, as upon an altar, and pour out the broth, as a drink offering or a libation (see Judges 13:19).
