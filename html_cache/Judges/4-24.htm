And the hand of the Israelites grew stronger and stronger
This phrase indicates a progressive increase in power and influence. In the context of Judges, it reflects the cyclical pattern of Israel's history during the time of the judges, where periods of oppression were followed by deliverance and victory. The strengthening of Israel's hand can be seen as a fulfillment of God's promise to give them victory over their enemies when they are obedient to Him (Deuteronomy 28:7). This growing strength is also a testament to God's faithfulness in empowering His people despite their previous disobedience.

against Jabin king of Canaan
Jabin was a title used for the king of Hazor, a major Canaanite city. Historically, Hazor was a significant city-state in northern Canaan, and archaeological evidence supports its prominence during this period. The mention of Jabin connects this narrative to earlier biblical accounts, such as Joshua 11, where another Jabin, king of Hazor, was defeated by Joshua. This suggests a recurring theme of conflict between Israel and the Canaanite powers, symbolizing the ongoing struggle between God's people and the forces opposed to His rule.

until they destroyed him
The complete destruction of Jabin signifies the total victory granted by God to the Israelites. This destruction is not just a military victory but also a spiritual triumph, as it represents the eradication of Canaanite influence and idolatry from among the Israelites. The phrase echoes the divine mandate given to Israel to drive out the inhabitants of the land (Deuteronomy 7:1-2). It also prefigures the ultimate victory of Christ over sin and death, as seen in 1 Corinthians 15:57, where believers are assured of victory through Jesus Christ.

3. Deborah
A prophetess and judge of Israel, who played a crucial role in leading Israel to victory over Jabin's forces.

The military leader who, under Deborah's guidance, led the Israelite army against Sisera, Jabin's commander.

The commander of Jabin's army, whose defeat was pivotal in the weakening of Jabin's reign.
Teaching Points
Divine Empowerment
God empowers His people to overcome their adversaries. Just as the Israelites grew stronger against Jabin, believers today can trust in God's strength to face their challenges.

Leadership and Obedience
Deborah and Barak's account highlights the importance of godly leadership and obedience to God's commands. We should seek to follow God's guidance in our lives.

Persistence in Faith
The gradual strengthening of the Israelites teaches us the value of persistence in faith. Victory may not be immediate, but steadfastness in God’s promises leads to triumph.

God's Sovereignty
The eventual destruction of Jabin underscores God's sovereignty over nations and rulers. We can rest assured that God is in control, even when facing powerful opposition.

What Does Judges 4:24 Mean
And the hand of the Israelites

• Scripture often uses “hand” to picture strength and action. Here the phrase highlights God-given capacity, just as in Exodus 15:6 “Your right hand, O LORD, is majestic in power”.

• Although Deborah and Barak led the charge (Judges 4:4–9, 14), the credit for the “hand” goes to the Lord who “subdued Jabin king of Canaan before the children of Israel” (Judges 4:23).

• Like Psalm 118:15, where “the right hand of the LORD performs with valor,” Israel’s victory flows from divine empowerment rather than human prowess.

Grew stronger and stronger

• The Hebrew narrative stresses a sustained, escalating triumph. 2 Samuel 3:1 uses the same wording: David “grew stronger and stronger, while the house of Saul grew weaker.”

• God’s deliverance is often progressive—peace by peace rather than all at once—mirroring Proverbs 4:18 where “the path of the righteous is like the first gleam of dawn, shining ever brighter till the full light of day.”

• Every fresh success affirmed Deborah’s earlier word: “Has not the LORD gone out before you?” (Judges 4:14).

Against Jabin king of Canaan

• Jabin personifies oppressive power. Judges 4:2–3 notes his nine hundred iron chariots and twenty-year domination, yet Psalm 20:7 reminds us, “Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God.”

• God’s people are never called to make peace with bondage. As in Ephesians 6:12, their real struggle is against the forces that stand behind earthly thrones.

Until they destroyed him

• God did not settle for partial relief. Deuteronomy 31:3 promised that “the LORD your God Himself will cross over ahead of you. He will destroy these nations.” Judges 5:31 celebrates the fulfillment: “So may all Your enemies perish, O LORD!”

• The finality answers the earlier defeat under Joshua 11:10–11, showing God’s resolve to finish what earlier generations left incomplete.

• For today’s believer, Romans 16:20 echoes the same certainty: “The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet.”

summary

Judges 4:24 presents a steady, God-directed advance. The Lord strengthens His people (“hand”), enables continual growth in victory (“stronger and stronger”), targets every oppressive foe (“against Jabin”), and secures complete deliverance (“until they destroyed him”). What began with Deborah’s call ends with freedom for all Israel, proving that God’s purposes never stall halfway; He finishes what He starts.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) The hand of the children of Israel prospered, and prevailed.--Literally, as in the margin, The hand. . . . going went, and was hard--i.e., "became heavier and heavier in its pressure." The battle of the Kishon was the beginning of a complete deliverance of Israel from the yoke of the Canaanites.
