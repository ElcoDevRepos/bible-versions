Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
When Judah attacked
The tribe of Judah, one of the twelve tribes of Israel, was often seen as a leading tribe, both in terms of military prowess and spiritual leadership. This attack follows the death of Joshua, during a time when the Israelites were tasked with taking possession of the Promised Land. Judah's leadership in this attack is significant, as it foreshadows the eventual rise of the Davidic line, from which Jesus Christ would come. The act of attacking also reflects the ongoing struggle between the Israelites and the inhabitants of Canaan, a struggle that was both physical and spiritual.

the LORD delivered the Canaanites and Perizzites into their hands
This phrase emphasizes the divine intervention and sovereignty of God in the battles of Israel. The Canaanites and Perizzites were among the various groups inhabiting the land of Canaan, and their defeat was crucial for the Israelites to establish themselves in the land. The deliverance by the LORD highlights the fulfillment of God's promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob regarding the land. It also serves as a reminder of the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where obedience to God results in His favor and victory.

and they struck down ten thousand men at Bezek
Bezek is identified as a location where this significant battle took place. The striking down of ten thousand men indicates a decisive victory, showcasing the might and effectiveness of Judah's forces under divine guidance. This victory at Bezek is a testament to the power of God working through His chosen people. The number ten thousand, often used in scripture to denote a large, complete number, underscores the totality of the victory. This event can be seen as a type of Christ's ultimate victory over sin and death, where He conquers the spiritual enemies of His people.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Judah
One of the twelve tribes of Israel, descended from Judah, the fourth son of Jacob and Leah. Known for its leadership role among the tribes.

2. The LORD
Refers to Yahweh, the covenant God of Israel, who actively participates in the history and battles of His people.

3. Canaanites
A group of ancient people living in the land of Canaan, often seen as adversaries to the Israelites in their conquest of the Promised Land.

4. Perizzites
Another group of people living in Canaan, often mentioned alongside the Canaanites as inhabitants of the land that Israel was to conquer.

5. Bezek
A location where the battle took place, significant for its strategic importance in the conquest account.
Teaching Points
Divine Sovereignty in Battle
The victory at Bezek underscores God's sovereignty and His active role in the battles of His people. Believers can trust in God's power and presence in their own spiritual battles.

Obedience to God's Commands
Judah's attack on the Canaanites and Perizzites was in obedience to God's command to take possession of the land. This teaches the importance of obedience to God's directives in our lives.

The Role of Leadership
Judah's leadership in the battle reflects the importance of strong, godly leadership in achieving victory and fulfilling God's purposes.

Faith and Action
The tribe of Judah acted in faith, trusting that God would deliver their enemies into their hands. This illustrates the necessity of combining faith with action in the Christian walk.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The victory at Bezek is a testament to God's faithfulness in fulfilling His promises to Israel. Believers can be assured of God's faithfulness in their own lives.

What Does Judges 1:4 Mean
When Judah attacked

• Judah’s advance flows directly from the Lord’s earlier word: “Judah shall go up; I have delivered the land into their hands” (Judges 1:2).

• The tribe is acting in obedience, not presumption—echoing Jacob’s blessing, “Judah, your brothers will praise you; your hand will be on the neck of your enemies” (Genesis 49:8-10).

• Their leadership role mirrors later moments when Israel asks who should lead and the Lord again answers, “Judah first” (Judges 20:18).

the LORD delivered

• Scripture consistently credits victory to God rather than military might. As David told Goliath, “the battle belongs to the LORD” (1 Samuel 17:47).

• Proverbs 21:31 reminds us, “Victory is of the LORD,” and Psalm 44:3 celebrates that Israel won the land “not by their sword… but by Your right hand.”

• Judges 1:4 underscores that same pattern: human participation, divine deliverance.

the Canaanites and Perizzites into their hands

• These two peoples had long occupied the land (Genesis 12:6; 13:7). Their removal fulfills God’s earlier pledge to drive out seven nations mightier than Israel (Deuteronomy 7:1-2).

• Joshua’s generation began the work (Joshua 24:11); now the next generation is continuing it, showing that God’s promises span generations.

• The specific naming of both peoples underlines the historical reality of the conquest rather than a vague spiritual allegory.

and they struck down ten thousand men

• The number points to a decisive, large-scale victory, yet Scripture often pairs such figures with reminders of God’s power: “Five of you will chase a hundred, and a hundred of you will chase ten thousand” (Leviticus 26:8).

• Deuteronomy 32:30 marvels, “How could one man pursue a thousand… unless their Rock had sold them?”—precisely what is happening here.

• Later, David’s triumph over eighteen thousand Edomites (2 Samuel 8:13) continues the theme: God amplifies His people’s efforts beyond human capacity.

at Bezek

• Bezek lies in the hill country of Judah and became famous for Adoni-Bezek’s defeat (Judges 1:5-7).

• The location resurfaces when Saul musters Israel there (1 Samuel 11:8), suggesting it remained a strategic rally point.

• Naming the place anchors the event in real geography, reinforcing the reliability of the biblical record.

summary

Judges 1:4 portrays an obedient tribe stepping forward, a faithful God granting victory, identifiable enemies falling, an overwhelming casualty count underscoring divine power, and a specific battlefield anchoring the story in history. The verse reminds us that when God calls His people to act, He also supplies the deliverance; their role is trustful obedience, His role is sovereign conquest.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) And Judah went up.--Under the leadership of Caleb (Joshua 14:6).

The Canaanites and the Perizzites.--See Genesis 13:7; Genesis 34:30. The former seem to have been lowlanders--"by the sea and by the coast of Jordan" (Numbers 13:29), "on the east and on the west" (Joshua 11:3; Joshua 17:16). The Perizzites were the mountain and forest tribes (Joshua 11:3; Joshua 17:15). Their antiquity and importance appear from the allusions to them in Genesis 13:7; Genesis 34:30; 1Kings 9:20; 2 Esdras 1:21. The name itself seems to imply "open villages" (1Samuel 6:18; Deuteronomy 3:5), and may imply that they were agriculturists. The name does not occur in the genealogy of nations in Genesis 10 . . .
