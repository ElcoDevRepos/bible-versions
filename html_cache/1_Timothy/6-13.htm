Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I charge you in the presence of God
This phrase emphasizes the solemnity and seriousness of the charge being given. In biblical context, invoking the presence of God underscores the divine authority and accountability associated with the instruction. The concept of being in God's presence is a recurring theme in Scripture, highlighting His omnipresence and the believer's responsibility to live righteously. This echoes the Old Testament practice where leaders and prophets often acted under divine mandate (e.g., Deuteronomy 31:14).

who gives life to all things
This acknowledges God as the Creator and Sustainer of all life, a fundamental belief in Christian theology. It reflects the biblical teaching found in Genesis 1, where God is depicted as the source of all creation. This phrase also connects to Acts 17:25, where Paul speaks of God as the one who gives life and breath to everything. It serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and the life-giving power inherent in His nature.

and of Christ Jesus
The mention of Christ Jesus places Him on equal footing with God, affirming His divinity and central role in the Christian faith. This reflects the New Testament teaching of the Trinity, where Jesus is recognized as fully God and fully man. The use of "Christ Jesus" emphasizes His messianic role and mission, as seen throughout the Gospels and epistles.

who made the good confession in His testimony before Pontius Pilate
This refers to the account of Jesus before Pilate, as recorded in the Gospels, particularly John 18:37, where Jesus affirms His kingship and mission. The "good confession" highlights Jesus' unwavering commitment to truth and His divine purpose, even in the face of suffering and death. This moment is pivotal in the Passion narrative, illustrating Jesus as the faithful witness, a theme echoed in Revelation 1:5. It serves as a model for believers to remain steadfast in their faith and testimony.

What Does 1 Timothy 6:13 Mean
I charge you

Paul’s words carry the weight of a solemn, personal mandate. This is not a casual suggestion but a binding instruction, similar to his earlier urgings (1 Timothy 1:3; 5:21). Like a commanding officer, Paul entrusts Timothy with duties that must be carried out faithfully.

• Such charges remind us of 2 Timothy 4:1-2, where Paul calls Timothy to “preach the word” with urgency.

• They underscore that Christian service is stewardship, echoing 1 Corinthians 4:1-2—“it is required of stewards that they be found faithful”.

in the presence of God

Timothy’s obedience is to be performed with the full awareness that God Himself is watching. Nothing is hidden from the One “to whom we must give account” (Hebrews 4:13).

• Operating “before God” elevates ordinary tasks into acts of worship, reminiscent of Colossians 3:23—“Whatever you do, work at it with your whole being, for the Lord and not for men.”

• It also encourages courage; if God is the primary audience (Psalm 139:7-12), human opinions fade in importance.

who gives life to all things

Paul roots Timothy’s courage in God’s creative power. The One who speaks and breathes life into everything (Genesis 2:7) can surely sustain His servants.

• Acts 17:25 highlights God as the One who “gives all men life and breath and everything else”.

• John 5:21 shows that both Father and Son raise the dead, proving divine sovereignty over life, ministry, and every challenge Timothy will face.

and of Christ Jesus

The charge carries the authority of the risen Lord, the Head of the church (Ephesians 1:22).

• Matthew 28:18—“All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me”—grounds every Christian commission in Christ’s supremacy.

• Linking God the Father and Christ Jesus in one breath emphasizes their unity of purpose and underscores the seriousness of Timothy’s responsibilities.

who made the good confession in His testimony before Pontius Pilate

Jesus boldly affirmed His kingship and truth (John 18:36-37; Mark 15:2), refusing to compromise even under threat of death.

• Revelation 1:5 calls Him “the faithful witness,” reminding Timothy that courageous testimony may cost but will never be wasted.

• By pointing to Jesus’ confession, Paul offers Timothy a living model: stand firm, speak truth, and trust God with the outcome (1 Peter 2:21-23).

summary

1 Timothy 6:13 is a weighty commissioning. Paul binds Timothy to unwavering faithfulness, reminding him that his ministry unfolds before the life-giving Creator and the authoritative, truth-speaking Christ. Rooted in the God who grants life and inspired by the Savior who stood firm before Pilate, Timothy—and every believer—can carry out their calling with boldness, integrity, and steadfast hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) I give thee charge in the sight of God.--Better rendered, I charge thee in the sight of God. If possible, with increased earnestness and a yet deeper solemnity as the letter draws to an end does St. Paul charge that young disciple--from whom he hoped so much, and yet for whom he feared so anxiously--to keep the commandment and doctrine of his Master spotless; and, so far as in him lay, to preserve that doctrine unchanged and unalloyed till the coming again of the blessed Master. So he charges him as in the tremendous presence of God.

Who quickeneth all things.--The older authorities adopt here a reading which implies, who keepest alive, or preservest, all things. The Preserver rather than the Creator is here brought into prominence. Timothy is exhorted to fight his good fight, ever mindful that he is in the presence of that great Being who could and would--even if Timothy's faithfulness should lead him to danger and to death--still preserve him, on earth or in Paradise.

And before Christ Jesus, who before Pontius Pilate witnessed a good confession.--Better rendered, who before Pontius Pilate bore witness to the good confession. The good confession which (1Timothy 6:12) Timothy confessed before many witnesses, Jesus Christ, in the presence of Pilate, had already borne witness to. In other words, Jesus Christ, before Pontius Pilate, bore witness by His own solemn words, that He was the Messiah--the long-looked-for King of Israel. If the preposition which we have, with the majority of expositors, construed "before" (Pontius Pilate) have here its local meaning, the "witness" must be limited to the scene in the Judgment Hall--to the interview between the prisoner Jesus and the Roman governor.

Although this meaning here seems the most accurate, it is possible to understand this preposition in a temporal, not in a local, signification--under (that is, in the days of) Pontius Pilate--then the "witness" was borne by the Redeemer to the fact of His being "Messiah:" first, by His own solemn words; secondly, by His voluntary death. The confession was that "He, Jesus, was a King, though not of this world." (See Matthew 27:11; John 18:36-37, where the noble confession is detailed.) He bore His witness with a terrible death awaiting Him. It was, in some respects, a model confession for all martyrs, in so far as it was a bold confession of the truth with the sentence of death before His eyes.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - I charge thee for I give thee charge, A.V.; of for before (in italics), A.V.; the for a, A.V. I charge thee. It has been well observed that the apostle's language increases in solemnity as he approaches the end of the Epistle. This word παραγγέλλω is of frequent use in St. Paul's Epistles (1 Corinthians 7:10; 1 Thessalonians 4:11: 2 Thessalonians 3:4, 6, 10, 12; and above, 1 Timothy 3; 1 Timothy 4:11; 1 Timothy 5:7). In the sight of God, etc. (compare the adjuration in 1 Timothy 5:21). Who quickeneth, etc. The T.R. has ζωοποιοῦντος. The R.T. has ζωογονοῦντος, with no difference of meaning. Both words are used in the LXX. as the rendering of the Pihel and Hiphil of תָיָה. As an epithet of "God," it sets before us the highest creative act of the Almighty as "the Lord, and the Giver of life;" and is equivalent to "the living God" (Matthew 26:63), "the God of the spirits of all flesh" (Numbers 16:22). The existence of "life" is the one thing which baffles the ingenuity of science in its attempts to dispense with a Creator. The good confession refers to our Lord's confession of himself as "the Christ, the Son of God," in Matthew 27:11; Luke 23:3; John 18:36, 37, which is analogous to the baptismal confession (Acts 8:37 (T.R.); 16:31; 19:4, 5). The natural word to have followed μαρτυρεῖν was μαρτυρίαν, as above ὁμολογίαν follows ὡμολόγησας; but St. Paul substitutes the word of cognate meaning, ὁμολογίαν, in order to keep the formula, ἥ καλὴ ὁμολογία.
