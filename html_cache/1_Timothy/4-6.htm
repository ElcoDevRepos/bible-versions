Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
By pointing out these things to the brothers
Paul is instructing Timothy to remind the believers of the truths he has been teaching. The "things" refer to the warnings against false teachings and encouragements to godly living mentioned earlier in the chapter. This reflects the role of a leader in the early church, emphasizing the importance of sound doctrine. The term "brothers" indicates the familial nature of the Christian community, highlighting unity and mutual care.

you will be a good servant of Christ Jesus
Being a "good servant" implies dedication and faithfulness in ministry. The term "servant" (Greek: diakonos) suggests humility and a willingness to serve others, following the example of Christ, who came not to be served but to serve (Mark 10:45). This phrase underscores the call to live a life that reflects Christ's teachings and character.

nourished by the words of faith
"Nourished" suggests a continuous process of growth and sustenance, akin to how physical food sustains the body. The "words of faith" refer to the core teachings of Christianity, including the gospel message and apostolic doctrine. This highlights the necessity of spiritual nourishment through Scripture and sound teaching, as seen in passages like Matthew 4:4, where Jesus emphasizes living by every word from God.

and sound instruction
"Sound instruction" implies teaching that is healthy, correct, and beneficial for spiritual growth. This contrasts with the false teachings Paul warns against. The emphasis on sound doctrine is a recurring theme in the Pastoral Epistles, as seen in Titus 1:9, where leaders are urged to hold firmly to trustworthy messages.

that you have followed
Timothy's adherence to sound teaching is commended, indicating his faithfulness and reliability. This phrase suggests a pattern of life and ministry that aligns with apostolic teaching. It also serves as an encouragement for believers to remain steadfast in their faith, as seen in 2 Timothy 3:14, where Paul urges Timothy to continue in what he has learned.

Teaching Points
Nourishment from Faith and Instruction
Just as physical nourishment is essential for the body, spiritual nourishment from faith and sound teaching is vital for the soul. Regular engagement with Scripture strengthens our faith and equips us for service.

Role of a Good Servant
Being a good servant of Christ involves not only personal growth in faith but also the responsibility to guide others in truth. This requires dedication to learning and teaching sound doctrine.

Importance of Sound Doctrine
In a world filled with diverse teachings, adhering to sound doctrine is crucial. It protects the church from false teachings and ensures that the community remains rooted in the truth of the Gospel.

Mentorship and Leadership
Timothy's relationship with Paul highlights the importance of mentorship in spiritual growth and leadership. Seeking guidance from mature believers can help us navigate challenges in our faith journey.

Consistency in Faith
Following the words of faith and sound instruction consistently is key to spiritual maturity. It involves a commitment to living out the teachings of Christ in everyday life.

What Does 1 Timothy 4:6 Mean
By pointing out these things

Paul has just warned about “deceitful spirits and doctrines of demons” (1 Timothy 4:1). Timothy’s task is to expose error and highlight truth.

• 2 Timothy 2:14 urges, “Remind them of these things and solemnly charge them before God.”

• Acts 20:27 shows the apostolic pattern: “For I did not shrink from declaring to you the whole counsel of God.”

Calling out false teaching is not optional; it is part of faithful ministry. When we lovingly clarify truth, we protect the flock and honor Christ.

to the brothers

The primary audience for Timothy’s corrections is the believing community. Ministry starts at home.

• Galatians 6:10 encourages doing good “especially to those who belong to the household of faith.”

• Hebrews 13:1 simply says, “Let brotherly love continue.”

Correction delivered in family love strengthens unity and maturity within the church.

you will be a good servant of Christ Jesus

The measure of success is Christ’s approval, not human applause. “This is how one should regard us, as servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God” (1 Corinthians 4:1).

• Matthew 25:21 celebrates the faithful servant: “Well done, good and faithful servant.”

• 2 Timothy 2:24 reminds that “the Lord’s servant must not be quarrelsome but must be kind to everyone.”

A “good servant” obeys, teaches, and reflects the Master’s character.

nourished by the words of faith

Spiritual leaders must feed on Scripture before feeding others. “Man shall not live on bread alone, but on every word that comes from the mouth of God” (Matthew 4:4).

• Jeremiah 15:16 testifies, “Your words were found, and I ate them.”

• 1 Peter 2:2 urges believers to “crave pure spiritual milk.”

Regular intake of God’s Word sustains, shapes, and energizes ministry.

and sound instruction

Healthy doctrine guards against the sickness of error.

• Titus 1:9 commands elders to “hold firmly to the trustworthy word as taught, so that he can give instruction in sound doctrine.”

• 2 Timothy 1:13 says, “Hold on to the pattern of sound teaching.”

Truth that is biblically balanced and Christ-centered equips believers for discernment and growth.

that you have followed

Timothy’s consistency matters; he cannot commend what he does not practice.

• 2 Timothy 3:14 urges him to “continue in what you have learned and have firmly believed.”

• Philippians 3:17 invites others to “join in imitating me.”

A lived-out faith lends credibility to teaching and offers a tangible model for others.

summary

1 Timothy 4:6 sketches the profile of a faithful minister: he exposes error, cares for the family of faith, seeks Christ’s approval, feeds continually on Scripture, clings to sound doctrine, and models what he teaches. When these qualities converge, the church is protected, nourished, and strengthened for every good work.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) If thou put the brethren in remembrance of these things.--The "things" of which he was to put the brethren in mind were those practices connected with that foolish, false asceticism alluded to in 1Timothy 4:3-5. Not a few, probably, in that Ephesian flock had been won over by the persuasive words of the false teachers to attribute a peculiar virtue to such practices--practices which, if persevered in, St. Paul well knew would tend to set up for imitation in the Church an unreal, unhealthy standard of life.

Thou shalt be a good minister of Jesus Christ.--A high title to honour, this, "a good minister of Jesus Christ," and one Timothy would well earn if he would set himself in all earnestness to oppose and discredit the sickly teaching of the Ascetic school.

He would by such opposition, indeed, earn the "title to honour," for St. Paul well knew how great was the danger of a comparatively young and ardent disciple like Timothy being attracted by such mistaken teachings of perfection. But "the good minister of Jesus Christ" must teach "a life" which may be led by all, not by a select few merely, of the believers on his Master. Asceticism is too often a winning and attractive school of teaching to ministers, as, at a comparatively easy price, they win a great, but at the same time thoroughly unhealthy, power over the souls of men and women who practise these austerities, which tend necessarily to remove them out of the stream of active life.

Nourished up in the words of faith and of good doctrine.--The Greek present participle rendered here "nourished up in," marks a continuous and permanent process of self-education. It might be translated "ever training thyself"--a wise and never-to-be-forgotten precept of St. Paul's, this reminder to his own dear son in the faith, Timothy--and through Timothy to all Christian ministers of every age--never to relax their efforts for self-improvement. The education of the good minister of Jesus Christ is never to be considered finished. He--the teacher of others--must ever be striving himself after a higher and a yet higher knowledge in things spiritual.

Whereunto thou hast attained.--More accurately translated, which thou hast closely followed. In the teaching respecting faith and practice which Timothy, as a disciple, has diligently followed out step by step--in that teaching he is to study to advance yet farther, so as to gain deeper and ever deeper knowledge of the mysteries of the kingdom.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Mind for remembrance, A.V.; Christ Jesus for Jesus Christ, A.V. and T.R.; nourished for nourished up, A.V.; the faith for faith, A.V.; the good for good, A.V.; which thou hast followed until now for whereunto thou hast attained, A.V. If thou put the brethren in mind of these things (παῦτα ὑποτιθέμενος τοῖς ἀδελφοῖς); if thou suggest these things to the brethren, lay them down as principles upon which their conduct is to be based; or, enjoin them (Liddell and Scott). It only occurs in this metaphorical sense here in the New Testament, but is very common in classical Greek, and not infrequent in the LXX. It has often the meaning of "to advise" or" counsel." Of course, "hypothesis," the assumed basis from which you start, is the same root. The brethren (τοῖς ἀδελφοῖς). The distinctive name for the members of Christ's Church, throughout the Acts of the Apostles and the Epistles. The whole body is called ἡ ἀδελφότης "the brotherhood" (1 Peter 2:17; 1 Peter 5:9). A good minister (διάκονος). The application of this term to Timothy, like that of ἐπίσκοπος to presbyters (1 Timothy 3:2), is an indication of the early date of the Epistle, before the distinctive names of the Church officers had quite hardened down into a technical meaning. Nourished (ἀντρεφόμενος); here only in the New Testament, and not used in the LXX.; but in classical Greek not uncommon in the sense of "brought up in," "trained in from childhood." In Latin, innutritus. The phrase, "nourished in the words of the faith," etc., explains the καλὸς διάκονος, and shows what a man must be to deserve the appellation - one, viz., who is nourished in the words of the faith, etc. The faith; here again objective, as in ver. 6 (see note). The good doctrine, etc. In opposition to the "doctrines of devils" in ver. 1. The different epithets of this true Christian doctrine are ἡ καλή (as here); ὑγιαίνουσα (1 Timothy 1:10; Titus 1:9; Titus 2:1); ἡ κατ εὐσεβείαν διδασκαλία (1 Timothy 6:3); and in 1 Timothy 6. I we have simply ηδιδασκαλία, without any epithet. In like manner, ἡ πίστις ἡ, ἀληθεία ἡ εὐσεβεία, severally denote the Christian religion. Which thou hast followed until now (η΅ι παρηκολουθήκας). This is a rather more faithful rendering than that of the A.V.; it is, literally, which thou hast kept close to, either for the purpose of imitating it, or, as 2 Timothy 3:10, for the purpose of observing it. Or, to put it differently, in one case so as to teach it identically, and in the other so as to know it perfectly. In this last aspect it is also used in Luke 1:3. The classical use is "to follow closely any one's steps," or "the course of events," when used literally; or, metaphorically, "to follow with one's thoughts," "to understand."
