When the people of Jabesh-gilead
Jabesh-gilead was a city located east of the Jordan River in the territory of Gilead. It is significant in Israel's history, particularly in the narrative of Saul. The people of Jabesh-gilead had a special connection to Saul because he had previously rescued them from the Ammonites (1 Samuel 11). This act of deliverance created a bond of loyalty and gratitude towards Saul, which is evident in their later actions. The city’s location in Gilead, a region known for its balm and healing properties, may symbolically reflect the healing and restoration they sought to bring to Saul’s legacy.

heard what the Philistines had done to Saul
The Philistines were a constant threat to Israel during the time of Saul. They were a seafaring people who settled along the coastal regions of Canaan and were known for their military prowess and advanced weaponry, including iron chariots. The Philistines' actions against Saul included not only defeating him in battle but also desecrating his body, which was a common practice in ancient warfare to humiliate the enemy and assert dominance. This act was particularly grievous to the Israelites, as it was seen as a dishonor not only to Saul but to the nation of Israel and their God. The news of Saul's defeat and the subsequent treatment of his body would have been a source of deep mourning and outrage for the people of Jabesh-gilead, prompting them to take action.

Persons / Places / Events
1. People of Jabesh-gilead
Residents of a city in Gilead, east of the Jordan River. They had a historical connection to Saul, as he had previously rescued them from the Ammonites (1 Samuel 11).

2. Philistines
A significant enemy of Israel during the time of Saul. They defeated Saul and his sons in battle, leading to the events described in this chapter.

3. Saul
The first king of Israel, anointed by Samuel. His reign ended tragically in battle against the Philistines, as described in 1 Samuel 31.

4. Jabesh-gilead
A city that played a crucial role in the aftermath of Saul's death, showing loyalty and gratitude to Saul by retrieving and burying his body.

5. Battle of Mount Gilboa
The battle where Saul and his sons were killed, marking a significant turning point in Israel's history.
Teaching Points
Loyalty and Gratitude
The people of Jabesh-gilead demonstrate loyalty and gratitude to Saul by risking their lives to retrieve his body. This act of kindness reflects the importance of honoring those who have impacted our lives positively.

Courage in Adversity
Despite the danger posed by the Philistines, the people of Jabesh-gilead acted courageously. This teaches us to stand firm in our convictions, even when faced with significant challenges.

The Impact of Leadership
Saul's earlier actions in rescuing Jabesh-gilead had a lasting impact, reminding us that leadership can leave a legacy of loyalty and respect.

Responding to Tragedy
The response of Jabesh-gilead to Saul's death shows us how to respond to tragedy with dignity and respect, providing a model for dealing with loss in our own lives.

What Does 1 Samuel 31:11 Mean
When the people

• The verse opens with an immediate, time-marked response. Something has just happened on Mount Gilboa, and word is spreading fast.

• Scripture often shows pivotal moments beginning with the people’s awareness—think of Numbers 14:1, where “all the congregation lifted up their voices and cried” upon hearing the spies’ report.

• Here the narrative pauses to spotlight the collective heart of Israel, preparing us to see courage rise from grief rather than fade into despair.

of Jabesh-gilead

• These were the very townsfolk Saul rescued years earlier when Nahash the Ammonite threatened to gouge out their right eyes (1 Samuel 11:1-11).

• Their gratitude toward Saul never cooled; loyalty forged in deliverance lasts. Compare 2 Samuel 2:5-6, where David commends this same city: “May you be blessed by the LORD, because you showed this kindness to Saul your lord.”

• Jabesh-gilead’s location east of the Jordan made their upcoming trip risky, yet they considered honor more valuable than safety.

heard

• The Hebrew narrative pulse races at the verb “heard.” Hearing triggers faith-filled action (Joshua 2:10-11—Rahab acts when she “heard” what God did at the Red Sea).

• The men of Jabesh-gilead do not wait for perfect plans; they translate information into obedience and gratitude.

• For believers today, Romans 10:17 reminds us that “faith comes by hearing.” Right hearing births right doing.

what the Philistines had done

• The Philistines didn’t merely defeat Saul; they humiliated him—cutting off his head, stripping his armor, and fastening his body to Beth-shan’s wall (1 Samuel 31:9-10; 1 Chronicles 10:10).

• Such desecration mocked the God of Israel (2 Samuel 1:20: “Tell it not in Gath… lest the daughters of the Philistines rejoice”).

• By understanding the full weight of this dishonor, we grasp why Jabesh-gilead felt compelled to act: they could not allow the covenant king’s corpse to remain a trophy in a pagan stronghold.

to Saul

• Though Saul’s reign ended in failure, he was still “the LORD’s anointed” (1 Samuel 24:6). Respect for God’s chosen office mattered more than the man’s shortcomings.

• Their coming night raid (1 Samuel 31:12) shows courage fueled by gratitude: they retrieve Saul’s body, burn it honorably, and later bury the bones under a tamarisk tree, fasting seven days.

• This reverence foreshadows David’s later act of gathering Saul and Jonathan’s remains for a royal burial (2 Samuel 21:12-14), underscoring that honoring leadership honors the Lord.

summary

1 Samuel 31:11 shows loyal hearts reacting to grievous news. The men of Jabesh-gilead remember Saul’s past deliverance, hear of his public humiliation, and move decisively to restore his honor. Their example teaches believers to let gratitude guide courageous action, to defend God’s reputation when it is maligned, and to respect the offices God establishes—even when leaders falter.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) The inhabitants of Jabesh-Gilead.--The memory of the splendid feat of arms of their young king Saul, when he gallantly rescued their city (1Samuel 11:1-11) years before, when they were threatened with deadly peril by the Ammonites, was still fresh in the city of Jabesh-Gilead, and they burned to rescue the body of their hero from shame. It was singular how that first deed of splendid patriotism, done in the early fervour of his consecration, bore fruit after so many long years.

"Good deeds immortal are--they cannot die;

Unscathed by envious blight, or withering frost,

They live, and bud and bloom, and men partake

Still of their freshness, and are strong thereby."

AYTOUN.

Jabesh-Gilead, a city of Manasseh, on the further side of Jordan, on the road from Pella to Gerasa. perhaps about fourteen miles from Beth-shan (see Judges 21:8, and following). Its name still survives in the Wady Yabez, running down to the east bank of Jordan, near the head of which are still visible some ruins named El Deir, which Robinson has identified with Jabesh-Gilead.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - Jabesh-Gilead. Eusebius describes this place as situated on the road from Pella to Gerasa, and therefore it would be much nearer the Jordan than Mahanaim, and probably was not more than twelve or fourteen miles distant from Beth-shan. The people there had not forgotten how bravely Saul had saved them, and now showed their gratitude by rescuing his remains from disgrace.
