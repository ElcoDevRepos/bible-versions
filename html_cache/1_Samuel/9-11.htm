Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And as they were climbing the hill to the city
This phrase sets the scene for Saul and his servant's journey to find the seer, Samuel. The ascent to the city suggests a location of significance, often associated with places of worship or governance in ancient Israel. Cities were typically built on elevated ground for defense purposes. The act of climbing indicates effort and anticipation, reflecting the spiritual journey Saul is unknowingly undertaking towards his anointing as king.

they met some young women coming out to draw water
Drawing water was a daily task typically performed by women in ancient Near Eastern cultures. Wells were social gathering places, and meeting women at a well is a recurring biblical motif, seen in the stories of Rebekah (Genesis 24) and Rachel (Genesis 29). This encounter is providential, as it provides Saul with the necessary information to find Samuel. The presence of young women highlights the normalcy of the scene, yet it is a divinely orchestrated moment leading to significant events.

and asked, “Is the seer here?”
The term "seer" refers to a prophet, one who receives visions from God. At this time, the term was commonly used before "prophet" became more prevalent. Saul's inquiry shows his respect and recognition of the seer's role as a spiritual guide. This question marks the beginning of Saul's direct interaction with divine guidance, as Samuel, the seer, is about to reveal God's plan for him. The use of "seer" connects to the broader biblical theme of God providing guidance and revelation through chosen individuals.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Saul
The son of Kish, from the tribe of Benjamin, who is on a journey to find his father's lost donkeys. This journey leads to his anointing as the first king of Israel.

2. Servant
Accompanies Saul on his journey and suggests seeking the guidance of a seer, demonstrating faith and practicality.

3. Young Women
They are drawing water, a common task in ancient times, and provide directions to Saul and his servant, showing the role of women in daily life and divine encounters.

4. Seer (Samuel)
The prophet and judge of Israel, who is later revealed to be the one Saul is seeking. The term "seer" (Hebrew: "ro'eh") indicates one who sees visions or has insight from God.

5. City
The unnamed city where Samuel resides, representing a place of divine appointment and revelation.
Teaching Points
Divine Appointments
God orchestrates events and encounters in our lives for His purposes. We should be attentive to seemingly ordinary moments that may have divine significance.

Seeking Godly Counsel
Like Saul and his servant seeking the seer, we should seek guidance from those who are spiritually mature and have insight from God.

Role of Women
The young women play a crucial role in directing Saul. This highlights the importance of women in God's plans and the value of their contributions.

Faith in Action
The servant's suggestion to seek the seer demonstrates faith in action. We should be proactive in seeking God's will and direction in our lives.

Preparation for Leadership
Saul's journey is part of his preparation for leadership. God often uses ordinary circumstances to prepare us for future roles and responsibilities.

What Does 1 Samuel 9:11 Mean
And as they were climbing the hill to the city

• Saul and his servant are literally ascending the slope toward Ramah, Samuel’s hometown. This ordinary climb is part of God’s precise orchestration of events, echoing Psalm 121:1–2 where help comes “from the LORD, the Maker of heaven and earth.”

• Hills and high places often mark significant encounters with God (Isaiah 2:3); their upward journey foreshadows the spiritual elevation Saul is about to experience when anointed.

• The straightforward narrative underscores that God directs even simple travel details, just as He guided Abraham’s servant to Rebekah in Genesis 24:27.

they met some young women coming out to draw water

• Wells were community hubs—everyday settings where God frequently intervenes (Genesis 24:11–16; Exodus 2:16–17; John 4:7).

• The timing is perfect: the women are “coming out,” not yet busy, ready to give directions. This highlights Proverbs 16:9—“A man’s heart plans his way, but the LORD determines his steps.”

• God often uses ordinary people—here unnamed young women—to advance His larger redemptive plan. Their routine task becomes a link in leading Israel’s first king to God’s prophet.

and asked, “Is the seer here?”

• In Saul’s day “seer” was the common term for a prophet (1 Samuel 9:9). Asking for the seer shows humility and recognition that divine guidance comes through God-appointed servants (1 Samuel 3:20).

• Saul seeks practical help—finding lost donkeys—yet God is steering him toward a calling far greater, reminding us of Ephesians 3:20 where the Lord works “above all we ask or think.”

• Respect for the seer emphasizes the need to consult God’s revealed word before acting (2 Kings 4:9; Psalm 119:105). The literal question, “Is the seer here?” still invites each reader to locate and listen to God’s messenger today through Scripture.

summary

1 Samuel 9:11 portrays an ordinary hillside climb, an everyday water-drawing scene, and a simple inquiry—all divinely synchronized. God uses commonplace moments and unassuming people to guide Saul to Samuel, setting the stage for Israel’s monarchy. The verse reminds us that every step, meeting, and question can be part of God’s precise, purposeful leading when we seek Him and honor His word.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 11, 12. - As they went up. Ramah was situated on a double hill, whence its name Ramathaim (1 Samuel 1:1). As, then, they go up the ascent - so the Hebrew, literally - they meet maidens on the way to the well, and ask them, Is the seer - the roeh - here? They answer, Yes; behold, he is before you. I.e. they are to go straightforward, and farther on in the town they will find him. He came today to the city. As Saul's servant knew that this city was Samuel's abode, the words must mean that he had just returned from visiting one of those places, probably, to which he was in the habit of going as judge. From 1 Samuel 16:2 we learn that Samuel went occasionally even to distant places to perform priestly duties. In the high place. Hebrew, Bamah. Samuel, we read, had built an altar at Ramah (1 Samuel 7:17), and probably the present sacrifice was to be offered upon it. Such altars, and the worship of the true God upon high places, were at this time recognised as right, and were, in fact, in accordance with, and were even the remains of, the old patriarchal religion. But gradually they were condemned, partly because of the glowing sanctity of the temple, but chiefly because of the tendency of religious rites celebrated in such places to degenerate into nature-worship, and orgies such as the heathen were in the habit of holding on the tops of mountains and hills. We thus find in the Bible an illustration of the principle that rites and ceremonies (as not being of the essentials of religion) may be changed, or even abolished, if they are abused, or lead on to evil consequences.
