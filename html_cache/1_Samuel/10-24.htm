Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Samuel said to all the people
Samuel, the last judge and a prophet of Israel, plays a pivotal role in transitioning Israel from a theocracy to a monarchy. His authority and leadership are recognized by the people, and his words carry significant weight. This moment is a public declaration, emphasizing the communal aspect of Israel's governance and the importance of divine guidance in leadership decisions.

Do you see the one the LORD has chosen?
The selection of Saul as king is portrayed as a divine choice, underscoring the belief that God is actively involved in the leadership of His people. This echoes the biblical theme of God choosing leaders, as seen with Moses and David. The emphasis on divine selection highlights the sovereignty of God in the affairs of nations and individuals.

There is no one like him among all the people.
Saul's physical stature and appearance set him apart, as he is described earlier in the chapter as being taller than any of the people. This phrase suggests that Saul's unique qualities make him suitable for kingship, reflecting the cultural importance of physical presence and leadership. However, it also foreshadows the potential pitfalls of valuing outward appearance over inner character, a theme further explored in the selection of David.

And all the people shouted, “Long live the king!”
This acclamation signifies the people's acceptance and support of Saul as their king, marking a significant shift in Israel's governance. The phrase "Long live the king" is a traditional expression of loyalty and hope for a prosperous reign. This moment reflects the cultural practices of surrounding nations, where kingship was a common form of government, and highlights Israel's desire to be like other nations, as mentioned earlier in 1 Samuel 8.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Samuel
The last judge of Israel, a prophet, and a key figure in the transition from the period of judges to the monarchy. He anointed Saul as the first king of Israel.

2. Saul
The first king of Israel, chosen by God and anointed by Samuel. He was from the tribe of Benjamin and was noted for his impressive physical stature.

3. The People of Israel
The collective nation of Israel, who desired a king to lead them like other nations. They responded to Saul's anointing with enthusiasm.

4. Mizpah
The place where Samuel gathered the people to present Saul as king. It was a significant location for assemblies and covenant renewals.

5. The LORD (Yahweh)
The God of Israel, who chose Saul to be king. His choice reflects His sovereignty and involvement in the leadership of His people.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Leadership
God is actively involved in the selection of leaders. His choices may not always align with human expectations, but they serve His purposes.

The Role of Physical Appearance
Saul's impressive stature was a factor in his selection, reflecting human tendencies to value outward appearance. However, God looks at the heart, as seen in later scriptures.

The People's Response to Leadership
The enthusiastic response of the people ("Long live the king!") shows their desire for a human leader. This can serve as a reminder to seek God's guidance in our leadership choices.

The Importance of Obedience to God
Saul's anointing as king was a divine appointment, but his future success depended on his obedience to God's commands.

Transition and Change
The transition from judges to a monarchy was significant for Israel. Change can be challenging, but trusting in God's plan is crucial.

What Does 1 Samuel 10:24 Mean
Samuel said to all the people

Samuel, the prophet-judge, calls the gathered tribes to attention. By speaking “to all the people,” he publicly authenticates what God has already revealed to him in private (1 Samuel 9:15-17; 10:1).

• Samuel’s role is to relay God’s word without alteration—just as he did at Mizpah when he interceded for Israel (1 Samuel 7:5-9).

• The scene underscores that leadership in Israel is not secured by human politics but by divine appointment, exactly as the Lord had promised centuries earlier (Deuteronomy 17:14-15).

• The crowd’s presence means they are eyewitnesses; no rumor or secret deal shapes this moment (Acts 26:26 reminds us that God often acts “in the open”).

Do you see the one the LORD has chosen?

The spotlight shifts from Samuel to Saul, but the emphasis is on “the LORD.”

• God had already singled out Saul: “Tomorrow about this time I will send you a man” (1 Samuel 9:16).

• By asking, “Do you see…?” Samuel invites the people to recognize God’s handiwork, echoing the call in Psalm 66:5—“Come and see what God has done.”

• Their demand for a king (1 Samuel 8:5) is met, yet the Lord retains control. Saul is not a concession alone; he is divinely commissioned, anointed with oil (1 Samuel 10:1), and confirmed by prophetic signs (10:6-7).

• The phrase also anticipates Christ, the ultimately “Chosen One” (Luke 23:35), teaching that every earthly ruler points forward to the perfect King.

There is no one like him among all the people

Samuel notes Saul’s exceptional qualities.

• Physically, Saul “was a head taller than anyone else” (1 Samuel 9:2), an outward sign that matched Israel’s desire for a king who could “go out before us and fight our battles” (1 Samuel 8:20).

• Socially, Saul comes from the least clan of Benjamin, highlighting God’s pattern of exalting the humble (1 Samuel 9:21; cf. 1 Corinthians 1:27-29).

• Spiritually, God has “changed Saul’s heart” (1 Samuel 10:9), equipping him for the task. Though later failures will emerge, the initial declaration is true: at this moment, no one else combines these God-given traits.

• This uniqueness prefigures David’s later description—“a man after God’s own heart” (1 Samuel 13:14)—and ultimately Christ, of whom Revelation 5:2 declares, “Who is worthy?” Only One.

And all the people shouted, “Long live the king!”

The crowd’s acclamation seals Saul’s coronation.

• “Long live the king” becomes Israel’s royal salute, reappearing in 1 Kings 1:25 and 2 Kings 11:12.

• The unanimous shout signals covenantal agreement: the people accept Saul just as they earlier accepted the Lord’s thunderous victory at Mizpah (1 Samuel 7:10-12).

• National unity emerges; the tribes that were once fractured in Judges 21 now rally under a single monarch.

• Yet their cry also carries prophetic tension. Psalm 72:17 prays that the king’s “name endure forever,” ultimately satisfied only in Messiah Jesus, whose kingdom “will never end” (Luke 1:33).

summary

1 Samuel 10:24 records a watershed: God publicly presents Saul, the people affirm him, and the monarchy in Israel formally begins. Samuel highlights divine choice, Saul’s distinctive suitability, and the nation’s enthusiastic consent. The verse teaches that leadership originates with God, that He equips those He appoints, and that human affirmation should echo divine selection. While Saul’s reign will reveal human frailty, the passage points beyond him to the unfailing reign of Christ, the true and everlasting King.
