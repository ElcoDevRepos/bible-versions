And if any man said to him, “The fat must be burned first; then you may take whatever you want,”
In the sacrificial system outlined in Leviticus, the fat of the offering was to be burned on the altar as a pleasing aroma to the Lord (Leviticus 3:16). This was a commandment given by God, signifying the best part of the sacrifice being offered to Him. The priests, however, were entitled to certain portions of the sacrifice after the fat was burned (Leviticus 7:31-34). The man’s insistence on burning the fat first reflects a desire to adhere to the proper worship practices and honor God’s commandments. This phrase highlights the tension between the corrupt practices of Eli’s sons and the faithful Israelites who sought to follow God’s law.

the servant would reply, “No, you must give it to me right now.
The servant’s response indicates a blatant disregard for the sacrificial laws and a prioritization of personal gain over divine command. This reflects the moral and spiritual decline among the priesthood during this period. The demand for immediate gratification without regard for God’s instructions is a recurring theme in Scripture, often leading to judgment and downfall (e.g., Esau selling his birthright in Genesis 25:29-34). This behavior contrasts with the patience and obedience exemplified by figures like Abraham, who waited for God’s promises (Hebrews 6:15).

If you refuse, I will take it by force!”
The threat of force underscores the corruption and abuse of power by Eli’s sons, Hophni and Phinehas. Their actions were not only a violation of God’s law but also an exploitation of their position, leading to widespread contempt for the sacrificial system (1 Samuel 2:17). This abuse of power is reminiscent of other biblical accounts where leaders failed to uphold justice and righteousness, such as the wickedness of the judges in the book of Judges. The coercion and violence used here foreshadow the eventual downfall of Eli’s house, as prophesied by the man of God in 1 Samuel 2:27-36. This narrative sets the stage for the rise of Samuel, a faithful leader who would restore integrity to Israel’s worship practices.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Eli's Sons (Hophni and Phinehas)
The sons of Eli, the high priest, who were corrupt and abused their priestly duties, leading to God's judgment against them.

2. Shiloh
The religious center where the tabernacle was located during this period, and where Eli and his sons served as priests.

3. The Sacrificial System
The context of this verse involves the sacrificial offerings made by the Israelites, which were being mishandled by Eli's sons.

4. The Israelites
The people bringing sacrifices to the tabernacle, who were being exploited by the priests.

5. The Servant
Likely a servant of Eli's sons, who enforced their corrupt practices.
Teaching Points
Respect for God's Commands
The actions of Eli's sons demonstrate a blatant disregard for God's instructions. As believers, we must prioritize obedience to God's Word, respecting His commands in all aspects of life.

The Consequences of Sin
The account of Eli's sons serves as a warning about the consequences of sin and corruption, especially for those in positions of spiritual leadership.

Integrity in Leadership
Spiritual leaders are called to a higher standard. Eli's sons failed in their duties, reminding us of the importance of integrity and accountability in leadership roles.

The Importance of Proper Worship
Worship should be conducted in a manner that honors God. The improper handling of sacrifices by Eli's sons underscores the need for reverence and adherence to God's instructions in worship.

God's Justice
The eventual judgment on Eli's house illustrates that God is just and will not tolerate ongoing sin, encouraging us to live righteously and seek His forgiveness.

What Does 1 Samuel 2:16 Mean
And if any man said to him

• The scene unfolds at the tabernacle (1 Samuel 2:13-14), where worshippers bring peace offerings.

• Ordinary Israelites knew God’s commands; they dared to question the priests’ servant when things looked wrong—echoing Numbers 15:40 and Acts 17:11, where God’s people are commended for guarding His word.

“The fat must be burned first; then you may take whatever you want”

• Leviticus 3:3-5; 7:30-31 state that the fat belongs to the LORD and must be burned on the altar before anyone eats.

• Giving God His portion first honored Him (Proverbs 3:9) and reminded everyone that worship is about Him, not us (Psalm 29:2).

• Only after the fat was burned could the priest lawfully take the breast and right thigh allotted to him (Leviticus 7:32-34; Deuteronomy 18:3).

the servant would reply

• The servant represents Hophni and Phinehas (1 Samuel 2:12), acting on their orders.

• His response shows systemic corruption; when leaders sin, they often enlist others (2 Samuel 11:14-15; Proverbs 29:12).

• It also illustrates how sin spreads through a community if left unchecked (1 Corinthians 5:6).

“No, you must give it to me right now”

• Greed overruled reverence. Immediate gratification replaced patient obedience (Genesis 25:32-34; Philippians 3:19).

• By demanding raw meat before the fat was offered, they stole from God (Malachi 3:8) and from the people, who waited to share in the fellowship meal (Leviticus 7:15).

• Their attitude mocked God’s timing (Ecclesiastes 8:11) and ignored the priestly role of serving, not dominating (Numbers 18:7).

“If you refuse, I will take it by force!”

• Violence backed their greed—an outright abuse of spiritual authority (Micah 2:1-2; Matthew 23:4).

• Such coercion turned worship into robbery, the opposite of the shepherd-hearted leader pictured in John 10:11-13.

• God’s law warned that anyone eating fat from a sacrifice would be “cut off” (Leviticus 7:25). Their threat invited divine judgment, which soon came (1 Samuel 2:34; 4:11).

summary

1 Samuel 2:16 reveals priests who despised the LORD’s portion, bullied His people, and fed their own appetites. It underscores that worship must honor God’s clear commands, that spiritual leaders are accountable for protecting rather than exploiting the flock, and that unchecked sin invites swift judgment.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) And if not, I will take it by force.--The solemn ritual of the sacrifice was not only transgressed by these covetous, greedy, ministering priests, but the worshippers were compelled by force to yield to these new lawless customs, probably introduced by these sons of the high priest Eli.
