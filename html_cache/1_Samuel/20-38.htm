Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Jonathan cried out
Jonathan, the son of King Saul, is a key figure in the narrative of 1 Samuel. His loyalty to David, despite his father Saul's animosity towards David, highlights the theme of covenant friendship. Jonathan's actions here are part of a prearranged signal to communicate with David, who is hiding nearby. This moment underscores Jonathan's role as a mediator and protector, reflecting the biblical principle of sacrificial love and loyalty.

“Hurry! Make haste! Do not delay!”
These urgent commands emphasize the danger David is in due to Saul's intent to kill him. The repetition of the commands underscores the immediacy and seriousness of the situation. In the broader biblical context, urgency in response to God's guidance or in the face of danger is a recurring theme, as seen in the Exodus narrative when the Israelites had to leave Egypt quickly. This urgency also reflects the need for swift obedience to divine direction.

So the boy picked up the arrow
The boy, an unnamed servant, is unaware of the significance of his actions. His role is purely functional, yet it is crucial for the communication between Jonathan and David. This reflects the biblical theme of God using seemingly insignificant people and actions to fulfill His purposes. The arrow itself is a symbol of the message being sent, akin to how God often uses physical signs to convey spiritual truths.

and returned to his master.
The boy's return to Jonathan signifies the completion of the task. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, servants were expected to obey their masters without question, reflecting the hierarchical social structure of the time. This obedience can be seen as a parallel to the biblical call for believers to faithfully serve God, their ultimate Master. The return also signifies the end of the covert communication, allowing Jonathan and David to proceed with their plans based on the outcome of this signal.

Teaching Points
The Value of True Friendship
Jonathan and David's relationship exemplifies the biblical ideal of friendship, characterized by loyalty, sacrifice, and mutual support. In our lives, we should seek to cultivate friendships that reflect these values.

Courage in the Face of Danger
Jonathan's actions demonstrate courage and faithfulness, even when it puts him at odds with his father, King Saul. We are called to stand firm in our convictions and support what is right, even when it is difficult.

God's Sovereignty and Protection
Despite the danger David faces, God's providence is evident through Jonathan's intervention. This reminds us that God often uses people in our lives to accomplish His purposes and provide protection.

Communication and Trust
The secret communication between Jonathan and David required trust and careful planning. In our relationships, clear and honest communication is essential, especially in times of crisis.

What Does 1 Samuel 20:38 Mean
Then Jonathan cried out

“Then Jonathan cried out” (1 Samuel 20:38) shows Jonathan actively putting into motion the secret signal he and David had arranged earlier (v. 20–22). His cry is more than casual speech; it is an intentional code. Like Samuel’s youthful cry that directed Eli’s attention to God’s voice (1 Samuel 3:4), Jonathan’s shout directs David’s attention to the outcome of Saul’s anger. It confirms that Jonathan’s loyalty is anchored in covenant faithfulness rather than personal safety (cf. Proverbs 17:17; John 15:13).

“Hurry!”

“Hurry!” underscores urgency. The same word conveys swift obedience in Israel’s exodus preparations (Exodus 12:11) and Elijah’s flight from Jezebel (1 Kings 19:3). By using it here, Jonathan signals to David that lingering is dangerous; Saul’s murderous intent is real (1 Samuel 20:31). Urgency guards life and honors the divine mandate to preserve it (Proverbs 27:12).

“Make haste!”

“Make haste!” doubles the warning. Repetition in Scripture heightens seriousness, as with Jesus’ “Truly, truly” (John 3:3). Jonathan’s double command stresses that David must leave immediately—mirroring the haste with which Lot was led out of Sodom (Genesis 19:15–17). Faith often demands prompt action when God-given discernment reveals peril.

“Do not delay!”

“Do not delay!” seals the message. Delay would equal disobedience to life-saving counsel, much like Israel was told not to tarry when marching around Jericho (Joshua 6:10). Jonathan’s triple call (Hurry—Make haste—Do not delay) forms an emphatic crescendo, paralleling Psalm 119:60: “I hurried without hesitating to keep Your commandments.” Swift compliance aligns with trust in God’s providence.

So the boy picked up the arrow

“So the boy picked up the arrow” shows the servant unaware of the drama yet faithfully completing his assigned task. His simple obedience mirrors the unnamed servant in Genesis 24 who performs duties that advance God’s covenant purposes. The arrow, having served its covert role, becomes an object lesson: God can use ordinary tools to convey extraordinary truth (cf. Moses’ staff, Exodus 4:2–4).

and returned to his master

“and returned to his master” closes the scene with quiet faithfulness. The boy’s return frees Jonathan to meet David privately (vv. 41–42). It also illustrates proper submission to authority (Ephesians 6:5–8) and fulfills Jesus’ principle that “Whoever is faithful in very little is also faithful in much” (Luke 16:10). The unseen obedience of a servant contributes to God’s larger redemptive storyline.

summary

1 Samuel 20:38 captures Jonathan’s urgent signal that Saul’s hostility makes David’s immediate flight essential. Each shouted command escalates the seriousness, urging swift obedience. Meanwhile, the unnamed boy’s simple faithfulness facilitates covenant loyalty between friends and preserves the future king of Israel. Together, the verse reveals how God orchestrates deliverance through urgent warnings, prompt action, and humble obedience—all woven into His unfailing plan.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(38) Make speed, haste, stay not.--Although Jonathan, of course, trusted to a certain extent the youth (probably an armour-bearer) who was with him, still he hurried this attendant away, that he might not see David, who was close by in hiding, and who, after the sign, would presently appear in sight. The next clause (1Samuel 20:39) expressly tells us how this meeting was unknown and unwitnessed. The youth was sent to the city that Jonathan might be alone once more with David.
