Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then he had the tribe of Benjamin come forward by its clans
The selection process for Israel's first king involved the tribe of Benjamin, the smallest of the tribes (1 Samuel 9:21). This highlights God's sovereignty in choosing leaders, often selecting the least expected. The tribe of Benjamin had a tumultuous history, including near extinction due to civil war (Judges 20-21), yet God chose to bring forth a king from this tribe, demonstrating His redemptive purposes.

and the clan of Matri was selected
The clan of Matri is not mentioned elsewhere in the Bible, indicating its obscurity. This selection process, likely involving casting lots (Proverbs 16:33), underscores the divine guidance in leadership choices. The use of lots was a common practice in ancient Israel to discern God's will, reflecting a reliance on divine providence rather than human decision-making.

Finally, Saul son of Kish was selected
Saul's selection as king was a pivotal moment in Israel's history, transitioning from a theocracy to a monarchy. Saul, from the tribe of Benjamin, was chosen despite his initial reluctance and humility (1 Samuel 9:21). His selection fulfilled the people's demand for a king to lead them like other nations (1 Samuel 8:5), yet it also set the stage for future challenges, as God had warned about the implications of having a human king (1 Samuel 8:10-18).

But when they looked for him, they could not find him
Saul's absence at the moment of his selection reflects his initial hesitance and humility, as he was hiding among the baggage (1 Samuel 10:22). This reluctance can be seen as a sign of humility, but it also foreshadows his later struggles with insecurity and leadership. Saul's hiding contrasts with the boldness expected of a king, highlighting the tension between human frailty and divine calling.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereign Choice
God chooses leaders according to His divine will, often beyond human expectations or qualifications.

Humility in Leadership
Saul's initial humility is a reminder that true leadership begins with a humble heart, recognizing one's dependence on God.

The Role of Prophets
Samuel's role in anointing Saul underscores the importance of spiritual guidance and prophetic insight in leadership decisions.

Divine Timing and Revelation
God's plans unfold in His perfect timing, as seen in the selection process of Saul, teaching us patience and trust in God's timing.

Hidden Potential
Saul's initial hiding reflects how God often calls those who may not see their own potential, encouraging us to trust in God's vision for our lives.

What Does 1 Samuel 10:21 Mean
Then he had the tribe of Benjamin come forward by its clans

• By lot, Samuel narrows the field just as Joshua once did with Achan (Joshua 7:14), underscoring that “The lot is cast into the lap, but its every decision is from the LORD” (Proverbs 16:33).

• Every Israelite sees that the choice of a king is God-driven, not man-driven (1 Samuel 8:7).

• The spotlight on Benjamin, the smallest tribe (1 Samuel 9:21; Judges 20:47), reminds us that God delights in exalting what seems insignificant.

and the clan of Matri was selected

• The process moves from tribe to clan, confirming God’s precision in guidance (Numbers 26:55-56).

• Each step removes human manipulation; Samuel merely presides, much like the apostles casting lots for Matthias (Acts 1:24-26).

• No detail escapes the Lord—He knows where His chosen servant comes from (Psalm 139:1-4).

Finally, Saul son of Kish was selected

• God publicly validates what He told Samuel earlier: “This is the man of whom I spoke to you” (1 Samuel 9:17).

• Saul’s lineage answers Israel’s demand for “a king like all the nations” (1 Samuel 8:5) yet still fulfills God’s law that a king be an Israelite brother (Deuteronomy 17:15).

• That the choice rests on a Benjamite, not a Judahite, shows the monarchy is God’s prerogative, not tied to human expectation (cf. 1 Corinthians 1:27).

But when they looked for him, they could not find him

• Saul is hiding among the supplies (1 Samuel 10:22); his reluctance reveals humility mixed with fear, much like Moses’ hesitation (Exodus 4:10-13) and Jeremiah’s youthfulness concern (Jeremiah 1:6).

• God exposes him, teaching Israel that their king must first be found by the Lord before he can lead them.

• The scene foreshadows Saul’s later struggles with insecurity (1 Samuel 15:17), warning that private doubts can affect public leadership.

summary

1 Samuel 10:21 shows God’s sovereign, step-by-step choice of Israel’s first king, narrowing from tribe to clan to man. Each stage underscores divine control, transparency, and purpose. Saul’s initial hiding highlights the tension between God’s calling and human frailty, setting the stage for his reign and reminding us that while God’s choice is perfect, the human vessel remains dependent on ongoing obedience and trust in the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) The family of Matri was taken.--In none of the Benjamite genealogies connected with the royal house of Saul does this name occur. We cannot account for the omission. Ewald conjectures that the name Matri is a corruption from "Bikri" (see 1Chronicles 7:8).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - The family of Matri, or of the Matrites. Matri is not mentioned anywhere else; and numerous as are the omissions in the genealogies, we can scarcely suppose that the name of the head of one of the main subdivisions of a tribe could be passed over. The conjecture, therefore, is probable that Matri is a corruption of Bikri, i.e. a descendant of Becher, for whom see 1 Chronicles 7:8. After the lot had fallen upon this family they would next cast lots upon its smaller subdivisions, as in Joshua 7:17, 18, until at last they came to households, when first Kish, and finally Saul was taken. The latter, foreseeing that this would happen, had concealed himself. For though a noble change had taken place in him (ver. 9), yet no really worthy man was ever promoted to high office without having to overcome his own unwillingness, and no one probably ever worthily discharged solemn duties without having felt oppressed and humbled with the consciousness of his own unfitness to undertake them. As a matter of fact, Saul was now called to a most weighty responsibility, and he failed and was rejected, though not without proving that he was a man of extraordinary genius and power. And it never can be said of him that presumption was the cause of his fall, or that he hastily undertook serious duties in the spirit of light-hearted levity.
