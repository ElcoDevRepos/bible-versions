Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
As soon as you enter the city, you will find him before he goes up to the high place to eat.
This phrase sets the scene in the city of Ramah, where Samuel, the prophet, resides. The "high place" refers to a location used for worship and sacrifices, common in ancient Israel before the temple was built in Jerusalem. High places were often on elevated ground, symbolizing closeness to God. Samuel's presence at the high place indicates his role as a spiritual leader. The immediacy of "as soon as you enter" suggests divine orchestration, emphasizing God's providence in guiding Saul to Samuel.

The people will not eat until he comes, because he must bless the sacrifice;
In ancient Israel, it was customary for a priest or prophet to bless a sacrifice before it was consumed. This blessing was an invocation of God's presence and favor. Samuel's role in blessing the sacrifice underscores his authority and the respect he commanded among the people. It also highlights the importance of seeking God's blessing before partaking in communal meals, a practice that reflects the sacredness of fellowship and thanksgiving.

after that, the guests will eat.
The sequence of events—blessing followed by eating—illustrates the orderliness and reverence in worship practices. It also points to the communal aspect of sacrifices, where the act of eating together signifies unity and shared faith. This practice foreshadows the New Testament concept of the Lord's Supper, where believers partake in communion, remembering Christ's sacrifice.

Go up at once; you will find him.”
The urgency in "go up at once" reflects the divine timing and purpose in Saul's encounter with Samuel. This meeting is pivotal, as it leads to Saul's anointing as the first king of Israel. The phrase "you will find him" assures Saul of God's guidance and the certainty of His plans. This encounter is a turning point in Israel's history, transitioning from the period of judges to the monarchy, and it sets the stage for the eventual rise of David, a type of Christ, who would establish a kingdom foreshadowing the eternal reign of Jesus.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Saul

2. Samuel

3. High Place

4. City

5. Sacrifice

Teaching Points
The Role of Divine Timing
God's timing is perfect, as seen in the precise moment Saul is to meet Samuel. Trust in God's timing in your own life.

The Importance of Spiritual Leadership
Samuel's role in blessing the sacrifice underscores the importance of spiritual leaders in guiding and blessing the community. Seek and respect godly leadership.

Preparation for God's Calling
Saul's journey to find donkeys leads to his anointing as king. Be open to God's calling, even in mundane tasks.

The Significance of Blessing
The act of blessing the sacrifice before eating highlights the importance of acknowledging God’s provision. Cultivate a habit of gratitude and blessing in your daily life.

Community and Worship
The communal aspect of waiting for Samuel to bless the sacrifice before eating emphasizes the importance of unity in worship. Engage actively in your faith community.

What Does 1 Samuel 9:13 Mean
As soon as you enter the city

• God arranged precise timing for Saul and Samuel to meet.

• Psalm 37:23 reminds us, “The steps of a man are ordered by the LORD,” underscoring that what looked like coincidence was divine appointment.

• Acts 10:17-22 shows a similar orchestration when Peter meets Cornelius’ messengers at exactly the right moment.

you will find him

• Samuel was where God said he would be, illustrating Numbers 23:19—“Has He spoken, and will He not fulfill it?”

• Like Eliezer finding Rebekah in Genesis 24:45, Saul’s finding of Samuel proves God’s faithful guidance.

before he goes up to the high place to eat

• High places were common worship sites before the temple (1 Kings 3:2-4).

• Samuel’s priority was worship before fellowship, echoing Matthew 6:33, “Seek first the kingdom of God.”

The people will not eat until he comes

• Leadership waits for the man of God, paralleling Joshua 3:1-4 where Israel waited for the priests before crossing the Jordan.

• Respecting God-appointed order protects unity, as Paul later teaches in 1 Corinthians 14:40.

because he must bless the sacrifice

• Samuel, functioning as judge and prophet, pronounced God’s blessing—similar to Moses in Exodus 18:12 and Jesus blessing the bread in Luke 24:30.

• This practice reflects Deuteronomy 8:10, “When you have eaten and are satisfied, you shall bless the LORD your God.”

after that, the guests will eat

• Fellowship follows worship. Acts 2:46 records believers “breaking bread from house to house” after devotion to teaching and prayer.

• The order teaches that provision flows from God’s presence; see John 6:11 where Jesus blesses before distributing loaves.

Go up at once; you will find him

• Immediate obedience was required. Delayed response could miss God’s prepared moment (Hebrews 3:15).

• Saul’s swift action mirrors Philip’s in Acts 8:27, who “got up and went,” leading to divine encounter.

summary

1 Samuel 9:13 portrays God’s meticulous orchestration of events so Saul meets Samuel at the exact moment of worship preparation. The verse highlights:

• God’s sovereign timing.

• The honor due to His appointed servant.

• The necessity of blessing before enjoyment.

Taken literally, the scene assures believers that when God directs, He aligns circumstances, leaders, and timing to accomplish His purpose, inviting us to obey promptly and expectantly.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) He doth bless the sacrifice.--It has been well remarked that we have here, in this note of the people's conduct at the sacrificial banquet of "Raman of the Watchers," a very early instance of the devout practice among the Hebrews of asking a blessing on meals.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - As soon as... straightway. This is too forcible a rendering of the Hebrew particles, and makes the talk of these water-carriers even more garrulous than it is in the original. The latter word should be omitted, as they simply say that on entering the city Saul and his servant would easily find Samuel; for he would not go up to the feast till all was ready, nor would the people begin till he had arrived, because it was his office to bless the sacrificial banquet. The pious custom of asking a blessing on meals, our Lord's "giving of thanks," is inherited by us from the Jews.
