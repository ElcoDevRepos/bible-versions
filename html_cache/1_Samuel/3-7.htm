Now Samuel did not yet know the LORD
Samuel, though serving in the temple under Eli, had not yet experienced a personal revelation or relationship with God. This highlights the distinction between religious service and personal knowledge of God. In the Hebrew context, "knowing" the LORD implies an intimate, covenantal relationship, as seen in Genesis 4:1 where Adam "knew" Eve. Samuel's lack of knowledge signifies that he had not yet encountered God in a direct, transformative way. This moment foreshadows the personal call and revelation that would soon come to Samuel, marking the beginning of his prophetic ministry.

because the word of the LORD had not yet been revealed to him
The "word of the LORD" refers to divine communication, often through visions or direct speech, which was a common way God communicated with His prophets, as seen with Moses in Exodus 3 and Isaiah in Isaiah 6. At this point, Samuel had not received such a revelation, indicating that prophetic insight and understanding are gifts from God, not based on human effort or position. This phrase underscores the importance of divine initiative in establishing a relationship with His chosen servants. It also sets the stage for the subsequent verses where God calls Samuel, marking a pivotal moment in Israel's history as God begins to speak through him, transitioning from the period of the judges to the era of the prophets.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Samuel
A young boy serving in the temple under the priest Eli. He is dedicated to God by his mother, Hannah, and is being raised in the temple.

2. Eli
The high priest of Israel at the time, responsible for mentoring Samuel. He plays a crucial role in guiding Samuel to recognize God's voice.

3. The Temple
The place where Samuel serves and where God calls out to him. It is a central place of worship and spiritual activity in Israel.

4. The LORD
The God of Israel, who is about to reveal Himself to Samuel, marking the beginning of Samuel's prophetic ministry.

5. The Word of the LORD
Refers to God's communication and revelation, which had not yet been personally experienced by Samuel at this point.
Teaching Points
Personal Revelation is Key
Knowing about God is different from knowing God personally. Samuel's experience teaches us the importance of personal revelation and relationship with God.

Mentorship in Faith
Eli's role in guiding Samuel highlights the importance of spiritual mentorship. We should seek and provide guidance in our faith communities.

Recognizing God's Voice
Like Samuel, we must learn to discern God's voice amidst the noise of life. This requires attentiveness and a heart open to God's leading.

Spiritual Growth is a Process
Samuel's journey shows that spiritual maturity and understanding develop over time. Patience and perseverance are essential in our walk with God.

The Role of Scripture
The Word of the LORD is central to knowing God. Regular engagement with Scripture is crucial for spiritual growth and understanding God's will.

What Does 1 Samuel 3:7 Mean
Now Samuel

- The boy is already serving: “And the boy Samuel ministered to the LORD before Eli” (1 Samuel 3:1).

- He grew up in a godly environment (1 Samuel 2:18, 26), yet God waited to speak until the perfect time—just as He later did with John the Baptist (Luke 1:80) and even Jesus (Luke 2:52).

- Context matters: “in those days the word of the LORD was rare” (1 Samuel 3:1). Samuel’s calling stands out precisely because the nation had grown dull to God’s voice (Judges 21:25).

did not yet know the LORD

- “Know” points to personal relationship, not mere information. Compare the tragic line, “another generation rose up who knew neither the LORD nor the works He had done” (Judges 2:10).

- Serving in the tabernacle did not automatically equal intimacy. Religious activity can precede real encounter (Matthew 7:22–23).

- The New Testament echoes this: “Now this is eternal life, that they may know You” (John 17:3). Samuel is on the cusp of that kind of knowledge.

because the word of the LORD

- God makes Himself known chiefly through His spoken, revealed word. From Abram (“the word of the LORD came to Abram,” Genesis 15:1) to the prophets (“the word of the LORD came to Jeremiah,” Jeremiah 1:4), the pattern is constant.

- Scripture itself testifies, “The unfolding of Your words gives light” (Psalm 119:130). Until that unfolding occurs, real knowledge of God remains partial.

had not yet been revealed to him

- Revelation is God’s initiative. “Surely the Lord GOD does nothing without revealing His plan to His servants the prophets” (Amos 3:7).

- In Samuel’s case the first revelation will be startling (1 Samuel 3:11) and prophetic, setting the course of his life.

- Jesus underscores the principle: “this was not revealed to you by flesh and blood, but by My Father” (Matthew 16:17). What Samuel lacks on his own, heaven is about to supply.

- When God does reveal Himself, He expects obedience; Samuel’s quick response (“Speak, for Your servant is listening,” 1 Samuel 3:10) shows the heart God seeks (Isaiah 66:2).

summary

Samuel’s upbringing and ministry duties could not substitute for a personal encounter with God. 1 Samuel 3:7 explains the gap: he served faithfully, yet intimacy awaited divine revelation. God bridged that gap through His word, turning a ministering boy into a knowing prophet. The verse reminds us that true knowledge of the LORD begins when He speaks and we listen—an unchanging pattern from Genesis to Revelation.
