Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then he turned from him toward another
David, having been dismissed by his brother Eliab, turns away to continue his inquiry. This action demonstrates David's determination and focus on the task at hand, undeterred by familial opposition. In the broader biblical narrative, this reflects the theme of perseverance in the face of discouragement, as seen in other figures like Joseph and Moses. David's turning away can also be seen as a foreshadowing of his future leadership, where he often had to make decisions independent of others' opinions.

and asked about the offer
David's inquiry about the offer shows his interest in the reward for defeating Goliath, which included wealth, the king's daughter in marriage, and tax exemption for his family. This reflects the cultural context of the time, where such rewards were significant incentives. It also highlights David's practical nature and his understanding of the stakes involved. Theologically, this can be seen as a precursor to the New Testament teaching of counting the cost before undertaking a task (Luke 14:28).

and those people answered him just as the first ones had answered
The repetition of the answer indicates the consistency of the message about the reward, emphasizing its importance and the seriousness of the challenge. This consistency can be seen as a reflection of God's unchanging promises, a theme prevalent throughout Scripture. The people's response also underscores the widespread fear and reluctance among the Israelites, contrasting with David's faith and courage. This sets the stage for David's role as a type of Christ, who also faced opposition and disbelief but remained steadfast in His mission.

Teaching Points
Perseverance in Seeking Truth
David's repeated questioning shows his determination to understand the situation fully. As believers, we should persist in seeking God's truth, even when faced with opposition or misunderstanding.

Faith Over Fear
David's actions contrast with the fear of the Israelite army. This teaches us to place our faith in God rather than succumbing to fear of worldly challenges.

Responding to Criticism
David does not let Eliab's criticism deter him. We should learn to respond to criticism with grace and continue to pursue God's calling.

God's Perspective Over Human Perspective
David's focus is on God's power and promises, not on the apparent strength of Goliath. We should align our perspective with God's, trusting in His sovereignty.

What Does 1 Samuel 17:30 Mean
Then he turned

“Then he turned…” (1 Samuel 17:30)

• David deliberately pivots away from Eliab’s angry words (vv. 28–29), refusing to let ridicule stall his faith.

• Turning is often the first step of obedience—shifting our gaze from discouragement to divine opportunity (Proverbs 29:25; Luke 9:62).

• Like Jesus “turned and rebuked” Peter when Peter’s words opposed God’s plan (Matthew 16:23), David redirects his attention to what God is about to do.

from him toward another

“…from him toward another…”

• David leaves the critic but stays in the conversation, showing that rejection by one person need not end a God-given assignment (Acts 18:6).

• Moving on protects unity; it also guards the heart against bitterness (Ephesians 4:31–32).

• Scripture commends shaking dust off where truth is unwelcome, yet pressing forward where hearts are open (Mark 6:11).

and asked about the offer

“…and asked about the offer…”

• The “offer” is Saul’s promise of great riches, the king’s daughter, and tax exemption for the victor over Goliath (v. 25).

• David’s repeated question is not greed but confirmation; he wants clarity on what God is setting before Israel (Hebrews 11:6).

• Knowing the reward strengthens resolve, as seen when Caleb fixed on Hebron’s promise (Joshua 14:12).

and those people answered him

“…and those people answered him…”

• Multiple witnesses confirm the facts (Deuteronomy 19:15; Matthew 18:16).

• Israel’s soldiers, though fearful, still recite the reward word-for-word, underscoring its authenticity.

• Hearing it from others solidifies David’s conviction that God’s victory will bless the nation.

just as the first ones had answered

“…just as the first ones had answered.”

• Repetition signals certainty; God often doubles a message to show it is fixed (Genesis 41:32).

• Consistent testimony removes doubt and readies David to step onto the battlefield (2 Corinthians 13:1).

• The unchanging report contrasts with the shifting courage of Israel, highlighting God’s steady purpose (James 1:17).

summary

David’s quick turn away from Eliab, his persistence in verifying Saul’s promise, and the unanimous confirmation from the troops reveal a heart unswayed by criticism and anchored in God’s unfolding plan. The verse teaches us to:

• Refuse distractions that undermine faith.

• Seek clear confirmation of God’s promises.

• Receive counsel from multiple witnesses.

With these attitudes, we, like David, can face giants fully assured that the Lord’s reward is real and His victory certain.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 30, 31. - Manner. Literally, word, the noun translated cause in ver. 29, and meaning in both verses "conversation." It occurs here thrice, the Hebrew being, "And he spake according to this word: and the people returned him a word according to the former word." And as David thus persisted in his indignant remonstrances at the ranks of the living God being thus dishonoured by no man accepting the challenge, they rehearsed them before Saul, who thereupon sent for him. And thus David a second time, and under very different circumstances, found himself again standing in the king's presence.
