So David sent ten young men and instructed them
David, at this point in his life, is a fugitive from King Saul, leading a group of men in the wilderness. The sending of ten young men indicates a formal delegation, suggesting the importance of the message and the respect David intends to show. In ancient Near Eastern culture, sending a group rather than a single messenger was a sign of respect and seriousness. The number ten can also symbolize completeness or a full representation, as seen in other biblical contexts such as the Ten Commandments.

Go up to Nabal at Carmel
Carmel here refers to a town in the territory of Judah, not the more famous Mount Carmel. This area was known for its fertile land and agricultural productivity. Nabal, whose name means "fool" in Hebrew, is a wealthy but harsh man, as described later in the chapter. The geographical setting is significant because it highlights the contrast between David's current life in the wilderness and Nabal's prosperity. This encounter at Carmel is a pivotal moment that demonstrates the tension between David's anointed future as king and his current status as an outlaw.

Greet him in my name
David instructs his men to greet Nabal in his name, which implies a request for hospitality and provision. In the ancient Near East, hospitality was a deeply ingrained cultural value, and it was customary to provide for travelers, especially those who had shown kindness or protection, as David's men had done for Nabal's shepherds. The use of his name signifies authority and the expectation of a favorable response due to David's reputation. This phrase also foreshadows the New Testament teaching of acting in the name of Christ, where believers are called to represent Jesus and His character in their interactions with others.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The future king of Israel, anointed by Samuel, who is currently leading a group of men while on the run from King Saul. In this passage, David is seeking provisions for his men.

2. Nabal
A wealthy but foolish man living in Carmel. His name means "fool" in Hebrew, and he is known for his harsh and surly behavior.

3. Carmel
A town in the territory of Judah, not to be confused with Mount Carmel. It is the location where Nabal resides and where David sends his men.

4. David's Young Men
A group of ten young men sent by David to deliver a message to Nabal, seeking his favor and provisions.

5. Event
David's request for provisions from Nabal during the time of sheep shearing, a period of festivity and abundance.
Teaching Points
Respectful Communication
David instructs his men to greet Nabal in his name, showing the importance of respectful and humble communication, even when making requests.

Seeking Peace
David's approach to Nabal is peaceful and diplomatic, reflecting a Christ-like attitude of seeking peace and goodwill.

Understanding Authority
David's use of his name in the greeting signifies the authority and respect he carries, reminding us of the power of acting under the authority of Christ.

Wisdom in Requests
The passage encourages believers to be wise and discerning in how they make requests, considering the character and disposition of the person they are addressing.

Trusting God's Provision
Despite Nabal's response, David's initial faith in God's provision is evident, teaching us to trust God to meet our needs through various means.

What Does 1 Samuel 25:5 Mean
So David sent ten young men

• David, already anointed yet still awaiting the throne (1 Samuel 16:13; 2 Samuel 5:3), leads with measured diplomacy rather than force.

• Ten is a sizeable group—enough to convey honor and seriousness (Genesis 24:10; 2 Samuel 15:18).

• The action reflects shepherd-king compassion: he provides an official approach to secure provision for his followers instead of raiding (1 Samuel 25:13 compared with 23:5).

• By sending messengers, David safeguards against needless bloodshed, echoing his earlier restraint with Saul (1 Samuel 24:4–7).

and instructed them

• David’s words shape the mission; obedience to a godly leader mirrors Israel’s call to heed the Lord’s voice (Joshua 1:7; John 15:14).

• Clear guidance averts confusion, much like Moses’ precise directions to Israel (Exodus 12:21–28).

• David’s foresight anticipates potential conflict and lays groundwork for peace (Proverbs 15:1; 1 Samuel 25:33).

Go up to Nabal at Carmel

• Carmel, the fertile ridge where Saul once erected a monument to himself (1 Samuel 15:12), now hosts Nabal’s vast flocks—an irony underscoring contrasts between pride and humility.

• Nabal’s name means “fool” (1 Samuel 25:25), foreshadowing his response; still, David extends courtesy first, honoring the principle of offering peace before battle (Deuteronomy 20:10).

• The upward journey hints at effort and respect—approaching a landowner on his own turf, paralleling the servants who “went up” to petition Joseph (Genesis 43:15).

Greet him in my name

• Representing David carries the weight of his character and covenant faithfulness (1 Samuel 17:45; 25:6).

• A proper greeting—“Peace to you, your household, and all that is yours” (1 Samuel 25:6)—echoes the priestly blessing (Numbers 6:24–26).

• Acting “in my name” foreshadows New-Testament ambassadorship: “We are therefore Christ’s ambassadors” (2 Corinthians 5:20) and “whatever you do…do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus” (Colossians 3:17).

• David models respectful negotiation, trusting the Lord to provide while keeping his conscience clear (Psalm 37:3).

summary

David’s simple directive in 1 Samuel 25:5 reveals a heart committed to peace, order, and godly representation. By sending ten messengers, giving exact instructions, approaching Nabal respectfully at Carmel, and ensuring the greeting bears his name, David demonstrates prudent leadership rooted in trust that God honors righteous, peace-seeking initiative.
