but Eli called to him
Eli, the high priest and judge of Israel, represents the spiritual leadership of the nation during a time of moral and spiritual decline. His calling to Samuel signifies a moment of transition, as God is about to reveal His word through Samuel, marking a shift in prophetic leadership. This calling also reflects the mentorship relationship between Eli and Samuel, highlighting the importance of spiritual guidance and discipleship.

and said, “Samuel, my son.”
Eli's address to Samuel as "my son" indicates a close, familial relationship, despite Samuel not being his biological child. This reflects the cultural practice of mentorship and spiritual fatherhood in ancient Israel. It also underscores Eli's affection and responsibility for Samuel, who was dedicated to the Lord by his mother, Hannah, and raised in the temple. This phrase can be seen as a foreshadowing of the father-son relationship between God and His people, and ultimately between God the Father and Jesus Christ.

“Here I am,” answered Samuel.
Samuel's response demonstrates his readiness and willingness to serve, a characteristic that defines his future role as a prophet. This phrase echoes the responses of other biblical figures like Abraham (Genesis 22:1) and Moses (Exodus 3:4), who also answered God's call with readiness. It signifies obedience and attentiveness to God's voice, qualities essential for those called to serve Him. Samuel's response sets the stage for his prophetic ministry, which will play a crucial role in Israel's history, including the anointing of its first kings.

What Does 1 Samuel 3:16 Mean
But Eli called to him and said

Eli, the aging high priest who had mentored young Samuel since infancy, hears movement in the darkness of the tabernacle courtyard and speaks up. His simple call reveals several layers:

• Authority—Eli remains Israel’s recognized spiritual leader (1 Samuel 3:2–3).

• Responsibility—he knows God may be speaking and wants clarity (1 Samuel 3:8).

• Relationship—Eli’s voice still matters to Samuel, illustrating God’s design for older believers to guide younger ones (Deuteronomy 6:6–7; Titus 2:2–6).

The verse reminds us that spiritual discernment is nurtured in community; Samuel’s call from the Lord is confirmed through Eli’s guidance (Proverbs 11:14).

“Samuel, my son.”

These tender words reveal more than affection:

• Adoption of faith—Samuel belongs to God, but Eli treats him as covenant family (1 Samuel 2:11).

• Pastoral care—like Paul calling Timothy “my true son” (1 Timothy 1:2), Eli speaks with warmth that steadies a nervous boy who has just heard God’s heavy message (1 Samuel 3:11).

• Urgency—Eli must hear what the Lord said, because the fate of his own household hangs in the balance (1 Samuel 2:27–36).

The phrase models how spiritual fathers and mothers nurture younger believers with dignity and love (1 Thessalonians 2:7–11).

“Here I am,” answered Samuel.

Samuel’s reply mirrors the response of faithful servants throughout Scripture:

• Immediate availability—he answers without delay, echoing Abraham (Genesis 22:1), Jacob (Genesis 31:11), Moses (Exodus 3:4), and Isaiah (Isaiah 6:8).

• Humble readiness—Samuel does not know what Eli will ask, yet he stands ready to obey (James 1:22).

• Growing maturity—this is Samuel’s fourth “Here I am” in the chapter; each time affirms his developing sensitivity to God’s voice (1 Samuel 3:4–10).

The verse challenges believers to cultivate an instinctive “yes, Lord” posture—attentive, teachable, and eager to act (Romans 12:1–2).

summary

1 Samuel 3:16 captures a quiet exchange pregnant with meaning: the elder priest calls, the beloved protégé listens, and availability meets authority. It showcases the beauty of multi-generational discipleship, the power of tender words, and a heart that says “Here I am” to both human mentors and the living God.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 16, 17, 18. - God do so to thee, etc. This adjuration shows how great had been the agony of Eli's suspense, yet, true to his sluggish nature, he had waited patiently till the morning came. Then he summons Samuel to him, calling him lovingly my son, and everything tends to show that there was a real affection between the two. He next asks, What is the thing that he hath said unto thee? The A.V. greatly weakens this by inserting the words "The Lord." The original is far more suggestive. Put quite indefinitely, it says, "Whoever or whatsoever be thy visitor, yet tell me all." Then, when Eli has heard the message, he says, It is Jehovah. Though he had not had the courage to do what was right, yet his submission to God, and the humility of his resignation, prove that the Holy Ghost had in these years of waiting being doing its work upon the old man's heart. Eli's adjuration, we must further note, was equivalent to putting Samuel upon his oath, so that any concealment on his part would have involved the sin of perjury.
