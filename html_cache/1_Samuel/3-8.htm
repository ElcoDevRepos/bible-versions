Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Once again, for the third time, the LORD called to Samuel.
The repetition of God's call to Samuel highlights the persistence and patience of God in communicating with His chosen servants. In biblical numerology, the number three often signifies completeness or divine perfection, as seen in the Trinity. This calling occurs during a time when "the word of the LORD was rare" (1 Samuel 3:1), indicating a period of spiritual drought in Israel. The LORD's direct communication with Samuel marks a significant turning point in Israel's history, as Samuel would become a pivotal prophet and judge.

He got up, went to Eli, and said, “Here I am, for you have called me.”
Samuel's response demonstrates his obedience and respect for Eli, his mentor and the high priest. This reflects the cultural importance of honoring one's elders and leaders. Samuel's willingness to serve is reminiscent of Isaiah's response to God's call, "Here am I. Send me!" (Isaiah 6:8). The phrase "Here I am" is a common biblical expression of readiness and availability to God's service, seen in figures like Abraham (Genesis 22:1) and Moses (Exodus 3:4).

Then Eli realized that it was the LORD who was calling the boy.
Eli's realization signifies a moment of spiritual insight, despite his earlier failures as a priest and father. This acknowledgment marks a transition in leadership from Eli to Samuel, as Eli recognizes the divine calling on Samuel's life. The term "boy" emphasizes Samuel's youth, underscoring the biblical theme that God often chooses the unlikely or the young to fulfill His purposes, as seen with David (1 Samuel 16:11-13) and Timothy (1 Timothy 4:12). Eli's guidance to Samuel to respond to the LORD's call reflects his role in preparing the next generation for God's work.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Samuel
A young boy dedicated to the Lord by his mother, Hannah, serving under the priest Eli in the tabernacle at Shiloh. Samuel is being called by God for the first time in this passage.

2. Eli
The high priest of Israel at the time, who is responsible for mentoring Samuel. Eli eventually discerns that it is God calling Samuel.

3. The LORD
The God of Israel, who is initiating communication with Samuel, indicating the beginning of Samuel's prophetic ministry.

4. Shiloh
The religious center of Israel at the time, where the tabernacle and the Ark of the Covenant were located. It is the setting for this divine encounter.

5. The Calling
This event marks the beginning of Samuel's role as a prophet, as God directly communicates with him, setting the stage for his future leadership in Israel.
Teaching Points
God's Persistent Call
God often calls us multiple times, patiently waiting for us to recognize His voice. We should be attentive and open to His calling in our lives.

Mentorship and Guidance
Eli's role in helping Samuel recognize God's voice highlights the importance of spiritual mentors who can guide us in our faith journey.

Recognizing God's Voice
Like Samuel, we may not always recognize God's voice immediately. We should seek discernment and wisdom to understand when God is speaking to us.

Obedience to God's Call
Samuel's willingness to respond to God's call is a model of obedience. We should be ready to say, "Here I am," when God calls us to action.

The Role of Prayer and Reflection
Regular prayer and reflection can help us become more attuned to God's voice, just as Samuel was in a place of service and worship when he heard God.

What Does 1 Samuel 3:8 Mean
The third call from the LORD

“Once again, for the third time, the LORD called to Samuel.” (1 Samuel 3:8a)

• Scripture records this as a literal, audible event, just as the earlier calls were (see 1 Samuel 3:4, 6).

• The repetition highlights God’s persistence; He does not give up on getting His servant’s attention (compare Revelation 3:20, where Christ keeps on knocking).

• In an era “when the word of the LORD was rare” (1 Samuel 3:1), the very fact that God keeps calling emphasizes His covenant faithfulness despite national spiritual neglect (contrast Judges 21:25).

Samuel’s obedient response

“He got up, went to Eli, and said, ‘Here I am, for you have called me.’” (1 Samuel 3:8b)

• Samuel does not grumble or hesitate; he immediately rises and runs to serve, displaying a servant’s heart (parallel Philippians 2:14–15).

• His words “Here I am” echo Abraham in Genesis 22:1 and Isaiah in Isaiah 6:8—ready availability to God’s purposes even before fully understanding them.

• Though still a boy and untrained in prophetic experience (1 Samuel 3:7), Samuel models humility by assuming the older priest is summoning him, not presuming heavenly revelation (compare Proverbs 3:5).

Eli’s dawning realization

“Then Eli realized that it was the LORD who was calling the boy.” (1 Samuel 3:8c)

• Eli, despite personal failings (1 Samuel 2:29), still has enough spiritual discernment to recognize God’s voice after the third occurrence (compare John 1:33, where recognition comes by divine insight).

• His realization underscores the role of mature believers guiding the young in recognizing God’s call (see 2 Timothy 1:5–6; Titus 2:2–6).

• The scene also warns against spiritual dullness: Eli needed multiple incidents before perceiving what should have been obvious, mirroring how repeated divine nudges awaken a sleepy conscience (Hebrews 5:11–14).

Implications for hearing God’s voice today

• God still speaks through His written Word, confirmed by the Holy Spirit (2 Timothy 3:16–17; John 16:13).

• Persistence: If the Lord repeats a message in Scripture or through clear providence, pay attention; He is patient yet purposeful.

• Obedience and availability position us to hear. Like Samuel, responding to ordinary authority (parents, church leaders) often precedes receiving extraordinary direction (Luke 16:10).

• Discipleship matters: Eli instructs Samuel in verse 9; believers benefit from wise mentors to help discern God’s leading (Proverbs 15:22).

summary

1 Samuel 3:8 shows God’s gracious persistence, Samuel’s ready obedience, and Eli’s eventual discernment. Literally and historically, the Lord audibly summons the boy a third time, preparing to raise up a faithful prophet. The passage calls readers to be attentive, responsive, and guided by mature counsel so that when God speaks through His living Word, we are able to answer, “Here I am.”

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) And Eli perceived that the Lord had called the child.--The whole story of the eventful night is told so naturally, the supernatural wonderfully interwoven with the common life of the sanctuary, that we forget, as we read, the strangeness of the events recorded. The sleeping child is awakened by a voice uttering his name. He naturally supposes it is his half-blind old master summoning him. The same thing occurs a second and a third time. Then it flashed upon Eli the boy had had no dream. We can well fancy the old man, when Samuel again came in, asking, "Where did the voice you thought was mine come from?" and the boy would reply, "From your chamber, master." And the old high priest would remember that in the same direction, only at the extremity of the sanctuary, behind the veil, was the Ark and the seat of God. Was, then, the glory of the Lord shining there? and did the voice as in old days proceed from that sacred golden throne? Se he bade his pupil go to his chamber again, and if the voice spoke to him again, to answer, not Eli, but the invisible King--"Speak, Lord; for thy servant heareth."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - But Eli was neither so inexperienced, nor so lost to all sense of Jehovah being the immediate ruler of Israel, as not to perceive, when Samuel came to him the third time, that the matter was Divine. Possibly he recalled to mind the visit of the man of God, and had some presage of what the message might be. At all events he bade Samuel lie calmly down again, because the best preparation for hearing God's voice is obedience and trustful submission.
