His flesh wastes away from sight
This phrase describes a physical deterioration that is visible and alarming. In the context of Job, it reflects the severe suffering and affliction that Job is experiencing. The wasting away of flesh can be seen as a metaphor for the spiritual and emotional anguish that accompanies physical suffering. Biblically, this imagery is reminiscent of other instances of suffering and disease, such as leprosy, which was a common affliction in biblical times and often led to social isolation. Theologically, this wasting away can be seen as a consequence of sin and the fallen state of humanity, echoing the curse of decay and death introduced in Genesis 3. It also foreshadows the suffering servant in Isaiah 53, who bears the iniquities of others and is "despised and rejected by men."

and his hidden bones protrude
This phrase emphasizes the severity of the physical condition, where even the bones, typically hidden beneath flesh, become visible. This can symbolize the exposure of one's innermost being and vulnerabilities during times of extreme distress. In the cultural context of the ancient Near East, such a condition would be seen as a sign of divine displeasure or judgment, as health and prosperity were often associated with divine favor. The protruding bones can also be seen as a type of Christ, who, in His crucifixion, was marred beyond human likeness (Isaiah 52:14) and whose bones were not broken (John 19:36), fulfilling prophecy. This imagery serves as a reminder of the frailty of human life and the need for divine intervention and redemption.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Job
A man of great faith and integrity, Job is the central figure in the Book of Job. He undergoes severe trials and suffering, which are the backdrop for the discussions and discourses in the book.

2. Elihu
A young man who speaks to Job and his friends in chapters 32-37. Elihu offers a different perspective on Job's suffering, emphasizing God's justice and sovereignty.

3. Uz
The land where Job lived, often associated with the region east of Israel. It serves as the setting for the events of the book.

4. Suffering
The central theme of the Book of Job, exploring the reasons for and responses to human suffering.

5. God's Sovereignty
A key theme in Elihu's speeches, highlighting God's ultimate control and purpose in all circumstances.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Physical Suffering
Suffering can manifest physically, as seen in Job's condition. It is a reminder of the frailty of human life and the reality of living in a fallen world.

Spiritual Implications of Physical Afflictions
Physical suffering often has spiritual implications, prompting introspection and a deeper reliance on God. It can be a tool for spiritual growth and refinement.

God's Sovereignty in Suffering
Elihu emphasizes that God is sovereign over all circumstances, including suffering. Trusting in God's purpose and timing is crucial, even when we do not understand our trials.

The Importance of Compassionate Counsel
Elihu's approach, though well-intentioned, reminds us of the need for compassion and sensitivity when offering counsel to those who are suffering.

Hope Beyond Physical Decay
While our bodies may waste away, our hope is in the eternal life promised through Christ. This perspective encourages perseverance and faithfulness.

What Does Job 33:21 Mean
His flesh wastes away from sight

• Elihu is describing a man whose physical condition has deteriorated so drastically that the change is obvious to everyone. The body that once looked strong now “wastes away” until it is painful to watch (Job 33:21a).

• Scripture often links visible physical decline with deep spiritual need. David cries, “My strength fails because of my iniquity, and my bones waste away” (Psalm 31:10). The psalmist also confesses, “My flesh and my heart fail” (Psalm 73:26), reminding us that God sometimes allows weakness to expose our dependence on Him.

• This wasting is not merely the normal course of aging; Elihu pictures severe affliction—like Job’s boils (Job 2:7) or the wasting sickness mentioned in Psalm 102:3-5—that strips away health, vanity, and self-reliance.

• Key takeaway: physical frailty can serve as God’s mercy signal, urging repentance and renewed trust (Proverbs 5:11-12; Revelation 3:19).

and his hidden bones protrude

• Bones normally “hidden” beneath muscle and flesh now jut out, a graphic sign of extreme emaciation (Job 19:20 offers Job’s own testimony: “I am nothing but skin and bones”).

• This image conveys helplessness, echoing Lamentations 4:8—“Their skin has shriveled on their bones; it has become as dry as a stick.”

• Yet Elihu’s goal is not mere pity; he is highlighting how God sometimes brings a person to the edge of death “to turn his soul back from the Pit” (Job 33:30). The protruding bones are a wake-up call, much like Paul’s reminder that “though outwardly we are wasting away, yet inwardly we are being renewed day by day” (2 Corinthians 4:16).

• God can use even wasting sickness to strip away every earthly prop, so that the sufferer hears His redemptive voice (Psalm 119:67, 71).

summary

Job 33:21 paints a stark portrait of a man brought to the brink of death: first his flesh visibly melts away, then even his concealed bones push through the skin. Elihu uses this vivid reality to show how God sometimes employs severe physical affliction to arrest a person’s attention, humble pride, and invite heartfelt repentance. The verse is therefore both literal—describing real, agonizing sickness—and instructive, reminding believers that when the body fails, God is often at work to rescue the soul and restore true life in Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - His flesh is consumed away, that it cannot be seen; literally, from the sight; but the Authorized Version gives probably the correct meaning. And his bones that were not seen stick out. These are general features of a wasting illness (comp. Psalm 22:17, "I may tell all my bones"). Such illness gives the sufferer time to review thoroughly his life and cow duct, and see to it "if there be any way of wickedness in him," or any particular form of sin to which he is tempted.
