Look to the heavens and see;
This phrase invites the reader to consider the vastness and majesty of the heavens, which in biblical times were often seen as the domain of God. The heavens are a testament to God's creation and power, as seen in Psalm 19:1, "The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands." This call to look upwards is a reminder of human limitations and the greatness of God, encouraging humility and reverence. The heavens are also a symbol of God's transcendence, being beyond human reach and understanding.

gaze at the clouds high above you.
Clouds in the Bible often symbolize God's presence and majesty. In Exodus 13:21, God leads the Israelites by a pillar of cloud, signifying His guidance and protection. The clouds are high above, emphasizing the distance between God's ways and human ways, as echoed in Isaiah 55:9, "As the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways and my thoughts than your thoughts." This imagery serves to remind believers of God's sovereignty and the need to trust in His divine plan, even when it is not fully understood. The clouds also evoke the imagery of Jesus' return, as described in Matthew 24:30, where the Son of Man will come on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory.

Persons / Places / Events

Teaching Points
God's Transcendence
Elihu reminds us that God is far above us, both in His physical creation and in His wisdom and understanding. We should approach God with humility, recognizing our limited perspective.

Creation as a Testament to God's Power
The heavens and clouds serve as a constant reminder of God's greatness and sovereignty. Observing nature can lead us to worship and trust in God's control over all things.

Perspective in Suffering
In times of personal trial, like Job, we are encouraged to lift our eyes to God, acknowledging His greater plan and purpose beyond our immediate understanding.

Humility in Questioning God
While it is natural to question during suffering, Elihu's words remind us to maintain a posture of humility, recognizing that God's ways are higher than ours.

Trust in God's Justice
Elihu's discourse encourages believers to trust in God's justice, even when it is not immediately apparent. God's timing and methods are perfect, though they may be beyond our comprehension.

What Does Job 35:5 Mean
Look to the heavens

“Look to the heavens …”

• Elihu’s first directive is upward. Instead of being trapped in earthly pain and unanswered questions, Job is told to lift his eyes. Psalm 19:1 reminds us that “The heavens declare the glory of God,” and Isaiah 40:26 urges, “Lift up your eyes on high and see: who created these?” The vast expanse above preaches God’s greatness without words, inviting humility and renewed perspective.

• We are small under that dome of stars and planets, yet each constellation exists by His word (Job 9:8). When discouragement looms, physical elevation of the gaze can spark spiritual elevation of the heart.

and see

“… and see …”

• Looking isn’t enough; Elihu presses Job to “see.” Recognition follows observation. Like the psalmist who considered the heavens and cried, “What is man that You are mindful of him?” (Psalm 8:3-4), Job is being asked to process what his eyes take in.

• Seeing turns facts into faith. Genesis 15:5 shows God calling Abram to “Look up at the sky and count the stars,” pushing him past sight into belief. Elihu wants Job to move beyond his circumstances and perceive God’s majesty, sovereignty, and nearness.

gaze at the clouds high above you

“… gaze at the clouds high above you.”

• Clouds illustrate both God’s accessibility and His transcendence. They hover over us yet remain out of reach—much like God, who is near enough to sustain us and exalted enough to remain holy. Job 37:15-16 asks, “Do you know how God controls the clouds?” emphasizing our limited understanding compared with His limitless wisdom.

• The elevation “high above you” highlights that our behavior, whether sin or righteousness, does not alter God’s essence (Job 35:7-8). He is unaffected in His being, though intimately involved with His creation. Clouds moving on unseen currents mirror His unseen hand guiding history.

summary

Elihu’s three-part call reminds Job—and us—to lift our eyes, truly see, and linger on the heavens that dwarf our problems while magnifying God’s power. The star-strewn sky and lofty clouds preach His glory, expose our smallness, and reassure us that the God who reigns above is more than able to sustain us below.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Look unto the heavens, and see; and behold the clouds which are higher than thou; i.e. "look to the material sky and heavens, so far above thee and so unapproachable, and judge from them how far the God who made them is above thy puny, feeble self - how incapable he is of being touched by any of thy doings."
