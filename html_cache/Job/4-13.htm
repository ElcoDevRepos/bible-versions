In disquieting visions in the night
This phrase introduces the concept of visions or dreams as a means of divine communication, a common theme in the Bible. Throughout Scripture, God often uses dreams to convey messages, as seen with Joseph in Genesis 37 and Daniel in Daniel 2. The term "disquieting" suggests a sense of unease or disturbance, indicating that the vision carries a significant and possibly troubling message. In the context of Job, these visions reflect the turmoil and spiritual struggle that Job is experiencing. The use of dreams as a medium for divine revelation underscores the mysterious and often unsettling nature of God's communication with humanity.

when deep sleep falls on men
This phrase refers to a state of profound rest, often associated with the time when God chooses to reveal Himself or His messages. In Genesis 15:12, a deep sleep falls on Abram as God makes a covenant with him, indicating a moment of significant divine interaction. The cultural context of the ancient Near East often viewed sleep as a vulnerable state where the divine could interact with humans. This vulnerability is mirrored in Job's situation, where he is open to receiving divine insight, albeit through troubling visions. The phrase emphasizes the idea that God can reach individuals in their most unguarded moments, highlighting His sovereignty and the unpredictability of His revelations.

A state of unconsciousness where visions or dreams are often received, as seen in various biblical accounts.
Teaching Points
The Role of Visions in Divine Communication
Visions are a means by which God communicates with His people, often providing guidance, warning, or revelation. Believers should be open to God's communication in various forms, including dreams and visions.

The Importance of Discernment
Not all visions are from God. Believers must exercise discernment, testing visions against Scripture and seeking confirmation through prayer and wise counsel.

God's Sovereignty in Revelation
God chooses when and how to reveal Himself. The timing and manner of His revelations are under His sovereign control, reminding us to trust in His perfect wisdom and timing.

The Mystery of God's Ways
Visions often contain elements of mystery and require faith to understand. This encourages believers to trust in God's greater plan, even when it is not fully revealed.

The Impact of Divine Encounters
Encounters with God, whether through visions or other means, can have a profound impact on a person's life, leading to transformation and a deeper understanding of God's will.

What Does Job 4:13 Mean
In disquieting visions

Eliphaz introduces his experience with language that hints at an unsettling encounter. Scripture often records God using visions to arrest human attention, yet those visions can be deeply disturbing when they expose the unseen realm or confront hidden sin. • Isaiah felt undone when he saw the Lord high and lifted up (Isaiah 6:1-5). • Daniel’s visions left him “distressed in spirit” and “pale” (Daniel 7:15; 8:27). • In Acts 10:10-16 Peter fell into a trance that challenged his assumptions about purity and mission. The common thread is the holy God breaking into ordinary life, and the human heart reacting with awe or dread. Eliphaz’s “disquieting” tone reminds us that even when the message is true, the encounter itself can be jarring.

in the night

Nighttime carries a symbolic weight in Scripture: it strips away distractions, heightens vulnerability, and magnifies the contrast between light and darkness. Jacob wrestled with God “until daybreak” (Genesis 32:24-30). Samuel first heard the Lord’s voice while lying down in the tabernacle at night (1 Samuel 3:3-10). Paul received the Macedonian call “during the night” in a vision (Acts 16:9-10). In each case, God used the stillness of night to speak clearly. Eliphaz is setting the stage for a message that, in his mind, bears divine authority—though later chapters show that his conclusions mix truth with error. The narrative’s accuracy underscores that God’s Word records even the flawed reasoning of Job’s friends for our instruction (Romans 15:4).

when deep sleep falls on men

The phrase echoes moments when the Lord induced a deep sleep to perform a work no human could accomplish unaided. • Adam’s deep sleep preceded the creation of Eve (Genesis 2:21-22). • Abram’s deep sleep came before the covenant vision of a smoking firepot and flaming torch (Genesis 15:12-17). • Job himself later acknowledges that “God speaks… in a dream, in a vision of the night, when deep sleep falls on men” (Job 33:14-15). Such sleep removes conscious resistance, allowing God to impress His message directly on the spirit. Eliphaz appeals to this pattern to lend credence to what he heard, although his later counsel misapplies it. The literal detail still stands: God can and does reach people when natural defenses are down.

summary

Job 4:13 portrays an unnerving nighttime vision that breaks into human vulnerability, aligning with a consistent biblical pattern: God sometimes speaks in the stillness and darkness to shake, warn, or reveal. While Eliphaz’s interpretation proved imperfect, the verse itself affirms that the Almighty may use disquieting visions, night seasons, and deep sleep to communicate His purposes—reminding us that He is sovereign over every moment, even the hours when we think we are least aware.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) In thoughts from the visions of the night.--The Book of Genesis exhibits the same idea of revelation through visions of the night, e.g., Job 15:1; Job 20:3; Job 30:11; Job 40:5; Job 41:1; afterwards it is not common, except in the Book of Daniel. The word rendered "thoughts" only occurs once again, in Job 20:2. The "deep sleep" of this place is like a reminiscence of Genesis 2:21; Genesis 15:12. It is used again in Job 33:15, otherwise only once in 1Samuel 26:12, once in Proverbs 19:15, and once in Isaiah 29:10. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - In thoughts from the visions of the night; literally, in the perplexities of the visions of night; i.e. "in that perplexing time when - how, they know not - visions come to men." The word translated "thoughts" occurs only here and in Job 20:2. When deep sleep falleth on men. Something more than ordinary sleep seems to be meant - something more approaching to what we call "trance" (comp. Genesis 2:21; Genesis 15:12; 1 Samuel 26:12, where the same word is used).
