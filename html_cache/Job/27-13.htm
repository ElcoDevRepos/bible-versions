This is the wicked man’s portion from God—
This phrase introduces the concept of divine justice, a recurring theme in the Book of Job. It reflects the belief that God, as the ultimate judge, dispenses justice according to one's actions. The "portion" implies a predetermined share or fate, suggesting that the wicked will inevitably face consequences. This aligns with the wisdom literature tradition, such as in Proverbs, where the wicked are often contrasted with the righteous. The idea of a "portion" also echoes the inheritance language found in the Old Testament, where land and blessings were allotted to the tribes of Israel, indicating a divinely ordained destiny.

the heritage the ruthless receive from the Almighty.
The term "heritage" suggests something passed down or inherited, indicating that the consequences of wickedness are as certain as an inheritance. The "ruthless" refers to those who act without mercy or regard for others, often associated with oppression and violence. This reflects the cultural and historical context of the Ancient Near East, where power and wealth were frequently obtained through ruthless means. The use of "the Almighty" emphasizes God's sovereignty and power, reinforcing the idea that divine justice is inescapable. This phrase can be connected to other scriptures, such as Psalm 37:28, which assures that the wicked will be cut off, while the righteous will inherit the land. The concept of divine retribution is also seen in the New Testament, where Jesus speaks of the final judgment and the separation of the righteous from the wicked (Matthew 25:31-46).

Teaching Points
Divine Justice
God's justice is perfect and impartial. The wicked will receive their due portion, which serves as a reminder of the moral order established by God.

The Consequences of Wickedness
Living a life contrary to God's commands leads to inevitable consequences. This should encourage believers to pursue righteousness.

The Sovereignty of God
God is in control of all things, including the fate of the wicked. Trusting in His sovereignty provides comfort and assurance to the faithful.

The Importance of Righteous Living
Believers are called to live upright lives, knowing that God rewards righteousness and punishes wickedness.

Reflection on Personal Conduct
This verse invites self-examination of one's life and actions, encouraging repentance and alignment with God's will.

What Does Job 27:13 Mean
This is the wicked man’s portion from God

Job begins his explanation by stressing that the outcome he is about to describe is determined “from God.” The focus is not on chance, fate, or human opinion, but on the just and sovereign judgment of the Lord.

• God Himself assigns the “portion”—a deliberate allotment similar to what Psalm 11:6 calls “fiery coals and burning sulfur” upon the wicked.

• The certainty of divine justice is echoed in Nahum 1:2: “The LORD is a jealous and avenging God.”

• Because the source is God, the result is unchangeable. Ecclesiastes 8:13 agrees: “It will not be well for the wicked, nor will they lengthen their days like a shadow.”

• This portion is not merely future; earthly consequences often accompany wickedness (Proverbs 11:21).

the heritage the ruthless receive from the Almighty

Job strengthens the idea by using “heritage,” a word that usually describes an inheritance passed down through generations. Here it is turned on its head: what the ruthless “receive” is a legacy of loss.

• “Ruthless” people—those who oppress and exploit others—ultimately inherit judgment. Isaiah 13:11 confirms: “I will punish the world for its evil and the wicked for their iniquity.”

• The inheritance language hints at permanence. Just as land passed to children remained in a family, so the consequences of cruelty can ripple through descendants (Exodus 34:7).

• Malachi 4:1 pictures the climactic fulfillment: “All the arrogant and every evildoer will be stubble.”

• Yet even now, God may allow earthly reversals—riches lost, reputations ruined, power stripped away (Psalm 37:35-38).

summary

Job 27:13 teaches that God Himself assigns a definite, unavoidable portion to the wicked—a legacy of judgment that even the most ruthless cannot escape. Scripture consistently confirms that divine justice is certain, both in temporal consequences and in ultimate accountability before the Almighty.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) This is the portion of a wicked man.--Some have thought that the remainder of this chapter, if not Job 28 also, constitutes the missing third speech of Zophar, and that the usual words, "Then answered Zophar the Naamathite, and said," have dropped out; but whatever may so be gained in symmetry seems to be lost in dramatic effect. We have seen that Bildad had but little to say, and that was only a few truisms; it is not surprising, therefore, that when it came to the turn of Zophar he had nothing more to say, and Job was left virtually master of the field. It is, however, a little remarkable that, supposing these words to be rightly ascribed to Job, he should precisely adopt those with which Zophar had concluded (Job 20:29). Perhaps Job is willing to show how completely he is prepared to accept the facts of his friends, although he will not admit their inferences. He, like them, is quite ready to allow that the prosperity of the wicked must be seeming rather than real, and that it must eventually come to nought.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - This is the portion of a wicked men with God. In "this" Job includes all that follows from ver. 14 to ver. 23 - "this, which I am going to lay down." He pointedly takes up the words of Zophar in Job 20:29, admitting their general truth. And the heritage of oppressors, which they shall receive of the Almighty. Retribution is "their portion" "their heritage" i.e. the natural result and consequence of their precedent sin.
