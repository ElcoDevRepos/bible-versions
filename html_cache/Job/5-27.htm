Indeed, we have investigated, and it is true!
This phrase reflects the confidence and authority with which Eliphaz speaks. In the context of the Book of Job, Eliphaz is one of Job's friends who believes that suffering is a result of sin. The phrase suggests a thorough examination or inquiry, which was a common practice in ancient wisdom literature. The assertion of truth here is based on human observation and traditional wisdom, rather than divine revelation. This reflects the cultural context of the time, where wisdom was often passed down through generations and considered reliable. The phrase also highlights the tension between human understanding and divine mystery, a central theme in the Book of Job.

So hear it and know for yourself.
Eliphaz urges Job to listen and internalize the wisdom being shared. This reflects the oral tradition of the time, where teachings were often communicated verbally and required active listening. The phrase "know for yourself" emphasizes personal responsibility in discerning truth, a concept that resonates throughout Scripture. It suggests that wisdom is not just to be heard but to be understood and applied personally. This can be connected to other biblical passages that emphasize the importance of wisdom and understanding, such as Proverbs 4:7, which encourages seeking wisdom above all else. The call to "know for yourself" also foreshadows the personal encounter with God that Job will eventually have, highlighting the difference between human counsel and divine revelation.

Teaching Points
The Limitations of Human Wisdom
Eliphaz's confidence in his own understanding serves as a reminder of the limitations of human wisdom. We must seek God's wisdom, which surpasses human insight.

The Importance of Discernment
While Eliphaz speaks with authority, his counsel is not entirely accurate. Believers must discern and test all teachings against the truth of Scripture.

The Value of Personal Experience
Eliphaz urges Job to "know for yourself," highlighting the importance of personal experience and relationship with God in understanding truth.

The Role of Community in Suffering
Job's friends come to support him, illustrating the role of community in times of trial. However, their counsel shows the need for compassionate and accurate support.

The Pursuit of Truth
Eliphaz's statement about investigating truth encourages believers to diligently seek and verify truth, aligning it with God's Word.

What Does Job 5:27 Mean
Indeed, we have investigated

- Eliphaz speaks as a representative of the wise who have searched life’s patterns thoroughly.

- Scripture commends such careful inquiry: “Now the Berean Jews were more noble… they examined the Scriptures daily to see if these teachings were true” (Acts 17:11).

- God invites His people to probe His works: “It is the glory of God to conceal a matter and the glory of kings to search it out” (Proverbs 25:2).

- The verse models due diligence before speaking—reminding us to “test all things; hold fast to what is good” (1 Thessalonians 5:21).

and it is true!

- Having investigated, Eliphaz claims certainty.

- Truth is not subjective; it rests on God’s revelation: “The sum of Your word is truth” (Psalm 119:160).

- Jesus prays, “Sanctify them by the truth; Your word is truth” (John 17:17).

- God cannot lie (Titus 1:2); therefore any conclusion aligned with His character and word stands firm.

- For Eliphaz, the “truth” is the principle he has just outlined: the righteous are delivered, and trouble comes to the wicked (Job 5:17–26). Though his application to Job is flawed, the principle itself remains biblically accurate in ultimate terms.

So hear it

- Eliphaz urges Job to listen respectfully, reflecting Proverbs 1:5: “Let the wise listen and gain instruction.”

- Faith is sparked by hearing: “So faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of Christ” (Romans 10:17).

- For spiritual growth we must be “quick to listen” (James 1:19), weighing counsel against Scripture.

and know for yourself

- Personal appropriation is essential; second-hand faith will not sustain.

- “Taste and see that the LORD is good” (Psalm 34:8) invites firsthand experience.

- The Samaritans told the woman at the well, “We no longer believe because of your words alone; we have heard for ourselves” (John 4:42).

- Paul urges Timothy, “Continue in what you have learned and firmly believed” (2 Timothy 3:14), highlighting a settled, personal conviction.

- Eliphaz challenges Job to internalize the lesson; God likewise calls every believer to move from head knowledge to heart certainty.

summary

Job 5:27 shows a progression: diligent investigation, confident recognition of truth, attentive listening, and personal ownership. Scripture validates each step, urging believers today to examine God’s Word carefully, acknowledge its absolute truth, listen humbly to counsel, and embrace its promises for themselves.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) So it is.--It is the boastful confidence of Eliphaz which is so hard to bear. He speaks as though Job's experience were as nothing to his. "This is mine: take it to thyself, and make it thine."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 27. - Lo this, we have searched it, so it is. Eliphaz does not claim to be delivering a Divine message, or in any way stating results which he has learnt from revelation. Rather is he declaring what he has "searched out;" i.e. gathered with much trouble from inquiry, observation, and experience. He is, however, quite confident that he has arrived at a true conclusion, and expects Job to accept it and act upon it. Hear it, and know thou it for thy good; literally, for thyself. Make the knowledge, i.e. which I have communicated to thee, thine own. Professor Lee observes, "Them is nothing in all this savouring of any asperity, as far as I can see, beyond the anxieties of true friendship. The sentiments delivered from ver. 17 to the end of the chapter are not only most excellent in themselves, but perfectly applicable to Job's case; and were, in the event, made good in every respect. It is true, we have not much sympathy expressed for Job's bereavements and afflictions. And, in this respect, Eliphaz was, no doubt, to blame" ('Book of the Patriarch Job' p. 216).
