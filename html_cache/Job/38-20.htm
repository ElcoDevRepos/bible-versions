so you can lead it back to its border?
This phrase is part of God's response to Job, highlighting human limitations compared to divine omniscience and omnipotence. The "border" here refers to the boundaries of light and darkness, a concept that underscores God's control over creation. In biblical context, boundaries are often set by God, as seen in Genesis 1:4 where God separates light from darkness. This separation is not just physical but also symbolic of order and divine authority. The rhetorical question implies that only God can control such cosmic elements, emphasizing His sovereignty. The idea of borders also connects to the Promised Land's boundaries given to Israel, symbolizing divine provision and protection.

Do you know the paths to its home?
This phrase continues the theme of divine wisdom versus human understanding. The "paths" suggest a journey or a course that light takes, which humans cannot fully comprehend or control. In ancient Near Eastern culture, paths often symbolized life's journey or destiny, which is ultimately directed by God. The "home" of light can be seen as its origin or resting place, a concept that aligns with God's role as the Creator who knows the beginning and end of all things. This echoes Proverbs 8:22-31, where wisdom is personified and present at creation, further illustrating that true understanding and knowledge belong to God alone. The imagery of paths and homes can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is described as the "light of the world" in John 8:12, guiding believers on the path to eternal life.

What Does Job 38:20 Mean
setting the scene

God finally answers Job out of the whirlwind (Job 38:1), turning the tables and questioning Job’s grasp of creation’s mysteries. By doing so, the Lord highlights that His ways are higher than human ways (Isaiah 55:8-9) and that “the fear of the LORD—that is wisdom” (Job 28:28).

breaking down the question

Job 38:20 reads: “So you can lead it back to its border? Do you know the paths to its home?”

• “it” points back to light and darkness mentioned in v. 19.

• Two probing questions follow:

– Can you return light or darkness to their appointed boundaries?

– Do you know the exact routes they travel?

Each question forces Job to admit his ignorance, contrasting creaturely limitation with the Creator’s perfect knowledge (Proverbs 3:19-20).

the border of light and darkness

• From the beginning, God separated light from darkness (Genesis 1:3-4).

• Psalm 104:20-23 affirms that night and day have fixed roles for all creatures.

• Jeremiah 31:35-36 shows these cosmic ordinances as guarantees of God’s faithfulness.

If Job cannot even trace the “border” where night ends and day begins—something God set in place—how could he hope to challenge God’s justice?

paths to its home

• “Paths” evokes the precise, unseen courses light and darkness follow—far beyond human mapping (Psalm 19:6).

• Modern science traces orbits and wavelengths, yet even these discoveries only echo the depth of the Lord’s design (Psalm 147:5).

• Proverbs 8:22-31 personifies wisdom at creation, underscoring that only God—and by extension His wisdom—knows these hidden routes.

God’s unmatched authority

• By questioning Job, God asserts that He governs both moral and physical realms (Job 38:12-13; Romans 11:33-36).

• The inability to guide something as constant as light underscores humanity’s dependence on divine governance (James 1:17).

• Recognizing this leads to humble trust rather than accusation (Job 42:1-6).

summary

Job 38:20 challenges Job: If you cannot send light or darkness back to their appointed borders or trace their secret paths, how can you judge the One who does? The verse magnifies God’s sovereign, detailed rule over creation and invites us to exchange complaint for reverent confidence in His flawless wisdom.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - That thou shouldest take it to the bound thereof. Can Job "take" light and darkness, and lead them to their proper places, and make them observe their proper "bounds," as God can (Genesis 1:4)? And that thou shouldest know the paths to the house thereof (comp. ver. 19).
