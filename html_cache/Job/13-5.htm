If only you would remain silent;
In the context of Job's discourse, this phrase highlights the value of silence in the face of suffering and misunderstanding. Job's friends have been speaking at length, offering explanations for his suffering that align with the retributive justice theology of the time, which posits that suffering is a direct result of sin. Job, however, challenges this notion, suggesting that silence might be more appropriate than misguided counsel. This echoes Proverbs 17:28, which states, "Even a fool is considered wise when he keeps silent." The cultural context of the Ancient Near East often valued wisdom literature, where silence was seen as a sign of wisdom and understanding.

for that would be your wisdom!
Job implies that true wisdom sometimes lies in restraint and the acknowledgment of one's limitations in understanding divine matters. This aligns with the broader biblical theme that human wisdom is limited compared to God's infinite wisdom, as seen in Isaiah 55:8-9, where God's thoughts and ways are higher than human thoughts and ways. The historical context of Job, set in a patriarchal society, often involved elders and wise men who were expected to provide counsel. However, Job suggests that their wisdom is flawed. This can be seen as a type of Christ, who, in the New Testament, often remained silent before His accusers, demonstrating wisdom through restraint and fulfilling prophecy (Isaiah 53:7).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Job
A man described as blameless and upright, who feared God and shunned evil. He is the central figure in the Book of Job, enduring immense suffering and questioning the reasons behind it.

2. Job's Friends
Eliphaz, Bildad, and Zophar are the three friends who come to comfort Job but end up arguing with him, suggesting that his suffering is due to sin.

3. The Land of Uz
The setting of the Book of Job, traditionally considered to be in the region of Edom or northern Arabia.

4. God
The ultimate authority and sovereign being who allows Job's testing and later speaks to Job and his friends.

5. Satan
The adversary who challenges Job's integrity, suggesting that Job is faithful only because of his prosperity.
Teaching Points
The Wisdom of Silence
Silence can be a powerful tool for wisdom. In moments of conflict or misunderstanding, choosing silence can prevent further harm and allow for reflection.

Discernment in Speech
Not every situation requires our input. Discernment in when to speak and when to remain silent is a mark of maturity and wisdom.

Empathy in Comforting Others
When comforting those who suffer, our presence and empathy often speak louder than words. Job's friends initially sat in silence with him, which was more comforting than their later speeches.

Humility in Understanding
Recognize the limits of our understanding. Job's friends assumed they knew the reasons for his suffering, but their lack of insight led to misguided counsel.

Trust in God's Sovereignty
In times of suffering, trust in God's ultimate wisdom and sovereignty, even when His ways are beyond our understanding.

What Does Job 13:5 Mean
If only you would remain silent

• Job’s words come in the middle of intense debate with his three companions (Job 13:1–4). After listening to their speeches—filled with assumptions about hidden sin—Job longs for a pause.

• Silence, in Scripture, is not avoidance but often the first step toward true understanding.

– Job 2:13 shows the friends at their best when “they sat on the ground with him seven days and seven nights, but no one spoke a word to him.”

– Ecclesiastes 3:7 reminds us there is “a time to be silent and a time to speak.”

– Proverbs 10:19 warns, “When words are many, sin is unavoidable, but he who restrains his lips is wise.”

• By asking for silence, Job is inviting sober reflection rather than knee-jerk theology. In suffering, quick explanations usually wound more than they heal.

for that would be your wisdom!

• Job links wisdom to restraint. Speech without insight is folly; silence that listens for God is discernment.

– Proverbs 17:28 underscores the principle: “Even a fool is considered wise if he keeps silent, and discerning when he holds his tongue.”

– James 1:19 echoes it: “Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak, and slow to anger.”

– Compare James 3:6 where the tongue is called “a world of wickedness”; control over it marks maturity.

• Genuine wisdom starts with humility—admitting, “I don’t know everything about your pain.” The friends’ relentless accusations (Job 4–11) displayed knowledge without compassion; Job says wisdom would have blossomed the moment they stopped talking.

• In the larger drama of Job, God Himself will later demand silence before speaking (Job 38:1–2). True counsel flows best after reverent quiet.

summary

Job 13:5 teaches that holding the tongue in the presence of suffering is not weakness but wisdom. Silence allows space for empathy, guards against reckless judgment, and prepares the heart to hear from God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) O that ye would altogether hold your peace! is singularly like the sentiment of Proverbs 17:28. Their wisdom will consist in listening to his wisdom rather than displaying their own folly.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Oh that ye would altogether hold your peace! The friends had "held their peace" for seven days after their arrival (Job 2:13). Oh that they would have held it altogether! Their words had done nothing but exasperate and goad almost to madness. There is a mournful pathos in Job's entreates to them to be silent (comp ver. 13). And it should be your wisdom. "Speech," it has been said, "is silvern, silence is golden." No doubt" there is a time for everything... a time to keep silence, and a time to speak" (Ecclesiastes 3:1, 7); nor is the rule of La Trappe altogether a wise one. But probably ten times as much harm is done in the world by speaking as by keeping silence. "Words for God" need especial care and caution. If they do not do good, the harm that they may do is incalculable.
