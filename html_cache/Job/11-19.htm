You will lie down without fear
This phrase suggests a state of peace and security, which is a recurring theme in biblical literature. In the context of Job, it contrasts with Job's current state of suffering and turmoil. The idea of lying down without fear is reminiscent of Psalm 4:8, where the psalmist expresses trust in God for safety. In ancient Near Eastern culture, the ability to lie down without fear was a sign of divine blessing and protection, often associated with a righteous life. This peace is also a foreshadowing of the ultimate rest and security found in Christ, who offers peace beyond understanding (Philippians 4:7).

and many will court your favor
This phrase indicates a restoration of social standing and influence. In the ancient world, having others seek your favor was a sign of respect and honor. Job, once a man of great wealth and influence, is being promised a return to his former status. This reflects the biblical principle that God exalts the humble and restores the fortunes of the faithful, as seen in the lives of Joseph (Genesis 41:41-43) and Daniel (Daniel 6:3). Theologically, it points to the ultimate favor found in Christ, who grants believers a place of honor in God's kingdom (Ephesians 2:6).

What Does Job 11:19 Mean
You will lie down without fear

Job 11:19 opens with a picture of total security: “You will lie down without fear.” Zophar is telling Job that repentance and renewed trust in the Lord would restore peace of mind and body. Scripture repeatedly connects right standing with God to restful, fearless sleep:

• Psalm 4:8—“In peace I will lie down and sleep, for You alone, O LORD, make me dwell in safety.”

• Proverbs 3:24–26 assures that when wisdom governs life, “When you lie down, your sleep will be sweet… you will not be afraid.”

• Psalm 91:5 links confidence in God’s protection to freedom from night terrors.

The verse reminds us that true safety is never anchored in circumstances but in the unchanging character of God. When sin is confessed and fellowship with Him is restored, the heart quiets; even in a fallen world, the believer can close his eyes with a calm conscience and untroubled mind.

and many will court your favor

The second promise expands outward: “and many will court your favor.” A life reconciled to God often results in harmonious relationships with people:

• Proverbs 16:7 declares, “When a man’s ways please the LORD, He makes even his enemies to be at peace with him.”

• Joseph’s story in Genesis 39:3–4 shows how God’s presence caused Potiphar to regard Joseph favorably.

• Psalm 5:12 adds, “For You bless the righteous, O LORD; You surround him with favor as with a shield.”

Living under God’s smile tends to attract respect, trust, and cooperation. While persecution can still come (John 15:18–20), the general principle holds: righteousness and humility make a person someone others want to bless rather than oppose.

summary

Job 11:19 assures that those who walk uprightly before God enjoy inward peace and outward favor. Restful nights without fear flow from a cleansed conscience and the certainty of divine protection, while God-given favor opens doors of goodwill among people. The verse invites every believer to pursue repentance, faith, and obedience, confident that the Lord rewards such a life with security and grace on every side.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - Also thou shalt lie down, and none shall make thee afraid; i.e. there shall be no more raids on the part of Sabeans (Job 1:15) or Chaldeans (Job 1:17) to affright and injure thee. Yea, many shall make suit unto thee. On the contrary, thy aid shall be invoked, thy interference on their behalf prayed for, by many.
