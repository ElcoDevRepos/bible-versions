Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then again, I am also writing to you a new commandment
This phrase suggests continuity and renewal. The "new commandment" echoes Jesus' words in John 13:34, where He instructs His disciples to love one another as He has loved them. This commandment is not new in the sense of being recently created, but new in its emphasis and depth, reflecting the transformative love of Christ. The Apostle John, writing to early Christians, reinforces the importance of this commandment in the context of their faith journey. The concept of a "new commandment" also ties back to the Old Testament, where the law was given to guide the Israelites, but now it is fulfilled and expanded in Christ.

which is true in Him and also in you
The truth of the commandment is embodied in Jesus Christ ("in Him") and is meant to be reflected in the lives of believers ("in you"). This duality emphasizes the incarnational aspect of Christian ethics—what is true in Christ should be manifest in His followers. The phrase underscores the transformative power of Christ's love and truth, which believers are called to emulate. This reflects the indwelling of the Holy Spirit, who empowers believers to live out this commandment. The truth being "in Him" points to Christ as the ultimate example and source of truth, while "in you" indicates the personal and communal application of this truth among believers.

For the darkness is fading
This phrase speaks to the eschatological hope present in the early Christian community. The "darkness" represents sin, ignorance, and the moral corruption of the world, which is gradually being overcome by the light of Christ. This imagery is consistent with the Johannine theme of light versus darkness, as seen in John 1:5, where the light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it. The fading darkness suggests the ongoing process of sanctification and the ultimate victory of God's kingdom. Historically, this reflects the early church's experience of moving from the old covenant to the new, as well as the anticipation of Christ's return.

and the true light is already shining
The "true light" refers to Jesus Christ, who is described as the light of the world in John 8:12. This light is not only a source of guidance and truth but also a revelation of God's character and will. The phrase "already shining" indicates the present reality of Christ's influence and the transformative power of the Gospel in the world. It suggests that the kingdom of God is both a present and future reality, with believers called to live in the light of Christ now, even as they await the fullness of His kingdom. This aligns with the prophetic vision of Isaiah 60:1-3, where the glory of the Lord rises upon His people, drawing nations to His light.

Persons / Places / Events

Teaching Points
The New Commandment
The commandment to love is both old and new. It is old because it has been part of God's law from the beginning, yet new in its expression through Jesus Christ.

True in Him and in You
The truth of this commandment is exemplified in Jesus and is to be lived out by His followers. Our lives should reflect His love and light.

The Passing of Darkness
As believers, we are called to recognize the diminishing power of darkness in our lives and the world, as the light of Christ prevails.

Living in the Light
We are encouraged to walk in the light, which means living in truth, love, and obedience to God's commandments.

Transformation through Christ
The presence of the true light in our lives brings transformation, enabling us to overcome sin and live in righteousness.

What Does 1 John 2:8 Mean
Then again, I am also writing to you a new commandment

John has just reminded his readers that the commandment to love is “not new, but old” (1 John 2:7), rooted in God’s unchanging nature. Yet he turns and calls it “new,” because:

• Jesus renewed and intensified it: “A new commandment I give you: Love one another” (John 13:34).

• It is new in scope—love is now measured by the self-giving sacrifice of Christ, not merely by reciprocal kindness.

• It is new in power—believers, regenerated by the Spirit, receive the capacity to obey from within (Ezekiel 36:26-27).

Which is true in Him

The commandment is “true” (genuine, fully lived out) in Jesus Himself. He is the flawless embodiment of divine love:

• He loved “to the very end” (John 13:1).

• On the cross He displayed love that “covers a multitude of sins” (1 Peter 4:8).

• Every teaching and act of Christ confirmed that love fulfills God’s law (Matthew 22:37-40; Romans 13:10).

Because He fulfills the command, its truth is anchored forever; it is not an abstract ideal but a life witnessed in history.

And also in you

What is true in Christ must become true in His people.

• New birth brings believers into the same light and love (1 John 3:14).

• “God’s love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit” (Romans 5:5).

• Love now serves as the family likeness that identifies children of God (John 13:35).

Progress may be imperfect, yet the presence of genuine, active love is evidence of salvation (Galatians 5:22; James 2:15-17).

For the darkness is fading

John explains why this commandment is now both fresh and urgent—spiritual night is retreating.

• Christ’s victory at Calvary broke the dominion of evil (Hebrews 2:14-15).

• “The night is nearly over; the day has drawn near” (Romans 13:12).

• Satan’s realm still opposes, but its defeat is certain; every act of Christian love hastens its demise (1 John 4:12).

And the true light is already shining

The verse ends in celebration: the light has dawned and continues to spread.

• Jesus declared, “I am the Light of the world” (John 8:12).

• Through union with Him, believers become “light in the Lord” (Ephesians 5:8) and “the light of the world” (Matthew 5:14-16).

• Wherever Christ’s people love, the radiance of the gospel pushes back darkness and guides others toward salvation (2 Corinthians 4:6).

summary

1 John 2:8 announces that the command to love, ancient yet renewed in Christ, is now vividly alive—demonstrated perfectly in Jesus and progressively in His followers. Because Christ’s light has already pierced the world’s darkness and that darkness is passing away, believers are called to display the same self-giving love, confident that every act of obedience participates in the unstoppable advance of the true light.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - Again. The πάλιν indicates another point of view - what in one sense was not fresh, in another sense was so. It is impossible to be certain as to the meaning of ὅ ἐστιν ἀληθὲς κ.τ.λ.. It may mean

(1) "which thing (the newness of the command) is true;" or

(2) "as a fresh commandment I am writing to you a thing which is true."

But for the practical example of the life of Christ, and men's acceptance of it, the command to love one's neighbour might have remained old and become obsolete. Ὅτι is almost certainly "because," not "that;" it introduces the reason why he writes, not the substance of, the fresh commandment. How can "the darkness is passing away," etc., be a commandment? The light, the true light τὸ φῶς τὸ ἀληθινόν; i.e., the real, the perfect, the very light, that which most fully realizes the ideal of light; in opposition to those "wandering stars, for whom the blackness of darkness hath been reserved for ever" (Jude 1:13; cf. John 1:4, 9; John 6:32; John 15:1). Christ is the perfect Light, as he is the perfect Bread and the perfect Vine.
