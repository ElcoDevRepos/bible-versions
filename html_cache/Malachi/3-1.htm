Behold, I will send My messenger,
This phrase introduces a divine promise of sending a messenger. In the historical context, this is often understood as a reference to John the Baptist, who is seen as the forerunner of Jesus Christ. The role of a messenger in ancient times was crucial for communication, often carrying important news or instructions from a king. This aligns with the prophetic tradition in the Old Testament, where God frequently used prophets to deliver His messages to the people.

who will prepare the way before Me.
The preparation of the way is a metaphor for making ready the hearts of the people for the coming of the Lord. This echoes Isaiah 40:3, which speaks of a voice calling in the wilderness to prepare the way for the Lord. John the Baptist fulfills this prophecy by calling people to repentance and baptizing them, thus preparing them spiritually for the arrival of Jesus.

Then the Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to His temple—
This phrase indicates the sudden and unexpected arrival of the Lord, which can be linked to Jesus' cleansing of the temple as described in the Gospels. The temple in Jerusalem was the center of Jewish worship and the presence of God, making it a significant location for the fulfillment of this prophecy. The suddenness emphasizes the need for readiness and vigilance among the people.

the Messenger of the covenant, in whom you delight—
Here, the "Messenger of the covenant" is understood to be Jesus Christ, who brings the new covenant prophesied in Jeremiah 31:31-34. This covenant is characterized by a personal relationship with God, written on the hearts of believers. The delight in this messenger reflects the deep longing and expectation for the Messiah, who would bring salvation and restoration.

see, He is coming,” says the LORD of Hosts.
The declaration "He is coming" underscores the certainty and imminence of the prophecy's fulfillment. The title "LORD of Hosts" emphasizes God's sovereignty and power, often used in contexts where divine intervention is assured. This assurance would have been particularly comforting to the Jewish people, who were awaiting deliverance and the establishment of God's kingdom.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Messenger
This refers to a prophetic figure who prepares the way for the Lord. In Christian theology, this is often identified with John the Baptist, who prepared the way for Jesus Christ.

2. The Lord
This is understood as a reference to Jesus Christ, who is seen as the fulfillment of the prophecy, coming to His temple.

3. The Temple
The physical place in Jerusalem where God's presence was believed to dwell. In the New Testament context, it also symbolizes the body of Christ and the church.

4. The Messenger of the Covenant
This is interpreted as Jesus Christ, who brings the new covenant through His life, death, and resurrection.

5. The LORD of Hosts
A title for God, emphasizing His sovereignty and command over the heavenly armies.
Teaching Points
Preparation for the Lord's Coming
Just as John the Baptist prepared the way for Jesus, believers are called to prepare their hearts and lives for Christ's return.

The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Malachi 3:1 highlights the reliability of God's word and the fulfillment of His promises through Jesus Christ.

The Role of Messengers
God uses individuals to prepare and proclaim His message. Believers are called to be messengers of the Gospel in their own contexts.

The New Covenant
Understanding Jesus as the Messenger of the covenant emphasizes the importance of living under the new covenant of grace and truth.

God's Sovereignty
Recognizing the LORD of Hosts reminds believers of God's ultimate authority and power over all creation.

What Does Malachi 3:1 Mean
Behold, I will send My messenger

• God Himself announces the initiative: “Behold,” drawing immediate attention to a sure, imminent act.

• The “messenger” is a real person, fulfilled literally in John the Baptist (cf. Matthew 11:10; Mark 1:2-4; Luke 1:76).

• God’s faithfulness shines—He promises and performs, proving again that “not one word has failed” (Joshua 21:45).

who will prepare the way before Me

• Preparation involves calling people to repentance so the Lord finds hearts ready (Isaiah 40:3; Matthew 3:1-3).

• John’s wilderness ministry, baptism of repentance, and bold preaching fulfill this task exactly (Luke 3:2-6).

• The phrase “before Me” underscores that the One coming after the messenger is none other than God in the flesh—Jesus Christ (John 1:14).

Then the Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to His temple

• “The Lord” is the Messiah, long desired by Israel (Haggai 2:7).

• His entrance is “sudden”: Jesus walked into the temple courts, cleansing them and teaching with authority (Mark 11:15-17; John 2:13-17).

• The temple setting highlights divine ownership; Christ claims what is His and judges corruption, fulfilling Psalm 69:9.

the Messenger of the covenant, in whom you delight

• Jesus is called “Messenger” (literally “Angel”) of the covenant because He both announces and inaugurates the new covenant promised in Jeremiah 31:31-34 (see Luke 22:20; Hebrews 8:6-13).

• Israel had professed delight in the covenant; Jesus reveals its full meaning—grace through His blood (Romans 3:25-26).

• This title also points back to earlier appearances of the “Angel of the LORD,” now manifest openly in the Messiah (Exodus 23:20-21).

see, He is coming,' says the LORD of Hosts

• The double assurance—“see…He is coming”—reinforces certainty; God’s word never fails (Isaiah 55:11).

• First Advent: fulfilled in Jesus’ birth, ministry, death, and resurrection (Galatians 4:4-5).

• Future Advent: the same Lord will return to judge and reign (Acts 1:11; Revelation 22:12). Believers live in readiness, trusting the unfailing promises of the LORD of Hosts.

summary

Malachi 3:1 lays out a precise, literal sequence: God sends a forerunner; the forerunner prepares hearts; the awaited Lord appears in His temple as covenant Messenger; and His coming is guaranteed by the LORD of Hosts. Historically this unfolded in John the Baptist and Jesus’ first coming, while also pointing ahead to Christ’s certain return. God’s faithfulness, the call to repentance, and the hope of the covenant all converge, inviting us to trust the One who was promised, who came, and who will come again.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
III.

(1) I will send.--Or, I send. It is the participle used as the prophetic present. (Comp. Note on Malachi 1:11.)

My messenger.--Heb., Malachi, my angel, or my messenger, with a play on the name of the prophet. In Malachi 2:7, he calls the priest the angel or messenger of the LORD. There can be little doubt that he is influenced in his choice of the term by his own personal name (see Introd.). This "messenger," by the distinct reference to Isaiah 40:3, contained in the words, "and he shall prepare," &c., is evidently the same as he whom [the deutero-] Isaiah prophetically heard crying, "In the wilderness, prepare ye the way of the LORD, make straight in the desert a highway for our God." Moreover, from the nature of his mission, he is proved to be identical with the "Elijah" of Malachi 4:3. These words had their first, if not their perfect fulfilment in John the Baptist (Matthew 17:12). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - Behold, I will send (I send) my messenger. God answers that he is coming to show himself the God of judgment and justice. Are they ready to meet him and to bear his sentence? Who this "messenger" is is disputed. That no angel or heavenly visitant is meant is clear from historical considerations, as no such event took place immediately before the Lord came to his temple. Nor can Malachi himself be intended, as his message was delivered nearly four, hundred years before Messiah came. The announcement is doubtless founded upon Isaiah 40:3, and refers to the same person as the older prophet mentions, who is generally allowed to be John the Baptist, the herald of Christ's advent (Matthew 11:10; John 1:6). Prepare the way before me. The expression is borrowed from Isaiah, loc. cit. (comp. also Isaiah 57:14; Isaiah 62:10). He prepares the way by preaching repentance, and thus removing the obstacle of sin which stood between God and his people. Whom ye seek. When ye ask, "Where is the God of judgment?" Shall suddenly come to his temple. The Lord (ha-Adon) is Jehovah, as in Exodus 23:17; Isaiah 1:24; Isaiah 3:1, etc. There is a change of persons here, as frequently. Jehovah shall unexpectedly come to his temple (τὸν ναὸν ἑαυτοῦ) as King and God of Israel (comp. Ezekiel 43:7). There was a literal fulfilment of this prophecy when Christ was presented in the temple as an infant (Luke 2:22, etc.). Even the messenger of the covenant. He is identified with the Lord; and he is the covenant angel who guided the Israelites to the promised land, and who is seen in the various theophanies of the Old Testament. The Divinity of Messiah is thus unequivocally asserted. In him are fulfilled all the promises made under the old covenant, and he is called (Hebrews 9:15) "the Mediator of the new covenant." Some render," and the Messenger," etc., thus distinguishing the Angel of the covenant from the forerunner who prepares the way. But this is already done by the expressions, "My Messenger," and "the Lord." Whom ye delight in. Whose advent ye expect with eager desire.
