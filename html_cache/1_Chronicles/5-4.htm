Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The descendants of Joel:
Joel is a name that appears multiple times in the Bible, often associated with prophetic or leadership roles. In this context, Joel is a patriarchal figure within the tribe of Reuben. The genealogies in 1 Chronicles serve to establish the historical and tribal lineage of Israel, emphasizing the continuity of God's covenant with His people. The tribe of Reuben, as the firstborn of Jacob, had a significant role, although they lost their birthright due to Reuben's transgression (Genesis 49:3-4).

Shemaiah his son:
Shemaiah is a name that means "heard by Yahweh." This name reflects the cultural importance of names in ancient Israel, often signifying a person's character or God's relationship with them. Shemaiah's inclusion in the genealogy underscores the importance of maintaining a record of lineage, which was crucial for tribal identity and inheritance rights. The name also appears elsewhere in the Bible, often associated with prophetic figures, indicating a possible tradition of spiritual leadership.

Gog his son:
Gog is a name that appears in prophetic literature, notably in Ezekiel 38-39, where Gog is a leader of a great army from the north. While the Gog mentioned here is not the same as the one in Ezekiel, the name may have been common in the region. The mention of Gog in this genealogy highlights the interconnectedness of biblical narratives and the recurring themes of conflict and divine intervention. It also serves as a reminder of the eschatological battles that are a part of biblical prophecy.

Shimei his son:
Shimei is another name that appears frequently in the Old Testament, often associated with individuals who played significant roles in Israel's history. The name means "God hears," which again reflects the cultural significance of names. Shimei's inclusion in the genealogy of Reuben emphasizes the importance of maintaining a detailed record of family lines, which was essential for the preservation of tribal heritage and identity. The repetition of names like Shimei throughout the Bible also illustrates the continuity of God's people and His ongoing relationship with them.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joel
A descendant of Reuben, the firstborn of Israel. Joel is part of the genealogical record that traces the lineage of the tribe of Reuben.

2. Shemaiah
The son of Joel. His name means "heard by God," indicating a possible emphasis on divine communication or response.

3. Gog
The son of Shemaiah. The name Gog is also mentioned in other biblical contexts, notably in Ezekiel, where it represents a leader of a hostile force against Israel.

4. Shimei
The son of Gog. His name means "famous" or "renowned," which may suggest a person of significance or influence within his lineage.

5. Tribe of Reuben
The tribe to which these individuals belong. Reuben was the firstborn of Jacob, and his tribe is often noted for its fluctuating fortunes and struggles for prominence.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Genealogy
Genealogies in the Bible serve to connect individuals to the larger account of God's people. They remind us of our place in God's account and the importance of heritage and legacy.

Names and Their Meanings
The meanings of names in biblical genealogies often carry significant theological or prophetic implications. Reflect on the meaning of your own name and how it might relate to your identity in Christ.

Lessons from Reuben's Lineage
The tribe of Reuben's fluctuating fortunes remind us of the consequences of actions and the importance of faithfulness. Consider how personal choices can impact not only your life but also future generations.

God's Sovereignty in History
The genealogies demonstrate God's sovereign hand in history, orchestrating events and lineages according to His divine plan. Trust in God's control over your life's circumstances.

What Does 1 Chronicles 5:4 Mean
The descendants of Joel:

BSB: “The descendants of Joel …”

- God carefully keeps track of His people, even down to family lines (see Genesis 5:1–32; Matthew 1:1–17).

- In 1 Chronicles 5 the writer is tracing Reuben’s tribe after explaining in verses 1–2 that Reuben forfeited his birthright, yet his genealogy still matters.

- The opening phrase signals that what follows is not random trivia; it declares that each generation is known by name before God (Isaiah 43:1).

- This reminder encourages us that the Lord values individuals and families, recording them in His book (Malachi 3:16).

Shemaiah his son,

BSB: “… Shemaiah his son …”

- By naming Shemaiah, Scripture affirms the literal, historical reality of this lineage (1 Chronicles 9:1).

- The faithful preservation of names shows God’s covenant faithfulness to Israel despite their failures (Deuteronomy 7:9).

- It also displays an ordered passing of heritage and responsibility, echoing passages like Deuteronomy 6:6–7 where fathers are to pass truth to sons.

Gog his son,

BSB: “… Gog his son …”

- This Gog is distinct from the later prophetic figure in Ezekiel 38–39; yet his mention ties the Chronicles readers to earlier eras, reminding them God has overseen every generation.

- The repetition “his son” highlights continuity; family faith can flourish when each child is intentionally identified and received (Psalm 78:5–7).

- By locating this Gog within Reuben’s tribe, the text distinguishes between faithful tribal history and later enemies of Israel.

Shimei his son,

BSB: “… Shimei his son.”

- Shimei marks the fourth generation mentioned. Four generations fulfill the pattern in Exodus 20:5–6, underscoring both accountability and mercy through family lines.

- Chronicles often lists four names to signify completeness (compare 1 Chronicles 6:4–7).

- The name “Shimei” appears several times in Scripture; here it reminds readers that God knows each bearer individually (2 Timothy 2:19).

- The verse closes the mini-genealogy, setting up verse 5, where further descendants lead to “Jeiel the chief,” linking past identity to present leadership responsibility (Numbers 1:5).

summary

1 Chronicles 5:4, though only a short genealogical note, underscores that God faithfully records and values every generation within His covenant people. Each name—from Joel to Shimei—testifies that lineage, identity, and divine purpose remain intact even when a tribe like Reuben has stumbled. The verse invites believers to trust the Lord’s meticulous care over their own families and to pass on a heritage of faith just as diligently.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4-6) The sons of Joel.--The connection of this leading house with one of the four sons just mentioned, is implied but not stated. The line of Joel is traced through seven generations to Beerah, who was transported to Assyria by Tiglath Pileser. II., 734 B.C., in the reign of Pekah, king of Israel. Supposing there are no gaps in the series, Joel flourished 280 years (7 x 40) before that date; that is, about 1014 B.C., under David and Solomon.

(4) The LXX. read: "Sons of Joel Shemaiah, and Banaia (Benaiah) his son; and sons of Gog, son of Shemaiah, his son Micah," &c.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 4-6. - From which of the four sons of Reuben the line came in which Joel would appear, we do not know. Juntas and Tremellius say Hanoch, others Carmi, while the Syriac Version has Carrot vice Joel. It is to be remarked that in Numbers 26:8-10 a line of descent through Pallu is given, but reaching only to the second generation, Beerah in the present list will be only ninth at furthest from Reuben, so that it is evident that it is a very fragmentary genealogy, whether the hiatus be only one, viz. between Reuben's son (whichever it may be in question) and Joel, or whether both there and elsewhere also. Of none of the eight persons beginning with Joel and ending with Beerah is anything else known, unless either Shemaiah or Shimei may be identical with the Shema of ver. 8, in which case it might be also that the Joel of ver. 8 is identical with that of ver. 4. In this passage and 1 Chronicles 8:30 Baal appears as the name of a man. In this passage, and in ver. 26 and 2 Chronicles 28:20, we have a different form in each part of the word, of the Tiglath-pileser of 2 Kings 15:29; 2 Kings 16:7. These slight differences in the position of the radicals, with the introduction or omission of the א, make as many as four different readings in the Hebrew. Tiglath-pileser, the second Assyrian king who came into conflict with the Israelites, reigned about B.C. 747-727. Gesenius thinks that the former half of the word is the same as Diglath, i.q. Tigris; and that the latter, a root occurring also in the name Nabo-pola-saris, is from an Assyrian verb meaning "to guard." He translates the word as "Lord of the Tigris." The Assyrian reproduction of the name is Tigulti-pal-tsira (Smith's 'Bible Dictionary'), or Tukulti-pal-zara ('Speaker's Commentary,' in loc.). The Captivity is spoken of further in the last verse of this chapter and in 2 Kings 15:27-31. The Septuagint reads vers. 4 and 5 differently: "The sons of Joel, Semei and Banaea his son; and the sons of Gog the son of Semei," etc., and this in all three editions - Vatican, Alexandrine, and Aldine.
