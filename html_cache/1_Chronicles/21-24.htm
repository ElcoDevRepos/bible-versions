Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“No,” replied King David,
This phrase highlights David's firm response, indicating his determination and leadership. David, as king, had the authority to command or take what he needed, yet he chooses to respond with integrity. This reflects his character and commitment to doing what is right in the eyes of God. David's response is a model of humility and righteousness, showing that true leadership involves making decisions that honor God.

“I insist on paying the full price,”
David's insistence on paying the full price for the threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite demonstrates his understanding of the value of sacrifice. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, offerings to God were meant to be costly, reflecting the worshiper's devotion and reverence. By paying the full price, David ensures that his offering is genuine and not merely a token gesture. This act prefigures the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who paid the full price for humanity's sins.

“for I will not take for the LORD what belongs to you,”
David acknowledges the principle of ownership and stewardship. He recognizes that taking something that belongs to someone else and offering it to God would not be a true sacrifice. This respect for personal property aligns with the biblical commandment against coveting and stealing. It also underscores the importance of giving to God from one's own resources, a theme echoed in the New Testament where believers are encouraged to give willingly and cheerfully.

“nor will I offer burnt offerings that cost me nothing.”
This phrase encapsulates the essence of sacrificial worship. In the Old Testament, burnt offerings were a significant part of the sacrificial system, symbolizing atonement and dedication to God. David's refusal to offer something that cost him nothing highlights the principle that true worship involves personal sacrifice and commitment. This foreshadows the sacrificial nature of Christ's atonement, where He gave Himself fully and willingly. The concept of costly sacrifice is a recurring theme in Scripture, emphasizing that genuine faith and devotion require giving up something valuable for the sake of honoring God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. King David
The second king of Israel, known for his heart for God and his leadership. In this passage, he demonstrates integrity and devotion to God.

2. Araunah
A Jebusite who owned the threshing floor where David intended to build an altar to the LORD. He offered it to David for free.

3. Threshing Floor of Araunah
The location where David decided to build an altar to stop the plague that had come upon Israel due to his sin of conducting a census.

4. The Plague
A divine punishment sent by God as a result of David's sin of numbering the people, which was against God's command.

5. Burnt Offering
A sacrificial offering made to God, symbolizing atonement and dedication. David's insistence on paying for the offering underscores the principle of sacrifice.
Teaching Points
The Cost of True Worship
True worship involves sacrifice. David's refusal to offer to God something that cost him nothing teaches us that genuine worship requires personal investment and sacrifice.

Integrity in Leadership
David's decision to pay the full price reflects his integrity and responsibility as a leader. Leaders are called to set an example of righteousness and accountability.

Repentance and Restoration
David's actions demonstrate a heart of repentance. When we sin, we must seek restoration with God, often requiring us to make amends and offer our best to Him.

God's Mercy and Justice
The account shows God's justice in response to sin but also His mercy in providing a way for restoration. We are reminded of the balance between God's holiness and His grace.

Personal Sacrifice in Service
Serving God often requires personal sacrifice. We are called to give our time, resources, and talents willingly, not out of compulsion or convenience.

What Does 1 Chronicles 21:24 Mean
No,

• David’s immediate response shows moral clarity and resolve (2 Samuel 24:24).

• He refuses to accept a shortcut in worship, echoing the principle that obedience sometimes begins with a firm “no” to anything that lessens God’s honor (Hebrews 12:28).

replied King David,

• The king himself speaks, underscoring that even earthly authority bows to God’s standards (Psalm 138:4–5).

• David’s reply demonstrates personal accountability; he will not delegate this sacred duty, reflecting Numbers 18:1 where leaders bear responsibility for the sanctuary.

I insist on paying the full price,

• True worship involves willing sacrifice, not partial measures (Romans 12:1).

• Paying “the full price” mirrors Abraham’s purchase of the cave of Machpelah (Genesis 23:16), confirming that consecrated spaces should be secured honorably.

• This protects against any later claim that the offering was coerced or cheapened (2 Corinthians 8:12).

for I will not take for the LORD what belongs to you,

• Offering God someone else’s property would violate the eighth commandment (Exodus 20:15).

• David safeguards the purity of worship by ensuring the gift is genuinely his, paralleling Malachi 1:13 where God rebukes offerings of stolen or blemished goods.

nor will I offer burnt offerings that cost me nothing.

• Sacrifice without cost is empty ritual; God values the heart behind the gift (Mark 12:41–44).

• Costly offerings foreshadow Christ’s ultimate sacrifice “who loved us and gave Himself for us” (Ephesians 5:2).

• The principle anticipates Jesus’ call to “take up your cross” (Luke 9:23), reminding believers that devotion entails personal investment.

summary

David’s words teach that worship must be intentional, personal, and costly. By purchasing the threshing floor and oxen, he honors God with integrity, safeguards against empty ritual, and prefigures the greater sacrifice of Christ. Genuine devotion still calls believers to offer God their best, never leftovers, trusting that He is worthy of every full-price gift.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) For the full price.--Samuel simply, "At a price" (different word). The next clause does not appear in Samuel, but may well be original.

Nor offer burnt offerings without cost.--So Samuel: "Nor will I offer to the Lord my God burnt offerings without cost." It was of the essence of sacrifice to surrender something valued in order to win from God a greater good (Ewald).
