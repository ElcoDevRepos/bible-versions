And David became greater and greater
This phrase highlights the progressive nature of David's rise to power and influence. David's growth in greatness is not merely a result of his own abilities but is attributed to divine favor. Historically, David's ascent began with his anointing by Samuel (1 Samuel 16:13) and continued through his military successes and leadership skills. His greatness is also seen in his ability to unite the tribes of Israel, establishing Jerusalem as the political and spiritual center. This growth can be compared to the parable of the mustard seed in the New Testament (Matthew 13:31-32), symbolizing the kingdom of God starting small and expanding greatly.

for the LORD of Hosts was with him
The title "LORD of Hosts" emphasizes God's sovereignty and command over the heavenly armies, underscoring His power and authority. This divine presence with David is a recurring theme in his life, as seen in his victories over Goliath (1 Samuel 17) and his success in uniting Israel. The phrase indicates that David's success is not due to human effort alone but is a result of God's active involvement and blessing. This divine accompaniment is a foreshadowing of the presence of God with Jesus Christ, who is called "Immanuel," meaning "God with us" (Matthew 1:23). The LORD's presence with David also fulfills the covenantal promises made to the patriarchs, ensuring the establishment of a kingdom that would ultimately lead to the Messiah.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The central figure in this verse, David is anointed king over Israel and is known for his heart after God. His reign is marked by military victories and the establishment of Jerusalem as the political and spiritual center of Israel.

2. LORD of Hosts
A title for God emphasizing His sovereignty and command over the heavenly armies. This title underscores God's power and presence with David, contributing to his success.

3. Israel
The nation over which David reigns. This period marks the unification of the tribes under a single monarchy, with David as the king.

4. Jerusalem
Although not mentioned directly in this verse, it is the city that David captures and establishes as his capital, symbolizing his growing power and God's favor.

5. The Anointing of David
The event where David is recognized as king over all Israel, which sets the stage for his increasing greatness as described in this verse.
Teaching Points
God's Presence Brings Success
David's increasing greatness is attributed to the LORD's presence. In our lives, true success comes from aligning with God's will and relying on His strength.

The Importance of God's Favor
David's account illustrates that God's favor is crucial for overcoming challenges and achieving our God-given purposes.

Leadership and Humility
Despite his growing power, David's heart remains humble before God. Effective leadership requires humility and dependence on God.

Spiritual Growth and Maturity
Just as David grew greater, we are called to grow in our spiritual walk, becoming more like Christ through the Holy Spirit's work in us.

Trust in God's Sovereignty
The title "LORD of Hosts" reminds us of God's ultimate control over all circumstances. We can trust Him to guide and protect us.

What Does 1 Chronicles 11:9 Mean
And David became greater and greater

1 Chronicles places this statement right after David captures Jerusalem and is publicly anointed king. Scripture records literal, observable growth:

• Military momentum—victories pile up just as 2 Samuel 5:10 repeats, “And David became greater and greater, for the LORD God of Hosts was with him.”

• Political consolidation—tribes unite under him, fulfilling the earlier promise in 1 Samuel 16:13 when “the Spirit of the LORD rushed upon David from that day forward.”

• Personal reputation—like Joseph in Genesis 39:2–3, David’s success is evident to friend and foe alike.

• Ongoing trajectory—1 Chronicles 17:8 recounts God’s words, “I have been with you wherever you have gone and have cut off all your enemies before you; I will make your name like the names of the greatest men on earth.”

The text presents no hint of self-made greatness; every advance is portrayed as God-given, underscoring Proverbs 10:22, “The blessing of the LORD enriches, and He adds no sorrow to it.”

for the LORD of Hosts was with him

The clause explains the cause behind the effect.

• “LORD of Hosts” emphasizes the Commander of heaven’s armies (Psalm 46:11), reminding readers that countless spiritual forces stand behind God’s earthly plans.

• God’s presence equals guaranteed victory—Joshua 1:5 promises, “I will be with you; I will never leave you nor forsake you,” and David lives out that pledge in real time.

• Divine partnership shields and guides: 1 Samuel 18:14 notes, “David continued to succeed in everything he did, for the LORD was with him.”

• The pattern stretches forward—Matthew 28:20 records Jesus saying, “I am with you always,” linking David’s experience to every believer’s mission; Romans 8:31 seals the confidence: “If God is for us, who can be against us?”

In short, David’s greatness is inseparable from God’s nearness; remove the LORD and the narrative unravels.

summary

1 Chronicles 11:9 shows a cause-and-effect relationship: David’s rising influence is the effect; God’s abiding presence is the cause. The verse reassures that genuine success springs from God’s hand, unfolds according to His timing, and displays His glory—not human ingenuity. As with David, any growth worth having still flows from the same unchanging “LORD of Hosts who is with us” (Psalm 46:7).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) This verse corresponds word for word with Samuel, only omitting "God" after "Lord." Literally, and David walked on, a walking and growing great--a common Hebrew metaphor of gradual and progressive increase or decrease. (Comp. Genesis 8:5, and the use of the term andante, "walking," in music.)

Lord of hosts was with him.--The Lord of Hosts is doubtless a contracted form of the fuller expression, Lord God of Hosts, as it appears in Samuel. The Lord (or God) of Hosts is a title derived from God's supremacy over the host of heaven, i.e., the stars, worshipped as deities by the races environing Israel, insomuch that the very word for God in the old Babylonian is represented by a star (*); and in the later Assyrian character star was represented by the symbol for God thrice repeated. Assur, the supreme deity of the Assyrian Pantheon, is called in the inscriptions "king of the legions of heaven and earth," or "of the great gods." Similar titles were given to the Babylonian Nebo and Merodach. The Hebrew phrase is therefore, in one sense, equivalent to a concise assertion of the statement, "Jehovah your God is God of gods, and Lord of lords" (Deuteronomy 10:17 : comp. also Psalm 95:3; Psalm 97:7). That the hosts in question are the stars appears from Psalm 33:6; Isaiah 40:26; Judges 5:20. . . .
