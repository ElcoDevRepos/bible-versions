Other Benjamites and some men from Judah
The tribe of Benjamin was historically aligned with King Saul, as Saul himself was a Benjamite. This allegiance made it significant that Benjamites were now coming to support David, indicating a shift in loyalty and recognition of David's anointed kingship. The tribe of Judah, David's own tribe, naturally supported him, but the inclusion of Benjamites highlights the growing acceptance of David's rule across tribal lines. This foreshadows the eventual unification of Israel under David's leadership. The mention of both tribes also reflects the broader theme of reconciliation and unity among the tribes of Israel, which is a recurring theme in the narrative of David's rise to power.

also came to David
David's gathering of support from various tribes and groups is a testament to his leadership and the fulfillment of God's promise to him. This movement towards David can be seen as a divine orchestration, as God had chosen David to be king over Israel. The coming of these men signifies the recognition of David's legitimacy and God's favor upon him. This gathering of support is reminiscent of the gathering of the nations to Christ, as people from all backgrounds come to acknowledge His lordship.

in his stronghold
David's stronghold is traditionally identified as the cave of Adullam or the fortress of Ziklag, places where David sought refuge while fleeing from Saul. These locations were strategically chosen for their defensibility and seclusion. The stronghold symbolizes a place of safety and divine protection, where David could consolidate his forces and plan his next moves. It also serves as a type of Christ, who is often referred to as a refuge and fortress for believers. The stronghold represents God's provision and protection for His anointed, even in times of adversity.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The anointed king of Israel, who at this time was not yet reigning over all of Israel. He was in a stronghold, likely in the wilderness, as he gathered support against King Saul.

2. Benjamites
Members of the tribe of Benjamin, which was the tribe of King Saul. Their coming to David signifies a shift in loyalty from Saul to David.

3. Men from Judah
Members of the tribe of Judah, David's own tribe, who were supporting him in his rise to kingship.

4. Stronghold
A fortified place where David was staying, possibly the cave of Adullam or another secure location in the wilderness.

5. Event
The gathering of support for David as he prepared to become king, indicating a growing recognition of his God-given leadership.
Teaching Points
Loyalty and Allegiance
The shift of loyalty from Saul to David by the Benjamites and men of Judah illustrates the importance of aligning ourselves with God's chosen leaders and purposes.

God's Sovereignty in Leadership
David's rise to power, despite being pursued by Saul, demonstrates God's sovereign hand in establishing leaders according to His will.

Unity Among Believers
The gathering of diverse tribes to support David highlights the importance of unity among God's people, transcending tribal and personal loyalties for a greater purpose.

Trust in God's Timing
David's patience and faithfulness while waiting for God's promise to be fulfilled encourage believers to trust in God's timing and plan for their lives.

What Does 1 Chronicles 12:16 Mean
Other Benjamites

- These men belong to Saul’s own tribe, yet they choose David over the house of Saul. That shift reveals God’s hand turning hearts (1 Samuel 13:14; 1 Chronicles 12:1–2).

- Their decision models courageous loyalty to God’s anointed king, even when it costs family favor or tribal pride (cf. Matthew 10:37).

- It also fulfills the pattern that the kingdom will not rest on bloodline alone but on obedience to God (1 Samuel 15:22–23).

And Some Men from Judah

- Judah is David’s tribe, prophesied to hold the scepter (Genesis 49:10). Their support is expected, yet every individual still has to choose faithfulness.

- Their presence shows a growing, unified core around David, preparing for his later coronation by the elders of Judah in Hebron (2 Samuel 2:4).

- Together with the Benjamites, they form the nucleus of what will become a united Israel (2 Samuel 5:1).

Also Came

- The phrase highlights movement and momentum. God is actively adding to David’s forces day by day (1 Chronicles 12:22).

- This gathering is voluntary, not coerced, mirroring how God draws His people willingly (Psalm 110:3).

- Each arrival further confirms David’s legitimacy to any doubters still following Saul (1 Samuel 24:20).

To David

- They do not simply migrate; they come to a specific person—God’s chosen king (1 Samuel 16:13).

- Their allegiance is personal and relational, reflecting covenant loyalty (2 Samuel 5:3).

- Coming to David foreshadows how all authority and unity will ultimately center in the Son of David, Christ Himself (Luke 1:32).

In His Stronghold

- David’s “stronghold” refers to his wilderness refuge, likely the cave of Adullam or the fortress at Ziklag (1 Samuel 22:1–2; 1 Samuel 23:14).

- God brings helpers into seemingly isolated places; the stronghold turns into a training ground for mighty men (1 Chronicles 12:8).

- The scene reminds believers that God’s purposes advance even in seasons of hiding or hardship (Psalm 31:3–4).

Summary

1 Chronicles 12:16 shows God gathering a diverse and devoted company around David—Benjamites forsaking Saul, Judahites affirming prophecy, all willingly coming to the anointed king even while he shelters in a rugged stronghold. The verse testifies that God’s plan marches on through loyal hearts, uniting His people under the rightful ruler and foreshadowing the greater kingdom of Christ.
