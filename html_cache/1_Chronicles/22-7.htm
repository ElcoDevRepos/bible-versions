Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“My son,” said David to Solomon,
David, the second king of Israel, addresses Solomon, his son and successor. This phrase highlights the father-son relationship, which is significant in biblical narratives. Solomon is chosen to continue David's legacy, emphasizing the importance of lineage and divine selection in Israel's monarchy. The fatherly address also reflects the passing of wisdom and responsibility, a common theme in Proverbs, many of which are attributed to Solomon.

“it was in my heart to build a house
David expresses a deep, personal desire to construct a temple for God. This reflects his devotion and the centrality of worship in his reign. The "house" refers to the temple, a permanent dwelling place for the Ark of the Covenant, which until then resided in a tent. This desire aligns with the cultural and religious practices of the time, where temples were seen as places where the divine presence dwelled among people.

for the Name of the LORD my God,
The phrase "for the Name of the LORD" signifies building the temple to honor and glorify God. In ancient Near Eastern culture, a name represented one's character and authority. Thus, the temple would be a testament to God's presence and power. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God’s name is revered and central to their identity. The temple would later become a focal point for worship and a symbol of God's covenant with David, as seen in 2 Samuel 7, where God promises David an everlasting dynasty.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The King of Israel who desired to build a temple for the Lord. He was a man after God's own heart, known for his leadership and devotion to God.

2. Solomon
The son of David, chosen by God to build the temple. He later became king and was known for his wisdom and wealth.

3. The House for the Name of the LORD
Refers to the temple that David wanted to build as a dwelling place for God's presence among His people.

4. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people.

5. Jerusalem
The city where the temple was to be built, serving as the spiritual and political center of Israel.
Teaching Points
Desire to Honor God
David's desire to build a temple reflects a heart that seeks to honor God. We should examine our own desires and ensure they align with honoring God.

God's Sovereign Plan
Despite David's good intentions, God had a different plan. We must trust in God's sovereignty and timing, even when our plans are not fulfilled as we expect.

Legacy of Faith
David prepared the way for Solomon to build the temple. We should consider how we can prepare and support the next generation in their walk with God.

Obedience and Submission
Solomon's role in building the temple shows the importance of obedience to God's calling. We should be willing to submit to God's plans for our lives.

Heart for God's Presence
The temple was a place for God's presence. We should cultivate a heart that longs for God's presence in our lives and communities.

What Does 1 Chronicles 22:7 Mean
My son

- David begins with a warm, relational address. He is not lecturing a subject but speaking to his own child—creating an atmosphere of affection and trust (Proverbs 4:1; Ephesians 6:4).

- The phrase reminds Solomon—and us—that God often delivers His most important truths through family relationships (Deuteronomy 6:7; 1 Corinthians 4:14–15).

- By calling Solomon “my son,” David models spiritual fatherhood: loving oversight that points the next generation toward wholehearted devotion to the Lord (3 John 4).

said David to Solomon

- These words come from a king whose reign is drawing to a close (1 Kings 2:1–3). David’s hard-won experience gives weight to his counsel.

- Speaking personally, he shows leadership that is relational, not merely positional (2 Samuel 23:2–3).

- In passing the torch, he mirrors Paul’s charge to Timothy: “The things you have heard from me… entrust to faithful men” (2 Timothy 2:2).

it was in my heart

- David’s desire sprang from deep, God-given longing (Psalm 37:4). This was no passing notion; it was a passion formed in fellowship with the Lord.

- God values desire that begins in the heart (1 Samuel 16:7). Even when He redirects our plans, He delights in sincere motives (2 Samuel 7:2–3).

- Acts 13:22 highlights David as “a man after My own heart,” underscoring why this yearning mattered to God.

to build a house

- The “house” is the temple—a permanent place for God’s presence among His people (Exodus 25:8).

- David’s ambition here is noble yet God disallows him from fulfilling it personally (1 Chronicles 28:2–3), teaching that good desires still submit to divine timing.

- The building foreshadows a greater dwelling: “In Him the whole building is fitted together… a holy temple in the Lord” (Ephesians 2:21).

for the Name

- David’s focus is God’s reputation, not personal legacy. The temple would exalt the Name above every other (Psalm 115:1).

- “Name” captures God’s character, glory, and authority (Exodus 34:5–7). Solomon later prays, “May Your Name be there” (2 Chronicles 6:20).

- Jesus carries this theme forward: “I have revealed Your name” (John 17:6), pointing to worship centered on who God is.

of the LORD my God

- David emphasizes covenant: “the LORD (YHWH) my God.” This is personal possession born of faith, not entitlement (Psalm 23:1).

- He acknowledges the One who chose, protected, and prospered him (2 Samuel 22:32).

- The phrase unites reverence (“the LORD”) with intimacy (“my God”), modeling how believers today can boldly yet humbly claim Him as their own (Philippians 4:19).

summary

1 Chronicles 22:7 captures an aging king’s heartfelt dream: to honor the covenant God he personally knows by building a dwelling that magnifies His Name. David’s affectionate address, seasoned authority, and God-centered motive invite us to nurture holy desires, submit them to God’s will, and labor so the next generation exalts the Lord even more than we do.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) My son.--So some MSS., the Hebrew margin, and LXX., Vulg., Targ. rightly. The Hebrew text reads, "His son," which is probably an oversight, due to "Solomon his son" in 1Chronicles 22:6.

As for me, it was in my mind.--Literally, I--it became with (near or in) my heart, i.e., it came into my mind, was my intention. The phrase is common in 2 Chronicles, but rare in the older books. (Comp. 1Kings 8:17; 1Kings 10:2; and also Joshua 14:7.) It recurs in 1Chronicles 28:2 exactly as here. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 7. - (Comp. 1 Chronicles 17:1, 2; 2 Samuel 7:2, 3.) For my son, the Chethiv shows "his son," the Keri substituting "my."
