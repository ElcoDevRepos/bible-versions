So Elisha left the oxen
Elisha was plowing with twelve yoke of oxen, indicating his wealth and status. Leaving the oxen symbolizes a significant life change, abandoning his former life to follow God's call. This act of leaving behind one's livelihood is reminiscent of the disciples leaving their nets to follow Jesus (Matthew 4:20).

ran after Elijah
Elisha's immediate response to Elijah's call demonstrates his eagerness and commitment. Running signifies urgency and willingness to embrace God's mission. This mirrors the urgency seen in other biblical figures who respond to God's call, such as Abraham (Genesis 12:1-4).

and said, “Please let me kiss my father and mother goodbye,
Elisha's request to bid farewell to his parents shows respect for family ties, a significant cultural value in ancient Israel. This act is similar to the request made by a potential disciple in Luke 9:61, highlighting the tension between familial obligations and divine calling.

and then I will follow you.”
Elisha's intention to follow Elijah indicates his acceptance of the prophetic call. This mirrors the call to discipleship in the New Testament, where following Christ requires leaving behind former ways (Luke 14:26-27).

“Go on back,” Elijah replied,
Elijah's response suggests a test of Elisha's commitment. By allowing Elisha to return, Elijah respects his decision, emphasizing the voluntary nature of following God's call. This reflects the principle of free will in responding to God's invitation.

“for what have I done to you?”
Elijah's rhetorical question implies that the call is ultimately from God, not Elijah himself. It underscores the divine origin of the prophetic mission. This phrase can be seen as a reminder that the true calling comes from God, similar to how Jesus calls His disciples (John 15:16).

Teaching Points
The Cost of Discipleship
Following God's call often requires leaving behind familiar comforts and relationships. Elisha's willingness to leave his family and livelihood is a powerful example of commitment.

Immediate Obedience
Elisha's prompt response to Elijah's call demonstrates the importance of immediate obedience to God's direction in our lives.

Honoring Family
While Elisha was ready to follow Elijah, he also sought to honor his parents by saying goodbye, showing a balance between commitment to God and respect for family.

God's Sovereign Call
God's call can come at unexpected times and places. Elisha was in the middle of his daily work when he was called, reminding us to be ready for God's call at any moment.

Transition and Transformation
Elisha's transition from farmer to prophet illustrates how God can transform our lives and use us for His purposes when we are willing to follow Him.

What Does 1 Kings 19:20 Mean
So Elisha left the oxen

• The narrative opens with decisive action. Elisha stops plowing and steps away from his livelihood.

• Such immediate obedience echoes Genesis 12:1–4, where Abram leaves familiar ground at God’s call, and Matthew 4:20, where Peter and Andrew “left their nets at once.”

• The plow in the field symbolized ordinary life; relinquishing it pictures turning from self-directed work to God-directed mission.

ran after Elijah

• Elisha pursues the prophet, not waiting for a second invitation. Urgency marks authentic calling (Philippians 3:12–14; Hebrews 12:1).

• Running shows eagerness, much like the prodigal’s father running to welcome his son (Luke 15:20), underscoring relational zeal in God’s kingdom.

and said, “Please let me kiss my father and mother goodbye”

• Elisha honors his parents (Exodus 20:12) while still prioritizing the prophetic summons.

• His request recalls Ruth 1:14–17—affectionate farewells paired with resolute commitment.

• Luke 14:26 will later clarify that allegiance to the Lord must surpass family ties, yet Deuteronomy 5:16 affirms showing proper respect; Elisha holds both truths in balance.

and then I will follow you.”

• “Follow” signals lifelong discipleship, not a temporary trip (2 Kings 2:1–6).

• The phrase anticipates Jesus’ repeated call, “Follow Me,” to the Twelve (Matthew 4:19; John 21:19).

• Elisha’s future ministry—recorded in 2 Kings—springs from this moment of obedient resolve.

“Go on back,” Elijah replied

• Elijah tests Elisha’s freedom, much as Jesus allowed potential disciples to count the cost (Luke 9:57–62).

• Genuine commitment endures examination (2 Corinthians 13:5). Elijah’s words remove coercion, ensuring Elisha’s yes will be wholehearted.

“for what have I done to you?”

• Elijah clarifies that the call is ultimately God’s, not a human agenda (1 Samuel 3:8–10).

• The mantle thrown earlier (1 Kings 19:19) signified divine appointment; Elijah merely served as God’s messenger.

• This question invites Elisha to remember who truly summons and empowers him (Zechariah 4:6).

summary

Elisha’s brief exchange with Elijah portrays the pattern of true discipleship: immediate surrender of personal ambitions, respectful closure of past ties, and wholehearted pursuit of a God-given calling. Elijah removes any hint of compulsion, emphasizing that the summons is from the Lord Himself. The scene invites every believer to respond to God’s call with the same urgency, freedom, and unwavering dedication.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) Let me, I pray thee.--It is impossible not to compare this with the similar request made to our Lord (Luke 9:61-62) by one who declared readiness to follow Him. The comparison suggests that the answer of Elijah is one of half-ironical rebuke of what seemed hesitation--"Go back, if thou wilt; what have I done to constrain thee?" In both cases we have the stern but necessary rejection of half-hearted service, even if the heart be distracted by the most natural and sacred love. But Elijah sees that Elisha means simply farewell, and he apparently waits till it is over.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - And he left the oxen [As, being the last in the line, he could do, without stopping the others. It is probable too that, Elisha being the last, Elijah's action would not have been observed by the rest], and ran after Elijah [It is clear that Elisha both understood the act, and made up his mind at once. No doubt he too had long sighed and prayed over the demoralization of his country and the dishonour done to his God. Elijah, after casting the mantle, strode on, leaving it for Elisha to take or reject it. The latter soon showed his choice by running after him], and said, Let me, I pray thee, kiss my father and my mother, and then I will follow thee. And he said unto him; Go back again [Heb. go, return]: for what have I done to thee? [There is not a word of reproof here, as Wordsworth and Rawlinson imagine. Indeed, it would have been strange if there had been. A greater readiness to obey the prophetic summons, Elisha could not well have showed. Forthwith, as soon as he realized his call, "he left the oxen and ran after" his newmaster. True, he asks permission - and why should he not? for "grace is no enemy to good nature" - to give a parting embrace to the father and mother to whom he owed his life, and whom he had been required by God to honour. But there is no proof of "a divided heart" here. If he had begged to be allowed to stay and bury his mother and father (St. Luke 9:59-61) it might have been otherwise. But he suggests nothing of the kind. He says: "One kiss, one farewell, and then I will follow thee." It is a complete mistake, consequently, to interpret Elijah's words to mean, "Go, return to thy ploughing, for why shouldst thou quit it?... Thou canst remain as thou art" (Rawlinson). Their true meaning, as evidenced by the sequel (ver. 21), clearly was, "Go back and kiss them; why shouldst thou not? For what have I done to thee? I have summoned thee to follow me. But I have not required thee to repudiate thine own flesh and blood."]
