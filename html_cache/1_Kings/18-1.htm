Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
After a long time
This phrase indicates a significant period has passed since the events of 1 Kings 17, where Elijah first announced the drought. The "long time" underscores the severity of the drought and the patience required of both Elijah and the people. It reflects the testing of faith and endurance, themes prevalent throughout the Bible, such as in James 1:3-4, which speaks of perseverance.

in the third year of the drought
The drought lasted three years, a period of divine judgment on Israel for its idolatry under King Ahab and Queen Jezebel. This timeframe is confirmed in Luke 4:25 and James 5:17, where it is mentioned that the drought lasted three and a half years. The number three often symbolizes completeness or divine perfection in the Bible, as seen in the Trinity and the resurrection of Christ on the third day.

the word of the LORD came to Elijah
This phrase emphasizes the prophetic authority of Elijah, who acts as a mouthpiece for God. The "word of the LORD" is a common biblical expression indicating divine revelation, as seen in the callings of other prophets like Jeremiah (Jeremiah 1:4) and Ezekiel (Ezekiel 1:3). It underscores the importance of obedience to God's commands.

Go and present yourself to Ahab
Elijah is instructed to confront King Ahab, a bold and dangerous task given Ahab's hostility towards him. This command highlights Elijah's role as a prophet who must deliver God's message regardless of personal risk. It parallels the courage required by other biblical figures, such as Moses before Pharaoh (Exodus 5:1) and John the Baptist before Herod (Mark 6:18).

and I will send rain upon the face of the earth
This promise of rain signifies the end of the drought and God's mercy and faithfulness in response to repentance. It reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where blessings follow obedience (Deuteronomy 28:12). The rain can also be seen as a type of spiritual renewal, foreshadowing the outpouring of the Holy Spirit (Joel 2:23, Acts 2:17).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Elijah
A prophet of God known for his boldness and faithfulness. He is tasked with delivering God's message to King Ahab.

2. Ahab
The king of Israel, known for his idolatry and leading Israel away from God. His reign is marked by conflict with Elijah.

3. The LORD
The covenant God of Israel, who communicates His will through prophets like Elijah.

4. Drought
A significant event in Israel, lasting three years, as a consequence of the nation's idolatry and disobedience.

5. Rain
Symbolizes God's mercy and the end of judgment, promised to return upon Elijah's obedience to God's command.
Teaching Points
Obedience to God's Word
Elijah's willingness to confront Ahab demonstrates the importance of obeying God's commands, even when it is difficult or dangerous.

God's Sovereignty and Timing
The timing of the rain shows that God is in control of nature and history, and His plans unfold according to His perfect timing.

Faith in Action
Elijah's actions remind us that faith often requires us to step out in boldness, trusting in God's promises.

Repentance and Restoration
The promise of rain signifies God's readiness to restore and bless His people when they turn back to Him.

The Power of Prayer
Elijah's account encourages believers to pray fervently and expectantly, knowing that God hears and responds to the prayers of the righteous.

What Does 1 Kings 18:1 Mean
After a long time

• The narrative pauses until “a long time” has passed, reminding us that God’s timetable often stretches our patience (cf. Galatians 6:9).

• The delay underscores divine sovereignty: the drought will not lift one day sooner than God ordains (see Exodus 9:5).

• This season of waiting is also Elijah’s preparation period; hidden in Zarephath, he learns to depend daily on God’s provision (1 Kings 17:7–16).

In the third year of the drought

• Scripture dates the encounter precisely, anchoring it in real history. Luke 4:25 and James 5:17 mention three-and-a-half years, a figure that includes the initial months before Elijah announced the drought and the final months before rain actually fell—no contradiction, simply two valid ways of reckoning the same span.

• God’s covenant word in Deuteronomy 28:23–24 promised drought for national rebellion; by year three the lesson is unmistakable—idolatry has tangible consequences.

• The statement hints at mercy: God limits judgment’s duration (Psalm 30:5).

The word of the LORD came to Elijah

• Divine initiative drives the storyline; Elijah moves only when God speaks (1 Kings 17:2, 8).

• “Came” signals fresh revelation, not mere recollection, assuring us that the living God remains actively involved (Hebrews 1:1).

• The prophet’s obedience model—waiting for God’s word, then acting—mirrors the believer’s daily walk (John 10:27).

Go and present yourself to Ahab

• God sends Elijah back to the very king who has hunted him (1 Kings 18:10). Bold obedience replaces hiding.

• Confrontation is sometimes God’s chosen path to repentance; prophets do not skirt difficult assignments (Acts 5:29).

• This command sets up Mount Carmel’s showdown, where the distinction between the LORD and Baal will be made unmistakable (1 Kings 18:21).

And I will send rain upon the face of the earth

• The promise is explicit: God—not Baal, the so-called storm god—controls the heavens (Jeremiah 14:22).

• Judgment gives way to grace once its purpose is served; rainfall signals both forgiveness and restoration (Joel 2:23–24).

• The sequence is vital: obedience first, blessing follows (Leviticus 26:3–4). Elijah must appear before Ahab before the clouds gather.

summary

1 Kings 18:1 captures the turning point in Israel’s drought narrative: after a divinely measured delay, God speaks, calls His prophet into risky obedience, confronts apostate leadership, and pledges to end judgment with life-giving rain. The verse showcases God’s faithfulness to His word, His control over nature and nations, and His readiness to restore when His people turn back to Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) The third year.--By the accurate tradition, preserved in Luke 4:25, James 5:17, it would seem that the drought lasted "three years and six months." If, therefore, the expression in the text is to be taken literally, it must be reckoned from the beginning of the visit to Zarephath.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - And it came to pass after [This word is wanting in the Heb. except in a few MSS.] many days that the word of the Lord came to Elijah in the third year [From what date is this "third year" to be counted? The prima facie view is that the words refer to "these years" mentioned in 1 Kings 17:1, i.e., to the date of the announcement of the drought, and this is the interpretation of the Rabbins and some of the moderns. But it is almost fatal to this view that the duration of the drought is distinctly stated in the New Testament to have been "three years and six months" (Luke 4:25; James 5:17). It is every way better, therefore, to connect the words with 1 Kings 17:7, i.e., with the date of the sojourn at Zarephath. It follows hence that the prophet spent about one year in the Wady Cherith, and two and a half in the house of the widow], saying, Go, show thyself [Heb. be seen] unto Ahab; and I will send [Heb. give] rain upon the earth. [Heb. on the face of the ground. Cf. 1 Kings 17:14.]
