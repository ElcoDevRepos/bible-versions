Now the LORD grew angry with Solomon
The anger of the LORD is a recurring theme in the Old Testament, often in response to covenant unfaithfulness. God's anger is not capricious but is a righteous response to sin and disobedience. Solomon, despite his wisdom and the blessings he received, failed to uphold the covenant by turning to idolatry. This anger reflects God's holiness and justice, as He cannot tolerate sin, especially from those He has chosen and blessed.

because his heart had turned away from the LORD
The heart in biblical terms often refers to the center of one's being, including mind, will, and emotions. Solomon's heart turning away signifies a deep-seated apostasy, not just a momentary lapse. This turning away is significant because Solomon had been given wisdom and understanding beyond any other king. His heart's departure from God is a cautionary tale about the dangers of complacency and the influence of foreign alliances and marriages, which led him to idolatry.

the God of Israel
This title emphasizes the covenant relationship between God and the nation of Israel. It underscores the special status Israel held as God's chosen people, a status that came with responsibilities and expectations. Solomon's actions were not just personal failings but had national implications, as the king's faithfulness was integral to the nation's covenantal blessings.

who had appeared to him twice
God's appearances to Solomon were significant events, marking divine favor and communication. The first appearance was at Gibeon, where God granted Solomon wisdom (1 Kings 3:5-14), and the second was after the dedication of the temple, where God reaffirmed His covenant with Solomon (1 Kings 9:2-9). These appearances highlight the gravity of Solomon's sin, as he turned away despite direct encounters with God. This serves as a reminder of the seriousness of turning away from God after receiving clear revelation and blessings.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Solomon
The son of King David and Bathsheba, Solomon was the third king of Israel, known for his wisdom, wealth, and building projects, including the Temple in Jerusalem. Despite his early devotion, Solomon's heart turned away from God due to his marriages to foreign women who led him to idolatry.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who had a personal relationship with Solomon, appearing to him twice and granting him wisdom and wealth. Yahweh's anger is a response to Solomon's disobedience and idolatry.

3. Israel
The united kingdom under Solomon's rule, which experienced peace and prosperity during his reign. However, Solomon's apostasy set the stage for the kingdom's eventual division.

4. Jerusalem
The capital city of Israel and the location of the Temple, which Solomon built as a place for God's presence among His people.

5. Idolatry
The worship of idols or false gods, which Solomon fell into by building high places for the gods of his foreign wives, leading to God's anger.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Compromise
Solomon's marriages to foreign women led him to compromise his faith. Believers must guard against small compromises that can lead to significant spiritual decline.

The Importance of Obedience
Despite his wisdom, Solomon's disobedience brought God's anger. Obedience to God's commands is crucial for maintaining a right relationship with Him.

God's Faithfulness and Justice
God appeared to Solomon twice, showing His faithfulness. However, God's justice required addressing Solomon's sin, reminding us of the balance between God's love and holiness.

Guarding the Heart
Solomon's heart turned away from God, emphasizing the need for believers to guard their hearts and remain steadfast in their devotion to God.

Consequences of Sin
Solomon's idolatry had lasting consequences for Israel, illustrating how personal sin can impact others and future generations.

What Does 1 Kings 11:9 Mean
Now the LORD grew angry with Solomon

• Scripture does not shy away from describing God’s righteous anger. Here, “the LORD grew angry” underscores His personal response to covenant violation, echoing earlier warnings in 1 Kings 9:6–9 where He said, “then I will cut off Israel from the land I have given them.”

• God’s anger is never arbitrary; it flows from His holiness (Exodus 34:14) and His faithfulness to the covenant promises and conditions (Deuteronomy 29:25–28).

• Solomon’s story reminds us that even great wisdom or past obedience does not exempt anyone from accountability (James 1:22–24).

because his heart had turned away from the LORD

• The issue is heart-level, not merely outward actions. Deuteronomy 17:17 had warned Israel’s kings not to multiply wives “lest his heart turn away.” Solomon ignored that safeguard (1 Kings 11:3–4).

• A “turned” heart pictures gradual drift: small compromises that accumulate until full-blown idolatry surfaces. Proverbs 4:23 urges, “Guard your heart with all diligence, for from it flow the springs of life.”

• God values undivided loyalty (Joshua 24:23; Matthew 6:24). Anything that competes for first place—relationships, ambitions, pleasures—will eventually estrange the heart from Him.

the God of Israel

• The title reminds us of the covenant relationship forged at Sinai (Exodus 19:5–6). Solomon was not dealing with an impersonal deity but with the personal Redeemer who had chosen, delivered, and shepherded the nation (2 Samuel 7:23).

• Calling Him “the God of Israel” highlights the public ramifications of Solomon’s sin. A king’s unfaithfulness jeopardized the entire people (1 Kings 14:15–16).

• For believers today, we follow “the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ” (2 Corinthians 1:3); our personal choices still impact Christ’s body, the church (1 Corinthians 12:26).

who had appeared to him twice

• God’s prior appearances at Gibeon (1 Kings 3:5) and after the Temple’s dedication (1 Kings 9:2) were extraordinary privileges. They magnify Solomon’s responsibility—“From everyone who has been given much, much will be required” (Luke 12:48).

• Repeated revelation intensifies accountability (Hebrews 2:1–3). Ignoring clear light invites discipline (Psalm 89:30–32).

• The Lord’s two personal encounters showed both generosity (granting wisdom and peace) and warning (calling for wholehearted devotion). Solomon’s later apostasy was therefore not ignorance but willful rebellion.

summary

1 Kings 11:9 reveals that God’s anger toward Solomon sprang from covenant love violated by a heart that drifted into idolatry, despite the unique grace Solomon had received. The verse teaches that unparalleled blessing does not shield anyone from consequences if the heart turns away. God, ever faithful as “the God of Israel,” calls His people—leaders and followers alike—to unwavering loyalty, reminding us that privilege heightens, rather than lessens, our accountability to walk in wholehearted obedience.
