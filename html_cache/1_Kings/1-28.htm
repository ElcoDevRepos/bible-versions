Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then King David said
King David, nearing the end of his life, is a central figure in Israel's history. His reign established Jerusalem as the political and spiritual center of Israel. This moment occurs during a time of political tension, as Adonijah, David's son, has declared himself king without David's approval. David's decision-making here is crucial for the future of the Davidic line, which is significant in biblical prophecy concerning the Messiah (2 Samuel 7:12-16).

“Call in Bathsheba for me.”
Bathsheba, the wife of David and mother of Solomon, plays a pivotal role in the succession narrative. Her relationship with David began controversially (2 Samuel 11), but she is now a key figure in ensuring Solomon's rightful place as king. This request signifies David's recognition of Bathsheba's influence and the importance of securing Solomon's succession, aligning with God's promise that Solomon would succeed David (1 Chronicles 22:9-10).

So she came into the king’s presence
Bathsheba's entrance into the king's presence indicates her respected status and the urgency of the situation. In the ancient Near Eastern context, approaching a king required permission, highlighting the gravity of her role. Her presence before David underscores the personal and political dynamics at play, as she advocates for her son's future.

and stood before him.
Standing before the king signifies respect and readiness to engage in important dialogue. This posture reflects Bathsheba's position as both a subject and a mother advocating for her son. It also sets the stage for David's subsequent actions to affirm Solomon's kingship, fulfilling God's covenantal promise and ensuring the continuation of the Davidic line, which is foundational to the messianic hope found in the New Testament (Matthew 1:1).

Persons / Places / Events
1. King David
The second king of Israel, known for his heart for God, his military conquests, and his complex personal life. At this point, he is elderly and nearing the end of his reign.

2. Bathsheba
The wife of David, originally the wife of Uriah the Hittite. She is the mother of Solomon and plays a crucial role in ensuring Solomon's succession to the throne.

3. Solomon
Although not directly mentioned in this verse, Solomon is the son of David and Bathsheba, whom David intends to succeed him as king.

4. Adonijah
Another son of David, who is attempting to usurp the throne, setting the stage for the events in this chapter.

5. Jerusalem
The capital city of Israel, where these events are taking place, and the center of political and religious life in the kingdom.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Godly Counsel
David's decision to call Bathsheba and later Nathan the prophet shows the importance of seeking wise and godly counsel in times of decision-making.

God's Sovereignty in Leadership
Despite human attempts to alter God's plans (as seen in Adonijah's actions), God's purposes prevail. This reminds us to trust in God's sovereignty over our lives and leadership.

The Role of Women in God's Plan
Bathsheba's involvement highlights the significant role women can play in God's unfolding plan, even in a patriarchal society.

The Legacy of Faithfulness
David's actions, even in his old age, demonstrate the importance of faithfulness to God's promises and instructions, ensuring a godly legacy.

Repentance and Redemption
David and Bathsheba's account is one of sin, repentance, and redemption, showing that God can bring good out of our failures when we turn back to Him.

What Does 1 Kings 1:28 Mean
Then King David said

• David, though frail and bedridden (1 Kings 1:1), remains Israel’s anointed ruler; his words still carry covenant authority as in 2 Samuel 23:1–2.

• By speaking, he takes decisive leadership at a critical moment when Adonijah is attempting to seize the throne (1 Kings 1:5).

• His initiative demonstrates that God keeps His promises through David’s line (2 Samuel 7:12–16), reaffirming that earthly weakness never cancels divine purpose.

Call in Bathsheba for me

• Bathsheba had just been dismissed so Nathan could confirm her report (1 Kings 1:22–24). David now recalls her to honor the oath he previously swore (1 Kings 1:13).

• Inviting Bathsheba vindicates her role as legitimate queen-mother, echoing the respectful place of other royal mothers like Solomon’s future counsel in Proverbs 31:1.

• It signals David’s intention to rectify the succession crisis swiftly, paralleling moments when righteous kings acted promptly to preserve covenant order (2 Chronicles 23:3–11).

So she came into the king’s presence

• Bathsheba responds without delay, modeling humble obedience similar to Esther approaching Xerxes (Esther 5:1-2) or Joseph’s brothers presenting themselves before Jacob (Genesis 43:26).

• Her entrance underscores court protocol: no one appears uninvited, reinforcing David’s deliberate summons (1 Kings 1:10, 1 Kings 1:16).

• The scene illustrates how God often works through faithful submission to accomplish His plans (James 4:7; Luke 1:38).

And stood before him

• Bathsheba’s standing posture conveys respect and readiness to hear, akin to Solomon later standing before the altar (1 Kings 8:22).

• In royal settings, standing also implies advocacy; she is poised to receive David’s pledge that Solomon will reign (1 Kings 1:29-30, 1 Kings 1:31).

• Her position anticipates her future influence as queen-mother, a role God uses to guide the kingdom (1 Kings 2:19; Jeremiah 13:18).

summary

1 Kings 1:28 captures a pivotal turning-point: the aged king reasserts authority, summons the rightful queen-mother, and sets in motion God’s ordained succession. Each phrase reveals the Lord’s faithfulness—David’s voice still commands, Bathsheba responds in reverent trust, and together they prepare the way for Solomon, fulfilling God’s covenant promise to David and securing Israel’s future.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 28. - Then king David [see on ver. I] answered and said, Call me Bathsheba [she evidently left the chamber when Nathan entered it. "This was done, not to avoid the appearance of a mutual arrangement (Cler., Then. al.), but for reasons of propriety, inasmuch as in audiences granted by the king to his wife or one of his counsellors, no third person ought to be present unless the king required his assistance." Keil.] And she came into the king's presence, and stood before the king. [Here, as in numberless other instances, our translators have disregarded literalness in favour of euphony. The Hebrew has here an exact repetition, "came before the king, and stood before the king." The Authorized Version rendering was adopted as the more spirited and rhythmical.
