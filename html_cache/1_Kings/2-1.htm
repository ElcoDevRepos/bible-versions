As the time drew near for David to die
This phrase marks a significant transition in the biblical narrative, as King David, a central figure in Israel's history, approaches the end of his life. David's reign was marked by military conquests, the establishment of Jerusalem as the capital, and the unification of the tribes of Israel. His impending death signifies the end of an era and the beginning of a new chapter under Solomon. The phrase also reflects the biblical theme of mortality and the passing of leadership from one generation to the next, a common motif throughout Scripture. David's life and reign are chronicled in 1 Samuel and 2 Samuel, providing a backdrop to this moment.

he charged his son Solomon
David's charge to Solomon is both a personal and a royal mandate. It underscores the importance of succession planning and the continuation of God's covenant promises through David's lineage. Solomon, whose name means "peace," is chosen to build the temple, fulfilling God's promise to David that his son would construct a house for the Lord (2 Samuel 7:12-13). This charge is not merely a transfer of power but a spiritual and moral exhortation, emphasizing obedience to God's laws and statutes. Solomon's reign is pivotal, as it represents the height of Israel's united monarchy and foreshadows the coming of Jesus Christ, who is often referred to as the "Son of David" (Matthew 1:1), fulfilling the messianic prophecies associated with David's line.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The second king of Israel, known for his heart for God and his leadership. At this point, he is nearing the end of his life and is preparing to pass on his legacy and responsibilities to his son.

2. Solomon
The son of David and Bathsheba, chosen by David to succeed him as king. Solomon is known for his wisdom, wealth, and building the first Temple in Jerusalem.

3. Israel
The nation over which David reigned as king. It is a united kingdom at this time, consisting of the twelve tribes of Israel.

4. Jerusalem
The capital city of Israel, established by David as the political and spiritual center of the nation.

5. The Transition of Leadership
This event marks a significant transition in Israel's history, as leadership is passed from David to Solomon, setting the stage for Solomon's reign.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Godly Legacy
David's charge to Solomon highlights the importance of leaving a spiritual and moral legacy. As believers, we are called to pass on our faith and values to the next generation.

Preparation for Leadership
David's instructions to Solomon underscore the need for preparation and guidance in leadership roles. We should seek wisdom and counsel as we step into positions of influence.

Facing Life's Transitions
Just as David prepared Solomon for a significant transition, we must rely on God's guidance during life's changes and uncertainties.

The Role of Obedience
David's charge includes a call to obedience to God's laws. Our success and fulfillment in life are closely tied to our faithfulness to God's Word.

The Power of a Father's Influence
David's relationship with Solomon demonstrates the profound impact a father can have on his children. Fathers are encouraged to lead by example and instill godly principles in their families.

What Does 1 Kings 2:1 Mean
As the time drew near

David senses that his earthly days are almost over (Genesis 47:29; Luke 22:37). Scripture treats such moments with sober clarity—the Lord numbers our days (Job 14:5), and David, a man after God’s own heart, lives within that divine timetable.

• Life has seasons; endings are part of God’s design (Ecclesiastes 3:1–2).

• Nearing death prompts reflection and readiness, much like Paul’s “the time of my departure is at hand” (2 Timothy 4:6).

David’s awareness invites us to live wisely now, “teaching us to number our days” (Psalm 90:12).

for David to die

The text does not soften the reality—death is certain (Hebrews 9:27). Yet for the believer it is not defeat. David “served the purpose of God in his own generation and fell asleep” (Acts 13:36).

• His reign of roughly forty years (1 Kings 2:11) is coming to a close.

• God’s covenant with David (2 Samuel 7:12–16) stands beyond the grave, proving that death cannot thwart divine promises.

In this moment we see both human frailty and God’s enduring faithfulness side by side.

he charged

A “charge” is more than advice; it is a binding, weighty commission (Joshua 23:2; 1 Timothy 6:13–14).

• Final words carry urgency—think of Moses to Joshua (Deuteronomy 31:7–8) or Paul to Timothy (2 Timothy 4:1–2).

• The Hebrew idea of charge implies accountability before God; Solomon must heed or answer to the Lord.

David will soon articulate the charge: “Be strong and prove yourself a man. And keep the charge of the LORD your God” (1 Kings 2:2–3). Obedience, not merely political savvy, is central.

his son Solomon

The baton passes within covenant lineage. Solomon isn’t just any successor; he is the chosen son (1 Chronicles 22:9–10).

• Fatherly instruction echoes in Proverbs: “Listen, my sons, to a father’s instruction” (Proverbs 4:1–4).

• The promise of wisdom (1 Kings 3:12) will equip Solomon to fulfill the charge.

• Relationship matters: leadership rooted in filial love and covenant obedience sets the tone for a kingdom aligned with God’s heart.

summary

1 Kings 2:1 captures a pivotal hinge in redemptive history: David, aware that his God-ordained time is closing, issues a solemn, covenant-anchored charge to his chosen heir. The verse reminds us that life is brief, death is certain, and godly responsibility must be passed on deliberately. David models finishing well; Solomon is summoned to begin well—both under the unchanging faithfulness of the LORD.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - I go the way [lit., I am walking (same word as in ver. 3) in the way] of all the earth [i.e., of all the sons of earth, all mankind (cf. 1 Samuel 17:46; 1 Kings 10:24; Psalm 66:4, etc.) The path to Sheol, the path which all his forefathers, and untold millions more, have trod, he is treading it now. The words sound like a reminiscence of Joshua 23, 24. Perhaps, too, the thought of Joshua suggested to his mind the next words]: but be thou strong, and be a man. [Similar, though not identical, words were four times addressed to Joshua (Joshua 1:6, 7, 9, 18), and David may well have thought that his son, in entering upon his difficult duties, was not at all unlike Joshua when he succeeded Moses in the leadership of Israel, and that he needed similar encouragement. It is not necessary to suppose, as Canon Rawlinson does ("Speaker's Com.," vol. 2 p. 489), that in the words, "show thyself a man," we have a reference to Solomon's youth; for words precisely similar were addressed to each other by the Philistines at Aphek (1 Samuel 4:9). The age of Solomon at his accession is very doubtful. David said, "Solomon my son is young and tender" (1 Chronicles 22:5; 1 Chronicles 29:1); and Solomon says of himself, "I am a little child" נַעַר קָטֹן (1 Kings 3:7). Josephus, probably reflecting the tradition of his time, fixes his age at fourteen; Eupolemus at twelve. I incline to think that the words "young and tender" almost forbid the favourite opinion that he was about twenty.]
