Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the king
This refers to King Solomon, the son of David, who was the third king of Israel. Solomon is known for his wisdom, wealth, and building projects, most notably the construction of the First Temple in Jerusalem. His reign marks a period of peace and prosperity in Israel's history. Solomon's role as king is significant as he leads the nation in worship and dedication to God, reflecting the king's responsibility to guide the people spiritually.

and all Israel with him
This phrase indicates the unity of the nation in worship and dedication. "All Israel" suggests a collective participation of the tribes, emphasizing national unity under Solomon's leadership. This gathering would have included representatives from the twelve tribes, highlighting the importance of communal worship and the central role of the Temple in the life of Israel. It also reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where the king and the nation act together in obedience and reverence.

offered sacrifices
Sacrifices were a central aspect of Israelite worship, serving as a means of atonement, thanksgiving, and dedication to God. The act of offering sacrifices was a way to express devotion, seek forgiveness, and maintain a right relationship with God. This event likely included burnt offerings, peace offerings, and other types of sacrifices as prescribed in the Law of Moses. The scale of the sacrifices offered by Solomon and the people underscores the significance of the Temple's dedication and the desire to honor God.

before the LORD
This phrase signifies that the sacrifices were made in the presence of God, who dwelt in the Temple's Holy of Holies. The Temple was considered the earthly dwelling place of God among His people, and offering sacrifices "before the LORD" indicates an acknowledgment of His sovereignty and holiness. It also reflects the understanding that true worship is directed towards God alone, in accordance with His commandments. This act of worship before the LORD is a precursor to the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who offered Himself once for all, fulfilling the sacrificial system and providing eternal access to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. King Solomon
The son of King David, Solomon is the king of Israel at this time. He is known for his wisdom and for building the temple in Jerusalem.

2. All Israel
This refers to the people of Israel who were present with Solomon. It signifies the unity of the nation in worship and dedication to God.

3. Sacrifices
These are offerings made to God, often involving the slaughter of animals, as a form of worship and atonement for sin.

4. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who is the central focus of Israel's worship and the recipient of the sacrifices.

5. The Temple in Jerusalem
The newly built temple, which Solomon has just dedicated, serves as the central place of worship for the Israelites.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Sacrifice in Worship
Sacrifices were central to Israel's worship, symbolizing dedication, atonement, and thanksgiving to God. Today, believers are called to offer spiritual sacrifices through acts of worship and service.

Unity in Worship
The collective participation of "all Israel" underscores the importance of communal worship. Believers are encouraged to gather together in unity to worship God.

Leadership in Worship
Solomon's role as a leader in worship highlights the responsibility of leaders to guide others in honoring God. Christian leaders today should model and facilitate genuine worship.

The Significance of the Temple
The temple was a physical representation of God's presence among His people. In the New Testament, believers are described as the temple of the Holy Spirit, emphasizing God's indwelling presence.

What Does 1 Kings 8:62 Mean
Then

• The word draws a line from Solomon’s long prayer of dedication straight into action. After the cloud of glory filled the temple (1 Kings 8:10-11), “then” signals the appropriate, timely response to God’s manifest presence.

• “Then” also anchors the event historically; it is not symbolic but real, much like in 2 Chronicles 7:4-5 where sacrifices immediately follow the fire from heaven.

• The sequence reminds us of Exodus 24:3-8: once God speaks and covenants with His people, worship follows right away.

The king

• Solomon, Israel’s anointed leader, takes initiative. Leadership in worship matters; compare David’s example in 1 Chronicles 29:20-21 where the king blesses the LORD and the people join in.

• Solomon’s obedience fulfills Deuteronomy 17:19-20—“that he may learn to fear the LORD his God….” Kingdom authority bows before divine authority.

• His involvement foreshadows the greater King, Jesus, who both offers Himself and leads many sons to glory (Hebrews 2:10).

And all Israel with him

• Worship is communal, not just royal. From elders to strangers, the entire nation participates, echoing Deuteronomy 29:10-12 where “all of you stand today before the LORD your God.”

• Unity around God’s dwelling place pictures Psalm 133:1—“How good and pleasant it is when brothers live together in harmony!”

• The scene also fulfills God’s promise to Abraham that his descendants would serve Him together (Genesis 12:2-3; 17:7).

Offered sacrifices

• The text expands later (v63) to list “22,000 oxen and 120,000 sheep.” Such numbers underline the seriousness of sin and the sufficiency of atonement, echoing Leviticus 1-3 on burnt and fellowship offerings.

• Sacrifice demonstrates costly devotion; David had said, “I will not offer to the LORD my God burnt offerings that cost me nothing” (2 Samuel 24:24).

• Every offering points forward to “the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or spot” (1 Peter 1:19). Hebrews 9:22 reminds us that “without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness.”

Before the LORD

• All of this happens in God’s immediate presence—“before the LORD.” The phrase underscores relationship, not ritual only.

• Exodus 29:42-46 shows sacrifices as daily meetings “where I will meet with you and speak with you.”

• The newly built temple, now filled with glory, becomes a tangible assurance that God dwells among His people, anticipating John 1:14, “The Word became flesh and tabernacled among us.”

• Because believers today have access “by the blood of Jesus…to enter the Most Holy Place” (Hebrews 10:19-22), the same posture of reverent boldness is ours.

Summary

“Then the king and all Israel with him offered sacrifices before the LORD” captures a moment where timely obedience, godly leadership, unified community, costly atonement, and conscious awareness of God’s presence converge. Solomon leads, the nation follows, blood is shed, and all is done face-to-face with the living God—a scene that ultimately points to the perfect King and final Sacrifice who brings His people into everlasting worship.
