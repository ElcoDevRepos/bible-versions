When you see, you will rejoice
This phrase anticipates a future event that will bring joy to the people of God. The context of Isaiah 66 is the promise of restoration and the coming glory of Jerusalem. The joy mentioned here is often associated with the fulfillment of God's promises and the manifestation of His salvation. In biblical history, moments of divine intervention, such as the return from Babylonian exile, were times of great rejoicing. This phrase also echoes the joy of the righteous at the second coming of Christ, as seen in Revelation 19:7, where the marriage of the Lamb brings rejoicing.

and you will flourish like grass
Flourishing like grass suggests rapid growth and renewal, symbolizing prosperity and blessing. In the arid climate of the Middle East, grass flourishes quickly after rain, representing the sudden and abundant blessings from God. This imagery is used throughout Scripture to describe the prosperity of the righteous (Psalm 92:12-14). It also contrasts with the fate of the wicked, who are often compared to grass that withers (Psalm 37:2). The flourishing here can be seen as both physical and spiritual, indicating a time of peace and divine favor.

then the hand of the LORD will be revealed to His servants
The "hand of the LORD" signifies His power and intervention. In biblical terms, God's hand is often associated with deliverance and protection for His people (Exodus 15:6). This revelation to His servants indicates a special manifestation of God's presence and action on behalf of those who are faithful to Him. It is a promise of divine support and vindication, aligning with the theme of God defending and blessing His chosen ones, as seen in passages like Isaiah 41:10.

but His wrath will be shown to His enemies
This phrase highlights the dual nature of God's intervention—salvation for His people and judgment for His adversaries. The wrath of God is a consistent theme in Scripture, representing His righteous anger against sin and injustice (Romans 1:18). In the context of Isaiah, this wrath is directed towards those who oppose God's purposes and oppress His people. The ultimate expression of this wrath is seen in eschatological prophecies, where God's final judgment is executed upon the wicked (Revelation 20:11-15). This serves as a warning and a call to repentance, emphasizing the seriousness of being aligned against God.

Teaching Points
Joy in God's Promises
Believers can find joy and hope in the fulfillment of God's promises, as seen in the rejoicing of hearts in Isaiah 66:14.

Flourishing in Faithfulness
Just as grass flourishes, those who remain faithful to God will experience spiritual growth and vitality.

Understanding God's Justice
Recognize the dual nature of God's hand—blessing for His servants and judgment for His foes. This calls for a life aligned with His will.

The Assurance of God's Presence
The hand of the LORD being made known signifies His active involvement in the lives of His people, offering assurance and guidance.

Living with an Eternal Perspective
The ultimate fulfillment of God's promises encourages believers to live with an eternal perspective, focusing on spiritual rather than temporal gains.

What Does Isaiah 66:14 Mean
When you see

Isaiah has just spoken of God returning comfort and glory to Jerusalem (Isaiah 66:10–13). The words “When you see” invite God’s people to expect a literal, observable fulfillment.

• Isaiah 60:5 echoes the same promise: “Then you will see and be radiant.”

• Simeon’s joy in Luke 2:30, “For my eyes have seen Your salvation,” shows how seeing God’s work ignites faith.

• Psalm 34:8 urges, “Taste and see that the LORD is good,” underscoring that God intends His acts to be experienced, not merely imagined.

In every age, God keeps His word openly so believers can point to real evidence of His faithfulness.

You will rejoice

Once God’s people witness His intervention, joy naturally follows.

• Psalm 16:11 affirms, “In Your presence is fullness of joy.”

• Jesus promised the same pattern in John 16:22: “Now you have sorrow, but I will see you again and your heart will rejoice, and no one will take your joy from you.”

Joy here is not a fleeting emotion; it is the settled gladness that springs from seeing God act exactly as He said He would.

You will flourish like grass

Flourishing “like grass” pictures rapid, widespread, and unmistakable growth.

• Hosea 14:5 states, “He will blossom like the lily and take root like the cedars of Lebanon,” pairing beauty with stability.

• Psalm 72:16 looks ahead to Messiah’s reign when “abundance of grain will be on the earth, flourishing on the tops of the hills.”

God is promising tangible blessing—spiritual vitality, restored communities, even physical renewal. The comparison to grass also reminds us that the growth comes from God’s provision of rain and sun, not human effort (cf. 1 Corinthians 3:6).

Then the hand of the LORD will be revealed to His servants

“Hand” signals God’s active, powerful intervention.

• Exodus 6:6 records the first great unveiling: “I will redeem you with an outstretched arm and with mighty acts of judgment.”

• Isaiah 40:10 repeats, “Behold, the Lord GOD comes with power, and His arm rules for Him.”

The revelation is not abstract; it is God rolling up His sleeves for His followers. Servants who trust Him will witness decisive rescue, protection, and reward (1 Peter 5:6).

His wrath will be shown to His enemies

God’s mercy and judgment arrive together; His covenant love toward believers necessitates righteous anger toward persistent rebellion.

• Nahum 1:2 declares, “The LORD is a jealous and avenging God; the LORD takes vengeance on His foes.”

• Romans 1:18 confirms that even now “the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness.”

• Revelation 19:15 pictures the final outworking: “He treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God Almighty.”

Isaiah’s listeners—and readers today—are warned that refusing God’s grace leaves only the certainty of just retribution.

summary

Isaiah 66:14 promises God’s people a future they will literally see: bursting joy, abundant growth, and an unmistakable display of His mighty hand. At the same moment, God’s enemies will encounter His righteous wrath. The verse calls every reader to stand among the servants who watch, rejoice, and flourish under His faithful care.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) Your bones shall flourish . . .--"Heart" and "bones" stand respectively as symbols of the inner and outer life. The "bones," the branches, so to speak, of the body, which had been dry and sere, should revive as with the sap of a new life, and be as the succulent herbage. His "hand," i.e., His manifested power, will show itself in love to His people, in indignation to their enemies.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - Your bones shall flourish like an herb (comp. Isaiah 58:11). In the time of calamity, Israel's "bones" have been "consumed" (Psalm 31:10), and "waxed old" (Psalm 32:3), and "burned with heat" (Job 30:30). Now they shall enjoy a time of refreshing from the Lord. New life shall enter them, and health and growth shall follow. The nation shall be rejuvenated, and "flourish" in more than its pristine strength. The hand of the Lord shall be known; or recognized, both in this merciful treatment of his servants, and also in the indignation with which he will visit his enemies. This last clause conveniently introduces the following "theophany" (vers. 15-1S).
