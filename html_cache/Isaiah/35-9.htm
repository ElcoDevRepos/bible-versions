Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
No lion will be there
This phrase signifies a place of safety and peace, free from threats and dangers. In the ancient Near East, lions were common predators and symbolized danger and destruction. The absence of lions indicates a divine protection and a restoration of creation to a state of harmony. This imagery aligns with the prophetic vision of a new creation where natural threats are eliminated, as seen in Isaiah 11:6-9, where the wolf dwells with the lamb.

and no vicious beast will go up on it
The mention of "vicious beast" extends the idea of safety to include all forms of danger, not just lions. This reflects the broader biblical theme of God’s protection over His people. In the context of Isaiah, this promise is part of the eschatological hope for Israel, where God’s kingdom is fully realized, and His people are secure. This can be connected to Revelation 21:4, where there is no more death or mourning, symbolizing the ultimate peace and safety in God's presence.

Such will not be found there
This phrase emphasizes the certainty and completeness of the safety promised. It reassures that the threats are not merely absent temporarily but are permanently removed. This assurance is part of the prophetic vision of the Messianic age, where God’s reign is fully established, and His people experience eternal peace. It reflects the fulfillment of God’s promises to Israel and, by extension, to all believers.

but the redeemed will walk upon it
The "redeemed" refers to those who have been saved or delivered by God. In the context of Isaiah, this likely refers to the Israelites returning from exile, symbolizing spiritual and physical restoration. The concept of redemption is central to the biblical narrative, pointing to the ultimate redemption through Jesus Christ. This phrase can be connected to passages like Isaiah 51:11 and Revelation 5:9, where the redeemed are those who have been purchased by the blood of the Lamb and walk in the newness of life. The imagery of walking suggests a journey or pilgrimage, indicating ongoing fellowship with God in a restored relationship.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Redeemed
Refers to those who have been saved or delivered by God. In the context of Isaiah, it often points to the Israelites who are restored and brought back to their land, but it also has a broader application to all who are saved by God's grace.

2. The Highway of Holiness
This is the path mentioned earlier in Isaiah 35:8, symbolizing the way of righteousness and purity that leads to God's presence. It is a place of safety and peace, free from danger and evil.

3. Lions and Vicious Beasts
Symbolic of danger, evil, and chaos. In the ancient Near East, lions were literal threats, but they also represent spiritual and moral dangers.

4. The Wilderness and Desert
The transformation of these places into fertile and safe areas is a recurring theme in Isaiah, symbolizing God's power to bring life and order out of barrenness and chaos.

5. The Return from Exile
Historically, this passage is often seen as a prophecy of the return of the Jewish people from Babylonian exile, but it also points to the ultimate redemption through Christ.
Teaching Points
Safety in God's Presence
The absence of lions and vicious beasts on the Highway of Holiness illustrates the complete safety and peace found in God's presence. Believers can trust in God's protection as they walk in His ways.

The Path of Redemption
The redeemed walking on this path signifies the journey of salvation. It is a reminder that our walk with God is one of continual sanctification and holiness.

Transformation through God
Just as the wilderness is transformed, God can transform our lives, bringing order and life where there was once chaos and barrenness.

Hope for the Future
This prophecy provides hope for ultimate redemption and restoration, encouraging believers to look forward to the fulfillment of God's promises in Christ.

What Does Isaiah 35:9 Mean
No lion will be there

Isaiah 35 pictures the coming restoration of Zion, a literal future when God’s people live in perfect safety. Lions, apex predators of the ancient Near East, stood for immediate, tangible danger. Their absence promises:

• Total removal of physical threats (see Isaiah 11:6; 65:25, where even natural predators are pacified).

• End of spiritual stalking—Satan “prowls around like a roaring lion” (1 Peter 5:8); in God’s redeemed realm his menace is gone.

• Fulfillment of the covenant hope that God Himself becomes the protective wall around His people (Zechariah 2:5).

and no vicious beast will go up on it

The “it” is the “Highway of Holiness” described in Isaiah 35:8. God guarantees that no ferocious creature can even set foot on that road.

• The route is divinely policed—nothing unclean or threatening may enter (Revelation 21:27).

• Echoes of God’s promise to “banish wild beasts from the land” under His covenant blessings (Leviticus 26:6; Ezekiel 34:25).

• For us, it underscores the certainty that nothing can ambush the pilgrim who travels under God’s covenant care (Psalm 91:9-13).

Such will not be found there

The phrase reinforces the absolute nature of the security. Not “few,” not “rare,” but “not be found.”

• God’s future kingdom is not merely improved; it is completely cleansed (Isaiah 4:4).

• The finality anticipates the day when “death will be no more” and every threat—physical, moral, or spiritual—is erased (Revelation 21:4).

• The earth itself is transformed, undoing the curse that introduced violence into creation (Romans 8:19-21).

but the redeemed will walk upon it

Contrast completes the verse: what is excluded (predators) highlights who is included (the redeemed).

• “Redeemed” points to those ransomed by the Lord’s own intervention (Isaiah 51:11; Titus 2:14).

• They “walk,” not merely arrive; their journey is free, joyful, unhurried—life as it was meant to be (Micah 4:2-4).

• The verse anticipates Jesus, the Way (John 14:6), whose atonement purchases safe passage for every believer into God’s restored world (Hebrews 10:19-22).

summary

Isaiah 35:9 promises a literal future in which God eradicates every threat, neutralizes all evil, and opens a perfectly secure highway for His redeemed people. No lions, no beasts, no lurking danger—only those bought by the Lord walking confidently in the fullness of His salvation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) No lion shall be there . . .--We have to remember that the lion had not ceased to haunt the valley of the Jordan, as it had done in the days of Samson (Judges 14:5), and David (1Samuel 17:3-4; 2Samuel 23:20). The recent depopulation of the northern kingdom had probably laid the country more open to their attack (2Kings 17:25), and thus gave a special force to the prophet's description. For "any ravenous beast," read the most ravenous.

The redeemed . . . (10) . . . the ransomed.--The Hebrew words express simply the idea of release and freedom, without implying, as the English words do, a payment as its condition.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - No lion shall be there. No great tyrannical power, like Assyria (Nahum 2:11, 12) or Babylon, shall arrest the energies of the Church, take it captive, or enslave it. No ravenous beast shall make it his prey. In proportion as the Church is holy (ver. 8) it shall be free from the molestation of bloody persecutors (see Isaiah 11:9). The redeemed - those whom God has purchased for his own (Exodus 6:6; Hosea 13:14) - shall be free to walk there, untroubled by cruel enemies. There is an under-current of comparison between the blessedness of the last times and the existing troubles of Israel, still threatened by Sennacherib.
