Then the word of the LORD to them will become:
This phrase indicates a shift in how God's message is perceived by the people. Historically, the Israelites had received clear instructions and prophecies from God through His prophets. However, due to their disobedience and hard-heartedness, the clarity of God's word becomes a source of confusion and judgment. This reflects a broader biblical theme where God's word is a double-edged sword, offering both guidance and judgment depending on the heart of the listener (Hebrews 4:12).

“Order on order, order on order, line on line, line on line;
This repetition emphasizes the tedious and monotonous nature of the instruction that the people of Israel are receiving. It suggests a mocking tone, as if the people are being treated like children who need simple, repetitive lessons. This reflects the cultural context of the time, where the Israelites were often compared to children in their understanding and obedience (Isaiah 28:9-10). The phrase also highlights the people's failure to grasp the deeper spiritual truths, as they are stuck on the surface level of God's teachings.

a little here, a little there,”
This part of the verse suggests fragmentation and a lack of coherence in the understanding of God's word. It implies that the people are only receiving bits and pieces of the truth, which leads to confusion and misinterpretation. This can be connected to the broader biblical narrative where partial understanding often leads to error and misjudgment (1 Corinthians 13:9-12). The phrase also underscores the importance of seeking the full counsel of God rather than relying on isolated teachings.

so that they will go stumbling backward
The imagery of stumbling backward indicates a regression rather than progress. In the historical context, this can be seen as a consequence of the Israelites' refusal to heed God's warnings and instructions. Instead of moving forward in their spiritual journey, they are falling back into sin and disobedience. This is a common theme in the Old Testament, where the people's failure to follow God's commands leads to their downfall (Jeremiah 7:24).

and will be injured, ensnared, and captured.
This phrase outlines the consequences of the people's spiritual blindness and disobedience. Being injured, ensnared, and captured suggests both physical and spiritual repercussions. Historically, this can be linked to the impending judgment and exile that the Israelites would face due to their unfaithfulness. Theologically, it serves as a warning of the dangers of ignoring God's word, leading to spiritual captivity and separation from God. This can also be seen as a type of Christ, where Jesus, the ultimate Word of God, offers freedom and healing to those who accept Him, contrasting with the captivity that comes from rejecting God's message (John 8:31-36).

Teaching Points
The Danger of Spiritual Immaturity
Just as the Israelites were unable to grasp the deeper truths of God's word, we must strive for spiritual maturity to understand and apply God's teachings effectively.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Ignoring God's instructions leads to spiritual stumbling and captivity. We must heed God's word to avoid similar pitfalls in our lives.

The Importance of Humility in Learning
The Israelites' pride led them to dismiss God's word as simplistic. We should approach Scripture with humility, ready to learn and grow.

God's Patience and Repetition
God's repetitive instructions demonstrate His patience. He desires for us to understand and follow His ways, even if it requires repeated lessons.

The Role of Prophecy in Warning and Correction
Prophecies like Isaiah's serve as warnings. We should take heed of biblical warnings and allow them to correct our paths.

What Does Isaiah 28:13 Mean
Then the word of the LORD to them will become

The Lord keeps speaking, yet the hearers are rebellious. His Word is not absent; it is persistently offered.

• Cross references that show God repeatedly sending His word: 2 Kings 17:13; Jeremiah 7:25–26; Hebrews 1:1–2.

• The people’s problem is not lack of revelation but hardened hearts that refuse to receive it.

Order on order, order on order

BSB: “Order on order…” The thought is of command after command, duty after duty.

• Like a parent repeating the same rule, God lays down clear, unvarnished instructions (Deuteronomy 6:1–2).

• The repetition stresses that no excuse remains; they cannot claim ignorance (Romans 1:20, 2 Peter 3:1–2).

Line on line, line on line

BSB: “Line on line…” The phrase pictures measured, precise guidance—each line added carefully to the last.

• God’s Word is orderly, not chaotic (Psalm 19:7–8; 1 Corinthians 14:33).

• Every “line” carries weight; ignoring even one breaks the whole (James 2:10).

A little here, a little there

BSB: “A little here, a little there.” The teaching seems simple, almost childlike, yet it is sufficient.

• Isaiah earlier offered a childlike sign (Isaiah 7:14) and simple trust (Isaiah 30:15).

• Jesus later echoes this principle when He says, “Whoever is faithful with very little will also be faithful with much” (Luke 16:10).

So that they will go stumbling backward

Rejecting the steady drip of truth turns the blessings of God’s Word into a stumbling block.

• Psalm 118:22; 1 Peter 2:7–8—those who reject the cornerstone stumble.

• Truth refused becomes judgment; what should have guided them now trips them up (Romans 11:7–10).

And will be injured, ensnared, and captured

The final outcome of persistent disbelief is unavoidable judgment.

• Proverbs 29:1 warns that repeated hardening leads to sudden destruction.

• Assyrian exile for Ephraim (2 Kings 17:6) and later Babylonian exile for Judah (2 Chronicles 36:15–17) fulfill this word.

• God’s just discipline underscores His holiness while preserving a remnant who heed (Isaiah 10:20–22; Romans 11:5).

summary

Isaiah 28:13 shows that when God’s clear, repeated Word is scorned, it ceases to instruct and begins to condemn. The Lord patiently lays out “order on order, line on line,” but those who refuse to bend will ultimately break. The verse warns that the same Scripture that can build a life of faith will, if resisted, become the very cause of stumbling, injury, and captivity.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) That they might go, and fall backward . . .--The words are an echo of those in Isaiah 8:14-15. The preaching which might have led to "rest and refreshing" would become to those who scorned it a "stumbling stone" on which they would fall, a "net" in which they, who boasted of their freedom, would be entangled.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - The word of the Lord was to them; rather, shall be to them. God will now speak to them, not by his prophet, but by the Assyrian conqueror, who will do what they said Isaiah had done, i.e. lay upon them command after command, rule alter rule, a constant series of minute injunctions, under which they will chafe and fret and at last rebel, but only to be "snared and taken." It is uncertain whether the reference is to the immediate future and to the Assyrians proper only, or whether the Babylonians are not taken into account also, and their oppression of Judaea pointed to. The yoke of Babylon was probably quite as difficult to endure as that of Assyria; and we find that, in the space of eighteen years, it produced at least three rebellions.
