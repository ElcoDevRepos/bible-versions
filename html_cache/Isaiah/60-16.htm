Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
You will drink the milk of nations
This phrase symbolizes abundance and prosperity that will come to God's people. In ancient times, milk was a staple of sustenance and a sign of wealth. The imagery suggests that the nations will provide for Israel, indicating a reversal of fortunes where Israel, once oppressed, will now be nourished by the wealth of other nations. This can be seen as a fulfillment of God's promises to bless Israel and make it a light to the nations (Isaiah 49:6).

and nurse at the breasts of royalty;
This imagery extends the idea of provision and care, suggesting that not only will nations provide for Israel, but even their leaders and nobility will contribute to its well-being. This reflects a divine reversal where those in power serve God's people, reminiscent of the prophecy in Isaiah 49:23 where kings and queens will be nursing fathers and mothers to Israel. It underscores the theme of God's sovereignty over all nations and His ability to use them for His purposes.

you will know that I, the LORD, am your Savior and your Redeemer,
This phrase emphasizes the personal relationship between God and His people. The titles "Savior" and "Redeemer" highlight God's role in delivering Israel from bondage and restoring them. The use of "know" implies an experiential understanding, not just intellectual acknowledgment. This is a recurring theme in Isaiah, where God repeatedly assures Israel of His commitment to their salvation (Isaiah 43:3, 49:26).

the Mighty One of Jacob.
This title for God connects Him to the patriarch Jacob, emphasizing continuity with the covenant promises made to the forefathers of Israel. It underscores God's strength and faithfulness in fulfilling His promises. The term "Mighty One" conveys God's power and ability to protect and provide for His people, a theme that resonates throughout the Old Testament (Genesis 49:24, Isaiah 1:24). This also points to the messianic hope, as Jesus Christ is seen as the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises to Jacob and his descendants.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is portrayed as the Savior and Redeemer.

2. Nations
Refers to the Gentile nations, indicating a future time when they will support and enrich Israel.

3. Kings
Symbolizes the leaders of these nations, who will also contribute to Israel's prosperity.

4. Jacob
Represents the nation of Israel, as Jacob was the patriarch whose name was changed to Israel.

5. Isaiah
The prophet who delivered this message, speaking to the future glory and restoration of Israel.
Teaching Points
God as Provider
God promises to provide for His people through unexpected sources, such as nations and kings. This teaches us to trust in God's provision, even when it seems unlikely.

Recognition of God's Sovereignty
The passage emphasizes knowing God as Savior and Redeemer. Believers are called to recognize and proclaim God's sovereignty in their lives.

Unity and Inclusion
The inclusion of nations and kings signifies a future unity under God's reign. Christians are encouraged to embrace unity and work towards reconciliation among diverse groups.

Hope in Restoration
The prophecy offers hope for restoration and future glory. Believers can find encouragement in God's promises of restoration in their personal lives and communities.

Witness to the Nations
As Israel is to be a light to the nations, Christians are called to be witnesses of God's love and salvation to the world.

What Does Isaiah 60:16 Mean
You will drink the milk of nations

• Picture a newborn thriving on rich nourishment; God promises His people will receive abundant provision from the Gentile world.

• This looks ahead to a future era when nations gladly supply resources and honor to Zion (compare Isaiah 49:23; Isaiah 61:6; Revelation 21:24–26).

• The scene also recalls God’s pledge that the wealth of Egypt, Cush, and Seba would come to Israel (Isaiah 45:14), showing tangible fulfilment of covenant blessings (Deuteronomy 33:19).

and nurse at the breasts of royalty;

• The imagery intensifies: not just common nations but “royalty” become channels of sustenance. Kings and queens, once oppressors, now nurture God’s people (Isaiah 49:23).

• This reversal underscores God’s sovereignty over political powers (Psalm 72:8–11; Isaiah 52:15).

• The promise affirms literal future honor for Zion when even the highest ranks acknowledge her favored status (Zechariah 8:22–23).

you will know that I, the LORD, am your Savior

• Material blessing points to a deeper goal: personal recognition of the LORD as Deliverer. External prosperity drives home internal assurance (Exodus 6:7; Ezekiel 34:30).

• “Savior” echoes earlier declarations that no other god can rescue (Isaiah 43:11; 45:21–22).

• The knowledge is experiential—Israel will see, taste, and conclude, “Only Yahweh could have done this” (Psalm 34:8).

and your Redeemer, the Mighty One of Jacob.

• “Redeemer” recalls the kinsman-redeemer who buys relatives out of bondage (Leviticus 25:25). God pledges to buy back His people from exile and sin (Isaiah 41:14; 44:22–24).

• “Mighty One of Jacob” stresses His unmatched strength to accomplish redemption (Genesis 49:24; Psalm 132:2, 5).

• The title ties the promise to the patriarchal covenant; the same God who vowed land and blessing to Abraham now guarantees their climactic realization (Micah 7:18–20).

summary

Isaiah 60:16 paints a vivid, literal picture of a coming age when Gentile nations and royal houses willingly pour their wealth and honor into Zion. This lavish nurture leads Israel to an unshakeable realization: the LORD alone is their Saving Redeemer, the covenant-keeping Mighty One of Jacob, fully able to reverse fortunes and fulfill every promise.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Thou shalt also suck the milk of the Gentiles . . .--The metaphor is bold, but the prophet had already presented it in a less startling form in Isaiah 49:23. What is meant in either case is that the new Jerusalem shall be supported by the offerings of the Gentiles.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Thou shalt also suck the milk of the Gentiles (comp. Deuteronomy 33:19). As a child at the mother's breast, thou shall obtain kindly nourishment through the means of the Gentiles, who acknowledge thee for their superior, and place all their means at thy disposal (supra, vers. 5-11). Among these, the most liberal, and the most prompt to render aid, will be their kings (see the comment on ver. 10). Thou shall know that I the Lord am thy Saviour. This clause is repeated from Isaiah 49:26. It is a phrase containing in it a mysterious depth of promise.
