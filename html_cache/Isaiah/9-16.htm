For those who guide this people mislead them
This phrase highlights the responsibility of leaders and the severe consequences of their failure. In the context of Isaiah, the leaders of Israel, including kings, priests, and prophets, were leading the people astray from God's commandments. Historically, this period was marked by political alliances with pagan nations and idolatry, which were contrary to God's instructions. The leaders' failure to adhere to God's law resulted in spiritual and moral decay. This theme is echoed in other scriptures, such as Jeremiah 23:1-2, where God condemns the shepherds who destroy and scatter the sheep of His pasture. The New Testament also warns against false teachers in passages like Matthew 7:15 and 2 Peter 2:1, emphasizing the importance of sound doctrine and faithful leadership.

mislead them
The act of misleading implies intentional or negligent guidance away from truth. In the biblical context, this often involved leading people into idolatry or false worship, as seen in the reigns of kings like Ahab, who promoted the worship of Baal (1 Kings 16:30-33). The Hebrew word used here can also suggest causing someone to err or wander, which is significant in a spiritual sense. Jesus Christ, in contrast, is depicted as the Good Shepherd in John 10:11, who leads His flock in truth and righteousness, highlighting the stark difference between false leaders and the true Messiah.

and those they mislead are swallowed up
This phrase indicates the dire consequences for those who follow false guidance. The imagery of being "swallowed up" suggests destruction and judgment, reminiscent of the earth swallowing Korah and his followers in Numbers 16:32 as a result of their rebellion against God's appointed leadership. In Isaiah's time, the people faced the threat of Assyrian invasion and exile as a direct consequence of their leaders' misguidance. This serves as a warning of the spiritual peril that comes from following false teachings, a theme reiterated in the New Testament, where Jesus warns of the blind leading the blind in Matthew 15:14, resulting in both falling into a pit. The ultimate fulfillment of righteous leadership is found in Christ, who offers salvation and truth to those who follow Him.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book bearing his name. He prophesied during a time of great turmoil and moral decline in Israel and Judah.

2. The People of Israel
The audience of Isaiah's prophecy, often depicted as straying from God's commandments and suffering the consequences of poor leadership.

3. Leaders/Guides
Refers to the leaders of Israel who were responsible for guiding the people but instead led them astray.

4. Misleading
The act of leading someone in the wrong direction, which in this context refers to spiritual and moral misguidance.

5. Swallowed Up
A metaphor for being consumed or destroyed, indicating the severe consequences of being misled.
Teaching Points
The Responsibility of Leadership
Leaders have a profound responsibility to guide their people in truth and righteousness. Misleading others can have devastating spiritual consequences.

The Importance of Discernment
Believers must cultivate discernment to recognize when they are being led astray. This involves knowing Scripture and seeking the Holy Spirit's guidance.

Consequences of Misguidance
Being misled can lead to spiritual destruction. It is crucial to remain vigilant and anchored in God's Word to avoid being "swallowed up."

Accountability in Leadership
Leaders will be held accountable for their influence. It is vital for those in positions of authority to seek God's wisdom and lead with integrity.

The Role of the Community
The community of believers should support one another in truth, holding each other accountable and encouraging adherence to God's Word.

What Does Isaiah 9:16 Mean
For those who guide this people

• Isaiah is pointing first at Israel’s civil and religious leadership—kings, princes, prophets, priests (cf. Jeremiah 23:1-2; Ezekiel 34:2-4).

• Scripture treats leadership as a sacred stewardship. When leaders fear the LORD, people flourish (Proverbs 29:2). When they rebel, a whole nation deteriorates (2 Chronicles 24:17-18).

• Jesus echoes Isaiah’s concern: “Can a blind man lead a blind man? Will they not both fall into a pit?” (Luke 6:39). God holds leaders doubly accountable because their influence is multiplied (James 3:1).

mislead them,

• The verb highlights deliberate deviation—leaders steering people away from God’s clear path (Micah 3:5-7).

• False confidence replaces true repentance. Isaiah has just recorded, “The people do not return to Him who strikes them” (Isaiah 9:13).

• Our Lord confronts the same issue: “They are blind guides. If a blind man leads a blind man, both will fall into a pit” (Matthew 15:14).

• Today, any teaching that contradicts Scripture—however winsome—still qualifies as misleading (Galatians 1:8-9).

and those they mislead

• The tragedy widens: people follow error because it often sounds soothing (2 Timothy 4:3-4).

• God never excuses personal responsibility, yet He recognizes how persuasive corrupt leadership can be (Hosea 4:6-9).

• Isaiah earlier warned, “Do not call conspiracy everything this people calls conspiracy… the LORD of Hosts is the One you are to regard as holy” (Isaiah 8:12-13). We are called to test every voice by the Word (Acts 17:11).

are swallowed up.

• The phrase signals judgment that is sudden and total. Compare Korah’s rebellion: “The earth opened its mouth and swallowed them” (Numbers 16:32).

• Isaiah’s context confirms it: “Therefore the Lord will take no pleasure in the young men… every mouth speaks folly. For all this, His anger is not turned away” (Isaiah 9:17).

• Being “swallowed up” previews the ultimate fate of the unrepentant—cut off from God’s mercy (Revelation 20:15).

• Yet the same chapter promises a righteous King whose reign ends all deception (Isaiah 9:6-7). Those who trust Him are kept from judgment (John 5:24).

summary

Isaiah 9:16 delivers a sober, literal warning: corrupt leaders drag entire communities into ruin, and the people who willingly follow them share in that ruin. God’s Word calls every leader to truth and every follower to discernment. Wherever Christ’s light is embraced, deception is shattered and both shepherds and sheep find safety under the everlasting government of the Prince of Peace.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - The leaders of this people cause them to err (comp. Isaiah 3:12). Both the peoples were led into idolatry by their rulers, but Israel especially. Jeroboam, the first king, introduced the calf-worship, and his successors from first to last persisted in his sin. Ahab added the still grosset idolatry of Baal. Those who held high position under the kings were equally bad examples to the people (see above, Isaiah 1:2:3). Are destroyed. First, morally corrupted and debased, then physically given over to destruction - slaughtered by Philistines, Syrians, and Assyrians.
