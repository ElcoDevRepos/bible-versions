Listen and hear my voice.
This phrase emphasizes the importance of attentiveness to God's message. In the context of Isaiah, the prophet often calls the people of Israel to listen to God's warnings and instructions. The repetition of "listen" and "hear" underscores the urgency and necessity of heeding divine guidance. Biblically, listening to God's voice is a recurring theme, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:4, "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one." This call to listen is not just about auditory reception but involves obedience and response. In the New Testament, Jesus often uses similar language, such as in Matthew 11:15, "He who has ears to hear, let him hear," indicating the need for spiritual discernment.

Pay attention and hear what I say.
This phrase further stresses the need for focus and understanding. The call to "pay attention" suggests an active engagement with the message being delivered. In the historical context of Isaiah, the people of Judah were often distracted by political alliances and idolatry, leading them away from God's commandments. The prophet's role was to redirect their attention back to God's covenant. This call to attentiveness is echoed in Proverbs 4:20, "My son, pay attention to what I say; turn your ear to my words." It highlights the importance of prioritizing God's word above all else. In a broader biblical context, this phrase can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate Word of God (John 1:1), calling believers to focus on His teachings and live accordingly.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Isaiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, Isaiah is the author of the book bearing his name. He served as a prophet in Judah during the reigns of several kings and is known for his messages of both judgment and hope.

2. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, where Isaiah primarily ministered. During Isaiah's time, Judah faced threats from surrounding nations and internal spiritual decline.

3. God's Call to Attention
This verse is part of a larger passage where God, through Isaiah, calls the people to listen and understand His teachings. It emphasizes the importance of heeding divine wisdom.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Listening to God
Just as Isaiah calls the people to listen, we are reminded of the importance of being attentive to God's voice in our lives. This requires intentionality and a willingness to quiet our own thoughts and distractions.

Active Engagement with Scripture
Listening involves more than just hearing; it requires active engagement. We should approach Scripture with a heart ready to understand and apply its truths.

The Role of Obedience
Hearing God's word is the first step, but it must lead to obedience. True listening results in action that aligns with God's will.

Spiritual Discernment
In a world filled with competing voices, discernment is crucial. We must learn to distinguish God's voice from others and prioritize His guidance.

Community and Accountability
Engaging with others in Bible study and discussion can enhance our understanding and application of God's word. Community provides support and accountability in our spiritual journey.

What Does Isaiah 28:23 Mean
Listen

“Listen …” (Isaiah 28:23a).

• This word calls for an immediate, deliberate decision to open our ears. Scripture often prefaces pivotal truth with a simple summons to listen (Deuteronomy 6:4; Psalm 81:13).

• The verse sits in the middle of Isaiah’s rebuke of Judah’s leaders; the Lord interrupts their complacency with a plain “listen” because His coming illustration about the farmer (vv. 24-29) will expose their folly.

• Genuine listening is a mark of wisdom (Proverbs 1:5). God does not waste words; every syllable is life-giving and authoritative.

and hear my voice

“… and hear My voice …” (v. 23b).

• Listening progresses to recognizing exactly whose voice is speaking—“My voice.” The personal pronoun makes it clear: this is no human opinion but the Lord Himself (John 10:27; Hebrews 3:7-8).

• Hearing His voice involves relationship, familiarity, and trust. Sheep do not debate the shepherd’s credentials; they follow.

• Isaiah’s audience had been tuning in to scoffers (v. 14). God reorients them: only His voice interprets reality accurately.

Pay attention

“Pay attention …” (v. 23c).

• The original readers were distracted by political alliances and religious ritual. God demands focused, undivided attention (Proverbs 4:20-22; Luke 9:44).

• Attention implies humility—setting aside our own chatter so His words dominate our mental landscape.

• Practically:

– Silence competing noises.

– Slow down enough to ponder.

– Expect that God will speak plainly and helpfully.

and hear what I say

“… and hear what I say.” (v. 23d).

• The Lord moves from simply hearing His voice to embracing “what I say”—the content that follows. The next verses compare His calibrated dealings with the farmer’s methods, proving His judgments are measured, purposeful, and wise (vv. 24-29).

• Real hearing results in obedient response (James 1:22; Luke 11:28). The command is not merely to admire His words but to align life with them.

• In Isaiah’s day, that meant repenting of scoffing and resting in God’s plan; for us, it still means surrendering to every clear directive of Scripture (Isaiah 30:21).

summary

Isaiah 28:23 is a four-fold summons: open your ears, recognize God’s personal voice, fix your full attention, and obey the specific truth He is about to unfold. The verse presses us from passive listening into active, responsive faith, reminding us that every word from the Lord is authoritative, precise, and intended for our good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Give ye ear . . .--The words remind us of the style of the "wisdom" books of the Old Testament (Proverbs 2:1; Proverbs 4:1; Proverbs 5:1; Psalm 34:11) in which Isaiah had been trained. Isaiah is about to set before those who have ears to hear a parable which he does not interpret, and which will, therefore, task all their energies. The idea that lies at the root of the parable is like that of Matthew 16:2-4, that men fail to apply in discerning the signs of the times the wisdom which they practise or recognise in the common phenomena of nature and the tillage of the soil. As that tillage presents widely varied processes, differing with each kind of grain, so the sowing and the threshing of God's spiritual husbandry presents a like diversity of operations. What that diversity indicates in detail the prophet proceeds to show with what may again be called a Dante-like minuteness.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 23-29. - A PARABLE TO COMFORT BELIEVERS. Isaiah is always careful to intermingle promises with his threats, comfort with his denunciations. Like his great Master, of whom he prophesied, he was fain not to "break the bruised reed" or "quench the smoking flax." When he had searched men's wounds with the probe, he was careful to pour in oil and wine. So now, having denounced the sinners of Judah through three long paragraphs (vers. 7-22), he has a word of consolation and encouragement for the better disposed, whose hearts he hopes to have touched and stirred by his warning. This consolation he puts in a parabolic form, leaving it to their spiritual insight to discover the meaning. Verse 23. - Give ye ear (comp. Psalm 49:1; Psalm 78:1). A preface of this kind, enjoining special attention and thought, was appropriate to occasions when instruction was couched in a parabolic form.
