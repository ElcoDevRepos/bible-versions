Then they will look to the earth
This phrase indicates a turning away from God to seek solutions or hope in worldly or earthly things. In the context of Isaiah, the people of Judah were often tempted to rely on political alliances or material resources rather than trusting in God. This reflects a broader biblical theme where reliance on human strength or wisdom is contrasted with faith in God (Jeremiah 17:5-6).

and see only distress and darkness
The imagery of distress and darkness symbolizes the consequences of turning away from God. Biblically, darkness often represents judgment, sin, and separation from God (Exodus 10:21-23; John 3:19). In Isaiah's time, this could refer to the impending Assyrian invasion, which would bring great suffering and turmoil to the land.

and the gloom of anguish
This phrase emphasizes the depth of despair and hopelessness that comes from rejecting God's guidance. The "gloom of anguish" suggests a profound emotional and spiritual suffering. This can be seen as a fulfillment of the warnings given by the prophets about the consequences of disobedience (Deuteronomy 28:28-29).

And they will be driven into utter darkness
The final outcome of their choices is described as being "driven into utter darkness," indicating a complete and total separation from God's presence and blessings. This can be seen as a type of spiritual death or exile, similar to the exile experienced by Israel and Judah. In the New Testament, darkness is often associated with the absence of Christ, who is the Light of the World (John 8:12). This phrase foreshadows the ultimate judgment and separation from God for those who reject Him.

The southern kingdom of Israel, facing threats from surrounding nations and internal spiritual decay.

The dominant empire during Isaiah's time, posing a significant threat to Judah and often used as an instrument of God's judgment.

4. Darkness and Gloom
Symbolic of the spiritual and moral state of the people who have turned away from God.

5. Anguish
Represents the emotional and physical suffering resulting from separation from God and His guidance.
Teaching Points
Recognizing Spiritual Darkness
Understand that spiritual darkness is a result of turning away from God. It manifests in moral decay, confusion, and despair.

The Consequences of Forsaking God
Acknowledge that distancing oneself from God leads to distress and anguish, as seen in the historical context of Judah.

Hope in the Midst of Darkness
Remember that even in the darkest times, God promises light and redemption through Jesus Christ.

The Call to Repentance
Embrace the opportunity for repentance and turning back to God to escape the gloom of spiritual darkness.

Living as Children of Light
As believers, we are called to reflect Christ's light in a dark world, offering hope and guidance to others.

What Does Isaiah 8:22 Mean
Then they will look to the earth

• Isaiah pictures Judah’s people frantically scanning their surroundings for relief.

• Earlier in the chapter (Isaiah 8:19), they had been dabbling in occult counsel; now they search the physical world for answers and safety.

• Cross references: 2 Chron 16:12 shows King Asa looking to physicians, not the Lord; Psalm 121:1–2 reminds believers to lift eyes higher, to the Maker of heaven and earth.

and see only distress and darkness

• Their outlook matches their spiritual state. Refusing God’s light leaves nothing but “distress” (Deuteronomy 28:65–67) and “darkness” (Proverbs 4:19).

• The darkness is literal (impending Assyrian invasion, Isaiah 8:7–8) and moral (John 3:19: “people loved darkness rather than light”).

• Jesus warns the same for the end times—cosmic darkness mirrors human rebellion (Matthew 24:29).

and the gloom of anguish

• “Gloom” deepens the picture: an oppressive, suffocating sorrow (Joel 2:2).

• Anguish follows idolatry and unbelief (Jeremiah 13:16–17). The people experience exactly what they chose: separation from God’s joy.

• Yet, Isaiah 9:1 promises the dawn breaking upon this very gloom, foreshadowing Christ in Galilee.

And they will be driven into utter darkness

• God’s judgment is portrayed as expulsion—“driven.” Like Adam and Eve cast from Eden (Genesis 3:24) and Judah later exiled to Babylon (2 Kings 24:15–16).

• “Utter darkness” suggests finality, paralleling Jesus’ phrase “outer darkness” where there is weeping (Matthew 25:30).

• The severity underscores God’s holiness: persistent rejection ends in total blackout, both nationally and eternally (2 Thessalonians 1:8–9).

summary

Isaiah 8:22 shows the inevitable outcome when God’s people seek guidance and security anywhere but in Him. Looking down to the earth, they find only distress; rejecting His light, they sink into crushing gloom; resisting correction, they are finally driven into complete darkness. Yet the very next verse (Isaiah 9:1) introduces hope in the coming Messiah, proving that God’s ultimate purpose is not doom but redemption for all who turn back to His light.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - They shall look unto the earth. For necessary nutriment, or simply as the place to which downcast and despairing eyes are turned naturally. They shall be driven to darkness. So Kay, who thinks the Captivity is meant; but it seems better to render the whole passage, with Mr. Cheyne, "They shall look to earth, and behold, distress and darkness, gloom of affliction, and thick darkness driven (upon them)." The darkness is spoken of as if it were a thing palpable, like rain or snow (comp. Exodus 10:21).
