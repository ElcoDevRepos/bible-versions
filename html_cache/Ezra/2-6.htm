the descendants of Pahath-moab
Pahath-moab is a name that means "governor of Moab," indicating a possible connection to the region of Moab, east of the Dead Sea. This suggests that the family may have had historical ties or administrative roles in that area. The mention of Pahath-moab in the context of the return from Babylonian exile highlights the diverse backgrounds of the returning exiles. This group is also listed in Nehemiah 7:11, showing consistency in the records of those who returned to Jerusalem.

(through the line of Jeshua and Joab)
Jeshua and Joab are likely leaders or notable figures within the family of Pahath-moab. Jeshua is a common name in the post-exilic period, often associated with leadership, as seen in the high priest Jeshua who returned with Zerubbabel. Joab, another common name, may indicate a warrior or leader, reminiscent of King David's military commander. The mention of these names suggests a structured lineage and leadership within the group, emphasizing the organized nature of the return.

The specific number of 2,812 descendants indicates a significant family group, reflecting the importance of Pahath-moab's descendants in the restoration community. This number underscores the detailed record-keeping of the returning exiles, which was crucial for re-establishing tribal and family identities in the post-exilic society. The precision of this count also highlights the fulfillment of God's promise to restore His people to their land, as prophesied in Jeremiah 29:10-14.

What Does Ezra 2:6 Mean
The descendants of Pahath-moab

Ezra 2:6 begins, “the descendants of Pahath-moab…”. This clan name reminds us that returnees were counted by identifiable families, proving God preserved their lineage even in exile. The same family surfaces later when more men return with Ezra (Ezra 8:4) and when covenant signers pledge renewed obedience in Jerusalem (Nehemiah 10:14). Their repeated appearance underlines faithfulness: God kept His promise to “bring them back to this land” (Jeremiah 29:10) and they, in turn, kept their identity intact.

Through the line of Jeshua and Joab

The parenthetical note narrows the branch—“through the line of Jeshua and Joab.” Scripture often traces lines to show continuity of leadership and worship: Jeshua the high priest leads rebuilding worship in Ezra 3:2, and Joab’s name echoes a warrior heritage (2 Samuel 23:18). By linking this clan to recognizable forebears, the text highlights the way God knits past and present: the promise to the fathers (Isaiah 44:3) now touches their descendants who stand ready to rebuild altar, temple, and city walls.

2,812 Returned

The verse ends with the precise tally “2,812.” Nehemiah’s parallel census records “2,818” (Nehemiah 7:11); the slight variance likely reflects two counts taken at different moments of the same restoration era—both accurate for their context, showing God’s care for every individual (compare Luke 12:7). Such an exact figure testifies that this is not myth but history. Each of those 2,812 lives fulfilled Isaiah 11:11, where the Lord promised a second gathering of His people. Their presence also contributes to the overall total of about 50,000 exiles returning (Ezra 2:64-65), enough hands to begin temple reconstruction (Ezra 3:8) and to repopulate Judah as foretold in Ezekiel 36:10.

summary

Ezra 2:6 records one family in God’s grand roll call of the restored remnant. By naming the descendants of Pahath-moab, specifying their ancestral line through Jeshua and Joab, and counting exactly 2,812 people, Scripture affirms that the Lord keeps covenant, preserves identity, and values every individual He brings home.
