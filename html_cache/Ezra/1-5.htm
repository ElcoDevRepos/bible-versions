Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
So the family heads of Judah and Benjamin
The tribes of Judah and Benjamin were the primary tribes that made up the Southern Kingdom of Judah. After the Babylonian exile, these tribes were the most prominent in returning to Jerusalem. The mention of family heads indicates the importance of lineage and leadership in the Jewish community. This return fulfills the prophecy of Jeremiah 29:10, where God promises to bring His people back after 70 years of exile.

along with the priests and Levites
The priests and Levites were essential for the religious life of Israel, responsible for temple worship and sacrifices. Their inclusion highlights the restoration of proper worship practices in Jerusalem. The Levites, descendants of Levi, were set apart for religious duties, emphasizing the need for spiritual leadership in rebuilding the temple.

everyone whose spirit God had stirred
This phrase indicates divine intervention and inspiration. God actively moved in the hearts of certain individuals to fulfill His purposes, reminiscent of how He stirred the heart of Cyrus in Ezra 1:1. This stirring is a reminder of God's sovereignty and His ability to guide history according to His will, as seen in Philippians 2:13.

prepared to go up and rebuild the house of the LORD
The preparation to rebuild the temple signifies a renewal of covenant faithfulness and worship. The temple was central to Jewish identity and religious practice, symbolizing God's presence among His people. This rebuilding effort is a precursor to the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises through Jesus Christ, who referred to His body as the temple in John 2:19-21.

in Jerusalem
Jerusalem holds significant historical and theological importance as the city of David and the location of the original temple built by Solomon. It is the focal point of Jewish worship and the prophetic center of God's redemptive plan. The return to Jerusalem fulfills prophecies such as Isaiah 44:28, where God declares the rebuilding of the city and temple.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Family Heads of Judah and Benjamin
These were the leaders of the two tribes that made up the southern kingdom of Judah. They played a crucial role in the return from Babylonian exile and the rebuilding of the temple.

2. Priests and Levites
These were the religious leaders responsible for temple worship and rituals. Their involvement was essential for the restoration of proper worship in Jerusalem.

3. God's Stirring
This refers to the divine influence on the hearts of the people, prompting them to take action. The Hebrew word used here means to awaken or incite.

4. Rebuilding the House of the LORD
This event marks the beginning of the restoration of the temple in Jerusalem, a significant moment in Jewish history as it symbolizes the renewal of worship and covenant relationship with God.

5. Jerusalem
The central place of worship for the Israelites and the location of the temple. Its restoration was vital for the spiritual and national identity of the Jewish people.
Teaching Points
Divine Initiative
Recognize that God is the initiator of His plans. Just as He stirred the hearts of the Israelites, He can stir our hearts today to fulfill His purposes.

Role of Leadership
The involvement of family heads, priests, and Levites highlights the importance of leadership in spiritual renewal. Leaders should be attentive to God's prompting and guide others in obedience.

Community Response
The collective response of the people shows the power of community in achieving God's work. We should seek to be part of a community that is responsive to God's call.

Restoration and Renewal
The rebuilding of the temple signifies spiritual renewal. In our lives, we should be open to God's work of restoration, whether it be in our personal lives, families, or communities.

Faith in Action
The preparation to go up and rebuild required faith and action. We are called to step out in faith when God stirs our hearts, trusting in His provision and guidance.

What Does Ezra 1:5 Mean
So the family heads

- In God’s design, leadership begins in the home. The “family heads” (or “chief fathers”) willingly stepped forward.

- They model Joshua 24:15, where a leader declares, “As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD.”

- Their initiative reminds us that revival and obedience often start with parents and grandparents who say yes first (Deuteronomy 6:5-7).

of Judah and Benjamin

- These are the two tribes that made up the remnant of the southern kingdom after the exile (2 Kings 25:21).

- God keeps covenant promises to specific people in specific places; Jeremiah 33:24-26 affirms He will never cast off Judah’s seed.

- Even in judgment He preserves a remnant (Isaiah 10:20-22), proving His faithfulness generation after generation.

along with the priests and Levites

- Spiritual leadership joins family leadership. The priests and Levites were entrusted with worship (Numbers 3:5-10).

- When both household heads and church leaders move in unity, God’s people advance (2 Chronicles 30:12).

- Ezra later underscores the need for qualified ministers to oversee sacred duties (Ezra 8:15-20).

everyone whose spirit God had stirred

- True restoration is Spirit-initiated, not merely human enthusiasm (Philippians 2:13).

- This stirring fulfills Proverbs 21:1, where God turns hearts “like a watercourse.”

- Haggai 1:14 uses the same idea: “So the LORD stirred up the spirit of Zerubbabel… and they came and began to work on the house of the LORD.” The identical phrase shows a divine pattern.

prepared to go up

- Obedience moves from conviction to action. Like Abraham, they rose and went (Genesis 12:4).

- Preparation involved gathering materials (Ezra 2:68-69) and organizing travel (Ezra 8:21-23).

- Psalm 84:5 pictures such pilgrims: “Blessed are those whose strength is in You, whose hearts are set on pilgrimage.”

and rebuild the house of the LORD in Jerusalem

- The focal point is worship. God’s house must be central (Psalm 132:3-5).

- Rebuilding fulfills Isaiah 44:28, where the LORD says of Cyrus, “He will say of Jerusalem, ‘Let it be rebuilt,’ and of the temple, ‘Let its foundations be laid.’”

- Their labor foreshadows the New Testament call to build up the church as a living temple (1 Peter 2:4-5), yet here the literal stones matter because God’s promise is concrete.

summary

Ezra 1:5 shows a coordinated, Spirit-led response to God’s faithfulness. Family heads, tribal remnants, spiritual leaders, and every stirred heart unite. They prepare, rise, and act to restore worship at Jerusalem. The verse teaches that when God moves hearts, practical obedience follows, families and ministers stand together, and the priority remains the glory of God in His house.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5-11) Immediate result of the decree.

(5) With all them whose spirit God had raised.--Namely, all is the more exact rendering. The same influence that prompted the decree of Cyrus was necessary to overcome the inertness of the captives: many preferred to remain in Babylon.--The people were enumerated as tribes, families, and fathers' houses; the second and third orders of classification are not here distinguished from each other.

(6) Precious things.--The Hebrew equivalent is a rare word, which, when it occurs, is connected only with the precious metals.

Willingly offered.--Although it is not so said, the people of Cyrus were "stirred up" like himself: how much he gave, and how much he valued the worship of the Temple, we shall hereafter see.

(7) His gods.--Rather, his god. Merodach, to wit, whom he called "his lord" (Daniel 1:2). From 2Kings 25:13-17 it appears that much had been taken away which Cyrus had not been able to find.

(8) Mithredath.--"Dedicated to Mithra," the sun god of the Persians, whose worship among the Vedic Indians had thus early reached Persia.

Sheshbazzar.--The Chaldee name of Zerubbabel, whose title, however, as Prince of Judah is given him from the Hebrew side. He was the legal heir of Jehoiachin, being the son of Pedaiah (1Chronicles 3:19), who possibly married the widow of Salathiel or Shealtiel. And the title "Prince of Judah," or "Prince of the captivity," was specially given to him in common with a very few others. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Then rose up the chief of the fathers. The "chief of the fathers" are the hereditary heads of the families recognized as distinct and separate (see Ezra 2:3-19).
