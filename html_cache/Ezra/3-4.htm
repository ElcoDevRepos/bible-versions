They also celebrated the Feast of Tabernacles
The Feast of Tabernacles, also known as Sukkot, is one of the three major Jewish festivals, commemorating the Israelites' 40 years of wandering in the desert and living in temporary shelters. This celebration is significant as it reflects obedience to God's commands found in Leviticus 23:33-43. It is a time of joy and thanksgiving for the harvest, symbolizing God's provision and protection. The feast also points forward to the messianic age, as seen in Zechariah 14:16-19, where all nations will celebrate it in the future kingdom.

in accordance with what is written
This phrase underscores the importance of adhering to the Scriptures, specifically the Torah, which contains the laws and instructions given by God to Moses. The Israelites' commitment to follow the written law highlights their desire to restore proper worship and align themselves with God's covenant. This obedience is a recurring theme in the post-exilic period, as seen in Nehemiah 8:14-18, where the people rediscover and observe the Feast of Tabernacles after reading the Law.

and they offered burnt offerings daily
Burnt offerings were a central aspect of Old Testament worship, symbolizing atonement and dedication to God. These offerings, described in Leviticus 1, were completely consumed by fire, representing the worshiper's total surrender to God. The daily offerings reflect a continuous act of worship and devotion, emphasizing the need for regular communion with God. This practice is a precursor to the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who offered Himself once for all (Hebrews 10:10).

based on the number prescribed for each day
The specific number of offerings for each day is detailed in Numbers 29:12-38, which outlines the sacrifices required during the Feast of Tabernacles. This meticulous adherence to the prescribed offerings demonstrates the Israelites' commitment to follow God's instructions precisely. It also highlights the structured and ordered nature of worship in the Old Testament, which foreshadows the order and peace found in Christ's kingdom. The precision in these offerings points to the fulfillment of the law in Jesus, who perfectly met all its requirements (Matthew 5:17).

Teaching Points
Faithfulness to God's Commands
The Israelites' observance of the Feast of Tabernacles as prescribed demonstrates their commitment to following God's laws. We are called to be faithful in our obedience to God's Word.

Community Worship and Unity
The collective celebration of the festival highlights the importance of community in worship. As believers, we should prioritize gathering together to worship and encourage one another.

Remembrance and Gratitude
The Feast of Tabernacles serves as a reminder of God's provision and faithfulness during the Israelites' wilderness journey. We should regularly reflect on and give thanks for God's faithfulness in our own lives.

Daily Devotion
The daily offerings signify a continual dedication to God. We are encouraged to maintain a daily practice of devotion and worship, offering our lives as living sacrifices.

Restoration and Renewal
The rebuilding of the temple and the re-establishment of religious practices symbolize spiritual renewal. We should seek God's restoration in our lives, allowing Him to rebuild and renew our hearts.

What Does Ezra 3:4 Mean
They also celebrated the Feast of Tabernacles

The remnant who returned from exile wasted no time restoring the rhythms God had set for His people. The Feast of Tabernacles (or Booths) was commanded in Leviticus 23:33-43; it reminded Israel that God sheltered them in the wilderness. Re-establishing it showed they trusted the same God to shelter them now. Nehemiah 8:14-17 later records another joyful observance, proving how central this feast was to post-exilic faith. By celebrating, they publicly declared, “We are still God’s covenant people.” John 7:2, 37-39 shows Jesus standing in the temple during this feast, pointing to its ultimate fulfillment in Him.

in accordance with what is written

Obedience was measured by Scripture, not by convenience or innovation. The returned community aligned every detail with the written law (Deuteronomy 12:32). Doing “what is written” echoes Ezra’s own heart in Ezra 7:10: “Ezra had set his heart to study the Law of the Lord, to practice it, and to teach.” This phrase highlights that revival is inseparable from unwavering submission to God’s Word (Joshua 1:8; Psalm 19:7-11).

and they offered burnt offerings daily

The burnt offering (Leviticus 1) symbolized total dedication—everything on the altar, consumed for God alone. Offering them “daily” (Numbers 28:3) kept worship from being a one-time event. It signaled ongoing surrender and gratitude. In the exile they had lived without a temple or altar; now continual sacrifice restored the heartbeat of national worship (Psalm 51:19). Hebrews 10:1-14 later explains that these offerings pointed ahead to Christ, who offered Himself once for all.

based on the number prescribed for each day

God had even numbered the sacrifices for each day of this feast—see Numbers 29:12-38. By following that pattern exactly, the people affirmed that every detail of God’s instruction mattered. Their precise obedience mirrored the care shown when Moses built the tabernacle “just as the Lord commanded” (Exodus 40:16). First Corinthians 14:40 reminds believers today that orderly worship still pleases God.

summary

Ezra 3:4 reveals a community restored to covenant life: they gather for the Feast of Tabernacles, obey Scripture down to the letter, maintain daily whole-hearted worship through burnt offerings, and honor God’s exact prescriptions. Their faithfulness after exile models how God’s people today can rebuild—by returning to His Word, offering continual devotion, and trusting His faithful shelter.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - As it is written. According to the mode of celebration prescribed in the law; i.e. for seven consecutive days, from the fifteenth to the twenty-second of Tisri, with burnt offerings every day, and a holy convocation on the first day and the last, and a "dwelling in tents" during the whole period (see Leviticus 23:31-42). The daily burnt offerings by number, according to the custom. The offerings for each day of the festival are carefully laid down in Numbers 29:13-38. We must understand that all the particulars there enjoined were carefully observed. PERMANENT ESTABLISHMENT OF THE DAILY SACRIFICE, THE SET FEASTS, AND THE OFFERING OF FREE-WILL OFFERINGS (vers. 5, 6). Having set up the altar, and celebrated the particular festival which the revolving year happened to have brought round, and which it would have been wrong to neglect, the exiles re-established permanently three things: -

1. The daily sacrifice;

2. The celebration of the new moons and other regular feasts; and

3. The practice of allowing the people to bring offerings whenever they pleased, to be offered on the great altar by the priest or priests in attendance. . . .
