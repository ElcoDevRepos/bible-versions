Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Jehoshaphat consulted with the people
Jehoshaphat, the king of Judah, demonstrates wise leadership by seeking counsel from his people. This reflects the biblical principle of seeking wisdom and guidance from others, as seen in Proverbs 15:22, "Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed." It also shows a participatory leadership style, which contrasts with the autocratic rule often seen in ancient Near Eastern monarchies.

and appointed those who would sing to the LORD
Jehoshaphat's decision to appoint singers highlights the importance of worship in spiritual warfare. The Levites, who were traditionally responsible for temple worship, likely comprised these singers. This act underscores the power of praise and worship as a means of invoking God's presence and favor, as seen in Psalm 22:3, which states that God inhabits the praises of His people.

and praise the splendor of His holiness.
The focus on God's holiness emphasizes His otherness and purity. Holiness is a central attribute of God, as seen throughout Scripture, including Isaiah 6:3, where the seraphim proclaim, "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty." Praising God's holiness acknowledges His supreme authority and sets the tone for divine intervention.

As they went out before the army,
The singers leading the army is a strategic and symbolic act, placing worship at the forefront of the battle. This unusual military tactic demonstrates faith in God's power rather than human strength. It echoes the story of Jericho in Joshua 6, where the Israelites marched around the city with priests and trumpets leading the way.

they were singing: “Give thanks to the LORD,
The act of giving thanks in the face of impending battle is an expression of faith and trust in God's deliverance. Thanksgiving is a recurring theme in the Psalms, such as Psalm 136, which repeatedly calls for giving thanks to the LORD for His enduring love.

for His loving devotion endures forever.”
This phrase highlights God's covenant faithfulness, a central theme in the Old Testament. The Hebrew word "hesed," often translated as "loving devotion" or "steadfast love," signifies God's unwavering commitment to His people. This assurance of God's eternal love is echoed in Lamentations 3:22-23, which speaks of His mercies being new every morning.

What Does 2 Chronicles 20:21 Mean
Then Jehoshaphat consulted with the people

• Jehoshaphat does not act as a lone monarch; he gathers input from those he leads (2 Chronicles 19:4).

• Scripture applauds the safety of shared counsel—“Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed” (Proverbs 15:22).

• Consulting the people demonstrates humility before God and reliance upon godly wisdom rather than mere royal authority.

and appointed those who would sing to the LORD

• The king intentionally sets worshipers at the forefront, echoing David’s earlier organization of Levitical choirs (1 Chronicles 15:16).

• These singers are not an afterthought; they are officially commissioned, showing that praise is strategic in spiritual battle (2 Chronicles 29:27–30).

• In every age, God’s people are urged to “speak to one another with psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs” (Ephesians 5:19), placing music at the heart of faith and warfare alike.

and praise the splendor of His holiness

• Worship centers on God’s character, not human need. “Ascribe to the LORD the glory due His name; worship the LORD in the splendor of His holiness” (Psalm 29:2).

• Focusing on divine holiness turns eyes away from intimidating armies to the incomparable purity and majesty of God (Isaiah 6:3).

• Praise becomes a declaration: the battle belongs to a holy, set-apart God who cannot be defiled or defeated.

As they went out before the army

• The choir marches ahead of the soldiers, a deliberate reversal of human logic, much like priests carrying trumpets around Jericho (Joshua 6:3–5).

• This order says, in effect, “Our true weapon is worship.” Victory comes by spiritual means before physical engagement (2 Chronicles 20:17).

• Placing praise first models the principle that God’s presence leads and protects His people (Psalm 22:3).

they were singing: “Give thanks to the LORD, for His loving devotion endures forever.”

• Their song repeats the timeless refrain of Israel (Psalm 136:1; Ezra 3:11; Jeremiah 33:11).

• “Loving devotion” (ḥesed) underscores God’s covenant loyalty; it does not waver with circumstances.

• Gratitude voiced in advance of victory expresses faith: God’s steadfast love guarantees the outcome before a sword is drawn (Psalm 118:1–4).

• The melody of thankfulness drowns out fear, making room for God to act (2 Chronicles 20:22).

summary

Jehoshaphat’s sequence—consultation, commissioning of singers, worship rooted in God’s holiness, praise marching ahead, and a chorus exalting His enduring love—reveals that spiritual triumph flows from trusting, thankful hearts more than from military might. When God’s people elevate worship above strategy, He secures the victory for them.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) And when he had consulted with.--Or, advised, given counsel to, warned. (Comp. 2Kings 6:8.) Vulg., "deditque consilium populo, et statuit cantores domini."

And that should praise the beauty of holiness.--Rather, and men praising, in holy apparel. i.e., Levitical vestments (1Chronicles 16:29; Psalm 29:2).

And to say.--And saying.

Praise the Lord.--Give thanks unto Jehovah The refrain of the singers. (See on 1Chronicles 16:34; 1Chronicles 16:41; 2Chronicles 5:13; 2Chronicles 7:3.) The band of Levitical minstrels were to march before the army (hal-q, the armed host; Joshua 6:7). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - And when he had consulted with the people; i.e. possibly simply "conferred with" those who were over the singers, as to who should be the most prominent in leading the service of praise, or as to what should be the words sung and other like matters of detail; or more probably, considering the exact form of language used, the reference is to what we are told Jehoshaphat had just done, to wit, counselled well the people and given good advice to them. Praise the beauty of holiness. The rendering should no doubt be in the beauty of holiness, i.e. in garments of beauty (1 Chronicles 16:29; Psalm 29:2; Revised Version margin," in holy army "). Praise the Lord; Revised Version, give thanks to the Lord (2 Chronicles 5:13; 2 Chronicles 7:3, 6; Psalm 106; Psalm 136.).
