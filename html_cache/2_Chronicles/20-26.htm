Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
On the fourth day
The mention of the "fourth day" signifies a specific time frame following a significant event. In the context of 2 Chronicles 20, this refers to the aftermath of a miraculous victory granted by God to Jehoshaphat and the people of Judah over a coalition of enemies. The number four in biblical numerology often symbolizes completeness or universality, suggesting the fulfillment of God's promise and the completeness of their victory.

they assembled in the Valley of Beracah
The Valley of Beracah, meaning "Valley of Blessing," is a geographical location where the people of Judah gathered to praise God. This valley is believed to be located near Tekoa, south of Jerusalem. The assembly in this valley highlights the communal aspect of worship and thanksgiving, emphasizing the importance of coming together to acknowledge God's intervention and blessings. The gathering also reflects the cultural practice of marking significant events with communal worship and celebration.

where they blessed the LORD
Blessing the LORD in this context involves offering praise and thanksgiving for His deliverance and provision. This act of worship is a response to God's faithfulness and power, acknowledging His sovereignty and goodness. The concept of blessing God is rooted in the Hebrew tradition of recognizing God's hand in the affairs of His people, as seen throughout the Psalms and other Old Testament scriptures. It underscores the reciprocal relationship between God and His people, where divine intervention is met with human gratitude.

Therefore that place is called the Valley of Beracah to this day
The naming of the valley serves as a lasting memorial to God's deliverance and the people's gratitude. Such naming practices are common in the Old Testament, where significant events are commemorated by naming places, as seen with Bethel (Genesis 28:19) and Gilgal (Joshua 4:20). This enduring name serves as a testimony to future generations of God's faithfulness and the importance of remembering His acts. It also reflects the cultural practice of using place names to convey historical and spiritual truths, ensuring that the story of God's deliverance is preserved and retold.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jehoshaphat
The king of Judah who led the people in seeking the Lord's guidance and deliverance from their enemies.

2. The Valley of Beracah
The location where the people of Judah gathered to bless the Lord after their victory. The name "Beracah" means "blessing" in Hebrew.

3. The Assembly
The gathering of the people of Judah to give thanks and praise to God for their deliverance.

4. The Enemies
The Moabites, Ammonites, and some of the Meunites who came to make war against Jehoshaphat and Judah.

5. The LORD
The central figure in the account, whose intervention and deliverance are celebrated by the people.
Teaching Points
The Power of Praise
Praise is a powerful response to God's deliverance. Just as the people of Judah gathered to bless the Lord, we too should make praise a central part of our lives, especially after experiencing God's intervention.

Remembering God's Faithfulness
Naming the place "Valley of Beracah" served as a lasting reminder of God's faithfulness. We should also create reminders of God's work in our lives to encourage us and others.

Community in Worship
The assembly in the Valley of Beracah highlights the importance of communal worship and thanksgiving. Gathering with fellow believers to praise God strengthens our faith and unity.

Trust in God's Deliverance
Jehoshaphat's account teaches us to trust in God's deliverance, even when faced with overwhelming odds. Our response should be one of faith and reliance on God's power.

Gratitude as a Lifestyle
The act of blessing the Lord in the Valley of Beracah underscores the importance of gratitude. Cultivating a lifestyle of thankfulness can transform our perspective and deepen our relationship with God.

What Does 2 Chronicles 20:26 Mean
On the fourth day

The battle was over, the spoils gathered for three full days (2 Chronicles 20:24-25), and now an entire nation took time for something more important than counting treasure—worship.

• God’s deliverance clears space for praise; victory is never an excuse to hurry on (Exodus 15:1-2, Psalm 50:14).

• Four days after the crisis began, Judah’s fears turned to joy, fulfilling the promise “Weeping may stay the night, but joy comes in the morning” (Psalm 30:5).

• The specific mention of “the fourth day” reminds us God’s timing is perfect; nothing is random in His salvation plan (Galatians 4:4).

they assembled in the Valley of Beracah

“Beracah” means “blessing,” and the valley sat on the route from Jerusalem toward Tekoa.

• The whole nation gathered—worship is communal, not merely private (Psalm 133:1, Hebrews 10:25).

• God often turns places of impending disaster into arenas of blessing (Genesis 50:20).

• Meeting outside the city walls showed confidence: the God who had defeated the enemy could protect them in the open field (Psalm 27:1-3).

where they blessed the LORD

Judah’s first impulse was not to boast but to bless.

• Blessing the LORD means speaking well of Him—publicly acknowledging His character and works (Psalm 103:1-2, 1 Chronicles 29:20).

• Their praise focused on who God is and what He had just done: “The LORD set ambushes” (2 Chronicles 20:22-23).

• Gratitude kept the spoil from becoming an idol; they honored the Giver before enjoying the gifts (Deuteronomy 8:10).

• Like the healed Samaritan who “returned, glorifying God with a loud voice” (Luke 17:15-16), Judah modeled immediate, wholehearted thanksgiving.

Therefore that place is called the Valley of Beracah to this day

The name became a living memorial.

• Scripture frequently links place-names with God’s acts: “The LORD Will Provide” at Moriah (Genesis 22:14), “The LORD Is My Banner” at Rephidim (Exodus 17:15), “Ebenezer” after victory over the Philistines (1 Samuel 7:12).

• Such markers teach future generations; every time a traveler heard “Valley of Beracah,” the story of God’s rescue resurfaced (Joshua 4:6-7).

• Personal application: create tangible reminders of answered prayer—journals, songs, family traditions—so God’s faithfulness is not forgotten (Psalm 145:4-7).

summary

2 Chronicles 20:26 records a nation’s deliberate pause to celebrate God’s deliverance. On the fourth day, Judah assembled in a valley renamed “Blessing,” lifted united praise to the LORD, and left a permanent landmark so no one would overlook what He had done. The verse calls every believer to carve out time after victory, gather with others, bless the Lord out loud, and memorialize His faithfulness for the generations that follow.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(26) Valley of Berachah--i.e., blessing. The place is still called Wady Beraikut, a wide, open valley west of Tekoa, near the road from Hebron to Jerusalem. St. Jerome speaks of a Caphar Barucha, "village of blessing," in the same neighbourhood.

For there . . . unto this day.--A notice after the manner of the ancient historians (Genesis 11:9; Genesis 28:19; Exodus 17:7; 2Samuel 5:20).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 26. - Berachah. This is just the Hebrew fern. subst, from a verb. It is used in 1 Chronicles 12:3 as the name of a man. The present name of the valley survives in the Waddy Bereikat on the Hebron road, beyond, therefore, the date unto this day of the writer.
