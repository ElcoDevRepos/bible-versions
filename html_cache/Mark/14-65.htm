Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then some of them began to spit on Him.
Spitting on someone in ancient Jewish culture was a profound act of contempt and humiliation. This act fulfilled the prophecy in Isaiah 50:6, where the suffering servant is described as being spat upon. It signifies the deep rejection and scorn Jesus faced from the religious leaders and others present.

They blindfolded Him,
Blindfolding Jesus was a way to mock His prophetic abilities. In the context of a trial, it was also a method to disorient and further humiliate the accused. This act underscores the irony of the situation, as Jesus, the true prophet, was being mocked for His divine insight.

struck Him with their fists,
Physical violence against Jesus highlights the brutality and injustice of the trial. It reflects the fulfillment of prophecies such as Isaiah 53:3-5, which speaks of the Messiah being despised and afflicted. This treatment was illegal according to Jewish law, which prohibited such abuse before a formal conviction.

and said to Him, “Prophesy!”
The demand for Jesus to prophesy while blindfolded was a form of ridicule, challenging His identity as a prophet. This mockery is deeply ironic, as Jesus had already accurately predicted His suffering and death (Mark 8:31, 9:31, 10:33-34). Their taunt was a denial of His messianic role and divine knowledge.

And the officers received Him with slaps in His face.
The officers, likely temple guards, participated in the abuse, showing the widespread rejection of Jesus. Slapping was a common form of insult and punishment, further emphasizing the dishonor Jesus endured. This treatment reflects the fulfillment of prophecies about the Messiah's suffering and the world's rejection of Him (Psalm 22:6-8).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus Christ
The central figure of the New Testament, who is being mocked and abused in this passage. This event takes place during His trial before the Sanhedrin.

2. The Sanhedrin
The Jewish council that held trials for religious matters. They are responsible for the proceedings against Jesus.

3. The Officers
These are the temple guards or officers who are carrying out the physical abuse against Jesus.

4. The Mockers
Unspecified individuals who are participating in the abuse by spitting, blindfolding, and striking Jesus.

5. Jerusalem
The city where these events are taking place, specifically in the high priest's courtyard.
Teaching Points
The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Jesus' suffering was foretold in the Old Testament, demonstrating the divine plan and purpose in His sacrifice.

The Reality of Persecution
Believers should be prepared for persecution and mockery, as Jesus Himself endured such treatment.

Responding to Injustice
Jesus' response to His abusers was one of silence and submission to God's will, teaching us to trust in God's justice.

The Depth of Christ's Love
The willingness of Jesus to endure such humiliation and pain underscores the depth of His love for humanity.

Following Jesus may involve suffering and sacrifice, but it is part of the Christian journey.

What Does Mark 14:65 Mean
They began to spit on Him

- “Then some of them began to spit on Him.” (Mark 14:65a)

- Spitting conveyed the deepest contempt. Isaiah 50:6 foresaw this moment: “I did not hide My face from shame and spitting.”

- Jesus had foretold it: “They will mock Him, spit on Him, flog Him, and kill Him” (Mark 10:34).

- Their contempt highlights both human hatred of holiness (John 3:19) and Christ’s willingness to bear shame for our redemption (Hebrews 12:2).

They blindfolded Him

- “They blindfolded Him.” (Mark 14:65b)

- Blindfolding turned the beating into a cruel game, mocking any claim of His prophetic sight (Luke 22:64).

- Light of the world (John 8:12) is treated as though He cannot see; yet He sees hearts (John 2:25).

- Fulfilled the picture of the Suffering Servant led “like a lamb to the slaughter” (Isaiah 53:7).

They struck Him with their fists

- “…struck Him with their fists…” (Mark 14:65c)

- Violence escalates from insult to assault. Micah 5:1 predicted Israel’s ruler would be “struck on the cheek with a rod.”

- Isaiah 52:14 speaks of His appearance marred beyond human likeness.

- Peter later recalls the scene: “When He was maligned, He did not retaliate” (1 Peter 2:23), modeling true meekness.

They said to Him, “Prophesy!”

- “…and said to Him, ‘Prophesy!’” (Mark 14:65d)

- Mockers demand a sign after ignoring countless signs (Mark 8:11–12).

- Irony: at that very moment Jesus’ earlier prophecies (Mark 8:31; 9:31) are being fulfilled in front of them.

- Their taunt fulfills Psalm 22:8: “He trusts in the LORD; let Him deliver him.”

The officers received Him with slaps

- “And the officers received Him with slaps in His face.” (Mark 14:65e)

- Temple guards, charged with order, join the abuse (John 18:22).

- Legal authorities violate justice (Isaiah 59:14), underscoring humanity’s universal guilt (Romans 3:23).

- Yet every blow moves the Lamb closer to the cross where justice and mercy meet (Romans 5:8).

summary

Mark 14:65 records escalating contempt—from spitting to blindfolding, fist strikes, mocking demands, and official slaps—fulfilling prophecy, exposing human sin, and displaying Christ’s willing submission. Jesus endures unjust shame to satisfy divine justice, proving both His messianic identity and His boundless love for sinners.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(65) And to cover his face.--It was this (recorded by St. Mark and St. Luke, but not by St. Matthew) which gave point to the taunt "Prophesy." They blindfolded the Prophet, and then called on Him to use His power of supernatural vision.

The servants did strike him.--Better, as before, the officers. The two forms of outrage, with the clenched fist and with the open palm, are specified by both St. Matthew and St. Mark.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 65. - And some began to spit on him. St. Matthew (Matthew 26:67) says, "Then did they spit in his face." That Divine face, to be reverenced and adored by every creature, was exposed to this vile contumely; and he bore it patiently. "I hid not my face from shame and spitting" (Isaiah 1:61). And the officers received him with blows of their hands (οἱ ὑπηρέται ῤαπίσμασιν αὐτὸν ἔλαβον).
