Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
As they approached Jerusalem
This phrase marks the beginning of a significant event in Jesus' ministry, often referred to as the Triumphal Entry. Jerusalem is the central city of Jewish worship and the location of the Temple, making it a focal point for religious and political activity. Jesus' approach to Jerusalem signifies the fulfillment of His mission and the culmination of His earthly ministry. This journey is a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies, such as Zechariah 9:9, which speaks of a king coming to Jerusalem.

and came to Bethphage and Bethany
Bethphage and Bethany are two small villages located near Jerusalem. Bethphage, meaning "house of unripe figs," is less mentioned in the Bible but is significant as the starting point of Jesus' entry into Jerusalem. Bethany, meaning "house of affliction," is more frequently mentioned and is known as the home of Mary, Martha, and Lazarus. It is also the place where Jesus performed the miracle of raising Lazarus from the dead (John 11), which heightened His popularity and the anticipation of His arrival in Jerusalem.

at the Mount of Olives
The Mount of Olives is a ridge east of Jerusalem, significant in both Jewish and Christian traditions. It is a place of prophecy and eschatological importance, as seen in Zechariah 14:4, which predicts the Lord's feet standing on the Mount of Olives. This location is also where Jesus would later ascend into heaven (Acts 1:9-12). The Mount of Olives offers a panoramic view of Jerusalem, symbolizing Jesus' impending entry into the city and His ultimate sacrifice.

Jesus sent out two of His disciples
This action demonstrates Jesus' authority and foreknowledge. By sending two disciples, Jesus is preparing for His entry into Jerusalem, ensuring that all details align with prophetic fulfillment. The sending of the disciples also reflects a pattern in Jesus' ministry of involving His followers in His work, teaching them obedience and trust. This act is reminiscent of the instructions given to the disciples in other parts of the Gospels, emphasizing the importance of following Jesus' commands.

What Does Mark 11:1 Mean
As they approached Jerusalem

• “When the days drew near for Him to be taken up, Jesus resolutely set out for Jerusalem” (Luke 9:51). Mark’s wording shows the fulfillment of that purposeful journey—every step measured, every mile foreshadowing the cross.

• Psalm 122:1 captures the pilgrim joy Jesus surely shared with His followers: “I was glad when they said to me, ‘Let us go to the house of the LORD.’” Yet here the gladness is tinged with the weight of redemption.

• By noting the approach, Mark signals the climax of Jesus’ earthly mission. The city of David will witness both rejection and ultimate victory.

and came to Bethphage and Bethany

• These sister villages sit on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, about two miles from Jerusalem (John 11:18). Their mention grounds the narrative in real geography—tangible evidence of Scripture’s historical reliability.

• Bethany is familiar territory: the home of Mary, Martha, and Lazarus (John 11). Jesus chooses relationship-rich places as staging grounds for His greatest works.

• Bethphage, likely meaning “House of Unripe Figs,” recalls Jesus’ forthcoming object lesson with the fig tree (Mark 11:12-14), tying location to teaching.

at the Mount of Olives

• The ridge overlooks the Temple Mount, giving Jesus a clear view of the city He loves (Luke 19:41).

• Zechariah 14:4 foretells, “His feet will stand on the Mount of Olives on that day,” linking this setting with end-times hope. Mark subtly invites readers to connect the present entry with future glory.

• After His resurrection, Jesus will ascend from this very mount (Acts 1:9-12), promising His return in the same manner—a reminder that every detail in God’s plan is purposeful and connected.

Jesus sent out two of His disciples

• Delegation is deliberate. As in Luke 10:1, Jesus sends disciples ahead to prepare the way, modeling trust and training for future ministry.

• The command underscores His sovereign authority—He orchestrates events down to the loan of a colt (Mark 11:2-6), fulfilling Zechariah 9:9.

• Obedience becomes the doorway to witnessing prophecy in real time. The disciples’ simple “Yes, Lord” places them inside God’s unfolding story.

summary

Mark 11:1 is far more than a travel note. Each phrase spotlights divine intentionality: Jesus’ determined approach to Jerusalem, His choice of familiar villages, the prophetic stage of the Mount of Olives, and the empowering of obedient disciples. Together they reveal a Savior who steers history with loving precision, inviting His followers—then and now—to step into His redemptive purposes.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - And when they drew nigh unto Jerusalem, unto Bethphage and Bethany, at the mount of Olives. St. Matthew (Matthew 21:1) says, "When they drew nigh unto Jerusalem, and came unto Bethphage." St. Mark mentions the three places together, because Bethphage and Bethany, being near together, were also both of them close to Jerusalem. The distance from Jericho to Jerusalem (about seventeen miles) would involve a journey of about seven hours. The country between Jerusalem and Jericho is hilly, rugged, and desolate. It is from the height overhanging Bethany that the finest view of Jerusalem is gained. It appears from St. John (John 12:1) that our Lord on the preceding sabbath had supped, and probably passed the night, at Bethany; and that on the following day (answering to our Palm Sunday) he had come still nearer to Jerusalem, namely, to Bethphage; and from thence he sent two of his disciples for the ass and the colt. So his way to Jerusalem was from Bethany by Bethphage, the Mount of Olives, and the Valley of Jehoshaphat. The Valley of Jehoshaphat, through which flows the brook Kedron, lies close to Jerusalem. Bethphage literally means "the house of green figs," as Bethany, lying a short distance west of it, means "the house of dates." The date palm growing in the neighbeurhood would furnish the branches with which the multitude strewed the way on the occasion of our Lord's triumphal entry. He sendeth two of his disciples. Who were they? Bede thinks that they were Peter and Philip. Jansonius, with greater probability, thinks that they were Peter and John, because a little after this Christ sent these two to prepare for the Passover. But we know nothing certain on this point.
