Now it was already evening.
The timing of events is crucial in understanding the sequence of Jesus' crucifixion and burial. Evening in Jewish reckoning begins at sunset, marking the end of one day and the beginning of the next. This detail indicates that Jesus' crucifixion occurred on the day of preparation, and His body needed to be taken down before the Sabbath began at sunset. The urgency to bury Jesus before the Sabbath reflects Jewish customs and laws regarding work and rest on the Sabbath (Exodus 20:8-11).

Since it was Preparation Day
Preparation Day refers to the day before the Sabbath, a time when Jews would prepare meals and complete tasks forbidden on the Sabbath. This day was particularly significant during Passover week, as it was a time of heightened religious observance. The mention of Preparation Day underscores the importance of adhering to Jewish law, even amidst the extraordinary events of Jesus' death. It also highlights the fulfillment of Jesus as the Passover Lamb, as His crucifixion coincides with the preparation for Passover (John 19:14).

(that is, the day before the Sabbath),
The clarification that Preparation Day is the day before the Sabbath emphasizes the immediacy of the situation. The Sabbath, a day of rest instituted by God (Genesis 2:2-3), was strictly observed, and no work, including burial, could be performed. This context explains the haste in which Joseph of Arimathea sought to bury Jesus (Mark 15:43). The timing also aligns with the prophecy of Jesus being in the grave for three days and three nights (Matthew 12:40), as His burial before the Sabbath allowed for this timeline to be fulfilled.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Evening
This refers to the time of day, specifically the period after sunset. In Jewish tradition, the day begins at sunset, so this marks the transition from one day to the next.

2. Preparation Day
This is the day before the Sabbath, a time when Jews would prepare for the Sabbath by completing tasks that could not be done on the Sabbath itself. It is significant because it sets the context for the urgency in the burial of Jesus.

3. Sabbath
The Sabbath is a day of rest observed from Friday evening to Saturday evening. It is a holy day in Jewish tradition, dedicated to rest and worship, as commanded in the Ten Commandments.
Teaching Points
Understanding Jewish Customs
Recognizing the significance of the Preparation Day and the Sabbath helps us understand the urgency and actions of those involved in Jesus' burial. It reminds us of the importance of respecting cultural and religious practices.

The Importance of Rest
The Sabbath is a reminder of the need for rest and reflection. In our busy lives, we should prioritize time for rest and spiritual renewal, following the example set by God in creation.

Urgency in Obedience
The actions taken on the Preparation Day demonstrate the urgency and importance of obedience to God's commandments. We should strive to act promptly and faithfully in our own spiritual lives.

Preparation for Worship
Just as the Jews prepared for the Sabbath, we should prepare our hearts and minds for worship, ensuring that we are ready to meet with God and honor Him.

What Does Mark 15:42 Mean
Now it was already evening

• Jesus had been on the cross from “the sixth hour… until the ninth hour” (Mark 15:33). By the time His body hung lifeless, “evening” had set in, signaling the close of that same day (Matthew 27:57).

• God’s law required a body not remain on a tree overnight (Deuteronomy 21:22-23), so the approach of nightfall put urgency on removing Jesus from the cross.

• The moment underscores fulfilled prophecy: His suffering ended exactly within the limits set by Scripture, highlighting the Father’s perfect timing (Galatians 4:4-5).

• Practical takeaway: God works on a precise timetable. When circumstances seem delayed, remember His clock never misfires (Psalm 31:15).

Since it was Preparation Day

• “Preparation Day” was the common Jewish term for Friday, the day set aside to ready meals, lamps, and hearts for the Sabbath rest (Luke 23:54).

• Joseph of Arimathea therefore “gathered up courage” to request Jesus’ body quickly (Mark 15:43), knowing dusk would close the workday.

• The rush to prepare reminds us:

– Obedience sometimes calls for courageous, time-sensitive action (John 19:38-40).

– True devotion includes practical service—clean linen, a new tomb, spices—alongside private belief (James 2:17).

That is, the day before the Sabbath

• The Sabbath, commanded in Exodus 20:8-11 and modeled by God at Creation, began at sunset (Leviticus 23:32). Everything had to cease by then.

• By stating “the day before the Sabbath,” Mark affirms a literal, fixed weekly rhythm that framed the crucifixion narrative (John 19:31).

• The Sabbath pointed to rest; Christ’s finished work on the cross secured the ultimate rest for all who believe (Hebrews 4:9-10).

• Application: The weekly pause still invites worshippers to remember redemption accomplished—a rhythm of rest rooted in the completed work of Jesus.

summary

Mark 15:42 sets the scene: evening has fallen, Friday’s preparations are pressing, and the Sabbath’s arrival looms. Every detail—time of day, urgency of duties, and sacred calendar—aligns to showcase God’s unerring sovereignty and Christ’s fulfillment of the law. The verse reminds believers that our Lord bowed to time’s limits so we might enter eternal rest, calling us to timely obedience and confident trust in His perfect schedule.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(42) The preparation, that is, the day before the sabbath.--The explanation, like that in Mark 7:2-3, is characteristic of St. Mark, as writing for Gentile readers. It fixes, with hardly the shadow of a doubt, the meaning of the word "preparation," as given in the Note on Matthew 27:62.
