When the Sabbath came
The Sabbath, observed from Friday evening to Saturday evening, was a day of rest and worship for the Jewish people, as commanded in Exodus 20:8-11. It was customary for Jews to gather in synagogues for prayer and teaching. Jesus often used the Sabbath as an opportunity to teach, emphasizing the fulfillment of the Law and the Prophets in Himself (Matthew 5:17).

He began to teach in the synagogue
Synagogues were central to Jewish community life, serving as places of worship, education, and legal proceedings. Jesus teaching in the synagogue aligns with His role as a rabbi or teacher, a respected position in Jewish society. His teaching often challenged traditional interpretations, emphasizing the spirit rather than the letter of the Law (Matthew 7:28-29).

and many who heard Him were astonished
The astonishment of the listeners reflects the authority and wisdom with which Jesus spoke, distinguishing Him from other teachers of the Law. This reaction is consistent with other accounts of Jesus' teaching, where His insights and understanding of Scripture left audiences amazed (Luke 4:32).

“Where did this man get these ideas?” they asked
The question highlights the perceived incongruity between Jesus' humble origins and His profound teaching. Jesus, known as the carpenter's son from Nazareth, did not have formal rabbinical training, which puzzled those who heard Him (John 7:15).

“What is this wisdom He has been given?
The wisdom of Jesus is a recurring theme in the Gospels, often linked to His divine nature and mission. This wisdom is seen as a fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah, such as Isaiah 11:2, which speaks of the Spirit of wisdom resting upon Him.

And how can He perform such miracles?
The miracles of Jesus served as signs of His divine authority and the inauguration of God's Kingdom on earth. They fulfilled prophecies like those in Isaiah 35:5-6, which describe the miraculous works of the coming Messiah. The people's amazement at His miracles underscores their recognition of something extraordinary, yet they struggled to reconcile this with their expectations of the Messiah.

Teaching Points
The Authority of Jesus' Teaching
Jesus' wisdom and authority come from God, not human institutions. This challenges us to seek divine wisdom over worldly credentials.

The Power of Miracles
Miracles serve as a testament to Jesus' divine nature and authority. Believers are called to recognize and testify to God's power in their lives.

Familiarity and Unbelief
Familiarity can breed contempt and unbelief. We must guard against dismissing God's work due to preconceived notions or familiarity.

The Role of the Sabbath
The Sabbath is a time for rest and spiritual renewal. It is an opportunity to engage deeply with God's Word and community.

Responding to Astonishment
Astonishment should lead to faith and deeper inquiry, not skepticism. We are encouraged to respond to God's work with openness and belief.

What Does Mark 6:2 Mean
When the Sabbath came

- Jesus waits for the seventh day, the God-ordained day of rest (Exodus 20:8-11), to begin His public teaching in Nazareth.

- By choosing the Sabbath, He honors the Father’s timing and ensures the largest possible gathering of worshipers, just as His custom was elsewhere (Luke 4:16).

- The setting reminds us that the Lord of the Sabbath (Mark 2:27-28) is about to speak, underscoring both His authority and His submission to the Law.

He began to teach in the synagogue

- Synagogues were places of Scripture reading and exposition; Jesus steps into that role, fulfilling Isaiah 61:1-2 as He had in Capernaum and elsewhere (Matthew 4:23).

- His teaching carries weight because He expounds the Word He authored (John 1:1).

- Like Ezra who read and explained the Law (Nehemiah 8:8), Jesus brings clarity, not adding or subtracting but unfolding God’s intent.

and many who heard Him were astonished

- Their amazement mirrors reactions in other towns: “the crowds were astonished at His teaching, because He taught as one who had authority” (Matthew 7:28-29).

- The word describes a jaw-dropping realization that something divine is taking place (Luke 4:22).

- Yet amazement alone is not faith; it is possible to marvel at Christ and still reject Him (John 6:60-66).

"Where did this man get these ideas?"

- The hometown listeners know Jesus’ human background and cannot see beyond it (Mark 6:3).

- Their question echoes later skepticism in Jerusalem: “How does this man know letters, having never been taught?” (John 7:15).

- They miss the prophetic promise that the Spirit of the LORD would rest upon the Messiah, giving Him counsel and understanding (Isaiah 11:2).

"What is this wisdom He has been given?"

- Scripture views wisdom as a gift from God (James 1:5). The crowd senses it but attributes it to an unknown source rather than to the Father.

- Solomon’s God-given wisdom drew worldwide attention (1 Kings 4:29-34); Jesus embodies a greater wisdom, for “in Him are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge” (Colossians 2:3).

- Recognizing wisdom without surrendering to its Author leaves the heart unchanged (Matthew 13:54-58).

"And how can He perform such miracles?"

- Their question concedes the reality of His mighty works (Mark 5:41-42; John 2:11) yet stops short of confession.

- Nicodemus reaches the right conclusion: “No one could perform the signs You do unless God were with him” (John 3:2).

- Miracles confirm the message (Hebrews 2:3-4), but hardened hearts can still dismiss them (Matthew 11:20-24).

summary

Mark 6:2 paints a vivid scene: on the Sabbath, Jesus stands in His hometown synagogue, teaching with divine authority. The congregation cannot deny His wisdom or His miracles, yet their familiarity breeds contempt. They stumble over His earthly origins instead of embracing His heavenly mission. The passage reminds us that exposure to truth demands a decision—astonishment must give way to faith, or it fades into unbelief.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) Many hearing him.--The better MSS. give, "the many," i.e., the majority of those who were present.

Such mighty works.--As the Evangelist notes in Mark 6:5 that no mighty work had been done in Nazareth, these must refer to what had been reported there.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - As usual, he made the sabbath the special time for his teaching. And many hearing him were astonished. They were astonished at the ability, the sublimity, the holiness of his teaching, as well as at the signs and wonders by which he confirmed it. "Many" hearing him; not all. Some listened with faith; but "the many" (there is some authority for οἱ πολλοὶ)were envious of him. Whence hath this man these things? The expression, "this man," is repeated, according to the best authorities, in the next clause, What is the wisdom that is given (not "unto him," but) unto this man? There is a contemptuous tone about the expression.
