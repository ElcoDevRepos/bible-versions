And a woman was there
This phrase introduces a specific individual within the crowd surrounding Jesus. The presence of the woman in the crowd is significant, as it highlights her desperation and determination to seek healing despite societal norms. In the cultural context of first-century Judea, women often had limited public roles, and her presence in a public space, especially in her condition, would have been unusual and potentially frowned upon.

who had suffered from bleeding
The woman's condition is described as a chronic issue of bleeding, likely a menstrual disorder, which would have rendered her ceremonially unclean according to Levitical law (Leviticus 15:25-27). This uncleanness would have isolated her socially and religiously, preventing her from participating in normal community and worship activities. Her suffering was not only physical but also emotional and spiritual due to this isolation.

for twelve years
The duration of her suffering emphasizes the chronic nature of her condition and the depth of her desperation. Twelve years is a significant period, suggesting that she had exhausted all available medical and spiritual remedies without success. The number twelve can also be seen as symbolically significant in the Bible, often representing completeness or the people of God (e.g., twelve tribes of Israel, twelve apostles). Her long-term suffering contrasts with the immediate healing she receives from Jesus, highlighting His power and compassion.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Woman with the Issue of Blood
This unnamed woman had been suffering from a chronic bleeding condition for twelve years. Her condition made her ceremonially unclean according to Jewish law, isolating her from society and religious practices.

2. Jesus
Central to this account, Jesus is the healer and the one whom the woman seeks out in faith, believing that touching His garment will heal her.

3. The Crowd
A large group of people surrounds Jesus, making it difficult for the woman to reach Him. The crowd represents both a physical and social barrier.

4. Capernaum
While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the events occur in the region of Capernaum, a significant location in Jesus' ministry.

5. The Twelve-Year Duration
The length of the woman's suffering is significant, highlighting the chronic nature of her condition and her desperation for healing.
Teaching Points
Faith in Action
The woman's faith led her to take bold action despite societal and religious barriers. Her belief in Jesus' power to heal was greater than her fear of public shame or failure.

Perseverance in Suffering
Her twelve-year struggle teaches us about enduring faith and the hope that healing and restoration are possible, even after long periods of suffering.

Breaking Barriers
Jesus' response to the woman shows that He is willing to break societal and religious barriers to bring healing and restoration. This challenges us to consider how we can break down barriers in our own lives to reach out to others.

The Power of Jesus
This passage highlights Jesus' divine power and authority over physical ailments, encouraging believers to trust in His ability to heal and restore.

Personal Encounter with Jesus
The woman's healing came through a personal encounter with Jesus, reminding us of the importance of seeking a personal relationship with Him for our spiritual and physical needs.

What Does Mark 5:25 Mean
And a woman was there

• In the midst of Jairus’s urgent situation, the Holy Spirit pauses to spotlight an unnamed, ordinary woman.

• Scripture often elevates the overlooked (cf. John 4:7–30; Luke 7:37–38), reminding us that no one is invisible to God.

• Her presence in this crowd is intentional, showing God’s sovereignty over timing and encounters (Proverbs 16:9).

who had suffered

• The verb highlights real, ongoing pain—physical, emotional, and social.

• Like Job (Job 2:7–8) and Hannah (1 Samuel 1:6–7), she lived under prolonged affliction, demonstrating that suffering is not evidence of God’s absence but can be the setting for His power (Romans 5:3–5).

• Her suffering prepares her heart to seek Christ above all else (Psalm 34:18).

from bleeding

• According to Levitical law, her hemorrhage rendered her ceremonially unclean (Leviticus 15:25–27), barring her from temple worship and close relationships.

• She embodies the isolation of sin and impurity that only Jesus can touch and heal (Isaiah 64:6; Ephesians 2:12–13).

• Her ailment pictures humanity’s need for cleansing beyond human means (Jeremiah 17:14).

for twelve years

• Twelve years equals Jairus’s daughter’s entire lifetime (Mark 5:42), contrasting a young life beginning to fade with a long life fading away.

• The number underscores the completeness of her trial; it did not lessen with time. God sometimes allows lengthy seasons to magnify His eventual deliverance (Genesis 15:13–14; Psalm 13:1).

• Her perseverance models steadfast faith that waits until God acts (Lamentations 3:25–26; Galatians 6:9).

summary

Mark 5:25 introduces a real woman whose dozen-year hemorrhage left her isolated and hopeless—yet perfectly positioned for Jesus. Her prolonged suffering, uncleanness, and perseverance set the stage for Christ’s power to cleanse and restore, assuring believers that no pain or delay is wasted when it drives us to the Savior.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 25, 26. - A woman, which had an issue of blood twelve years. All the synoptic Gospels mention the length of time during which she had been suffering. Eusebius records a tradition that she was a Gentile, a native of Caesarea Philippi. This disease was a chronic hoemorrhage, for which she had found no relief from the physicians. Lightfoot, in his 'Horae Hebraicae,' gives a list of the remedies applied in such cases, which seem quite sufficient to account for St. Mark's statement that she was nothing bettered, but rather grew worse. St. Luke, himself a physician, says that she "had spent all her living upon physicians, and could not be healed of any."
