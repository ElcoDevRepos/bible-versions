Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Jesus replied
In this passage, Jesus is responding to the Pharisees, who were questioning His disciples' actions on the Sabbath. This response is significant as it demonstrates Jesus' authority and knowledge of Scripture, setting the stage for His teaching on the true purpose of the Sabbath.

Have you never read
This phrase indicates Jesus' challenge to the Pharisees' understanding of Scripture. It implies that despite their extensive study, they have missed the deeper meaning. Jesus often used this rhetorical question to highlight the Pharisees' lack of insight into the Scriptures they claimed to know so well.

what David did
Jesus refers to an event from the life of King David, found in 1 Samuel 21:1-6. By invoking David, a revered figure in Jewish history, Jesus draws a parallel between David's actions and His own, suggesting that human need can take precedence over ceremonial law.

when he and his companions were hungry and in need
This context emphasizes the human necessity that justified David's actions. David and his men were fleeing from King Saul and found themselves in dire need of sustenance. Jesus uses this example to illustrate that the Sabbath law should not be a burden but a means to serve human needs, aligning with the principle that "the Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath" (Mark 2:27).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is responding to the Pharisees' criticism of His disciples. He uses Scripture to defend their actions.

2. David
Jesus references an event from the life of King David to illustrate His point. David is a key figure in the Old Testament, known for his heart for God and his leadership of Israel.

3. Abiathar the High Priest
Mentioned in Jesus' response, Abiathar was a priest during the time of David. The event Jesus refers to actually involves Ahimelech, Abiathar's father, but Abiathar is noted as the high priest during David's reign.

4. Pharisees
A religious group in Jesus' time known for their strict adherence to the Law. They challenge Jesus regarding His disciples' actions on the Sabbath.

5. The House of God
Refers to the tabernacle, the place of worship and the presence of God during the time of David.
Teaching Points
Understanding the Spirit of the Law
Jesus emphasizes the importance of understanding the purpose behind God's laws, rather than adhering to them rigidly without compassion or understanding.

Jesus' Authority
By referencing David, Jesus asserts His authority over the Sabbath, indicating that He is greater than David and has the right to interpret the Law.

Compassion Over Ritual
Jesus highlights that human need can take precedence over ritualistic observance, teaching us to prioritize compassion and mercy.

Scriptural Knowledge
Jesus uses Scripture to defend His actions, demonstrating the importance of knowing and understanding the Bible to address challenges and questions.

Historical Context
Understanding the historical and cultural context of biblical events can deepen our comprehension and application of Scripture.

What Does Mark 2:25 Mean
Jesus replied,

• The response comes after Pharisees accuse the disciples of breaking the Sabbath (Mark 2:23-24).

• Christ answers with authority, demonstrating the Word-made-flesh correcting misused tradition (Mark 1:22; Luke 6:3).

• His reply models how believers should ground every answer in Scripture.

Have you never read

• Jesus directs His critics back to the written Word, affirming its final authority (Matthew 12:3; 2 Timothy 3:16).

• The phrase exposes their familiarity with the text yet failure to grasp its intent—reminding all readers that mere knowledge without understanding leads to legalism.

what David did

• Christ cites 1 Samuel 21:1-6, where David, God’s anointed yet fugitive, received consecrated bread.

• By invoking David—highly revered by the Pharisees—Jesus underscores that Scripture itself records an exception grounded in divine mercy (Matthew 12:3; Luke 6:4).

• The example affirms continuity between Old and New Testament principles: grace precedes ritual.

when he and his companions were hungry

• Hunger represents legitimate human necessity; meeting it aligns with God’s heart (Isaiah 58:6-7).

• The showbread, normally for priests alone (Exodus 25:30), became provision because preserving life outweighed ceremonial restriction.

• Jesus thus affirms that God’s law never negates compassion (Mark 12:33).

and in need?

• “Need” clarifies the moral priority: Sabbath observance exists to bless, not burden (Mark 2:27).

• Mercy fulfills the law’s true intent (Hosea 6:6; Micah 6:8).

• By highlighting necessity, Jesus teaches that legal obedience divorced from love misrepresents God’s character.

summary

Mark 2:25 shows Jesus appealing to Scripture to reveal that God’s commands serve humanity’s good. By recalling David’s reception of the showbread, Christ establishes the precedence of mercy over ritual, affirming that meeting genuine need accords with, rather than violates, God’s law.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 25, 26. - David... and they that were with him. This seems opposed to what we read in 1 Samuel 21, where David is stated to have been alone. But the facts appear to have been these, that David, fleeing from Saul, went alone to Ahimelech the high priest, and sought and obtained five loaves of the "shewbread," which he carried away with him to his companions in flight, and shared with them; for he says (1 Samuel 21:2), "I have appointed my servants to such and such a place." This incident actually happened in the high priesthood of Ahimelech the father of Abiathar. Bede says that they were both present when David came in his distress and obtained the shewbread. But Ahimelech having been slain, together with eighty-six priests, by Saul, Abiathar fled to David, and became his companion in his exile. Moreover, when he succeeded to the high priesthood on the death of Ahimelech, he did far more good service than his father had done, and so was worthy of being spoken of with this special commendation, and as though he was actually high priest, even though his father was then living. The words may properly mean "in the days when Abiathar was living who became high priest, and was more eminent than his father." The shewbread; literally, the bread of the face, that is, of the Divine presence, symbolizing the Divine Being who is the Bread of life. It was directed by the Law that within the sanctuary there should be a table of shittim (or acacia) wood; and every sabbath twelve newly baked loaves were placed upon it in two rows. These leaves were sprinkled with incense, and then remained there until the following sabbath. They were then replaced by twelve newly baked loaves, the old loaves being eaten by the priests in the holy place, from which it was unlawful to remove them. These twelve loaves corresponded to the twelve tribes. The force of our Lord's reasoning is this: David, a man after God's own heart, when sorely pressed by hunger, applied to the high priest and took some of these sacred loaves, loaves which under ordinary circumstances it was not lawful for the lay people to eat, because he wisely judged that a positive law, forbidding the laity to eat this bread, ought to yield to a law of necessity and of nature; which intimates to us that in a grave necessity of famine, life may be lawfully preserved by eating even sacred bread which has been dedicated to God. Therefore, in like manner, nay, much more, was it lawful for Christ and his disciples to pluck the ears of corn on the sabbath day, that by rubbing them in their hands they might pick out the good grain and satisfy their hunger.
