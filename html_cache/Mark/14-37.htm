Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Jesus returned and found them sleeping.
In the context of the Garden of Gethsemane, Jesus had asked His disciples to keep watch while He prayed. This moment occurs after the Last Supper, during which Jesus had foretold His betrayal and impending crucifixion. The disciples' inability to stay awake highlights human frailty and the struggle against spiritual lethargy. The garden setting is significant, as Gethsemane means "oil press," symbolizing the intense pressure Jesus faced. This scene parallels the spiritual vigilance required of believers, as emphasized in 1 Peter 5:8, which warns to be alert and of sober mind.

“Simon, are you asleep?” He asked.
Jesus specifically addresses Peter, using his original name, Simon. This personal address may serve as a reminder of Peter's humanity and previous weaknesses, despite his earlier bold declarations of loyalty (Mark 14:29). The use of "Simon" instead of "Peter" (meaning rock) could indicate a moment of testing and failure, reminiscent of Peter's earlier confession of faith in Matthew 16:16-18. This question underscores the need for personal accountability and vigilance in spiritual matters.

“Were you not able to keep watch for one hour?
The phrase "one hour" suggests a relatively short period, emphasizing the disciples' inability to remain spiritually alert even for a brief time. This reflects the urgency and importance of prayer and watchfulness, especially in times of trial. The call to "keep watch" is a recurring biblical theme, seen in passages like Matthew 24:42, where Jesus instructs His followers to be watchful for His return. This moment foreshadows Peter's upcoming denial and serves as a lesson on the necessity of spiritual preparedness and reliance on God's strength rather than human resolve.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is in the Garden of Gethsemane, experiencing deep anguish as He prepares for His impending crucifixion. He seeks support from His disciples during this critical moment.

2. Peter (Simon)
One of Jesus' closest disciples, Peter is specifically addressed by Jesus in this verse. Despite his earlier declarations of loyalty, Peter is found sleeping instead of keeping watch.

3. The Disciples
Although Peter is singled out, the other disciples present (James and John) are also asleep. They represent human frailty and the struggle to remain spiritually vigilant.

4. Garden of Gethsemane
This is the setting of the event, a place of prayer and testing for Jesus and His disciples. It symbolizes a place of spiritual struggle and surrender.

5. The Event of Jesus' Agony
This moment is part of the larger account of Jesus' passion, highlighting His human vulnerability and divine mission.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Spiritual Vigilance
Just as Jesus asked Peter to stay awake, we are called to be spiritually alert in our daily lives. This requires intentionality and discipline in prayer and study of the Word.

Human Weakness and Divine Strength
The disciples' inability to stay awake highlights human frailty. We must rely on God's strength to overcome our weaknesses and fulfill His purposes.

The Role of Prayer in Temptation
Jesus' instruction to watch and pray is a reminder that prayer is a powerful tool in resisting temptation and aligning our will with God's.

Accountability in Community
Jesus addressing Peter specifically suggests the importance of accountability among believers. We should encourage and support one another in our spiritual journeys.

The Cost of Discipleship
Following Jesus requires sacrifice and perseverance. We must be willing to endure discomfort and remain faithful, even when it is challenging.

What Does Mark 14:37 Mean
Then Jesus returned

- Jesus has just poured out His heart to the Father in Gethsemane (Mark 14:32-36) and immediately turns back to check on His disciples.

- His movement shows a Shepherd’s concern, echoing the way He “saw” the disciples’ struggle on the lake before walking on the water (Mark 6:47-48).

- Scripture presents Christ as both fully engaged with heaven and fully involved with His followers on earth (Hebrews 4:15).

and found them sleeping

- Instead of standing guard, the three closest friends nod off at the most critical hour. Matthew 26:40 and Luke 22:45 confirm that exhaustion—not just indifference—overwhelmed them.

- Their drowsiness illustrates the flesh’s weakness contrasted with the spirit’s willingness (Mark 14:38).

- Other passages warn against spiritual sleep when decisive moments arrive (Romans 13:11-12; Proverbs 6:9-10).

“Simon, are you asleep?”

- Jesus addresses Peter by his old name, Simon, the same name used when Peter’s self-confidence swelled earlier that evening (Mark 14:29-31).

- By singling him out, the Lord gently exposes the gap between bold promises and actual performance.

- Personal accountability is a consistent biblical pattern: God called “Adam, where are you?” (Genesis 3:9) and later asked Elijah, “What are you doing here?” (1 Kings 19:9).

“Were you not able to keep watch for one hour?”

- “Keep watch” is the same verb Jesus used in His end-times teaching only days earlier (Mark 13:35-37), linking future vigilance with present obedience.

- One hour is a short, reasonable span, underscoring how quickly human resolve can falter.

- Paul later echoes the call to sustained alertness in prayer—“pray in the Spirit on all occasions… be alert” (Ephesians 6:18; Colossians 4:2).

- The verse also shows that Jesus expects partnership: while He wrestles in solitary prayer, He invites His friends to stand guard with Him.

summary

Mark 14:37 reveals a watchful Savior and weary disciples. Jesus’ return highlights His shepherding heart; the disciples’ sleep exposes human frailty. By calling Peter “Simon,” the Lord lovingly confronts empty self-confidence, and His question about one hour reminds believers that spiritual vigilance is both attainable and necessary. The verse urges every follower to stay awake—to live alert, prayerful, and faithful in the moments that matter most.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) Simon, sleepest thou?--Note that while St. Matthew and St. Luke give the question in the plural, St. Mark reports it in the singular, and joins it with the emphatic utterance of the name of the disciple. His report, too, includes the two questions which appear separately in the other two Gospels.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 37. - And he cometh, and findeth them sleeping, and saith unto Peter, Simon, sleepest thou? Couldest thou not watch one hour? St. Luke says (Luke 22:45) that they were "sleeping for sorrow." So on the Mount of Transfiguration he says (Luke 9:32) that they were "heavy with sleep." This rebuke, which St. Mark tells us here was pointedly addressed to Peter, seems to glance at his earnest protestations of fidelity made not long before. And our Lord calls him by his old name of Simon. In St. Matthew (Matthew 26:40) it is less pointed; for there, while our Lord looks at Peter, he addresses them all. "He saith unto -Peter, What, could not ye watch with me one hour?" This is just one of those graphic little incidents which we may suppose St. Mark to have received directly from St. Peter.
