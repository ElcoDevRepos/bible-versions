Early in the morning
This phrase indicates the time of day when Jesus chose to engage in prayer. In Jewish culture, the day began at sunset, but the early morning was often considered a time for new beginnings and spiritual renewal. This practice of rising early for prayer is seen throughout the Bible, such as in the life of King David (Psalm 5:3) and the patriarchs. It underscores the importance of prioritizing communion with God before the day's activities begin.

while it was still dark
The mention of darkness highlights the solitude and quietness of the time, allowing for undisturbed communion with God. Darkness can symbolize the challenges and trials of life, and Jesus' choice to pray during this time may reflect His desire to seek strength and guidance from the Father before facing the day's demands. This also emphasizes the discipline and intentionality in Jesus' spiritual life.

Jesus got up
This action demonstrates Jesus' commitment and intentionality in seeking time with the Father. It reflects His humanity, as He needed rest, yet chose to prioritize prayer. This serves as a model for believers to actively pursue a relationship with God, even when it requires sacrifice or effort.

and went out
Jesus' decision to leave His current location suggests a deliberate choice to find a place free from distractions. This act of physically moving to a different place for prayer is a practice seen in other biblical figures, such as Moses and Elijah, who often sought solitude to hear from God. It highlights the importance of creating an environment conducive to focused prayer.

to a solitary place
The solitary place, or "desolate place," is significant as it represents a setting away from the busyness and demands of daily life. In the Gospels, Jesus frequently withdrew to such places to pray (Luke 5:16). This practice underscores the necessity of solitude in spiritual life, allowing for deeper reflection and connection with God.

to pray
Prayer was central to Jesus' ministry and life, serving as a means of communication with the Father. This act of prayer before significant events or decisions is a recurring theme in the Gospels, such as before choosing the twelve apostles (Luke 6:12) or before His crucifixion (Matthew 26:36-44). It highlights the dependence on God for guidance, strength, and alignment with His will. Jesus' prayer life serves as a model for believers, emphasizing the importance of regular, intentional communication with God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jesus
The central figure in this passage, Jesus is the Son of God, who demonstrates the importance of prayer and communion with the Father.

Teaching Points
The Priority of Prayer
Jesus prioritized prayer above all else, even when He was busy. We should also make prayer a priority in our lives, setting aside time to commune with God daily.

The Importance of Solitude
Jesus sought a solitary place to pray, free from distractions. We should find our own "solitary places" where we can focus on God without interruptions.

Early Morning Devotion
Jesus prayed early in the morning, setting the tone for His day. Starting our day with prayer can align our hearts and minds with God's will.

Modeling After Jesus
As followers of Christ, we should emulate His example of regular, intentional prayer, seeking guidance and strength from the Father.

Spiritual Refreshment
Just as Jesus was refreshed and empowered through prayer, we too can find spiritual renewal and strength in our times of prayer.

What Does Mark 1:35 Mean
Early in the morning

“Early in the morning” signals deliberate, intentional priority. Jesus chose the dawn hours—before the day’s demands—to meet with the Father (Psalm 5:3; Mark 16:2). Because the Son is our perfect example (1 Peter 2:21), His pattern invites us to carve out first-fruits time with God, giving Him the best of our attention rather than leftovers.

while it was still dark

The darkness underscores both the quiet and the cost. Jesus sought communion before distractions stirred (John 20:1). Rising when others slept reflected discipline (Proverbs 6:9-11) and underscored dependence on divine strength rather than public acclaim (Matthew 6:6). The unseen moments of fellowship prepared Him for the visible moments of ministry.

Jesus got up

Action follows desire. Christ physically left the comfort of rest to fulfill spiritual necessity (Luke 4:42). His getting up shows that intimacy with the Father is chosen, not accidental (Ephesians 5:14-16). If the sinless Son needed this communion, how much more do we?

and went out

Stepping outside the house marked separation from competing voices (Mark 1:29-33). Movement away from noise and need—though temporary—was essential for renewed effectiveness (Matthew 14:13). Jesus teaches that withdrawal for prayer is not escape but preparation (Ecclesiastes 3:1).

to a solitary place

Solitude provides an uncluttered arena for hearing God (Mark 6:31). In lonely places prophets were shaped (1 Kings 19:9-13), and here the Messiah models the same rhythm. Silence amplifies Scripture and stirs sensitivity to the Spirit (Psalm 46:10). Such spaces can be anywhere we intentionally remove distractions.

to pray

Prayer crowned the purpose of His early rising. Jesus communed, listened, and aligned with the Father’s will (Luke 6:12; Hebrews 5:7). His intercession fuels ours; the Savior who prayed on earth now intercedes in heaven (Romans 8:34). Prayer is partnership, warfare, and communion all at once (Ephesians 6:18).

summary

Mark 1:35 shows Jesus setting a pattern: intentional time, chosen discipline, purposeful solitude, and wholehearted prayer. The verse calls believers to prioritize private fellowship with the Father, trusting that hidden moments with Him empower public ministry and daily obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) A great while before day.--Literally, very early, while it was yet night. The note of time is peculiar to St. Mark. Prayer seems to have been sought now, as at other times, after a day of extraordinary and exhausting labour.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - And in the morning, at great while before day, he rose up and went out, and departed into a desert place, and there prayed. Our Lord thus prepared himself by prayer for his first departure on a missionary tour. This would be the morning of the first day of the week. A great while before day he left the scene of excitement. That was not a time for preaching the Gospel of the Kingdom. The miracles attracted attention to him, but they were not the object for which he came. They were necessary as means of stirring and awakening men's minds, and of fixing their attention upon him and upon the great salvation which he came to reveal. So he left the miracles to do their subordinate work; and he himself went into a desert place, that he might pray with more quiet and less distraction. He retired that he might escape the applause of men, which they were ready to lavish upon him after seeing so many miracles; that he might thus teach us to shun the praise of men. Let us learn from Christ to give the early morning to prayer, and to rise with the dawn of day, that we may have time for meditation, and give the firstfruits of the morning to God. The early morning is favorable for study; but it is specially dear to God and his angels.
