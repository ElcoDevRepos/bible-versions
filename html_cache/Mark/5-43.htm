Then Jesus gave strict orders that no one should know about this
In this passage, Jesus has just performed a miraculous healing by raising Jairus's daughter from the dead. His command for secrecy is consistent with other instances in the Gospels where He instructs those He heals to remain silent (e.g., Mark 1:44, Mark 7:36). This could be to prevent misunderstandings about His mission, to avoid drawing premature attention from authorities, or to manage the crowds that often followed Him. The "Messianic Secret" is a theme in Mark, where Jesus seeks to control the revelation of His identity and mission until the appropriate time. This reflects His focus on teaching and preparing His disciples for His ultimate purpose, rather than simply being known as a miracle worker.

and He told them to give her something to eat
This instruction highlights Jesus' concern for the practical needs of the girl, emphasizing His compassion and understanding of human needs. It also serves as a confirmation of her return to life; a spirit does not require food, but a living body does. This act is reminiscent of other instances where Jesus provides for physical needs, such as feeding the 5,000 (Mark 6:30-44). It underscores the reality of the miracle and the restoration of the girl to her normal life. Additionally, it reflects the holistic nature of Jesus' ministry, addressing both spiritual and physical aspects of human existence.

The setting for this miracle, it becomes a place of transformation from mourning to joy.
Teaching Points
The Power of Jesus
Jesus demonstrates His divine authority over life and death, affirming His identity as the Son of God.

The Importance of Faith
Jairus's faith, despite the hopeless situation, is rewarded. Faith in Jesus can lead to miraculous outcomes.

The Role of Secrecy
Jesus instructs secrecy, possibly to avoid misunderstanding His mission or to prevent premature opposition. Sometimes, discretion is necessary in our spiritual walk.

Practical Care
Jesus' instruction to give the girl something to eat highlights the importance of addressing practical needs after spiritual experiences.

Witnessing Miracles
The disciples and family members are witnesses to the miracle, reminding us of the importance of sharing our testimonies of God's work in our lives.

What Does Mark 5:43 Mean
Then Jesus gave strict orders that no one should know about this

“Then Jesus gave strict orders that no one should know about this” (Mark 5:43).

• Jesus often instructed silence after miracles (Mark 1:43–44; 7:36; 8:30; Matthew 9:30). His purpose was not to hide truth but to keep crowds from forcing a premature political agenda on Him (John 6:15).

• The command protected Jairus’s family from public spectacle while they processed the wonder just witnessed, echoing His concern for individuals (Isaiah 42:3).

• Secrecy guarded the unfolding timeline leading to the cross; events had to occur in the Father’s perfect order (John 2:4; 7:30).

• The directive also emphasized faith over fame—He wanted disciples to trust His word, not chase sensational headlines (Luke 8:56, immediate context).

and He told them to give her something to eat

“…and He told them to give her something to eat” (Mark 5:43).

• A simple meal proved she was truly alive, not a vision or hallucination—just as Jesus later ate fish to show His bodily resurrection (Luke 24:41–43; Acts 10:40–41).

• Caring for her physical needs showed that miraculous deliverance does not cancel ordinary responsibilities (James 2:15–16; Proverbs 27:23).

• Involving the parents drew them into the ongoing nurture of their daughter, confirming family stewardship (Deuteronomy 6:6–7).

• The detail underlines Christ’s tenderness: He not only conquers death but attends to breakfast (Psalm 23:1; John 21:12–13).

summary

Mark 5:43 reveals a Savior who manages both the timing of His revelation and the practical care of those He rescues. His strict charge of silence shields the mission from misplaced excitement, while His instruction to feed the girl grounds the miracle in everyday life. Jesus rules over death with absolute authority and, at the same time, remembers lunch.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(43) That something should be given her to eat.--This, again, is common to St. Mark and St. Luke, but is not given by St. Matthew. It suggests the thought that the fuller report must have come from one who had been present in the chamber where the miracle was wrought.
