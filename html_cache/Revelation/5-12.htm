Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
In a loud voice they were saying:
The phrase "in a loud voice" signifies the importance and urgency of the proclamation. In biblical literature, a loud voice often indicates divine revelation or significant announcements (e.g., Revelation 1:10, 7:10). The collective voice suggests unity and agreement among the heavenly beings, emphasizing the universal acknowledgment of the Lamb's worthiness.

Worthy is the Lamb who was slain:
The "Lamb" is a title for Jesus Christ, symbolizing His role as the sacrificial offering for humanity's sins (John 1:29). The imagery of the Lamb is rooted in the Passover lamb of Exodus 12, which was a type of Christ. The phrase "who was slain" refers to Jesus' crucifixion, highlighting the centrality of His sacrificial death in Christian theology. This echoes Isaiah 53:7, where the suffering servant is likened to a lamb led to slaughter.

to receive power and riches and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and blessing:
This sevenfold ascription of praise reflects the completeness and perfection of the Lamb's attributes. Each term represents a facet of Christ's divine authority and character:
Power: Signifies Christ's sovereign authority over all creation (Matthew 28:18).
Riches: Denotes spiritual wealth and the abundance of God's grace (Ephesians 1:7).
Wisdom: Reflects Christ's divine insight and understanding (Colossians 2:3).
Strength: Indicates His might and ability to accomplish God's purposes (Philippians 4:13).
Honor: Represents the respect and reverence due to Him (John 5:23).
Glory: Refers to His divine majesty and the manifestation of God's presence (Hebrews 1:3).
Blessing: Implies the praise and adoration He receives from creation (Ephesians 1:3).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Lamb
Refers to Jesus Christ, symbolizing His sacrificial death and victory over sin. The imagery of the Lamb is central to the book of Revelation, representing purity, sacrifice, and redemption.

2. Heavenly Hosts
The multitude of angels and heavenly beings who are proclaiming the worthiness of the Lamb. This scene takes place in the heavenly throne room, a place of divine authority and worship.

3. The Throne Room
The setting of this vision, where God’s sovereignty and the Lamb’s worthiness are acknowledged by all creation.

4. The Slain Lamb
This event refers to the crucifixion of Jesus, highlighting His role as the sacrificial Lamb who takes away the sins of the world.

5. The Sevenfold Praise
The attributes listed (power, riches, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, blessing) are a complete and perfect acknowledgment of the Lamb’s divine authority and worthiness.

What Does Revelation 5:12 Mean
In a loud voice they were saying

The scene thunders with unified, unashamed worship. Heaven does not whisper about Jesus; it proclaims Him (Revelation 5:11; 19:1). Psalm 98:4 calls all creation to “shout for joy,” echoing this heavenly pattern. Corporate praise magnifies the Lamb and reminds believers that worship is a public declaration, not a private hobby.

Worthy is the Lamb

“Worthy” points to intrinsic value, proven by the Lamb’s flawless character and redeeming work (Revelation 5:6; John 1:29). Unlike earthly heroes who earn praise by achievement, Jesus possesses worth eternally and uniquely (1 Peter 1:18-19). All acclaim fits Him perfectly because He alone bridges God and humanity.

who was slain

The adoration centers on the cross. Jesus’ literal, historical death satisfies divine justice (Isaiah 53:5-6). Revelation 13:8 calls Him “the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world,” confirming God’s plan of redemption was never an afterthought. The wounds that purchased salvation forever adorn His glorified body (John 20:27).

to receive power

After resurrection, Jesus declared, “All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me” (Matthew 28:18). Revelation 11:17 shows heaven celebrating that power. Believers can trust His sovereign control over nations, history, and personal trials (Colossians 1:17).

and riches

Christ already owns “the cattle on a thousand hills” (Psalm 50:10) and the wealth of the universe (Haggai 2:8). Yet heaven formally ascribes these riches to Him because He became poor for our sake (2 Corinthians 8:9). Spiritual riches—grace, peace, every heavenly blessing—flow to us through His abundance (Ephesians 1:3).

and wisdom

All the treasures of wisdom and knowledge reside in Christ (Colossians 2:3). 1 Corinthians 1:24 calls Him “the wisdom of God.” By acknowledging His wisdom, heaven confirms that every divine purpose culminates in the Lamb. Followers seek guidance from One who never miscalculates.

and strength

Psalm 24:8 names Him “the LORD strong and mighty.” Revelation 12:10 credits the Lamb with the final overthrow of evil. His strength secures victory over sin, death, and Satan, empowering believers for daily obedience (Ephesians 6:10).

and honor

Honor speaks of esteem shown publicly (John 5:23). Philippians 2:9-11 displays universal honor when every knee bows to Jesus. Today, lives marked by humility, integrity, and confession of Christ extend that honor on earth.

and glory

Glory is divine radiance revealed (John 17:5). In the New Jerusalem, “the glory of God gives it light, and the Lamb is its lamp” (Revelation 21:23). Giving Jesus glory means reflecting His character and recognizing His supreme beauty in worship and witness.

and blessing

To “bless” the Lamb is to speak well of Him continually (Psalm 103:1; Revelation 7:12). This final note in the sevenfold doxology crowns the list, inviting unending praise from every creature.

summary

Revelation 5:12 paints a cosmic chorus exalting Jesus for who He is and what He has done. The Lamb’s sacrificial death secures sevenfold fullness—power, riches, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, and blessing—rightly returned to Him. Heaven’s worship models the believer’s calling: loudly, joyfully, and comprehensively proclaim the worth of the slain yet victorious Lamb.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) Saying with a loud voice . . .--The second chorus: the chorus of angels--

"Worthy is the Lamb,

That hath been slain,

To receive the power.

And riches, and wisdom, and might,

And honour, and glory, and blessing."

The doxology is seven-fold. We have noticed (Revelation 1:6) the increasing strength of the doxologies in which the redeemed take part. This, though a sevenfold one, does not interrupt that advance of praise; for in this chorus the redeemed do not take part. The definite article is prefixed to the word "power" only; in the doxologies of Revelation 4:11; Revelation 7:12 it stands before each word. This has led some to view the single article as prefixed to all that follows, and to regard all the words as though they formed one word. May it not, however, be used to give emphasis to the "power"? None, above or below, was "able" (same word as "power" here) to open the book (Revelation 5:3); but the Lamb has conquered to open it, and the chorus proclaims the Lamb worthy of that power. Some have thought that the seven terms of the doxology refer to the seven seals which the Lamb is about to open. This seems strained. The notion of completeness is common to this seven-fold blessing and the seven seals; this is the only connection between them. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - Saying with a loud voice; a great voice (Revised Version); λέγοντες, "saying," is irregular construction, and to be referred to angels as being a nominative understood. Worthy is the Lamb that was slain; that hath been slain (Revised Version). Again, as in ver. 9, the worshippers give the reason for considering Christ worthy to receive their adoration. It is because he had been slain and thus redeemed the world. To receive power, and riches, and wisdom, and strength, and honour, and glory, and blessing. The sevenfold nature of the adoration attributed to the Lamb is probably indicative of its complete and perfect nature. (On the meaning of λαβεῖν, "to receive," to take as a right what is offered, see Thayer-Grimm.) Power (δύναμις) is the ability to perform which is inherent in one's nature. Strength (ἰσχύς) is the attribute by which that power is put into operation; it frequently denotes physical strength. Riches (cf. John 1:16, "And of his fulness have all we received;" also Ephesians 3:8, "The unsearchable riches of Christ;" also James 1:17, "Every good gift and every perfect gilt is from above, and cometh down from the Father of lights;" also Acts 17:25, "He giveth to all life, and breath, and all things "). The whole sevenfold ascription is spoken as one, only one article being prefixed. In this respect it differs from Revelation 4:11 and Revelation 7:12, where we have "the glory" and "the honour," etc. (see on Revelation 4:11).
