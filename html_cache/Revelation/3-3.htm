Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Remember, then, what you have received and heard.
This phrase calls the church to recall the teachings and traditions of the apostles, emphasizing the importance of holding onto the foundational truths of the Christian faith. The early church received the gospel message through oral tradition and letters, which were considered authoritative. This call to remembrance is similar to Paul's exhortations in 1 Corinthians 15:1-2, where he reminds believers of the gospel he preached. The emphasis on "received and heard" underscores the importance of both the transmission and reception of divine truth.

Keep it and repent.
The instruction to "keep it" suggests a continuous, active guarding of the faith, akin to the vigilance required of a watchman. Repentance is a recurring theme in the New Testament, signifying a turning away from sin and a return to God. This call to repentance is consistent with the messages to other churches in Revelation, such as Ephesus (Revelation 2:5), where believers are urged to return to their first love. The dual command to keep and repent highlights the necessity of both fidelity to the truth and a lifestyle of ongoing transformation.

If you do not wake up,
The warning to "wake up" implies spiritual lethargy or complacency. In the context of the church in Sardis, this could reflect a community that has become comfortable and self-satisfied, neglecting the vigilance required of them. The metaphor of waking up is used throughout the New Testament, such as in Romans 13:11, where Paul urges believers to awaken from their slumber because salvation is nearer than when they first believed. This call to alertness is a reminder of the imminent return of Christ and the need for readiness.

I will come like a thief,
The imagery of a thief is used to describe the unexpected and sudden nature of Christ's return. This metaphor is also found in Matthew 24:43 and 1 Thessalonians 5:2, where the day of the Lord is likened to a thief in the night. The unpredictability of a thief's arrival serves as a warning to remain vigilant and prepared. In the historical context, Sardis was a city that had been captured twice due to a lack of watchfulness, making this warning particularly poignant for its inhabitants.

and you will not know the hour when I will come upon you.
This phrase emphasizes the uncertainty of the timing of Christ's return, reinforcing the need for constant readiness. The unpredictability of the hour is a common theme in eschatological teachings, as seen in Mark 13:32, where Jesus states that no one knows the day or hour of His return. This uncertainty serves as a motivation for believers to live in a state of perpetual preparedness, maintaining their faith and commitment to Christ in anticipation of His coming.

What Does Revelation 3:3 Mean
Remember what you have received and heard

“Remember, then, what you have received and heard.” (Revelation 3:3a)

• The call is to look back at the apostolic gospel the church originally embraced (Galatians 1:11–12; 1 Corinthians 15:1–3).

• “Received” highlights the gracious gift of truth; “heard” points to the reliable testimony delivered to them (1 Thessalonians 2:13).

• For us, this means intentionally recalling Scripture we already know—just as Peter reminds believers, “I will always remind you of these things” (2 Peter 1:12–15).

Keep it

• “Keep” involves guarding and obeying (John 14:23; 1 Timothy 6:20).

• Truth isn’t meant merely to be stored in memory; it must shape decisions, worship, and witness (James 1:22–25).

• Practical ways to keep:

– Regular reading and meditation on the Word (Psalm 119:11).

– Corporate worship and sound teaching (Acts 2:42).

– Accountability among believers (Hebrews 10:24–25).

And repent

• Repentance is more than sorrow; it is a decisive turn from compromise to wholehearted devotion (Revelation 2:5; Acts 17:30).

• The church at Sardis had a reputation for life but was “dead” (Revelation 3:1). Repentance revives what is dying (Psalm 51:10–12).

• Believers today guard against spiritual formalism by confessing sin quickly and renewing first-love passion (2 Corinthians 7:10–11).

If you do not wake up

• Spiritual lethargy is dangerous; Paul urges, “Awake, O sleeper, rise from the dead” (Ephesians 5:14).

• “Wake up” echoes Jesus’ call in Gethsemane (Mark 14:38). Constant vigilance keeps temptation and doctrinal drift at bay (1 Thessalonians 5:6).

I will come like a thief

• The Lord’s coming is certain yet sudden (Matthew 24:42–44; 2 Peter 3:10).

• For believers who watch, the return is blessed hope (Titus 2:13). For the unprepared, it is unsettling judgment (Revelation 16:15).

You will not know the hour when I will come upon you

• The unknown timing intensifies the call to readiness (Luke 12:40).

• No sign guarantees a last-minute rally; ongoing faithfulness is the safeguard (1 John 2:28).

• Jesus’ personal “I will come” underscores His authority and intimate knowledge of each congregation (Revelation 2:23).

summary

Revelation 3:3 presses believers to:

1. Recall the once-for-all gospel deposit.

2. Guard and obey that truth daily.

3. Turn from complacency to renewed zeal.

4. Stay spiritually alert, knowing Christ may appear without warning.

Holding fast to Scripture and living in continual repentance keep us ready for the Lord who loves us enough to warn and to come.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Remember therefore how (or, after what sort) thou hast received and heard (or, didst hear--the tense changes).--Remembering that the words are addressed primarily to the angel himself, the change of tense may have been designed to point him back to some particular period of his life, such as the time when he was set apart to his ministerial work. The further expectation is to hold fast, or keep--i.e., as an abiding habit. It has been noticed that this counsel is identical with that given to Timothy to "keep the good thing which had been committed to his charge" (2Timothy 1:14; comp. also 2Timothy 2:2). "Repent" is the closing word; combined with the exhortation to hold fast, it reminds us that formal tenacity of truth and a fruitless inactive regret are alike useless. There must be the sorrow for the past, and a sorrow which shows itself in action--a repentance whereby sin is forsaken. (Comp. Revelation 2:5; Revelation 2:21.)

If therefore thou shalt not watch.--Better, If thou shalt not watch (or, have been awake), I will come (omit "on thee") as a thief, and thou shalt not know what hour I will come upon thee. The warning is an echo from the Gospels (Matthew 24:42-43; Luke 12:39-40). The coming of Christ to judge His Church would be in an hour unlooked for. What kind of hour He would so come was' unknown; the sound of His approaching footsteps unheard. Shod with wool, according to the ancient proverb, stealthily as a thief, the Judge would be at the door. Yet they could not plead that they had been in darkness (1Thessalonians 5:4). . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Remember therefore how thou hast received and didst hear (comp. Revelation 2:5). Like the Ephesians, the Sardians are reminded of the better condition from which they have receded. They are of those "who, when they have heard the Word, straightway receive it with joy; and they have no root in themselves, but endure for a while" (Mark 4:16, 17). The "how," as is shown by the verbs "receive" and "hear," refers to the readiness with which they accepted the gospel, rather than to the power with which it was preached to them. The tenses are instructive: the aorist applies to the hearing at some definite period in their history; the perfect implies the permanent result of the act of reception. Keep and repent. Keep what thou didst hear. "Keep" is better than "hold fast," to mark the difference between τηρεῖν (Revelation 1:3; Revelation 2:26; Revelation 3:3, 8, 10, etc.), and κρατεῖν (Revelation 2:1, 13, 14, 15, 25; Revelation 3:11, etc.). Here again the tenses should be noted: the present imperative indicates that they are to continue to keep; the aorist, that they are to repent once for all. We have a similar combination of tenses in" Take these things hence at once; continue to refrain from making my Father's house a house of merchandise" (John 2:16; comp. John 5:8, 11; Acts 12:8; 1 Corinthians 15:34). "Remember" here and in Revelation 2:5 is with equal fitness the present imperative: "continue to remember." I will come as a thief. The "on thee" after "come," though well supported, is probably not genuine. Wherever this figure is used in the New Testament of the coming of Christ, the word used is κλέπτης, "a thief," and not ληστής, a "robber" or "bandit." This shows, what is also plain from the context, that secrecy, not violence, is the point of the similitude (comp. Revelation 16:15; Matthew 24:43; Luke 12:39; 1 Thessalonians 5:2; 2 Peter 3:10). Thou shalt not know what hour; literally, thou shalt in no wise come to know during what kind of an hour. The negative is the strongest form, οὐ μή (Revelation 2:11; Revelation 3:5, 12). The verb is γινώσκειν, which implies acquisition of knowledge (Revelation 2:23, 24; Revelation 3:9). The pronoun is ποῖος (John 10:32; John 12:33; John 18:32; John 21:19; and especially Matthew 24:42; Luke 12:39); and "hour" is in the accusative (John 4:52).
