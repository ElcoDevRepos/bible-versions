The coming of the lawless one
This phrase refers to the Antichrist, a figure prophesied in the New Testament who embodies opposition to Christ and His teachings. The term "lawless one" highlights his rebellion against God's laws. This concept is rooted in Jewish apocalyptic literature and is echoed in other New Testament passages such as 1 John 2:18 and Revelation 13. The Antichrist is seen as a culmination of evil, a personification of sin and rebellion that will arise before the second coming of Christ.

will be accompanied by the working of Satan
This indicates that the Antichrist's power and influence are not of human origin but are empowered by Satan himself. The "working of Satan" suggests a supernatural element, as Satan is often depicted in the Bible as a deceiver and adversary (1 Peter 5:8, Revelation 12:9). This aligns with the broader biblical narrative of spiritual warfare, where Satan opposes God's plans and seeks to lead people astray.

with every kind of power, sign, and false wonder
The Antichrist will perform miraculous acts that mimic the signs and wonders of God, but these are deceptive in nature. The use of "every kind" implies a wide array of supernatural phenomena designed to deceive. This mirrors the warnings given by Jesus in Matthew 24:24, where false prophets and false Christs will perform great signs and wonders to deceive, if possible, even the elect. These false wonders serve to validate the Antichrist's authority in the eyes of the deceived, contrasting with the true miracles performed by Jesus and His apostles.

What Does 2 Thessalonians 2:9 Mean
The coming of the lawless one

“The coming of the lawless one …” (2 Thessalonians 2:9)

• Scripture speaks of a single, literal figure—also called “the man of lawlessness” (2 Thessalonians 2:3)—who will step onto the world stage before Christ returns.

• He is the final Antichrist (1 John 2:18) foretold in earlier prophecy (Daniel 7:25; 11:36; Revelation 13:1–8).

• His “coming” imitates Christ’s Parousia, yet it arises out of rebellion and leads multitudes away from the truth (2 Thessalonians 2:3–4).

• The phrase underscores that his appearance is scheduled on God’s prophetic calendar; history is not spiraling out of control.

Will be accompanied by the working of Satan

“… will be accompanied by the working of Satan …”

• The lawless one operates under direct satanic empowerment, just as “the dragon gave the beast his power” (Revelation 13:2).

• This connection echoes Satan’s earlier attempts to counterfeit God’s work—tempting Jesus with worldly kingdoms (Luke 4:6) and masquerading as an angel of light (2 Corinthians 11:14).

• While Satan supplies the power, God’s sovereignty remains unshaken; even in Job 1:12 the adversary can act only within divine limits.

• Knowing the source of this influence alerts believers to test every spirit (1 John 4:1) and cling to the true gospel.

With every kind of power, sign, and false wonder

“… with every kind of power, sign, and false wonder.”

• “Power” points to real supernatural ability—miracles that impress the senses (Exodus 7:11).

• “Signs” are attention-grabbing acts meant to validate the impostor’s authority (Deuteronomy 13:1–3).

• “False wonders” describe spectacular deceptions that steer hearts away from Christ (Matthew 24:24; Revelation 13:13–14).

• The trio shows a full-court press of counterfeit revelation:

– Physical phenomena that look irresistible.

– Spiritual experiences that feel authentic.

– Intellectual arguments that seem convincing.

• Those who “refused to love the truth” (2 Thessalonians 2:10) will swallow these lies, confirming the justice of God’s coming judgment.

summary

2 Thessalonians 2:9 teaches that a real Antichrist will emerge, empowered by Satan and backed by dazzling yet deceptive miracles. His arrival imitates Christ’s second coming but serves the agenda of darkness. Because God has forewarned His people, believers can recognize the counterfeit, stand firm in truth, and await the genuine glory of Jesus’ return.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) Even him, whose coming.--The "even him" does not stand in the Greek; and "whose" might, again, be rendered by for his own, or perhaps "though his own." The purpose of the verses following is not merely to describe Antichrist more fully, but to compare word for word his coming with that which will annihilate him. Again is used of Antichrist a peculiar word consecrated to the Christ: "coming" (literally presence), being the word used in 2Thessalonians 2:8, as well as 2Thessalonians 2:1, and often. In spite of the sham being well got up, it will be seen to be a sham.

Is . . . with all power.--"Is:" St. Paul sees the future as present. The predicate is not "after the working," but "in all power," &c. The advent of Antichrist will be in (i.e., surrounded with, accompanied by) all kinds of miracles, "according to the working of Satan:" i.e., not only wrought by Satan, but up to the full capacity of Satan to work them. The word "lying" (literally, of falsehood) should go with all three names, "all counterfeit power and signs and wonders." The three words are piled up to heighten the terror of the description; if you press them they mean that there will be a display of power, to attest Antichrist's doctrine (signs), and to keep men spellbound in admiration of him (wonders). Antichrist, like Christ (1Timothy 6:15), has one to support him--Satan instead of God; he, like Christ (Luke 21:25), will have his miracles--but miracles of trickery, not of truth.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - The apostle resumes his description of the man of sin. Even him; not in the original, but necessary for the sense. Whose coming. The use of the same term, parousia, employed to denote the coming of Christ exhibits the counterpart of the man of sin. Is after the working - according to the energy - of Satan. Satan is the agent who works in the man of sin; he being the organ or instrument of Satan. With all power and signs and lying wonders. The adjective "lying" ought to be rendered as a substantive, and applied to all three: "With all powers and signs and wonders of falsehood;" whose origin, nature, and purpose is falsehood. Here, also, the counterpart to Christ is manifest; for the same terms - "powers," "signs," and "wonders," are employed to denote his miracles (Acts 2:22; Hebrews 2:4). The miracles of Christ were miracles of truth; the miracles of the man of sin would be miracles of falsehood. There does not seem to be any essential difference between powers, signs, and wonders; but the words are employed as a mere rhetorical enumeration. It is not to be supposed that the man of sin will be enabled to perform real miracles; they are the wonders of falsehood; but still by them his followers will be deceived (comp. Matthew 24:24).
