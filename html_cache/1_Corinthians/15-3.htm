For what I received I passed on to you as of first importance:
This phrase highlights the apostolic tradition and the transmission of core Christian beliefs. Paul emphasizes that the message he is delivering is not of his own invention but something he received, likely through revelation and instruction from other apostles (Galatians 1:11-12). The phrase "of first importance" underscores the foundational nature of the gospel message. In the early church, oral tradition was crucial for preserving teachings before the New Testament was fully written and compiled. This reflects the Jewish tradition of passing down teachings through generations, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:6-7.

that Christ died for our sins
This statement is central to Christian doctrine, affirming the sacrificial death of Jesus as atonement for humanity's sins. The concept of substitutionary atonement is rooted in the Old Testament sacrificial system, particularly the sin offerings described in Leviticus. The death of Christ fulfills the prophecy of the Suffering Servant in Isaiah 53, who bears the iniquities of many. The phrase also connects to the Passover lamb (Exodus 12), a type of Christ, symbolizing deliverance from sin and death.

according to the Scriptures,
This phrase asserts that Christ's death was in fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies and not a random event. Scriptures such as Psalm 22 and Isaiah 53 provide detailed descriptions of the suffering and purpose of the Messiah's death. The phrase indicates the continuity and consistency of God's redemptive plan throughout the Bible. It also reflects the early Christian practice of interpreting Jesus' life and mission through the lens of Hebrew Scriptures, affirming the divine inspiration and authority of the biblical text.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of 1 Corinthians, Paul is writing to the church in Corinth to address various issues and to affirm the core truths of the Christian faith.

2. Christ (Jesus Christ)
Central to this verse, Jesus is the Messiah who died for the sins of humanity, fulfilling Old Testament prophecies.

3. Corinth
A major city in ancient Greece, known for its diverse population and moral challenges, where Paul established a church during his missionary journeys.

4. The Crucifixion
The event of Jesus Christ's death on the cross, which is the focal point of the gospel message Paul is emphasizing.

5. The Scriptures
Refers to the Old Testament prophecies and writings that foretold the coming of the Messiah and His sacrificial death.
Teaching Points
The Primacy of the Gospel
The message of Christ's death for our sins is of "first importance." It is the foundation of Christian faith and should be central in our teaching and evangelism.

The Fulfillment of Prophecy
Christ's death was "according to the Scriptures," affirming the reliability and divine inspiration of the Bible. Believers can trust in God's promises and His plan of salvation.

The Necessity of Atonement
The phrase "for our sins" highlights the necessity of atonement. Recognizing our sinfulness and need for a Savior is crucial for genuine faith.

Receiving and Delivering the Gospel
Paul received this message and faithfully delivered it. Christians are called to both receive the gospel personally and share it with others.

Living in Light of the Gospel
Understanding the significance of Christ's sacrifice should lead to a life of gratitude, obedience, and transformation.

What Does 1 Corinthians 15:3 Mean
For what I received

Paul stresses that the gospel he is about to rehearse did not originate with him. It was “received”—handed down by the risen Christ (Galatians 1:11-12). As he tells another church, “For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you” (1 Corinthians 11:23), the same pattern appears. Because the gospel is revealed, not invented, it carries divine authority.

• Source: Jesus Himself, not human opinion.

• Implication: tampering with it means tampering with God’s truth (Galatians 1:8-9).

I passed on to you

Receiving leads to transmitting. Paul faithfully relayed the very message that saved him. Acts 18:1-11 shows him spending eighteen months in Corinth doing just that. He models the principle of 2 Timothy 2:2—what we receive we entrust to others who will pass it on again.

• The gospel is a relay baton, not a private possession.

• The unbroken chain from Christ to Paul to the Corinthians assures us we hold the original gospel.

As of first importance

Among all doctrines Paul taught, this truth sits at the top. “For I resolved to know nothing while I was with you except Jesus Christ and Him crucified” (1 Corinthians 2:2).

• Without the death and resurrection of Jesus, no other Christian teaching matters (Romans 10:9).

• This core message unites believers across cultures and centuries.

That Christ died

The gospel is historical, not mythical. “And Jesus cried out again with a loud voice and yielded up His spirit” (Matthew 27:50). Eyewitnesses saw His lifeless body taken from the cross (John 19:33-35).

• His death satisfied divine justice (Romans 3:25-26).

• It fulfilled His own predictions (Mark 8:31).

• It displayed God’s love (Romans 5:8).

For our sins

He did not die merely as a martyr but as a substitute for people. Isaiah foretold, “He was pierced for our transgressions” (Isaiah 53:5-6). Peter echoes, “Christ also suffered for sins once for all, the righteous for the unrighteous” (1 Peter 3:18).

• Our sins created a debt we could never pay (Romans 6:23).

• His death removes wrath and reconciles us to God (2 Corinthians 5:21; 1 John 2:2).

• The phrase “for our sins” makes the gospel intensely personal.

According to the Scriptures

Nothing in the gospel is accidental; every detail fulfills the written Word. Jesus said, “Everything written about Me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled” (Luke 24:44).

• Psalm 22 pictures crucifixion centuries before it existed.

• Isaiah 53 lays out the suffering Servant bearing iniquity.

• Daniel 9:26 foretells Messiah being cut off.

By anchoring the gospel in Scripture, Paul shows God’s plan is consistent from Genesis to Revelation.

summary

1 Corinthians 15:3 distills the heart of the gospel: a divinely delivered message, faithfully transmitted, occupying first place, declaring that the real, historical Christ died a substitutionary, Scripture-foretold death for our sins. Every hope we have rests on this bedrock truth, and every believer is called to receive it, rejoice in it, and pass it on unchanged.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) For I delivered . . .--Here follows the explanation and illustration of what he meant, in 1Corinthians 15:2, by "with what word I preached the gospel." We see here what the subject of apostolic teaching was--not indeed all the gospel that the Apostle taught, but what he considered of the first importance, and therefore put in the forefront of his teaching--viz., the historical fact of Christ's death for our sins, His burial, His resurrection. This was the first Creed of Christendom.

For our sins.--Not only because of, but in behalf of our sins, in order to take them away (Galatians 1:4; 1Peter 2:24; 1John 3:5). The fact of the Atonement was not something evolved by the Apostle's own consciousness, but a fact revealed to him by Christ. (See 1Corinthians 11:23, and Note there.) . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - First of all; literally, among the first things; but this idiom means "first of all." It does not occur elsewhere in the New Testament, but is found in Genesis 33:2; 2 Samuel 5:8 (LXX.). This testimony to the Resurrection is very remarkable, because:

1. It is the completest summary.

2. It refers to some incidents which are not mentioned in the Gospels.

3. It declares that the death and resurrection of Christ were a subject of ancient prophecy. . . .
