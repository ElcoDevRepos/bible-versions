So about eating food sacrificed to idols:
In the Greco-Roman world, it was common for meat sold in markets to have been previously offered in sacrifice to idols. This practice posed a dilemma for early Christians, particularly in Corinth, a city known for its many temples and diverse religious practices. The issue was whether consuming such meat constituted participation in idol worship. The Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:29) had advised Gentile believers to abstain from food sacrificed to idols, yet Paul addresses the matter with a nuanced understanding of Christian liberty and conscience.

We know that an idol is nothing at all in the world:
Paul emphasizes the futility and powerlessness of idols, aligning with Old Testament teachings such as Isaiah 44:9-20, which mock the creation and worship of idols. This statement reflects the monotheistic belief that idols, being man-made objects, have no real existence or power. The early church had to navigate a world filled with polytheistic practices, and this assertion reinforces the Christian understanding that idols hold no spiritual significance.

and that there is no God but one:
This declaration is rooted in the Shema, a central tenet of Jewish faith found in Deuteronomy 6:4: "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one." It underscores the monotheistic belief that distinguishes Christianity from the surrounding pagan cultures. This affirmation of one God is foundational to Christian doctrine and is echoed in other New Testament passages, such as Ephesians 4:6 and James 2:19. It also points to the exclusivity of worship due to the one true God, rejecting the legitimacy of any other deities.

Understanding Idolatry
Idols have no real existence or power; they are mere objects. This understanding should shape our perspective on cultural practices that conflict with Christian beliefs.

Affirming the Shema, Christians are reminded of the foundational truth that there is only one God, which should guide our worship and allegiance.

While knowledge of the truth frees us from fear of idols, we must consider the conscience of others, especially those who are weaker in faith.

What Does 1 Corinthians 8:4 Mean
So about eating food sacrificed to idols

Paul is responding to a practical issue in Corinth, where meat from pagan temples often found its way to public markets (Acts 15:29; 1 Corinthians 10:25). Because Scripture is clear that “everything God created is good” (1 Timothy 4:4), food in itself is morally neutral. Yet:

• Believers must weigh how their choices affect the conscience of others (1 Corinthians 8:9–13).

• Freedom is never an excuse to cause a weaker brother to stumble.

• The gospel calls us to love-driven self-restraint, modeling Christ’s own self-sacrifice (Romans 14:15).

We know that an idol is nothing at all in the world

Quoting the first half of 1 Corinthians 8:4, Paul reminds us that carved images “are nothing.” They possess no real power or life (Psalm 135:15-18; Isaiah 44:9-20). Therefore:

• Idols cannot contaminate food; only the heart’s allegiance matters (Mark 7:18-19).

• Spiritual danger lies not in the meat itself but in participating in worship that belongs to demons (1 Corinthians 10:20-21).

• Knowing idols are empty frees us from superstition, yet knowledge must be tempered by love (1 Corinthians 8:1).

and that there is no God but one

The second half of the verse anchors the argument in God’s exclusive sovereignty: “Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is One” (Deuteronomy 6:4). Isaiah echoes, “I am the LORD, and there is no other” (Isaiah 45:5). Paul reaffirms this in Ephesians 4:6 and 1 Timothy 2:5. Consequently:

• Only the one true God deserves worship; any rival claim is false.

• Because God alone is real, believers can eat with a clear conscience—provided they are not endorsing idolatry in the process.

• Monotheism unites the diverse body of Christ, Jews and Gentiles alike, around a single allegiance (Romans 3:30).

summary

1 Corinthians 8:4 teaches that food offered to idols has no intrinsic spiritual defilement because idols are powerless and the living God is the only true deity. This truth grants believers liberty, yet the greater call is to exercise that liberty in love, always seeking the spiritual good of others.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) As concerning therefore the eating of those things.--See 1Corinthians 8:1. The subject resumed after the parenthesis. We have, perhaps, in this repetition of the words a characteristic of a letter written by another from the author's dictation, as was the case with this and other epistles.

An idol is nothing in the world.--It is nothing in itself but a piece of wood or metal, and it really represents nothing, for we know that there is "no God but one." The word "other" was inserted in later MSS., probably from a recollection of the words of the first commandment.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - We know that an idol is nothing in the world. After his brief but pregnant digression on the nature of true knowledge, he returns to these questions, and probably once more quotes their own words. They had given this reason for open and public indifference with respect to meat offered to idols. With respect to idols, three views were possible to Christians: either

(1) that they were "demons" - the spirits of deified dead men; or

(2) that they were evil spirits - a favorite view among the Jews (via 10:20; Deuteronomy 32:17; 2 Chronicles 11:15; Psalm 106:37; Revelation 9:20); or

(3) that they were merely (lead images corresponding to nothing at all (Isaiah 44 etc.). That there is none other God but one. This belief is the signature of Judaism, according to their daily and oft repeated shema (Deuteronomy 6:4, etc.).
