Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Love is patient
Patience, or long-suffering, is a key attribute of love, reflecting God's own nature as seen throughout Scripture. In the Old Testament, God's patience is evident in His dealings with Israel, despite their repeated disobedience (Exodus 34:6). The Greek word used here, "makrothumei," implies a willingness to endure difficult circumstances and people without retaliation. This patience is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22) and is essential for maintaining unity within the body of Christ (Ephesians 4:2). It is a call to emulate Christ, who demonstrated ultimate patience in His earthly ministry and sacrificial death.

Love is kind
Kindness is an active expression of love, characterized by benevolence and a desire to do good to others. The Greek term "chresteuetai" suggests a gentle and gracious disposition. This kindness mirrors God's kindness toward humanity, as seen in His provision and grace (Titus 3:4-5). In the cultural context of Corinth, where social status and personal gain were often prioritized, this call to kindness challenged believers to act selflessly. Kindness is also a reflection of Jesus' ministry, where He consistently showed compassion and care for the marginalized and needy.

It does not envy
Envy, or jealousy, is a destructive emotion that contrasts sharply with love. The Greek word "zeloi" indicates a desire for what others have, leading to resentment. Envy was a significant issue in the Corinthian church, where divisions and comparisons were prevalent (1 Corinthians 3:3). Love, however, seeks the best for others and rejoices in their successes. This aligns with the biblical principle of contentment and gratitude (Philippians 4:11-12). The absence of envy in love reflects the selfless nature of Christ, who humbled Himself for the sake of others (Philippians 2:3-8).

It does not boast
Boasting, or vainglory, is incompatible with love, which is humble and self-effacing. The Greek term "perpereuetai" suggests an arrogant display of one's achievements or qualities. In Corinth, boasting was common, as individuals sought to elevate themselves above others (1 Corinthians 4:7). Love, however, focuses on building others up rather than seeking personal recognition. This humility is exemplified in Jesus, who, despite His divine nature, did not seek His own glory but the glory of the Father (John 8:50).

It is not proud
Pride, or arrogance, is the antithesis of love, which is characterized by humility and selflessness. The Greek word "phusioutai" conveys a sense of being puffed up or inflated with self-importance. Pride was a significant issue in Corinth, contributing to divisions and conflicts within the church (1 Corinthians 5:2). Love, in contrast, esteems others above oneself and seeks their welfare (Philippians 2:3-4). This humility is a hallmark of Christ's character, who, though equal with God, took on the form of a servant (Philippians 2:6-7).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Apostle Paul
The author of 1 Corinthians, Paul was an apostle who played a significant role in the spread of Christianity in the first century. He wrote this letter to the church in Corinth to address various issues and to teach them about the nature of true Christian love.

2. Corinth
A major city in ancient Greece, known for its wealth and moral challenges. The church in Corinth faced many issues, including divisions and misunderstandings about spiritual gifts, which Paul addresses in this letter.

3. The Corinthian Church
The recipients of Paul's letter, this early Christian community struggled with issues of unity, morality, and understanding spiritual gifts, prompting Paul to write about the supremacy of love.
Teaching Points
The Nature of Love
Love is not just an emotion but a deliberate choice to act with patience and kindness. It requires self-control and a focus on the well-being of others.

Avoiding Envy and Boasting
True love does not compare itself to others or seek to elevate itself. It finds contentment in the success and happiness of others.

Humility in Love
Love is not proud. It involves humility and a willingness to put others first, reflecting the character of Christ.

Practical Patience
In our daily interactions, practicing patience means being slow to anger and quick to listen, allowing others the grace to grow and change.

Kindness as an Action
Kindness is love in action. It involves tangible acts of service and compassion towards others, reflecting God's love for us.

What Does 1 Corinthians 13:4 Mean
Love is patient

“Love is patient” (1 Corinthians 13:4a).

• Patience means we give others time to grow, just as God “is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish” (2 Peter 3:9).

• It is the Spirit-produced attitude that waits without resentment (Galatians 5:22).

• Practical picture: bearing with a difficult friend, spouse, child, or church member “with all humility and gentleness, with patience, bearing with one another in love” (Ephesians 4:2).

Bottom line: True love refuses to hurry people toward our agenda; it mirrors the long-suffering character of God.

Love is kind

“Love is … kind” (1 Corinthians 13:4a).

• Kindness is love in action—meeting needs, offering words that build up (Ephesians 4:32).

• God’s own kindness drew us to repentance (Romans 2:4), and we reflect that kindness when we serve, give, and speak gently.

• Titus 3:4 reminds us that God’s kindness appeared in Christ; likewise, our kindness should make Jesus visible today.

In practice, kindness looks like:

– A listening ear when someone is hurting.

– A meal delivered to the sick.

– A gentle reply that diffuses anger (Proverbs 15:1).

Love does not envy

“It does not envy” (1 Corinthians 13:4b).

• Envy resents another’s blessing; love celebrates it.

• Proverbs 14:30 warns that envy “rots the bones,” while James 3:16 links it to “disorder and every evil practice.”

• Instead of envy, love chooses gratitude and rejoices when others are honored (Romans 12:15).

Try this heart check: when a friend succeeds, do I thank God for them or wish it were me? Love picks the first response.

Love does not boast

“It does not boast” (1 Corinthians 13:4b).

• Boasting inflates self; love magnifies God.

• Jeremiah 9:23-24 urges, “Let not the wise man boast of his wisdom … but let him who boasts boast in this: that he understands and knows Me.”

• Paul echoes the same in 2 Corinthians 10:17-18, pointing all credit back to the Lord.

Practical steps:

– Speak of achievements as gifts, not personal glory.

– Redirect praise: “Thank you—God has been kind.”

Love is not proud

“It is not proud” (1 Corinthians 13:4b).

• Pride puts self first; love puts others first (Philippians 2:3-4).

• God “opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble” (James 4:6).

• Proverbs 16:18 warns pride precedes a fall; humility paves the way for God’s blessing.

Daily application: seek opportunities to serve in unseen ways, allowing others to take the spotlight.

summary

Each phrase in 1 Corinthians 13:4 paints a portrait of Christlike love: patient enough to wait, kind enough to act, secure enough to resist envy, humble enough to avoid boasting, and lowly enough to reject pride. By the Spirit’s power we live out this love, reflecting the Savior who perfectly embodies every line.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4) Charity suffereth long.--Better, Love is long-suffering. Here follows a description of love. Descriptions of positive characteristics and negations of evil qualities are now employed by the Apostle in what he would have us believe to be his impossible task of adequately describing true love.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 4-7. - The attributes of love. Verse 4. Suffereth long, and is kind. Passively it endures; actively it does good. It endures evils; it confers blessings. Envieth not. Its negative characteristics are part of its positive perfection. Envy - "one shape of many names" - includes malice, grudge, jealousy, pique, an evil eye, etc., with all their base and numerous manifestations. Vaunteth not itself. The meaning would probably be most nearly expressed by the colloquialism, does not show off. It does not, for instance, "do its alms before men to be seen of them" (Matthew 6:1). The Latin perperus, which is from the same root as this word, means "a braggart," or "swaggerer." Cicero, speaking of a grand oratorical display of his own before Pompey, says to Atticus, "Good heavens! how I showed myself off (ἐνεπερπερευσάμην) before my new hearer, Pompeius!" ('Ad. Art.,' 1:14). Is not puffed up. Has no purse proud or inflated arrogance." Love, therefore, is free from the characteristic vice of the Corinthian Church (1 Corinthians 4:6, 18, 19; 1 Corinthians 5:2; 1 Corinthians 8:1).
