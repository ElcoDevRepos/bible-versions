Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And last of all
This phrase indicates the finality of the appearances of the risen Christ. Paul emphasizes that he was the last to witness the resurrected Jesus, underscoring the completion of the post-resurrection appearances. This is significant in establishing the credibility and authority of Paul's apostleship, as he was not among the original disciples. The phrase also highlights the unique nature of Paul's encounter with Christ, which occurred after the ascension.

He appeared to me also
Paul's encounter with the risen Christ on the road to Damascus (Acts 9:3-6) is a pivotal moment in his life and ministry. This appearance is distinct from the others because it was a vision rather than a physical meeting. It underscores the transformative power of Christ's resurrection, as Paul, once a persecutor of Christians, becomes a devoted apostle. This appearance validates Paul's message and mission, aligning him with the other apostles who witnessed the resurrected Jesus.

as to one of untimely birth
The term "untimely birth" (Greek: ektroma) can be understood as an abnormal or premature birth. Paul uses this metaphor to express his sense of unworthiness and the abrupt, unexpected nature of his conversion. It reflects his humility and acknowledgment of God's grace in calling him to be an apostle. This imagery may also connect to the prophetic theme of God's unexpected choices, as seen in the calling of unlikely individuals throughout Scripture, such as David or Moses. Paul's use of this term highlights the miraculous and sovereign work of God in his life, transforming him from a persecutor to a proclaimer of the Gospel.

Teaching Points
The Grace of God in Calling
Paul's reference to himself as "one of untimely birth" highlights the grace of God in calling him despite his past as a persecutor of the church. This underscores that God's grace can reach anyone, regardless of their past.

The Importance of the Resurrection
The appearance of the risen Christ to Paul is a testament to the reality and power of the resurrection. It serves as a foundation for Christian faith and hope.

Personal Encounter with Christ
Paul's transformation began with a personal encounter with Jesus. This emphasizes the necessity of a personal relationship with Christ for true spiritual transformation.

Humility in Service
Despite his significant role in the early church, Paul acknowledges his unworthiness, teaching us the importance of humility in our service to God.

The Power of Testimony
Paul's testimony of encountering the risen Christ is a powerful witness to the truth of the Gospel. Our personal testimonies can also be effective tools for sharing our faith.

What Does 1 Corinthians 15:8 Mean
And last of all

- Paul marks the chronological close of the resurrection appearances that began with Peter (1 Corinthians 15:5) and continued through “more than five hundred brothers at once” (15:6).

- By adding “last,” he underscores how every appearance before his had already established the factual reality of Christ’s bodily resurrection (Acts 1:3; Luke 24:39).

- His statement also signals the completion of the apostolic eyewitness list, reinforcing that no further authoritative resurrection appearances were needed (Revelation 1:17–18 shows Christ alive forever; Hebrews 1:1–2 highlights final revelation in the Son).

He appeared

- Paul insists on a literal, visible encounter: “He appeared” means Christ showed Himself in objective reality, not a mere vision (Acts 9:17; Acts 22:14).

- This continues the pattern of tangible post-resurrection meetings where Jesus ate, spoke, and was touched (Luke 24:41–43; John 20:27).

- The wording shores up the gospel’s foundation: “If Christ has not been raised, our preaching is worthless and so is your faith” (1 Corinthians 15:14).

to me also

- The risen Lord’s appearance to Paul personalizes grace: the persecutor becomes a witness (Acts 9:3–6; 1 Timothy 1:12–13).

- Paul’s authority as an apostle rests on this encounter received “not from man” but “by revelation of Jesus Christ” (Galatians 1:12, 15–16).

- The inclusion of “also” links his testimony with the earlier eyewitnesses, creating a unified chorus affirming one resurrection and one gospel message (1 Corinthians 15:11).

as to one of untimely birth

- Paul likens himself to an infant born prematurely—sudden, unexpected, seemingly ill-prepared (1 Corinthians 15:9; Ephesians 3:8).

- The picture highlights:

• His earlier hostility toward the church (Acts 8:3)

• His sense of unworthiness compared with those who walked with Jesus before the cross (Acts 26:9–11)

• The miraculous mercy that transformed him “by the grace of God” (1 Corinthians 15:10; 1 Timothy 1:15–16).

- God’s timing overrules human expectations: even an “untimely” apostle becomes a chosen instrument to carry Christ’s name “before Gentiles, kings, and the children of Israel” (Acts 9:15).

summary

Paul’s words in 1 Corinthians 15:8 complete the roster of resurrection eyewitnesses, affirming a real, historical appearance of the risen Jesus to Paul himself. Though he views his calling as sudden and undeserved, the same risen Lord who met earlier disciples met Paul, proving the universality of grace and sealing the authoritative testimony that “Christ has indeed been raised from the dead” (1 Corinthians 15:20).

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) Was seen of me also, as of one born out of due time.--Better, Last of all, as to an untimely born one he appeared also to me. The Apostle here distinctly states that he saw the Lord at the time of his conversion as really as St. Peter and others had seen him, though with touching pathos and strongly marked emphasis he adds that it was not at the same time as the "firstborn" had seen Him, but only as an "untimely born" one.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - He was seen of me also. The reference undoubtedly is to the vision on the road to Damascus (Acts 9:5; Acts 22:14; Acts 26:16). As of one born out of due time; literally, as to the abortive born. The word means "the untimely fruit of a woman," a child born out of the due time or natural course; and hence "diminutive" and "weakly." The Greek ektroma is represented by the Latin abortivus. St. Paul, when he remembered the lateness of his conversion, and his past persecution of the saints, regards himself as standing in this relation to the twelve.
