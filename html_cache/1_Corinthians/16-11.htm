Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
No one, then, should treat him with contempt.
This phrase refers to Timothy, a young disciple and companion of Paul. In the cultural context of the early church, age and experience were often respected, and Timothy's youth might have led some to dismiss his authority or message. Paul emphasizes the importance of respecting Timothy, highlighting the biblical principle of honoring those who labor in the Lord's work (1 Timothy 4:12). This aligns with the broader scriptural theme of God using the young and seemingly insignificant to accomplish His purposes, as seen with David (1 Samuel 16) and Jeremiah (Jeremiah 1:6-7).

Send him on his way in peace
Paul instructs the Corinthians to ensure Timothy's safe and peaceful departure. This reflects the early Christian practice of supporting and encouraging fellow believers, especially those in ministry. The phrase underscores the importance of unity and cooperation within the body of Christ, as seen in other passages like Romans 12:18 and Hebrews 12:14. The peace mentioned here is not just the absence of conflict but the presence of God's blessing and favor, a common theme in Pauline letters.

so that he can return to me
Paul expresses his desire for Timothy to return to him, indicating the close relationship and partnership they shared in ministry. This reflects the early church's emphasis on mentorship and discipleship, where more experienced leaders like Paul would guide and support younger ones like Timothy. The return also suggests the itinerant nature of early Christian ministry, where leaders traveled extensively to establish and strengthen churches, as seen in Acts 16:1-5.

for I am expecting him along with the brothers.
Paul anticipates Timothy's return with other believers, highlighting the communal aspect of early Christian missions. The "brothers" likely refers to fellow workers in the gospel, emphasizing the collaborative nature of Paul's ministry. This phrase connects to the broader New Testament theme of the church as a family, united in purpose and mission (Ephesians 2:19-22). It also reflects the practice of sending and receiving emissaries within the early church, as seen in Acts 15:22-33, to maintain doctrinal unity and mutual support.

Teaching Points
Respect for Fellow Believers
The instruction to not treat Timothy with contempt highlights the importance of respecting all members of the body of Christ, regardless of age or status.

Support for Ministry Workers
Sending Timothy on his way in peace emphasizes the need to support and encourage those who are serving in ministry roles.

Unity in the Church
The expectation of Timothy's return with the brothers underscores the value of unity and cooperation among believers in fulfilling the mission of the church.

Encouragement in Leadership
Timothy's example as a young leader serves as an encouragement for emerging leaders to step confidently into their roles, trusting in God's calling and the support of the church community.

What Does 1 Corinthians 16:11 Mean
No one, then, should treat him with contempt

• Paul is speaking of Timothy (v.10), a young but faithful minister.

• The command is clear: not a single member of the Corinthian church is to belittle or look down on him.

• Respect for God-given workers safeguards unity (1 Thessalonians 5:12-13; Hebrews 13:17).

• Timothy had already faced possible scorn for his youth (1 Timothy 4:12). Paul anticipates this and heads it off.

• When we honor those who labor in the Word, we honor the Lord who sent them (Matthew 10:40).

Send him on his way in peace

• The church is to provide a warm, encouraging farewell—free from conflict, full of practical support.

• “In peace” recalls the early church’s practice of commending travelers to God’s grace (Acts 15:33; Acts 20:38).

• Tangible help—food, lodging, funds, and prayers—equips servants for the next assignment (Titus 3:13; 3 John 6-8).

so that he can return to me

• Paul counts on the Corinthians’ cooperation so Timothy can quickly reunite with him.

• Partnership in ministry operates on trust and mutual responsibility (Philippians 2:19-23).

• When a local church assists a worker, it advances not just that church’s mission but the wider mission of Christ (2 Corinthians 1:1).

for I am expecting him along with the brothers

• Paul’s plans include a team—Timothy and unnamed “brothers” who share the work (Acts 18:5; 2 Corinthians 8:23).

• This expectation shows an organized, interconnected church life where believers rely on one another (1 Corinthians 16:12,17).

• By welcoming Timothy, Corinth joins a broader gospel network, displaying the unity Jesus prayed for (John 17:20-23).

summary

Paul commands the Corinthians to esteem Timothy, treat him kindly, supply what he needs, and speed him back to Paul, because faithful workers belong to the whole body of Christ. Honoring and assisting them honors the Lord and strengthens the church’s shared mission.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) For I look for him with the brethren.--Timothy and Erastus (Acts 19:22) had been sent through Macedonia to Corinth some time before this Epistle was written, but when they had been despatched the full knowledge of the state of affairs at Corinth had not reached St. Paul. Now that he knows how very bad is the condition of the Corinthian Church, and what need it has of vigorous treatment, he sends not only his Epistle, but with it Titus and two other brethren. (See 2Corinthians 8:18; 2Corinthians 8:22-23.) In energy and firmness of character Titus was a striking contrast to Timothy, while he equally shared the spirit and confidence of St. Paul. (See Introduction, and 2 Corinthians 7, 8) He therefore was not only a bearer of this Epistle, but one fully competent and willing to deal energetically with the recalcitrant spirit of some sections of the Corinthian Church. The Apostle here expresses the hope that Timothy may join Titus and his party when they take their departure from Corinth.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - Let no man therefore despise him. His youth and modesty seemed to invite a contempt which was only too consonant with the character of the Corinthians. I look for him with the brethren. There was a reason for adding this. The Corinthians would see that any unkindness or contempt shown towards Timothy would at once be reported to St. Paul. Who "the brethren" are is not mentioned, for in Acts 19:22 we are only told that Timothy was accompanied by Erastus. Perhaps St. Paul means with the brethren who conveyed this letter (see ver. 12), and who, as he supposed, would meet with Timothy at Corinth, or fall in with him on their return to meet St. Paul in Macedonia. One of these brethren must have been Titus (2 Corinthians 2:13; 2 Corinthians 7:6, 7), and there were two others.
