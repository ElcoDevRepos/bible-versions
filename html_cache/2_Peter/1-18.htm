And we ourselves heard this voice from heaven
This phrase emphasizes the firsthand experience of the apostles, particularly Peter, James, and John, who were present at the Transfiguration of Jesus. The voice from heaven is identified as the voice of God the Father, affirming Jesus as His beloved Son. This divine affirmation is crucial as it confirms Jesus' identity and mission. The concept of a voice from heaven is consistent with other biblical instances where God communicates directly, such as during Jesus' baptism (Matthew 3:17) and Saul's conversion (Acts 9:3-6). The phrase underscores the reliability of the apostolic witness, as they were not relaying secondhand information but were direct recipients of divine revelation.

when we were with Him on the holy mountain
The "holy mountain" refers to the Mount of Transfiguration, traditionally identified as Mount Tabor, although some scholars suggest Mount Hermon due to its proximity to Caesarea Philippi, where the preceding events took place. The designation of the mountain as "holy" highlights its significance as a place of divine encounter, paralleling other biblical mountains like Mount Sinai, where Moses received the Law (Exodus 19), and Mount Zion, the site of the Temple in Jerusalem. The Transfiguration event itself is a pivotal moment in the Gospels, where Jesus' divine glory is revealed to the apostles, prefiguring His resurrection and second coming. This moment also connects to Old Testament theophanies, where God's presence is manifested in a tangible way, reinforcing the continuity of God's redemptive plan through Jesus Christ.

Teaching Points
Eyewitness Testimony
Peter emphasizes the reliability of his message by recounting his firsthand experience. This underscores the importance of personal testimony in sharing the gospel.

Divine Affirmation
The voice from heaven serves as a divine endorsement of Jesus' identity and mission. Believers can find assurance in the truth of Jesus' divinity and His role as the Savior.

The Importance of Revelation
The Transfiguration was a moment of revelation for the disciples, deepening their understanding of Jesus' nature. Christians are encouraged to seek deeper revelation through prayer and study of the Word.

Faith and Experience
While faith is foundational, experiences like Peter's can strengthen and confirm our beliefs. Believers should remain open to experiencing God's presence in their lives.

The Role of Scripture
Peter's account aligns with the prophetic word, highlighting the harmony between personal experience and Scripture. Christians should test their experiences against the truth of the Bible.

What Does 2 Peter 1:18 Mean
And we ourselves

Peter, James, and John were eyewitnesses—not repeating secondhand stories. Just as Acts 4:20 says, “we cannot stop speaking about what we have seen and heard,” Peter stresses personal experience. This eyewitness testimony grounds our faith in verifiable history (1 John 1:1–3).

• Eyewitnesses confirm truth (John 19:35).

• Their shared memory protects against fabrication (Deuteronomy 19:15).

heard this voice

They physically heard God’s audible declaration at the Transfiguration. Hebrews 12:26 reminds us that when God speaks, creation responds; here, His voice validates Jesus’ identity. The apostles weren’t relying on inner impressions but on an external, objective sound (John 12:28–30).

• God’s voice affirms His Son (Matthew 3:17).

• Faith comes by hearing God’s word (Romans 10:17), and this moment supplied that word directly.

from heaven

The origin of the voice leaves no doubt about its authority. James 1:17 says every good gift is “from above, coming down from the Father of lights.” By noting heaven as the source, Peter underscores divine endorsement, echoing the pattern in Exodus 20:22 where God speaks “from heaven” to His people.

• Heavenly revelation carries absolute weight (Galatians 1:11–12).

• It separates truth from human speculation (Isaiah 55:8–9).

when we were with Him

Peter links the voice to their physical presence with Jesus, emphasizing relationship and proximity (Mark 3:14). They were not distant observers; they lived alongside Him, reinforcing that the gospel rests on real interaction with the incarnate Christ (John 15:27).

• Shared life led to deeper understanding (Luke 24:27, 45).

• Their companionship fulfills God’s desire to dwell with His people (Revelation 21:3).

on the holy mountain

Mount Hermon (or possibly Tabor) became “holy” because God manifested His glory there, much like Sinai in Exodus 19:18. This setting mirrors Old Testament theophanies, bridging God’s past revelations with the climax of revealing His Son (Matthew 17:1–8).

• Mountains often mark covenant moments (Genesis 22:14; 1 Kings 19:8–13).

• God’s presence sanctifies places and people (Isaiah 6:3).

summary

Peter’s brief sentence packs a powerful reminder: the apostles personally heard God’s heavenly voice affirm Jesus while accompanying Him on a mountain made holy by that encounter. Their eyewitness testimony assures us that the gospel rests on historical events, divine revelation, and a Savior uniquely accredited by the Father.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(18) And this voice which came from heaven we heard.--Rather, And this voice we heard borne from heaven: We were ear-witnesses of the voice coming from heaven, as we were eye-witnesses of His majesty. It was no vision, it was no hallucination. We all heard, and we all saw; so that I have the highest authority for what I would now impress upon you. A voice which I myself heard borne from heaven to earth, in the midst of glory which I myself saw, foretelling the glory that is yet to come.

In the holy mount.--It is, perhaps, not even "partly right" to say that the epithet "holy" indicates a view of the event later than that of the Evangelists, and points to a miracle-loving age. Rather, it indicates a view many centuries older than the Evangelists--that wherever God had specially manifested Himself was "holy ground" (Exodus 3:5; Josh. V. 15. Comp. Genesis 28:16-17; Exodus 19:12; Acts 7:33.) The expression would be natural to any Jew speaking of the Transfiguration. (See Introduction, I. c.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 18. - And this voice which came from heaven we heard; rather, and this voice borne from heaven we heard. The pronoun is emphatic; we, the apostles who had that high privilege. They heard the voice when it was borne (ἐνεχθεῖσαν; he repeats for emphasis the remarkable word of verse 17) from heaven, they heard it come from heaven. When we were with him in the holy mount. This description of the Mount of the Transfiguration supposes a knowledge of the history in St. Peter's readers; but it gives no support to the theory of a post-apostolic date. Mount Horeb was "holy ground," because God appeared there to Moses, because it was the scene of the giving of the Law. Mount Zion was a holy hill, because God had chosen it to be a habitation for himself; the Mount of the Transfiguration was holy, because there God the Son manifested forth his glory. God hallows every place which he pleases to make the scene of his revealed presence. This whole passage shows the deep and lasting impression which the Transfiguration made on those who were privileged to witness it (comp. John 1:14).
