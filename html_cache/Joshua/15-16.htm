Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And Caleb said,
Caleb, a prominent figure among the Israelites, was one of the two faithful spies (along with Joshua) who trusted in God's promise to give the land of Canaan to Israel. His leadership and faith are highlighted throughout the conquest narratives. Caleb's words here reflect his role as a leader and a man of action, willing to incentivize the conquest of the land.

“To the man who strikes down Kiriath-sepher and captures it,
Kiriath-sepher, also known as Debir, was a Canaanite city located in the hill country of Judah. The challenge to capture it indicates its strategic importance and the resistance faced by the Israelites. This reflects the ongoing struggle to fully possess the Promised Land, as God had commanded. The act of striking down and capturing the city signifies a complete victory over the Canaanite inhabitants, aligning with the divine mandate to drive out the nations from the land.

I will give my daughter Acsah in marriage.”
Offering a daughter in marriage as a reward was a common practice in ancient times, often used to forge alliances and reward bravery. Acsah's marriage would not only be a personal union but also a means to strengthen familial and tribal bonds. This reflects the cultural norms of the time, where marriages were often arranged and served broader social and political purposes. The mention of Acsah also highlights the role of women in biblical narratives, where they often play significant roles in the unfolding of God's plans.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Caleb
A leader from the tribe of Judah, known for his faithfulness and courage. He was one of the two spies who trusted in God's promise to give Israel the land of Canaan.

2. Acsah
Caleb's daughter, who becomes a central figure in this account as a reward for bravery and military success.

3. Kiriath-sepher
A city in the territory of Judah, also known as Debir. It was a Canaanite city that needed to be conquered as part of Israel's settlement in the Promised Land.
Teaching Points
Faith and Courage in Action
Caleb's offer demonstrates the importance of faith and courage in fulfilling God's promises. Believers are encouraged to act boldly in faith, trusting in God's provision and guidance.

Incentives for Righteous Actions
The promise of a reward for capturing Kiriath-sepher illustrates how God often uses incentives to motivate His people towards righteous actions and fulfilling His purposes.

Family and Legacy
Caleb's decision to offer his daughter in marriage as a reward highlights the significance of family and legacy in biblical times. It prompts reflection on how our actions today can impact future generations.

God's Sovereignty in Conquests
The capture of Kiriath-sepher is a reminder of God's sovereignty in the victories of His people. It encourages believers to rely on God's strength rather than their own.

What Does Joshua 15:16 Mean
And Caleb said

• Caleb, now an elder in the tribe of Judah, speaks with the same bold faith he showed when he spied out Canaan (Numbers 13:30; Joshua 14:10–12).

• His words carry weight because he has personally experienced God’s faithfulness and wants the next generation to taste that same victory (Deuteronomy 1:36).

• By speaking, he signals that godly leadership doesn’t retire; it keeps urging others toward courageous obedience.

To the man

• Caleb’s offer is open—not just to family insiders but to any Israelite willing to trust the Lord for the impossible (cf. Judges 6:12).

• This echoes God’s pattern of calling willing individuals—think of Gideon or David—rather than relying on pedigree alone (1 Samuel 16:7).

• It reminds us that God still looks for “the man”—or woman—whose heart is fully His (2 Chronicles 16:9).

Who strikes down Kiriath-sepher

• Kiriath-sepher (later called Debir) had already been defeated once under Joshua (Joshua 10:38–39), yet pockets of resistance remained.

• Caleb sees unfinished business and invites fresh faith to finish the task—just as believers are called to drive out lingering strongholds in their own lives (Ephesians 4:22–24).

• The city’s formidable reputation highlights the need for divine help, mirroring earlier victories such as Jericho (Joshua 6:20).

And captures it

• “Strikes down” addresses the battle; “captures” stresses possession. God’s people are to conquer and then occupy, not merely win a momentary fight (Deuteronomy 11:24).

• Success requires perseverance—pressing through until the promise is fully realized, as Paul later urges in Philippians 3:12–14.

• The full capture also secures Judah’s inheritance, fulfilling God’s covenant word to Abraham (Genesis 15:18–21).

I will give my daughter Acsah

• Caleb offers more than spoils; he offers family. In Israel, marriage weaves a warrior into the covenant lineage (Ruth 4:10–12).

• The pattern is seen again when Saul promises Michal to whoever defeats Goliath (1 Samuel 17:25), showing that great victories often bring relational blessing.

• By naming Acsah, Scripture affirms her value and hints at her own faith, later displayed when she asks for the upper and lower springs (Joshua 15:18–19).

In marriage

• Marriage is God-ordained (Genesis 2:24) and here becomes a reward that binds bravery to legacy.

• The union safeguards tribal inheritance, keeping land within Judah as commanded (Numbers 36:6–9).

• It foreshadows the greater reward promised to overcomers—the marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:7–8).

summary

Joshua 15:16 shows Caleb spurring Israel toward courageous obedience. He offers his daughter—an act that ties military victory to covenant family—to whoever will trust God enough to finish the conquest of Kiriath-sepher. The verse upholds themes of faith-filled leadership, open invitation to bold action, perseverance in claiming God’s promises, and the blessing that flows from victorious faith.
