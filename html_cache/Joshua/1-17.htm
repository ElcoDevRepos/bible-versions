Just as we obeyed Moses in all things, so we will obey you.
This phrase reflects the continuity of leadership from Moses to Joshua. Moses was a central figure in Israel's history, leading the Israelites out of Egypt and through the wilderness. His leadership was marked by direct communication with God, as seen in Exodus 33:11, where it is said that the LORD spoke to Moses face to face. The Israelites' commitment to obey Joshua as they did Moses signifies their recognition of Joshua's divinely appointed role. Historically, this transition of leadership was crucial for maintaining unity and direction as the Israelites prepared to enter the Promised Land. The phrase also underscores the importance of obedience to God's appointed leaders, a theme echoed in Hebrews 13:17, which calls for submission to those who lead in the faith.

And may the LORD your God be with you, as He was with Moses.
This part of the verse is a prayer and a blessing, invoking God's presence with Joshua as it was with Moses. The presence of God with Moses was evident through numerous miracles and guidance, such as the parting of the Red Sea (Exodus 14) and the provision of manna (Exodus 16). The assurance of God's presence is a recurring theme in Scripture, providing strength and courage to His people. In the New Testament, Jesus promises His presence with His followers in Matthew 28:20, "And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age." This continuity of divine presence from Moses to Joshua, and ultimately to all believers through Christ, highlights the unchanging nature of God's faithfulness. The phrase also serves as a reminder of the conditional nature of God's blessings, often linked to obedience and faithfulness, as seen in Deuteronomy 28.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joshua
The successor of Moses, chosen to lead the Israelites into the Promised Land. He is a central figure in the book of Joshua and is known for his faithfulness and leadership.

2. Moses
The former leader of the Israelites who led them out of Egypt and through the wilderness. He is revered as a prophet and lawgiver.

3. The Israelites
The people of God, descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who are preparing to enter the Promised Land under Joshua's leadership.

4. The Promised Land
The land of Canaan, promised by God to the descendants of Abraham, a symbol of God's faithfulness and provision.

5. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who promises to be with Joshua as He was with Moses, ensuring His presence and guidance.
Teaching Points
Obedience to God-Appointed Leadership
Just as the Israelites pledged obedience to Joshua, believers are called to respect and follow God-appointed leaders, trusting that God is with them.

The Importance of God's Presence
The assurance of God's presence is crucial for success and courage in any endeavor. Believers should seek and rely on God's presence in their lives.

Continuity of God's Promises
God's promises to His people are consistent and reliable. Just as He was with Moses and Joshua, He is with us today.

Faithfulness in Transition
Transitions in leadership or life stages can be challenging, but faithfulness to God's commands and trust in His presence provide stability.

Encouragement in Leadership
Leaders can draw strength from the knowledge that God is with them, just as He was with Joshua, empowering them to lead with confidence.

What Does Joshua 1:17 Mean
Just as we obeyed Moses in all things

- These words come from the tribes east of the Jordan (Joshua 1:12-16) affirming their previous loyalty to Moses.

- Their statement looks back to moments of corporate obedience (Exodus 24:3; Numbers 32:25-27) even while remembering their failures; they are pledging to learn from the past and fully submit this time.

- The phrase reinforces that the standard for following a God-appointed leader is total, practical obedience, not selective agreement (Deuteronomy 1:32; Hebrews 13:17).

so we will obey you

- Leadership succession in God’s plan is orderly; Joshua had already been commissioned publicly (Deuteronomy 31:7-8, 14; Numbers 27:18-23).

- The people recognize Joshua’s authority as rooted in God’s call, not human vote. This sets a precedent for future transitions—authority belongs to those God appoints (Romans 13:1; 1 Timothy 5:22), and the community’s task is respectful obedience.

- Practical obedience would soon be tested in risky steps—crossing the Jordan (Joshua 3-4) and assaulting Jericho (Joshua 6). Their vow prepares them to act, not merely speak.

And may the LORD your God be with you

- The people acknowledge that Joshua’s success depends entirely on God’s presence (Exodus 33:15-16; Psalm 127:1).

- By saying “your God,” they highlight Joshua’s personal walk with the LORD, inviting him to keep that relationship central (1 Samuel 30:6; John 15:5).

- Their words also function as an intercessory blessing, asking God to empower Joshua’s leadership (2 Samuel 7:29; 2 Thessalonians 3:16).

as He was with Moses

- God’s faithfulness is consistent; He does not change from one generation to the next (Malachi 3:6; Hebrews 13:8).

- The mighty acts done through Moses—plagues, Red Sea, manna—are the yardstick for what they now expect under Joshua (Deuteronomy 34:10-12; Joshua 3:7).

- This comparison reassures the people and Joshua alike: the same covenant-keeping God will act with equal power, providing continuity and confidence (Psalm 90:1-2; Isaiah 63:11-12).

summary

Joshua 1:17 records a unified commitment from Israel’s leaders: they promise Joshua the same thorough obedience they previously gave Moses and pray that the LORD’s manifest presence will rest on him just as it did on Moses. The verse underlines three core truths—God establishes leaders, His people are to respond with full obedience, and His unchanging presence guarantees success in every generation that trusts and follows Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 17. - As we hearkened unto Moses. Calvin remarks that the Israelites did not hearken unto Moses, but replies that, compared with the conduct of their fathers whose bodies lay in the wilderness, the conduct of this generation was obedience itself. It certainly appears as though for the last two years of the wandering in the wilderness there was far less rebellion against Moses than before; and after the solemn repetition of the precepts of the law to the new generation which had arisen, given in the Book of Deuteronomy, there seems to have been no rebellion at all (see Numbers 26:63).
