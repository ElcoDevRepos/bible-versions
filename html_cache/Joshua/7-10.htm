But the LORD said to Joshua
This phrase indicates direct communication from God to Joshua, emphasizing the personal relationship and leadership role Joshua holds. In the context of the Old Testament, God often spoke directly to leaders like Moses and Joshua, guiding them in their leadership of Israel. This direct communication underscores Joshua's role as Moses' successor and God's chosen leader for Israel. It also reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God actively guides and corrects His people.

Stand up!
The command to "Stand up!" is both literal and metaphorical. Literally, Joshua is being told to rise from his position of prostration, a common posture for prayer and supplication in ancient Near Eastern cultures. Metaphorically, it signifies a call to action and responsibility. Joshua's leadership is being reaffirmed, and he is being instructed to address the issue at hand rather than remain in a state of despair or confusion. This command can be seen as a call to faith and obedience, reminiscent of God's earlier commands to Joshua to be strong and courageous (Joshua 1:6-9).

Why have you fallen on your face?
This question highlights God's awareness of Joshua's distress and the situation's gravity. Falling on one's face was a sign of deep humility, repentance, or desperation. In the context of Joshua 7, this posture reflects Joshua's response to Israel's defeat at Ai, which was unexpected and demoralizing. The question implies that Joshua's response, while understandable, is not the solution to the problem. It suggests that there is a need for introspection and action rather than mere lamentation. This moment is pivotal, as it leads to the revelation of Achan's sin, which had brought about the defeat. The question also serves as a reminder of the need for holiness and obedience to God's commands, as seen in the broader narrative of Israel's conquest of Canaan.

Persons / Places / Events

The covenant God of Israel, who communicates directly with Joshua, guiding and instructing him.

2. Joshua
The leader of Israel, successor to Moses, who is responsible for leading the Israelites into the Promised Land.

3. Israelites
The people of God, who are in the process of conquering the land of Canaan.

4. Achan

5. Ai
A city in Canaan where the Israelites faced an unexpected defeat due to disobedience within their camp.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Obedience
Disobedience to God's commands can have serious consequences, not just for individuals but for the entire community.

Leadership Accountability
Leaders are called to seek God's guidance and address issues within their community, as Joshua is instructed to do.

God's Direct Communication
God speaks to His people, providing direction and correction when needed. We must be attentive to His voice.

Repentance and Restoration
When sin is present, it must be addressed through repentance to restore right standing with God.

Courage in Correction
It takes courage to confront sin and make necessary changes, but it is essential for spiritual health and victory.

What Does Joshua 7:10 Mean
But the LORD said to Joshua

- The narrative presents a literal, face-to-face moment between the living God and His appointed leader. Joshua has been prostrate in grief after Israel’s defeat at Ai (Joshua 7:6–9), yet the LORD promptly speaks, showing that heaven is never silent when His purposes are at stake (Exodus 33:11; Deuteronomy 31:8; Joshua 1:5).

- “But” signals a divine interruption. Human lament stops when God’s word enters the scene, affirming that His voice carries final authority (1 Samuel 15:10).

- This intervention underscores covenant relationship. The same God who promised victory now confronts Joshua because Israel’s sin has disrupted fellowship, not the LORD’s faithfulness (Joshua 7:11).

Stand up!

- The command is concise and urgent. God directs Joshua from posture to motion, from mourning to mission (Exodus 14:15: “Why are you crying out to Me? Tell the Israelites to go forward.”).

- Rising marks readiness in Scripture. Nehemiah rallies workers, “Let us arise and build” (Nehemiah 2:18). Paul exhorts believers, “Wake up, O sleeper… and Christ will shine on you” (Ephesians 5:14).

- Prayerful humility is never dismissed, yet when known sin is present, obedience can no longer be delayed. The LORD’s holiness calls for immediate action, not prolonged despair (1 Samuel 16:1).

Why have you fallen on your face?

- God is not rejecting prayer itself; He is exposing that prayer without repentance is empty. Israel’s defeat traces to Achan’s hidden transgression, not to a lack of piety (Joshua 7:11).

- Scripture echoes this principle: “If I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened” (Psalm 66:18). Isaiah records, “Even though you multiply your prayers, I will not listen. Your hands are covered with blood” (Isaiah 1:15).

- The question reveals divine expectation: deal with the sin first. Confession opens the way to restored fellowship (1 John 1:9; James 4:8).

- Joshua must rise, identify the offender, purge the camp, and then move forward in victory—demonstrating that obedience is the pathway to answered prayer and renewed blessing.

summary

Joshua 7:10 shows God interrupting grief with a call to action. The LORD’s direct word reminds Joshua that divine guidance is available, but sin must be addressed before progress resumes. Standing up signifies readiness to obey, and the probing question exposes the futility of prayer that ignores known disobedience. In every generation, the path from defeat to victory runs through honest repentance, decisive obedience, and confidence that God still speaks to lead His people onward.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) Wherefore liest thou thus upon thy face?--"Why is this, that thou art fallen upon thy face? Israel hath sinned." The pronoun "thou" is emphatic.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - Get thee up. Not puerile lamentation, but action, is ever the duty of the soldier of the Lord. If defeat assails either the individual or the cause, there is a reason for it, and this must be promptly searched out, and with God's aid be discovered. The sin or error once found out and put away, the combat may be renewed and brought to a successful issue.
