So Joshua did this
Joshua's actions here are a direct response to the covenant made with the Gibeonites, despite their deception. This reflects the importance of keeping one's word, a principle deeply rooted in the biblical narrative (Numbers 30:2). Joshua's leadership is characterized by adherence to oaths, even when made under false pretenses, highlighting the value of integrity and the fear of God over human judgment.

and delivered them from the hands of the Israelites
The Israelites were initially inclined to destroy the Gibeonites due to their deceit (Joshua 9:18-19). However, Joshua's intervention underscores the sanctity of covenants. This act of deliverance can be seen as a type of Christ, who delivers humanity from the consequences of sin. It also reflects the broader biblical theme of mercy triumphing over judgment (James 2:13).

and they did not kill the Gibeonites
The sparing of the Gibeonites, despite their deception, illustrates God's grace and the possibility of redemption for those outside the covenant community. This event foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles into God's plan of salvation, as seen in the New Testament (Acts 10:34-35). The Gibeonites' survival also serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and the unexpected ways He fulfills His purposes.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joshua
The leader of the Israelites after Moses, tasked with leading the people into the Promised Land. Known for his faithfulness and obedience to God.

2. Gibeonites
A group of people from the city of Gibeon who deceived the Israelites into making a peace treaty with them by pretending to be from a distant land.

3. Israelites
The chosen people of God, led by Joshua, who were conquering the land of Canaan as God had commanded.

4. Canaan
The land promised by God to the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, which the Israelites were in the process of conquering.

5. Treaty
An agreement made between the Israelites and the Gibeonites, which was based on deception but was honored by Joshua and the leaders of Israel.
Teaching Points
Integrity in Leadership
Joshua's decision to honor the treaty, despite the deception, demonstrates the importance of integrity and keeping one's word, even when it is difficult.

Consequences of Deception
The Gibeonites' deception led to their servitude, illustrating that deceit can have long-lasting consequences.

God's Sovereignty
Despite human errors and deception, God's plans and purposes prevail. The Gibeonites became servants, which ultimately served God's purposes for Israel.

Grace and Mercy
Joshua's sparing of the Gibeonites can be seen as an act of mercy, reflecting God's character and His willingness to extend grace.

Discernment in Decision-Making
The Israelites' failure to seek God's counsel before making the treaty serves as a reminder of the importance of seeking divine guidance in our decisions.

What Does Joshua 9:26 Mean
So Joshua did this

Joshua followed through on the covenant he had sworn to the Gibeonites, even though their plea had been laced with deception (Joshua 9:14-19). This resolves a tension: Deuteronomy 7:1-2 commanded Israel to destroy the Canaanite peoples, yet Numbers 30:2 insists that an oath made before the LORD must be kept. Joshua chose faithfulness to his word, mirroring the character trait celebrated in Psalm 15:4—“he keeps his oath even when it hurts.” By acting, Joshua shows that integrity before God outweighs the embarrassment of being fooled.

Key take-aways:

• Integrity is measured by follow-through, not convenience.

• God’s people model His truthfulness when they honor commitments (cf. Titus 1:2).

and delivered them from the hands of the Israelites

The phrase stresses Joshua’s decisive leadership: he “delivered” (protected) the Gibeonites from the wrath of the congregation that wanted immediate justice. Compare Moses interceding for Israel in Exodus 32:9-14; a single leader’s obedience can spare many lives. Joshua’s act also anticipates 2 Samuel 21:1-2, where Saul’s later violation of this covenant brings famine on Israel—showing God still took the promise seriously centuries later.

Leadership lessons:

• Spiritual authority includes the courage to restrain wrongful zeal (James 1:20).

• Covenant faithfulness safeguards the nation from future judgment (Ecclesiastes 5:4-5).

and they did not kill the Gibeonites

The outcome is crystal clear: life preserved. God’s mercy shines through Israel’s obedience. Later, the spared Gibeonites become wood-cutters and water-carriers “for the altar of the LORD” (Joshua 9:27), placing them in continual proximity to worship. In Joshua 10:6-10, Israel even goes to battle to defend Gibeon, turning former enemies into allies. Centuries afterward, descendants of Gibeon appear among temple servants returning from exile (Nehemiah 7:25), suggesting lasting inclusion.

Gospel echoes:

• Foreigners brought near by covenant foreshadow Gentile inclusion in Christ (Ephesians 2:12-13).

• Mercy triumphs over judgment when God’s people honor His ways (James 2:13).

summary

Joshua 9:26 reveals the power of covenant faithfulness. Joshua, though deceived, keeps his oath, shields the Gibeonites, and thereby models God’s integrity and mercy. His decision averts immediate bloodshed, prevents future judgment, and ultimately folds a pagan people into the worshiping community—illustrating how obedience to God’s word safeguards life and extends grace.
