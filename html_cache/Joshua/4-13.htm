About 40,000 troops
The number 40,000 signifies a substantial military force, indicating the readiness and strength of the Israelites as they prepared to enter the Promised Land. This number reflects the organized and disciplined nature of the Israelite army, which was necessary for the conquest of Canaan. The number 40 is often symbolic in the Bible, representing periods of testing or preparation, such as the 40 years of wilderness wandering.

armed for battle
The phrase emphasizes the preparedness and determination of the Israelites. They were equipped and ready to engage in the battles necessary to claim the land promised to them by God. This readiness for battle also reflects the fulfillment of God's promise to Abraham regarding his descendants possessing the land (Genesis 15:18-21).

crossed over before the LORD
Crossing over "before the LORD" indicates that the Israelites' actions were under divine guidance and approval. The presence of the Ark of the Covenant, which represented God's presence, led them, signifying that their military endeavors were not merely human efforts but divinely ordained missions. This phrase underscores the importance of divine leadership and obedience to God's commands.

into the plains of Jericho
The plains of Jericho were strategically significant as they were the entry point into Canaan. Jericho itself was a fortified city, representing the first major challenge in the conquest of the land. The geographical location highlights the beginning of the fulfillment of God's promise to give the Israelites the land of Canaan. Jericho's conquest, which follows in the narrative, serves as a testament to God's power and faithfulness in delivering His people.

Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness in Transition
Just as God was faithful to the Israelites in crossing the Jordan, He is faithful in our life's transitions. Trust in His guidance and provision.

Unity and Support Among Believers
The tribes of Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh honored their commitment to support their fellow Israelites. In the body of Christ, we are called to support and uphold one another in spiritual battles.

Preparedness for Spiritual Warfare
The forty thousand men were armed for battle, reminding us to be spiritually prepared with the armor of God as described in Ephesians 6.

Obedience to God's Commands
The Israelites' obedience in crossing the Jordan and preparing for battle is a model for us to follow God's commands, even when they require courage and faith.

God's Presence in Our Battles
The phrase "before the LORD" emphasizes that God is present in our struggles and battles. We are not alone, and His presence is our assurance of victory.

What Does Joshua 4:13 Mean
About 40,000 troops

- The figure highlights a sizeable yet specific contingent from the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh. Joshua 1:14-15 already recorded Joshua’s charge to these tribes: “All your mighty men of valor shall cross over armed before your brothers.”

- Though their total census numbers exceeded 100,000 (Numbers 26:7, 18, 34), only about 40,000 volunteered for the advance guard. Their willingness fulfills the promise they made to Moses in Numbers 32:20-22.

- The number underscores God’s orderly leadership—He is not sending a chaotic mob but a disciplined force prepared to secure Israel’s foothold in the land.

armed for battle

- “Armed” signals readiness, not reluctance. Just as Israel left Egypt “armed for battle” (Exodus 13:18), these warriors step forward equipped for the next stage of God’s plan.

- Their gear is physical, yet the deeper lesson is spiritual preparedness. Compare Paul’s later exhortation, “Put on the full armor of God” (Ephesians 6:11). God values a people ready in both body and spirit.

- Their obedience also protects the families remaining east of the Jordan; the front-line fighters ensure a united conquest rather than fragmented tribes (Joshua 22:1-4).

crossed over

- The phrase recalls the miracle of Joshua 3:14-17, where the Jordan’s waters stopped until “all Israel had crossed over on dry ground.”

- Crossing signifies transition: from wilderness wandering to promised-land warfare; from promise anticipated to promise possessed.

- Echoes of the Red Sea (Exodus 14) remind Israel—and us—that the God who begins deliverance completes it (Philippians 1:6).

before the LORD

- Moving “before the LORD” stresses divine oversight. They march under His gaze and by His command, not merely before Joshua or the nation.

- Moses had set this standard: “You shall pass armed before the LORD to war” (Deuteronomy 3:18). Obedience flows from covenant loyalty, not mere military strategy.

- God’s presence assures victory: “The battle belongs to the LORD” (1 Samuel 17:47). When He leads, no obstacle—whether river or walled city—stands firm.

into the plains of Jericho

- The fertile plains spread just north of the Dead Sea, offering Israel a staging ground opposite Jericho, the gateway fortress of Canaan.

- Jericho’s prominence explains why God brings His people here first: conquering the stronghold will send shockwaves through the land (Joshua 6:1-2).

- Stationing these 40,000 on the plains visually demonstrates to Canaanites that Israel, and Israel’s God, have arrived (Joshua 5:1).

summary

Joshua 4:13 captures a pivotal moment: a prepared remnant of 40,000 crosses the miraculously parted Jordan, fully armed, moving under God’s direct supervision toward Jericho’s plains. The verse testifies to obedient faith, covenant unity, and God’s faithful leadership—essentials for every believer called to step into God’s promises today.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) About forty thousand.--The totals of these three tribes at the last census (Numbers 26) were:--Reuben (Numbers 26:7), 43,730; Gad (Numbers 26:18), 40,500; Manasseh (Numbers 26:34), 52,700, or for the exact half, 26, 350. Thus the entire force of the two and a half tribes might amount to 110,580. They therefore left more than half their number to protect their families and their dwellings. This does not seem inconsistent with the spirit of their agreement with Moses, or with the interpretation of that agreement by Joshua and their fellow-Israelites. (See Numbers 32:16-17; Numbers 32:24; Numbers 32:26.) The permission to build cities implies the right to fortify and defend them. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - Prepared for war. εὔζωνοι, LXX. Literally, disencumbered, like the Latin expeditus. Unlike Numbers 31:5, the Hebrew has the article here. The meaning therefore may be "equipped men of the host," i.e., the light armed and active among them. If we translate thus, it is clear that all their armed men did not go over Jordan. The impedimenta were left behind, under a strong guard (see notes on Joshua 1:14). The plains of Jericho. Here the LXX. and Theodotion have τὴν Ιερίχὼ πόλιν, Symmachus renders by ἀοίκητον, the Vulgate by cumpestria. The original is עַרְבות literally, the deserts or uncultivated lands (see note on Joshua 3:16). They formed a "low-lying plain about four hours' journey in breadth," at that time largely covered with palm trees and thorny acacias, but apparently not cultivated. Since that time, the palms having disappeared, the plain has become "a very picture of fertility," "covered with luxuriant vegetation" (Bartlett, 'From Egypt to Palestine,' p. 453. See also note on Joshua 3:16). The valley narrows to a gorge at Jericho, through which the Kelt, according to Robinson the ancient Cherith, flows, the source of all the verdure which once bloomed around the city. The gorge of the Kelt Canon Tristram describes as "tremendous," but he believes the Cherith to have been eastward of Jordan, following Mr. Grove, who is here disposed to accept the. tradition of Eusebius and Jerome.
