When Pashhur the priest
Pashhur was a priest, indicating his role in the religious hierarchy of ancient Judah. Priests were responsible for temple rituals and maintaining the spiritual life of the nation. His position suggests he had significant influence and authority, especially in religious matters. The name Pashhur means "freedom" or "liberation," which is ironic given his actions against Jeremiah.

the son of Immer
The mention of Immer connects Pashhur to a specific priestly lineage. The family of Immer was one of the priestly divisions established by King David (1 Chronicles 24:14). This lineage underscores Pashhur's established role and authority within the temple system.

and the chief official in the house of the LORD
As the chief official, Pashhur held a high-ranking position, possibly akin to a temple governor or overseer. This role would have involved administrative duties and maintaining order within the temple precincts. His authority extended to enforcing religious laws and dealing with those perceived as threats to the temple's sanctity.

heard Jeremiah prophesying these things
Jeremiah's prophecies were often messages of impending judgment and calls for repentance, which were unpopular among the religious and political leaders of his time. The phrase "these things" refers to the specific prophecies of doom and destruction that Jeremiah had been delivering, particularly concerning the fate of Jerusalem and the temple (Jeremiah 19:14-15). Jeremiah's message challenged the status quo and threatened the complacency of the religious leaders, leading to conflict with figures like Pashhur.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Pashhur
A priest and the son of Immer, who held a significant position as the chief officer in the temple. His role was to maintain order and oversee temple activities, making him a person of authority and influence.

2. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of warning and judgment to the people of Judah. Known for his perseverance in the face of opposition and his deep emotional connection to his prophetic mission.

3. The House of the LORD
Refers to the temple in Jerusalem, the central place of worship for the Israelites. It was a place of significant religious and cultural importance.

4. Prophesying
The act of delivering messages from God, often involving warnings of judgment or calls for repentance. Jeremiah's prophecies were often met with resistance and hostility.

5. The Context of Opposition
This event occurs in a broader account where Jeremiah faces increasing opposition and persecution for his prophecies, highlighting the tension between true prophetic messages and established religious authority.
Teaching Points
Courage in the Face of Opposition
Jeremiah's experience teaches us the importance of standing firm in our faith and convictions, even when facing opposition from those in authority.

The Cost of Prophetic Ministry
True prophetic ministry often comes with a cost, including misunderstanding and persecution. Believers should be prepared for challenges when speaking God's truth.

Authority and Accountability
Pashhur's reaction to Jeremiah's prophecy reminds us that religious authority must be exercised with humility and openness to God's voice, rather than resistance to it.

The Role of the Temple
The temple, as a place of worship, should be a place where God's truth is proclaimed and heard, not suppressed. This calls for a reflection on how we maintain the integrity of our places of worship today.

Perseverance in Mission
Jeremiah's perseverance despite opposition encourages believers to remain faithful to their God-given mission, trusting in God's protection and purpose.

What Does Jeremiah 20:1 Mean
When Pashhur the priest

- The verse opens with a clear historical marker—“When”—anchoring the narrative in a real moment (cf. Luke 1:3).

- Pashhur is named as a priest, placing him among those responsible for spiritual leadership (cf. Exodus 28:1; Malachi 2:7).

- His priestly role should have aligned him with God’s Word, yet he opposes it, echoing earlier clashes between priests and prophets like those against Zechariah (2 Chronicles 24:20-21) and Jeremiah himself (Jeremiah 26:7-8).

the son of Immer

- Mentioning Immer ties Pashhur to a recognized priestly family (1 Chronicles 24:14).

- Lineage underscores accountability; inherited privilege carries an expectation of faithfulness (Numbers 3:10).

- A contrast surfaces between mere ancestry and true obedience, foreshadowing New Testament warnings that heritage alone does not guarantee righteousness (Matthew 3:9).

and the chief official in the house of the LORD

- “Chief official” (also used in 2 Kings 25:18) signals administrative authority over temple security and discipline.

- Pashhur’s title suggests influence comparable to the later “captain of the temple” (Acts 4:1), someone expected to protect godly order.

- Instead of guarding God’s truth, he will persecute God’s prophet, fulfilling Jesus’ observation that religious authorities often mistreat messengers of truth (Matthew 23:34-35).

heard Jeremiah prophesying these things

- “These things” points back to Jeremiah 19, where the prophet foretold Judah’s ruin, the Valley of Hinnom’s renaming, and Jerusalem’s siege (Jeremiah 19:3-15).

- Pashhur’s hearing is more than auditory; it sparks reaction. Like Amaziah silencing Amos (Amos 7:10-13), Pashhur chooses suppression over repentance.

- His response illustrates the heart-level divide Jesus later highlights: “Light has come into the world, and people loved darkness rather than light” (John 3:19).

- The scene sets up the following verses, where Pashhur’s persecution leads to Jeremiah’s imprisonment (Jeremiah 20:2) and God’s pronouncement of Pashhur’s new name, “Terror on Every Side” (Jeremiah 20:3-6), proving that opposing God’s word invites personal judgment.

summary

Jeremiah 20:1 introduces a confrontation between faithful prophecy and compromised priesthood. Pashhur—well-placed, well-connected, and in charge—reacts against the hard truth Jeremiah proclaims. His lineage and office fail to shield him from accountability; instead, they heighten it. The verse warns that religious position is no substitute for submission to God’s word, and it prepares us to see how God vindicates His messenger and judges those who try to silence Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XX.

(1) Pashur the son of Immer.--The description must be remembered as distinguishing him from the son of Melchiah of the same name in Jeremiah 21:1. We may probably identify him with the father of the Gedaliah named in Jeremiah 38:1 as among the "princes" that at a later date opposed the prophet's work, and with the section of the priesthood, the sixteenth, named in 1Chronicles 24:14, as headed in the time of David by Immer. The name here (like that of "the sons of Korah") may indicate simply the fact that he belonged to this section; or, possibly, the name of the patriarch (so to speak) who gave its name to it may have re-appeared from time to time in the line of his descendants. The name of Pashur appears again, after the Captivity, in Ezra 2:37-38.

Chief governor.--Better, deputy-governor. The word for governor is Nagid, and this office was assigned to the high priest as the "ruler of the house of God" (1Chronicles 9:11; 2Chronicles 31:13). In the case of Zephaniah, who appears as Nagid in Jeremiah 29:26, it was given to him as the "second priest" (2Kings 25:18; Jeremiah 52:14). Next in order to him was the Pakid, the deputy, or, perhaps, better, superintendent. Here Pashur is described by the combination of the two titles, possibly as implying that he was invested, though a "deputy," with the full powers of the "governor." By some commentators, however, the relation of the two words is inverted, the Nagid being added to the Pakid, to imply that Pashur was the chief warden or overseer. As such, on either view, the act and the words of Jeremiah came under his official notice. That such words should be spoken in the court of the Temple to the multitude assembled there was, we must believe, something new, and Pashur was resolved at any cost to prevent its repetition. . . .
