Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then the LORD said to me
This phrase indicates divine communication, emphasizing the prophetic role of Jeremiah. The LORD, Yahweh, is the covenant God of Israel, who speaks directly to His prophets. This underscores the authority and authenticity of the message Jeremiah is about to deliver.

Proclaim all these words
Jeremiah is commanded to publicly announce God's message. The term "proclaim" suggests a formal declaration, often associated with prophetic utterances. This highlights the importance and urgency of the message, as it is meant for public hearing and response.

in the cities of Judah and in the streets of Jerusalem
The geographical reference to Judah and Jerusalem indicates the specific audience of the prophecy. Judah was the southern kingdom, and Jerusalem its capital, central to Jewish religious and political life. This setting underscores the widespread nature of the message, meant for all inhabitants, from rural areas to the heart of the nation.

saying: Hear the words of this covenant
The call to "hear" is a summons to listen and obey, a common biblical motif. The "covenant" refers to the Mosaic Covenant, which was foundational to Israel's relationship with God. This covenant included blessings for obedience and curses for disobedience, as outlined in Deuteronomy 28.

and carry them out
This phrase emphasizes not just hearing but also doing. The call to action reflects the biblical principle that faith is demonstrated through obedience. It echoes James 1:22, which urges believers to be doers of the word, not hearers only. The historical context reveals that Judah's failure to obey the covenant led to their eventual exile.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who speaks to His prophet Jeremiah.

2. Jeremiah
The prophet chosen by God to deliver His messages to the people of Judah and Jerusalem.

3. Cities of Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, consisting of the tribes of Judah and Benjamin, where Jeremiah is instructed to proclaim God's message.

4. Streets of Jerusalem
The capital city of Judah, representing the heart of the nation's religious and political life.

5. The Covenant
Refers to the Mosaic Covenant, which includes the laws and commandments given by God to the Israelites at Mount Sinai.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Proclamation
God commands His word to be proclaimed publicly, emphasizing the need for His truth to be heard by all.

Hearing and Obedience
The call to "hear" and "carry out" the words of the covenant underscores the necessity of not just listening but also acting on God's commands.

Covenant Faithfulness
The covenant relationship requires faithfulness and obedience from God's people, reminding us of our commitment to God's word.

The Role of the Prophet
Jeremiah's role as a prophet highlights the responsibility of God's messengers to faithfully deliver His message, regardless of the audience's response.

Application to Modern Believers
Just as the people of Judah were called to hear and obey, modern believers are called to live out the teachings of Scripture in their daily lives.

What Does Jeremiah 11:6 Mean
Then the LORD said to me

• Scripture opens with God’s clear voice. The prophet’s authority rests on this divine initiation (Jeremiah 1:4–9; 2 Timothy 3:16).

• The moment reminds us that the Lord is personally involved in history, speaking real words to real people—words we can trust as fully accurate.

• Like Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:4–14) or Samuel in the tabernacle (1 Samuel 3:10), Jeremiah hears the living God who still speaks through His written Word today.

Proclaim all these words in the cities of Judah and in the streets of Jerusalem

• The message is not to be whispered in private; it is announced where daily life happens—markets, gates, neighborhoods (Jeremiah 2:2; Proverbs 1:20-21).

• “All these words” underscores completeness. God withholds nothing needed for life and obedience (Deuteronomy 29:29; Acts 20:27).

• Public proclamation anticipates the Great Commission, taking God’s truth to every place and people (Matthew 28:19-20).

Hear the words of this covenant

• “Hear” means attentive, reverent listening (Deuteronomy 6:4; Psalm 78:1). God’s people must first receive before they can respond.

• “This covenant” points back to Sinai, where Israel pledged, “We will do everything the LORD has spoken” (Exodus 24:7). It is a solemn, binding relationship, not a casual arrangement (Deuteronomy 4:13).

• The call to hear anticipates the promised New Covenant, written on hearts rather than stone (Jeremiah 31:31-33; Hebrews 8:10).

and carry them out

• Hearing without doing is disobedience (James 1:22). Covenant blessings hinge on active obedience (Deuteronomy 29:9).

• Obedience flows from love and trust, not mere ritual (1 Samuel 15:22; John 14:15).

• Jesus echoes this principle: “Blessed are those who hear the word of God and obey it” (Luke 11:28). The call is timeless—faith that works (Galatians 5:6).

summary

Jeremiah 11:6 captures a four-step pattern: God speaks, His servant announces, the people listen, and they obey. The verse affirms that Scripture is God’s literal, authoritative word, meant for public declaration, attentive hearing, and practical action. By embracing this pattern today, believers honor the covenant-keeping Lord and experience the blessings He delights to give.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) In the cities of Judah . . .--It is, at least, probable that the words are to be taken literally, and that the prophet went from city to city, doing his work as a preacher of repentance, and taking the new-found book of Deuteronomy as his text. The narrative of 2Kings 23:13-20 indicates an iconoclastic journey throughout the kingdom as made by Josiah; and the prophetic discourse now before us, enforcing the observance of the covenant just made, would have been a fit accompaniment for such a mission.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Proclaim all these words, etc. This command probably points to a missionary circuit of Jeremiah, as suggested above. Others render, "read aloud" (comp. 2 Kings 22:8, Hebrew); but Jeremiah receives the direction to "proclaim" or "cry" elsewhere (Jeremiah 2:2; Jeremiah 3:12, etc.). So Gabriel, in the Koran, directs Mohammed to "cry," i.e. to proclaim or preach (Sura 96:1).
