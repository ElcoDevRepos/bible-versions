You are to speak this word to them:
This directive is given to Jeremiah, emphasizing his role as a prophet who must deliver God's message to the people of Judah. The phrase underscores the authority and responsibility of the prophet to communicate divine revelation, regardless of its content. This mirrors the role of prophets throughout the Old Testament, such as Isaiah and Ezekiel, who were also tasked with delivering difficult messages to God's people.

My eyes overflow with tears;
This expression of deep sorrow and lamentation reflects God's compassion and grief over the impending judgment on Judah. It highlights the emotional aspect of God's relationship with His people, showing that divine justice is not devoid of divine empathy. This imagery is reminiscent of Jesus weeping over Jerusalem in Luke 19:41, illustrating a continuity of God's heart for His people.

day and night they do not cease,
The unceasing nature of the tears signifies the relentless and overwhelming nature of the coming judgment. It also reflects the continuous intercession and mourning that Jeremiah, as a prophet, experiences on behalf of the people. This can be connected to the concept of persistent prayer and intercession found in 1 Thessalonians 5:17, where believers are encouraged to "pray without ceasing."

for the virgin daughter of my people
The term "virgin daughter" is a poetic expression used to describe the nation of Judah, emphasizing purity and the special relationship between God and His people. It conveys a sense of innocence lost and the tragedy of impending destruction. This imagery is used elsewhere in the prophets, such as in Isaiah 37:22, to denote the beloved status of Jerusalem and Judah.

has been shattered by a crushing blow,
This phrase indicates the severity of the judgment that has befallen Judah, likened to a devastating and irreversible blow. It reflects the consequences of the nation's persistent sin and rebellion against God. The imagery of a "crushing blow" is often used in prophetic literature to describe divine judgment, as seen in Isaiah 30:14.

a severely grievous wound.
The description of the wound as "severely grievous" underscores the depth of the nation's suffering and the seriousness of their spiritual condition. It suggests a wound that is not easily healed, pointing to the need for repentance and divine intervention. This can be seen as a type of the ultimate healing that comes through Christ, who bore our wounds and transgressions, as prophesied in Isaiah 53:5.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his messages of warning and hope to the people of Judah. He is often called the "weeping prophet" due to his deep emotional connection to the suffering of his people.

2. The Virgin Daughter of My People
This phrase symbolizes the nation of Judah, often depicted as a young, vulnerable woman in prophetic literature. It emphasizes the purity and innocence that has been lost due to sin and rebellion.

3. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which faced destruction and exile due to its persistent idolatry and disobedience to God.

4. The Mighty Blow
Represents the severe judgment and calamity that has befallen Judah, often interpreted as the Babylonian invasion and subsequent exile.

5. The Sorely Infected Wound
Symbolizes the deep spiritual and moral corruption of the people, which has led to their downfall and suffering.
Teaching Points
The Heart of a Prophet
Jeremiah's tears reflect God's own sorrow over sin and its consequences. As believers, we are called to share in God's compassion for the lost and broken.

The Consequences of Sin
The imagery of a "mighty blow" and "sorely infected wound" serves as a stark reminder of the destructive power of sin. We must take sin seriously and seek repentance.

God's Relentless Love
Despite the judgment, God's message through Jeremiah is ultimately one of hope and restoration. God's love for His people remains steadfast, even in times of discipline.

Intercessory Prayer
Jeremiah's response to the plight of his people encourages us to engage in intercessory prayer, pleading for God's mercy and intervention in the lives of those who are far from Him.

Spiritual Vigilance
The passage calls us to remain vigilant in our spiritual walk, recognizing the subtle ways in which sin can infect and destroy our relationship with God.

What Does Jeremiah 14:17 Mean
You are to speak this word to them

- God personally commissions Jeremiah to deliver a specific message, stressing divine initiative (Jeremiah 1:9; Exodus 4:12).

- The phrase highlights that the forthcoming lament originates with God, not Jeremiah’s private emotion.

- It sets a solemn tone: refusal to heed is ultimately rejection of God’s own voice (1 Thessalonians 2:13).

My eyes overflow with tears

- The prophet, echoing God’s heart, is undone by Judah’s rebellion (Jeremiah 9:1; Luke 19:41).

- Tears reveal compassion, not weakness; judgment is never cold or detached (Ezekiel 18:32).

- The overflowing imagery suggests grief beyond containment—Israel’s sin has breached every boundary.

day and night they do not cease

- Continuous weeping underscores the seriousness of impending disaster (Lamentations 2:18-19).

- Unceasing sorrow mirrors unrelenting sin and the non-stop consequences that will follow (Jeremiah 8:5-6).

- The round-the-clock lament also hints at intercession: Jeremiah stands in the gap without respite (1 Samuel 12:23).

for the virgin daughter of my people has been shattered by a crushing blow

- “Virgin daughter” stresses Judah’s intended purity and covenant closeness (2 Colossians 11:2; Jeremiah 2:2).

- “Shattered” is abrupt and violent, pointing to Babylon’s invasion and the loss of national integrity (Jeremiah 4:6-7; 39:1-10).

- The contrast between cherished status and brutal outcome magnifies the tragedy and the justice of God’s response (Isaiah 5:1-7).

a severely grievous wound

- The wound is beyond human healing; only divine intervention can restore (Jeremiah 30:12-17).

- It signals both physical devastation and spiritual brokenness: temple, city, and heart are all stricken (Psalm 74:3-7).

- Such language anticipates the ultimate remedy in the New Covenant where God binds up the broken (Isaiah 61:1; Hebrews 8:10-12).

summary

Jeremiah 14:17 unveils God’s own tears over a people who refused to repent. The prophet’s ceaseless lament reflects divine compassion coupled with impending judgment. Judah, once a cherished “virgin daughter,” now faces a crushing blow and an incurable wound because of persistent sin. Even in this dire pronouncement, the depth of God’s sorrow hints at His desire to heal the very wounds that His justice must allow.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(17) Thou shalt say this word.--Though not in form a prediction, no words could express more emphatically the terrible nature of the judgments implied in the preceding verse. The language (in part a reproduction of Jeremiah 13:17) is all but identical with that which recurs again and again in the Lamentations (Jeremiah 1:16; Jeremiah 2:11; Jeremiah 2:18), and may be looked upon as the germ of which those elegies of woe were the development.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 17-21. - The prophet's grief, and second intercession. Verse 17. - Therefore thou shalt say, etc. There is something strange and contrary to verisimilitude in the prefixing of this formula, not to a Divine revelation, but to a mere expression of the pained human feelings of the prophet. It is possible that the editor of Jeremiah's prophecies thought the paragraph which begins here needed something to link it with the preceding passage, and selected his formula rather unsuitably. Let mine eyes run down, etc. (comp. Jeremiah 13:27). Jeremiah's tender compassion shows itself in his choice of the expression, the virgin daughter of my people, just as we feel an added bitterness in the premature death of a cherished maiden.
