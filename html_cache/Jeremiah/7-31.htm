They have built the high places of Topheth
The high places refer to elevated sites often used for worship, typically of pagan deities. Topheth, located in the Valley of Ben-hinnom, was notorious for its association with child sacrifice, particularly to the god Molech. This practice was strictly condemned in the Law of Moses (Leviticus 18:21, Deuteronomy 12:31). The construction of these high places indicates a significant departure from the worship of Yahweh, reflecting the influence of surrounding pagan cultures.

in the Valley of Ben-hinnom
The Valley of Ben-hinnom, located south of Jerusalem, became synonymous with idolatry and abomination due to the practices conducted there. It later became a symbol of divine judgment and was referred to as Gehenna in the New Testament, representing hell or the place of final punishment (Mark 9:43). The valley's historical and geographical significance underscores the severity of the Israelites' apostasy.

so they could burn their sons and daughters in the fire
This phrase highlights the horrific practice of child sacrifice, which was an abomination to God. Such acts were influenced by Canaanite religious practices and were a direct violation of God's commandments. The burning of children was seen as the ultimate expression of idolatry and rebellion against God, demonstrating the depth of Israel's spiritual corruption.

something I never commanded
God's laws, as given through Moses, explicitly forbade child sacrifice and any form of human sacrifice (Deuteronomy 18:10). This phrase emphasizes that such practices were not only unauthorized but also completely contrary to God's character and will. It underscores the distinction between the true worship of Yahweh and the detestable practices of the surrounding nations.

nor did it even enter My mind
This expression indicates the utter foreignness of such practices to God's nature and intentions. It serves to highlight the gravity of Israel's sin and the extent to which they had strayed from God's covenant. The phrase also underscores the concept of God's holiness and the expectation of His people to reflect His character, contrasting sharply with the abominable acts committed in Topheth.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of warning and judgment to the people of Judah. His ministry was marked by a call to repentance and a warning of impending destruction due to the people's unfaithfulness.

2. The People of Judah
The inhabitants of the southern kingdom of Israel, who were often rebuked by prophets for their idolatry and disobedience to God's commands.

3. Topheth
A site in the Valley of Hinnom, near Jerusalem, where the people of Judah engaged in child sacrifice, a practice detestable to God.

4. Valley of Hinnom
A geographical location outside Jerusalem associated with idolatrous practices, including child sacrifice. It later became synonymous with Gehenna, a term used for hell in the New Testament.

5. Child Sacrifice
A pagan practice adopted by some Israelites, involving the offering of children to false gods, which was strictly forbidden by God.
Teaching Points
The Sanctity of Life
God values human life, and practices like child sacrifice are abhorrent to Him. This underscores the importance of protecting and valuing life at all stages.

Idolatry's Consequences
The people of Judah's idolatry led them to commit heinous acts. Idolatry in any form can lead us away from God's will and into destructive behaviors.

God's Commands vs. Human Traditions
The practices in Topheth were not commanded by God. We must discern between God's true commands and human traditions that contradict His Word.

Repentance and Reform
Like King Josiah's reforms, we are called to turn away from sin and align our lives with God's commands, seeking His forgiveness and guidance.

God's Heart and Mind
The phrase "nor did it enter My mind" indicates that such practices are completely contrary to God's nature. Understanding God's character helps us align our actions with His will.

What Does Jeremiah 7:31 Mean
They have built the high places of Topheth

High places were elevated platforms where people believed they could approach deities more effectively. Judah copied the surrounding nations by erecting such places for pagan rites—an open rejection of the Lord, who had already chosen Jerusalem’s temple as the proper place of worship (Deuteronomy 12:5–6). • 2 Kings 23:10 shows Josiah later defiling Topheth to halt these practices. • Isaiah 30:33 hints that Topheth would ultimately become a place of God’s judgment, not pagan celebration.

in the Valley of Ben-hinnom

The valley just south of Jerusalem first appears as a boundary marker (Joshua 15:8), but by Jeremiah’s day it was infamous for idolatry and bloodshed. • 2 Chronicles 28:3 notes King Ahaz, and 33:6 records Manasseh, burning children there. • Jeremiah 19:2–6 foretells that the valley would be renamed the Valley of Slaughter, tying geographic memory to divine justice.

so they could burn their sons and daughters in the fire

Child sacrifice was the darkest expression of idolatry, aimed at manipulating gods for prosperity or protection. • Deuteronomy 12:31 warns, “They even burn their sons and daughters in the fire to their gods.” • 2 Kings 17:17 lists this atrocity among the sins that led to Israel’s exile, underscoring how such cruelty provoked God’s wrath.

something I never commanded

God’s law clearly forbade human sacrifice (Leviticus 18:21: “You shall not give any of your children to be sacrificed to Molech”). • Deuteronomy 18:10–12 links the practice to detestable occultism. By stressing “never commanded,” the Lord highlights that true obedience cannot invent worship or ethics; it must submit to His revealed will.

nor did it even enter My mind

The phrase underscores divine abhorrence: God’s moral nature is utterly opposed to such acts. • Jeremiah 19:5 repeats the line, and 32:35 adds, “It never entered My mind that they should do this abomination, causing Judah to sin.” The language shows that while God foreknows all, He never conceives evil as an acceptable option for His people.

summary

Jeremiah 7:31 exposes Judah’s plunge into horrific idolatry: building unauthorized worship sites in the Valley of Ben-hinnom, sacrificing their own children, and flagrantly violating God’s clear commands. The Lord’s emphatic denial—He neither ordered nor imagined such evil—highlights both His holiness and His covenant love. He calls His people to pure worship anchored in His revealed Word, not in the destructive practices of surrounding culture.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) High places.--Not the same word as in Jeremiah 7:29, but bamoth, as in the "high places" of Baal, in Numbers 22:41; Numbers 23:3, the Bamoth-baal of Joshua 13:17. The word had become almost technical for the mounds, natural or (as in this passage) artificial, on which altars to Jehovah or to other gods were erected, and appears in 1Samuel 9:12; 1Kings 3:4; Ezekiel 20:29; Amos 7:9. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - The high places of Tophet; rather, the high places of the Topheth - (on the "high places" (Hebrew bamoth) - hero probably artificial mounds to erect the altars upon, and on "the Topheth," see Commentary on 1 Kings). In the valley of the son of Hinnom. Hitzig and others would take Hinnom as a noun meaning "groaning" (Rashi, the great Jewish commentator. had already proposed this view), which is at first sight very plausible. But this name of the valley is already found in the description of the boundaries of Judah and Benin-rain in Joshua 15:8; Joshua 18:16. To burn their sons, etc. (On the worship of Moloch (Saturn), see on Leviticus 18:21, and comp. Ezekiel 16:20, 21, from which it appears that the children were first slain before being "caused to pass through the fire.")
