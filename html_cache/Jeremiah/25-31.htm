Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The tumult will resound to the ends of the earth
This phrase indicates a global impact, suggesting that the events described are not limited to a specific region but affect the entire world. In the context of Jeremiah, this tumult refers to the judgment God is pronouncing through the prophet. The imagery of a resounding tumult can be connected to other biblical passages that describe God's voice or actions as powerful and far-reaching, such as Psalm 29:3-9, where the voice of the Lord is described as mighty and majestic. The phrase also foreshadows the eschatological themes found in the New Testament, where the final judgment is depicted as a global event (Revelation 14:6-7).

because the LORD brings a charge against the nations
Here, the LORD is portrayed as a divine judge bringing a legal charge against the nations. This reflects the covenantal relationship between God and humanity, where God holds nations accountable for their actions. The concept of God as a judge is prevalent throughout the Old Testament, as seen in passages like Psalm 82:1-8, where God judges among the "gods" or rulers. The nations' accountability underscores the universal sovereignty of God, who is not limited to Israel but is the ruler of all creation. This charge against the nations can also be seen as a precursor to the final judgment described in the New Testament (Matthew 25:31-46).

He brings judgment on all mankind
This phrase emphasizes the comprehensive nature of God's judgment, extending to all humanity. It reflects the biblical theme of God's impartiality, as seen in Romans 2:6-11, where God is described as rendering to each person according to their deeds, without favoritism. The universality of this judgment highlights the seriousness of sin and the need for repentance. It also points to the ultimate fulfillment of God's justice, which is a recurring theme in prophetic literature.

and puts the wicked to the sword,’” declares the LORD.
The imagery of the sword is often used in the Bible to symbolize divine judgment and warfare. In this context, it signifies the execution of God's righteous judgment against the wicked. This phrase can be linked to other prophetic declarations of judgment, such as Isaiah 66:16, where the LORD is described as executing judgment by fire and sword. The declaration by the LORD underscores the certainty and authority of the pronouncement. The sword as a tool of judgment also foreshadows the New Testament depiction of Christ as the one who will ultimately judge the living and the dead (Revelation 19:15).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The sovereign God of Israel, who is depicted as the ultimate judge over all nations.

2. The Nations
Refers to all the peoples and kingdoms of the earth, not just Israel, indicating the universal scope of God's judgment.

3. The Wicked
Those who oppose God and His ways, representing individuals and nations that live in rebellion against Him.

4. The Sword
A symbol of divine judgment and warfare, indicating the means by which God will execute His judgment.

5. Jeremiah
The prophet through whom God delivers this message of impending judgment.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nations
God is not just the God of Israel but the God of all nations. His authority extends to the ends of the earth, and He holds all accountable.

The Reality of Divine Judgment
Judgment is a central theme in Scripture. It serves as a warning to the wicked and a call to repentance for all.

The Call to Righteousness
Believers are called to live righteously, knowing that God will judge the wicked. Our lives should reflect His holiness and justice.

The Urgency of the Gospel
Understanding the reality of God's judgment should compel us to share the Gospel with urgency, knowing that many are still in rebellion against God.

Hope in God's Justice
While judgment is sobering, it also brings hope, as it assures us that God will ultimately set things right and vindicate His people.

What Does Jeremiah 25:31 Mean
The tumult will resound to the ends of the earth

“The tumult will resound to the ends of the earth…” (Jeremiah 25:31)

• Jeremiah pictures a worldwide roar, an uproar so vast that no corner is untouched, echoing the chaos of divine judgment described in Isaiah 24:17-20 and Revelation 6:12-17.

• This is not localized turmoil but a global shaking, aligning with Jesus’ words in Matthew 24:29 that “the powers of the heavens will be shaken.”

• The phrase reminds us that God’s actions reverberate universally; no nation or individual can remain insulated when He moves (Amos 3:6-8).

because the LORD brings a charge against the nations.

“…because the LORD brings a charge against the nations.”

• Here God is portrayed as the righteous Judge, formally indicting the nations, just as He does in Micah 6:1-2 and Romans 3:19.

• The “charge” underscores accountability: every people—no matter their power, culture, or borders—must answer to the same moral law (Psalm 9:7-8).

• It recalls the courtroom scenes of Isaiah 1:2-4, where heaven and earth are summoned as witnesses to humanity’s rebellion.

He brings judgment on all mankind

“He brings judgment on all mankind…”

• The scope broadens from “nations” to “all mankind,” confirming the universality of sin and the certainty of divine reckoning (Romans 3:23; Acts 17:31).

• Unlike human courts, God’s judgment is perfectly just and utterly inescapable (Psalm 96:13).

• This truth presses believers to sober reflection, while offering hope that wrongs will ultimately be set right (Revelation 20:11-12).

and puts the wicked to the sword,’” declares the LORD.

“…and puts the wicked to the sword,’” declares the LORD.”

• The sword symbolizes decisive, often violent, judgment (Ezekiel 14:17; Revelation 19:15).

• God’s holiness demands that persistent wickedness be confronted; patience does not cancel justice (Nahum 1:2-3).

• The “wicked” are those who resist God’s ways; His sword separates righteousness from rebellion, fulfilling the warning of Proverbs 11:21 that “the wicked will not go unpunished.”

summary

Jeremiah 25:31 paints a vivid, literal picture of global upheaval sparked by God’s righteous indictment. The tumult rings worldwide because the Lord’s courtroom encompasses every nation and every person. His universal judgment assures that sin will be answered, and His sword guarantees that entrenched wickedness will not prevail. While sobering, this verse also affirms God’s unwavering justice and faithfulness to set all things right—truths that anchor our hope and call us to faithful obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) A noise.--i.e., the tumult of an advancing army (Isaiah 13:4; Isaiah 17:12).

A controversy.--The term properly denotes a legal process, like the "pleading" of Jeremiah 2:9; Jeremiah 2:35, rather than a debate or discussion, and is therefore rightly followed by the technical term "will plead" or "judge." Jehovah appears, so to speak, as the Accuser in the suit in which He is also the supreme Judge.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - A noise. The word is used elsewhere for the tumultuous sound of a marching army (see Isaiah 13:4; Isaiah 17:12). He will plead; rather, he will hold judgment. Jehovah's "contending" sometimes involves the notion of punishing, e.g. Ezekiel 38:22; Isaiah 66:16. In 2 Chronicles 22:8, the same verb in the same conjugation is forcibly rendered in the Authorized Version, "to execute judgment."
