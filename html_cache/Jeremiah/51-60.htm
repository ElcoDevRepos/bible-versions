Jeremiah had written on a single scroll
Jeremiah's use of a scroll signifies the importance and permanence of the message. Scrolls were the primary medium for recording important documents in ancient times. This act of writing on a scroll underscores the prophetic authority and the divine origin of the message. The use of a single scroll also indicates the completeness and unity of the prophecy against Babylon.

about all the disaster that would come upon Babylon
The disaster refers to the impending judgment and destruction of Babylon, a major world power at the time. This prophecy is significant as Babylon was seen as invincible. The disaster foretold is a direct consequence of Babylon's pride, idolatry, and oppression of God's people. Historically, this prophecy was fulfilled when the Medes and Persians conquered Babylon in 539 BC.

all these words that had been written concerning Babylon
This phrase emphasizes the comprehensive nature of the prophecy. The words written are not just predictions but divine decrees that will surely come to pass. The phrase also suggests that there were previous prophecies concerning Babylon, highlighting the consistency and reliability of God's word. This connects to other scriptures where Babylon is a symbol of human pride and opposition to God, such as in Revelation, where Babylon represents the ultimate rebellion against God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his prophecies of judgment and restoration. He is the author of the Book of Jeremiah and is often called the "weeping prophet" due to his heartfelt laments over the sins of his people and the coming destruction.

2. Babylon
An ancient city and empire known for its wealth, power, and idolatry. In the context of Jeremiah, Babylon is the instrument of God's judgment against Judah but is also subject to God's judgment for its own sins.

3. Scroll
A physical medium for writing in ancient times, often made of papyrus or parchment. Jeremiah's use of a scroll signifies the formal and official nature of the prophecy against Babylon.

4. Disaster
Refers to the impending judgment and destruction that God has decreed against Babylon due to its pride, idolatry, and oppression.

5. Prophecy
The divine message delivered by Jeremiah, which includes both the judgment against Babylon and the eventual restoration of God's people.
Teaching Points
The Sovereignty of God
God's control over nations and history is evident in His ability to predict and bring about the fall of mighty empires like Babylon.

The Certainty of God's Word
Just as Jeremiah's prophecies were written down and fulfilled, we can trust in the reliability and truth of God's Word today.

Judgment and Justice
God's judgment on Babylon serves as a reminder that He will hold all nations and individuals accountable for their actions.

Hope and Restoration
Even in the midst of judgment, God's ultimate plan includes restoration and hope for His people.

The Role of the Prophet
Jeremiah's faithfulness in delivering God's message, despite opposition, challenges us to be bold in sharing God's truth.

What Does Jeremiah 51:60 Mean
Jeremiah had written

• Jeremiah is the human pen through whom God speaks (Jeremiah 1:9; 2 Peter 1:21).

• Earlier the LORD told him, “Write in a book all the words I have spoken to you” (Jeremiah 30:2), underscoring divine initiative and inerrancy.

• His obedience mirrors the pattern in Jeremiah 36, where he dictated God’s words to Baruch.

• Cross references highlight prophetic writing as God’s chosen means of preserving truth (Exodus 17:14; 2 Timothy 3:16).

on a single scroll

• The phrase stresses a deliberate, official record—nothing accidental or casual.

• Scroll imagery ties to earlier prophetic acts (Jeremiah 36:2–4) and to Revelation 5:1 where God’s scroll seals history.

• A single scroll implies completeness; everything necessary to know about Babylon’s fate is contained here.

about all the disaster that would come upon Babylon

• God’s justice targets Babylon’s idolatry and cruelty (Jeremiah 50:29; 51:24).

• Prophecies detail sudden downfall: “Babylon will fall and be shattered” (Jeremiah 51:8), fulfilled when Cyrus captured the city (Daniel 5:30–31).

• Isaiah 13:19–22 and Isaiah 47 echo the same doom, showing a consistent biblical witness.

• The New Testament looks forward to a future, ultimate Babylon judged in Revelation 18, demonstrating the pattern of divine retribution.

all these words that had been written concerning Babylon

• The repetition underscores reliability; nothing Jeremiah predicted would fail (Joshua 21:45; Jeremiah 1:12).

• God had already spoken about Babylon in Jeremiah 25:12–14; now it is gathered into one prophetic dossier.

• Every word matters—Jesus said, “Scripture cannot be broken” (John 10:35).

• The totality of God’s message offers comfort to the oppressed (Jeremiah 51:35–36) and a warning to any nation that exalts itself against Him (Proverbs 16:18).

summary

Jeremiah 51:60 shows the prophet faithfully recording, on a single authoritative scroll, God’s complete and inerrant verdict against Babylon. The verse highlights divine initiative, prophetic obedience, the certainty of judgment, and the trustworthiness of every written word. What God decreed for ancient Babylon He performed precisely, proving that His written promises—of justice and of salvation—remain unbreakable for every generation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(60) So Jeremiah wrote in a book.--The "book" is, as elsewhere, a parchment roll. Stress is laid on the fact that the long prophecy was all written on one roll, so that it might be a fitter symbol of the city that was its subject.
