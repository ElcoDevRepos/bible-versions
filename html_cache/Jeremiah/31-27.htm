Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“The days are coming,” declares the LORD
This phrase introduces a prophetic declaration, indicating a future event or series of events. In the context of Jeremiah, it often signals a shift from judgment to restoration. The phrase is used frequently in prophetic literature to denote the certainty of God's plans. It emphasizes God's sovereignty and the unfolding of His divine timeline. This future orientation is a common theme in the prophetic books, where God reveals His plans for His people.

“when I will sow the house of Israel and the house of Judah”
The imagery of sowing suggests planting and growth, symbolizing restoration and renewal. The "house of Israel" and the "house of Judah" refer to the divided kingdoms of Israel and Judah, which had been separated since the reign of Rehoboam. This promise of sowing indicates a reunification and a new beginning for both kingdoms. Historically, this reflects the hope for the return from exile and the re-establishment of the nation. The agricultural metaphor would resonate with an agrarian society, emphasizing God's role as the one who brings life and prosperity.

“with the seed of man and of beast.”
This phrase indicates a comprehensive restoration involving both people and animals, suggesting a return to a state of abundance and blessing. The inclusion of both "man and beast" highlights the totality of God's restoration, affecting all aspects of life. This can be seen as a reversal of the curses and desolation that had come upon the land due to disobedience. It echoes the creation mandate in Genesis, where God blesses humanity and the creatures of the earth. Theologically, it points to a holistic redemption that encompasses all creation, aligning with the broader biblical narrative of restoration.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant-keeping God of Israel, who speaks through the prophet Jeremiah, promising restoration and renewal.

2. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his messages of both judgment and hope to the people of Judah and Israel.

3. House of Israel
Refers to the northern kingdom, which had been exiled by the Assyrians. This promise indicates future restoration.

4. House of Judah
Refers to the southern kingdom, which was facing exile to Babylon. The promise of sowing indicates a future hope beyond their current circumstances.

5. Seed of Man and Beast
Symbolizes the restoration of population and prosperity, indicating a time of blessing and growth.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Restoration
God is in control of history and has a plan for restoration, even when circumstances seem dire.

Hope Beyond Judgment
Despite the judgment that Israel and Judah faced, God's promises offer hope and a future.

The Importance of Spiritual Renewal
Just as God promises physical restoration, He also desires spiritual renewal in our lives.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
The promise to sow the house of Israel and Judah reminds us of God's faithfulness to His covenant promises.

Multiplication as a Sign of Blessing
The increase of people and animals is a sign of God's blessing and favor, encouraging us to seek His blessing in our lives.

What Does Jeremiah 31:27 Mean
The days are coming

• The opening phrase signals a specific, divinely appointed future. It is the same prophetic marker used in Jeremiah 30:3 and 31:31, both of which announce restoration and the New Covenant.

• Because the Lord Himself fixes these “days,” their arrival is certain; time cannot frustrate His purpose (cf. Isaiah 46:9-10; Habakkuk 2:3).

• Practically, this assures readers that every promise yet unfulfilled will be honored in its proper season, reinforcing our confidence in the entire biblical record.

declares the LORD

• The statement’s authority rests on the covenant-keeping God who cannot lie (Numbers 23:19; Titus 1:2).

• Jeremiah’s frequent “declares the LORD” (31 times in chapter 31 alone) underlines that the prophet is not offering personal optimism but transmitting God’s sworn word (Jeremiah 1:12).

• Believers can therefore treat the prophecy as already settled, awaiting only its visible outworking (cf. Isaiah 55:11).

when I will sow

• Sowing is intentional, patient, and productive. The same God who previously “plucked up” and “tore down” (Jeremiah 31:28) now takes up the farmer’s role to re-plant.

• Hosea 2:23: “I will sow her for Myself in the land” shows that sowing involves ownership and care; the people are God’s crop.

• Zechariah 10:9 echoes this: “Though I sow them among the nations, they will remember Me.” What was once judgment (scattering) becomes restoration (planting).

• The imagery guarantees not merely survival after exile but flourishing—God is investing in a bountiful future for His people.

the house of Israel and the house of Judah

• Both northern (Israel) and southern (Judah) kingdoms are named, proving that no tribe is forgotten (Jeremiah 30:3).

• The phrase anticipates the reunification later promised in Jeremiah 31:31-34, where one New Covenant binds the two houses together.

• Historically, return from Babylon (Ezra/Nehemiah) offered a preview; prophetically, Romans 11:26-29 points to a final, nationwide salvation and regathering.

• God’s fidelity to the literal descendants of Jacob undergirds His trustworthiness to the church grafted into those promises (Romans 11:17-24).

with the seed of man and of beast

• “Seed” stresses multiplication: abundant people and thriving livestock. Ezekiel 36:11 parallels this: “I will multiply upon you man and beast… and I will make you inhabited as in former times.”

• In an agricultural society, animals signal prosperity, peace, and covenant blessing (Deuteronomy 28:4).

• The pairing of human and animal life shows that God restores the whole ecosystem of covenant life—families, herds, fields, worship—nothing left barren (Ezekiel 36:37-38).

• Literally fulfilled in post-exilic Judea, partially seen in modern Israel’s resurgence, and ultimately completed in the millennial kingdom when Messiah reigns (Isaiah 65:21-25).

summary

Jeremiah 31:27 promises that a future, God-appointed day will see the Lord personally repopulate and prosper both Israel and Judah. He stakes His own name on this pledge, guarantees a full harvest of people and livestock, and thus assures believers of His unwavering commitment to every covenant word.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(27) I will sow the house of Israel . . .--The same image of a fertile and happy population appears in Hosea 2:23; Zechariah 10:9; Ezekiel 36:9-11. It will be noted that it embraces both Israel and Judah, which had once been rivals, each watching the increase of the other with jealousy and suspicion.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 27-30. - The physical side of the Messianic blessing. Its effect upon the heart of the pardoned sinners will be such that they will fully recognize the justice of the Divine judgments. There will no longer be any room for a certain favourite proverb; the death of a sinner will be universally acknowledged to be the reward of his personal sin (Keil). Verse 27. - I will sow, etc. The passage may be illustrated by Isaiah 26:18, where the Church of the restored exiles is represented as complaining that the land (of Judah) has not been brought into a state of security, and that inhabitants (in sufficient numbers) have not been begotten. Similarly here, only the tone of complaint is wanting. The thought has suggested itself - Will the Israelites of the latter days be sufficient to fill up the land? Yes, is the answer of revelation; for Jehovah will perform a wonder, and make the people and their cattle so prolific that it will seem as if children and young cattle grew up like plants.
