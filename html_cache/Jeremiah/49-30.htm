Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Run! Escape quickly!
This urgent call to flee indicates imminent danger and the need for immediate action. In the biblical context, such calls are often associated with divine warnings, as seen in the story of Lot's escape from Sodom (Genesis 19:17). The urgency reflects the seriousness of the threat posed by the Babylonian invasion.

Lie low, O residents of Hazor,
Hazor, in this context, refers to a region in Arabia, not the more famous city in northern Israel. The residents are advised to hide, suggesting that their usual defenses or strategies would be insufficient against the coming threat. This reflects a common biblical theme where human strength is inadequate without divine intervention.

declares the LORD,
This phrase emphasizes the authority and certainty of the message. It is not merely a human prediction but a divine proclamation. The use of "declares the LORD" is a common prophetic formula that underscores the reliability of the prophecy, as seen throughout the books of the prophets.

for Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon
Nebuchadnezzar was a powerful ruler known for his military conquests and the expansion of the Babylonian Empire. His campaigns are well-documented both in the Bible and in historical records. His role as an instrument of God's judgment is a recurring theme, as seen in the prophecies against other nations (Jeremiah 25:9).

has drawn up a plan against you;
This indicates a deliberate and strategic approach by Nebuchadnezzar, suggesting that the attack is not impulsive but carefully considered. The notion of divine sovereignty is implicit here, as God uses the plans of earthly kings to fulfill His purposes, similar to how He used Assyria against Israel (Isaiah 10:5-6).

he has devised a strategy against you.
The repetition of the idea of planning and strategy underscores the seriousness of the threat. It highlights the inevitability of the coming judgment, as human plans are often used by God to accomplish His divine will. This mirrors the biblical theme of God’s control over the nations, as seen in Proverbs 21:1, where the king's heart is in the hand of the LORD.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his messages of warning and hope to the people of Judah and surrounding nations.

2. Hazor
An ancient city in the northern part of Canaan, significant in biblical history and often associated with judgment due to its idolatry and opposition to God's people.

3. Nebuchadnezzar
The king of Babylon, known for his conquests and the Babylonian exile of the Jewish people. He is depicted as an instrument of God's judgment against nations.

4. Babylon
A powerful empire during the time of Jeremiah, often symbolizing human pride and opposition to God in biblical literature.

5. The LORD
The covenant name of God, Yahweh, who is sovereign over all nations and uses them to accomplish His purposes.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nations
God is in control of all nations and uses them to fulfill His divine purposes. We can trust in His ultimate plan, even when world events seem chaotic.

The Urgency of Obedience
The call to "run" and "escape quickly" underscores the urgency of responding to God's warnings. We should be quick to obey God's instructions in our lives.

The Reality of Divine Judgment
Just as Hazor faced judgment, we are reminded that God is just and will hold nations and individuals accountable. This should lead us to repentance and faithfulness.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Jeremiah were God's messengers, calling people to repentance and warning of impending judgment. We should heed the messages of Scripture and those who faithfully teach it today.

What Does Jeremiah 49:30 Mean
Run!

Jeremiah opens with a single, urgent verb: “Run!” The people are not told to negotiate, resist, or linger. Immediate flight is the only faithful response when God announces judgment (Genesis 19:17; Matthew 24:15-16). The call underscores both God’s mercy—He warns before He strikes—and His holiness—He will not delay forever (2 Peter 3:9-10).

Escape quickly!

Speed matters. In Proverbs 6:5 Solomon urges, “Free yourself, like a gazelle from the hunter.” Likewise Paul counsels, “Flee from idolatry” (1 Corinthians 10:14). Delay invites destruction. When God sounds the alarm, procrastination is rebellion in slow motion.

Lie low, O residents of Hazor

“Lie low” pictures taking cover, finding shelter, becoming inconspicuous. Isaiah 26:20 offers a similar refuge: “Come, my people, enter your rooms…hide yourselves for a little while.” Hazor’s nomadic tribes had enjoyed open spaces and apparent security, yet God now tells them to seek concealment. Even the most self-reliant community cannot stand when the Almighty moves (Psalm 33:16-17).

Declares the LORD

The oracle carries divine signature. When Jeremiah says, “declares the LORD,” he reminds hearers that these words originate from the covenant-keeping God, the same voice that created worlds (Jeremiah 1:9; Isaiah 40:8). Ignoring the prophet would mean ignoring God Himself.

For Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon has drawn up a plan against you

Here is the reason for haste: God is raising up Babylon as His instrument of discipline (Jeremiah 27:6; Habakkuk 1:5-7). Nebuchadnezzar’s military strategy is ultimately God’s sovereign strategy (Daniel 2:37-38). The Lord often uses earthly powers to accomplish heavenly purposes, proving that “the king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD” (Proverbs 21:1).

He has devised a strategy against you

The verb “devised” communicates calculation and certainty. Babylon’s plan will not misfire. Yet behind Babylon’s blueprint stands the unthwartable counsel of God (Isaiah 14:24-27; Psalm 33:10-11). For the faithful remnant, this truth offers comfort: history is not random; every “strategy” is either restrained or released by the Sovereign Lord (Romans 8:28).

summary

Jeremiah 49:30 is a trumpet blast of mercy-mingled judgment. God lovingly warns Hazor to flee, quickly and quietly, because He has authorized Babylon’s calculated invasion. The passage highlights the urgency of obedience, the futility of self-reliance, and the absolute authority of God’s word and plan. When God says “Run,” wise hearts do not walk.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(30) Dwell deep.--See Note on Jeremiah 49:8. The dwellers in the villages of Hazor are told, as those of Dedan had been, to flee into the furthest recesses of the wilderness. The words probably point to the time after the battle of Carchemish, when Nebuchadnezzar established his sovereignty over the lower Euphrates, Northern Arabia, and the Syrian desert.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 30. - The prophet turns to the Arabs in villages who have still more to tempt the cupidity of plunderers, and urges them to flee while there is still time. Dwell deep (see on ver. 8). Against you. This is the reading of the Septuagint (Alex. MS.), the Targum, the Vulgate, and many extant Hebrew manuscripts. The received text, however, has "against them." Such alternations of person have met us again and again, and there is no occasion to doubt the ordinary reading.
