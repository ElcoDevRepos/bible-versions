Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But they did not listen or incline their ears;
This phrase highlights the persistent disobedience of the people of Judah. Throughout the book of Jeremiah, the prophet repeatedly calls the people to repentance, yet they consistently refuse to heed his warnings. This reflects a broader biblical theme of human stubbornness and rebellion against God, seen in passages like Isaiah 6:9-10 and Ezekiel 12:2. The phrase "incline their ears" suggests an intentional act of listening, which the people deliberately chose not to do, indicating a hardened heart.

they did not turn from their wickedness
The refusal to turn from wickedness underscores the moral and spiritual decline of Judah. The term "wickedness" encompasses idolatry, injustice, and immorality, which were rampant in the society. This is consistent with the covenantal language found in Deuteronomy, where blessings and curses are contingent upon obedience to God's laws. The call to "turn" is a call to repentance, a central theme in prophetic literature, emphasizing the need for a change of heart and behavior.

or stop burning incense to other gods.
Burning incense to other gods was a common practice in ancient Near Eastern religions and was strictly forbidden in the Mosaic Law (Exodus 20:3-5). This act of idolatry was not just a religious offense but a breach of the covenant relationship with Yahweh. The worship of other gods often involved syncretism, blending the worship of Yahweh with pagan practices, which was prevalent in Judah despite repeated warnings from prophets like Jeremiah. This idolatry is a direct violation of the first commandment and is seen as spiritual adultery, drawing parallels to the unfaithfulness of Israel as depicted in Hosea.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Listening to God
God repeatedly calls His people to listen and obey. Ignoring His voice leads to spiritual and often physical consequences.

The Dangers of Idolatry
Idolatry is not just the worship of physical idols but can include anything that takes the place of God in our lives. We must examine our hearts for modern-day idols.

Repentance as a Turning Point
True repentance involves a change of heart and direction. It is not enough to feel remorse; we must actively turn away from sin.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Persistent disobedience leads to judgment. God's patience is immense, but it is not without limits.

The Role of Prophets and Scripture
Prophets like Jeremiah were sent to guide and warn. Today, Scripture serves as our guide, and we must heed its warnings.

What Does Jeremiah 44:5 Mean
But they did not listen

• God had repeatedly spoken through Jeremiah, yet the people “turned a stubborn shoulder” (Jeremiah 19:15; cf. Jeremiah 7:13–14, 25:4, 2 Chronicles 36:15–16).

• Listening in Scripture always implies obedience; ignoring His voice reveals a hard heart.

• The same contrast appears in John 10:27 where Jesus says, “My sheep listen to My voice,” showing that true relationship with God is marked by responsive hearing.

• Refusing to listen cut the nation off from the divine protection promised in Exodus 19:5–6.

or incline their ears

• “Incline” pictures a deliberate leaning in (Jeremiah 17:23). They would not so much as tilt an ear toward the Lord.

• Zechariah 7:11 describes the same attitude: “They turned a stubborn shoulder; they stopped their ears from hearing.”

• Stephen accuses the Sanhedrin of this exact posture in Acts 7:57, illustrating that the problem persisted into New Testament times.

• Willful deafness to God is never neutral; it hardens the conscience and invites judgment (Hebrews 3:12–13).

they did not turn from their wickedness

• God’s call was not merely to hear but to repent (Jeremiah 18:11; 26:3).

• “Turning” captures the Hebrew idea of an about-face; 2 Chronicles 7:14 links this turning to healing of the land.

• Ezekiel 18:30–32 underscores that repentance is the avenue to life: “Repent and turn from all your transgressions… Why should you die?”.

• Their refusal showed they loved darkness more than light (John 3:19).

or stop burning incense to other gods

• Idolatry was the outward symptom of inward rebellion (Jeremiah 11:12–13; 19:4).

• The first commandment—“You shall have no other gods before Me” (Exodus 20:3-5)—had been brazenly violated.

• Paul warns believers to “flee from idolatry” (1 Corinthians 10:14), proving the timeless relevance of this issue.

• Isaiah 42:8 reminds us God will not share His glory; persistent incense to false gods demands divine action.

summary

Jeremiah 44:5 exposes a fourfold refusal—no listening, no leaning in, no turning, and no abandoning idols. Each step reveals escalating hardness, moving the people farther from God’s mercy and nearer to judgment. The verse stands as a sober reminder that genuine hearing of God’s Word must lead to repentance and exclusive worship, lest hearts grow deaf and disaster follow.
