Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Then Jeremiah took another scroll
Jeremiah, a major prophet, is instructed by God to take another scroll after the first was destroyed. This act signifies the enduring nature of God's word, which cannot be extinguished by human actions. Scrolls were the primary medium for writing during this period, made from papyrus or parchment, emphasizing the labor-intensive process of recording God's messages.

and gave it to the scribe Baruch son of Neriah
Baruch, a faithful scribe and companion of Jeremiah, plays a crucial role in documenting the prophet's messages. Scribes were essential in ancient times for recording and preserving important texts. Baruch's lineage, being the son of Neriah, suggests a family of some standing, possibly with connections to the temple or royal court, highlighting the importance of his role.

and at Jeremiah’s dictation he wrote on it all the words of the scroll
This phrase underscores the divine inspiration of the scriptures, as Jeremiah dictates the words given by God. The process of dictation and transcription ensures the accuracy and preservation of the prophetic message. This mirrors the process by which many biblical texts were composed, emphasizing the collaborative effort between prophet and scribe.

that Jehoiakim king of Judah had burned in the fire
King Jehoiakim's act of burning the scroll represents a rejection of God's word and a defiance of divine authority. This historical event reflects the political and spiritual climate of Judah at the time, where leaders often resisted prophetic warnings. The burning of the scroll is a symbolic act of rebellion against God, yet it also sets the stage for the reaffirmation of His word.

And many similar words were added to them
The addition of "many similar words" indicates that God's message is not only preserved but expanded. This suggests that the destruction of the first scroll did not hinder the prophetic mission but rather enriched it. Theologically, this can be seen as a testament to God's sovereignty and the inexhaustible nature of His revelation. The expansion of the message may also include further warnings, promises, or insights relevant to the people of Judah, reinforcing the comprehensive nature of God's communication through His prophets.

Teaching Points
The Indestructibility of God's Word
Despite human efforts to suppress or destroy it, God's word remains eternal and unchanging. This should encourage believers to trust in the reliability and permanence of Scripture.

The Role of Faithful Servants
Like Baruch, believers are called to faithfully preserve and proclaim God's word, even in the face of opposition or danger.

The Consequences of Rejecting God's Word
Jehoiakim's actions serve as a warning of the spiritual peril that comes from rejecting divine revelation. Believers should heed God's word with reverence and obedience.

God's Sovereignty in Revelation
God's message will be delivered and fulfilled regardless of human resistance. This underscores the sovereignty of God in ensuring His purposes are accomplished.

The Expansion of Revelation
The addition of "many similar words" to the rewritten scroll suggests that God's revelation is not only preserved but can also be expanded to address new circumstances and needs.

What Does Jeremiah 36:32 Mean
Then Jeremiah took another scroll

Jeremiah 36 has just recorded King Jehoiakim’s shocking act of slicing up the prophet’s first scroll and tossing it into the brazier (Jeremiah 36:23). Yet the first words that follow are, “Then Jeremiah took another scroll.”

• God’s word is never silenced by human rejection; when one copy is destroyed, the Lord provides another (Isaiah 40:8; Matthew 24:35).

• Jeremiah’s immediate obedience models perseverance. He doesn’t argue or despair—he acts. Compare Elijah’s renewed mission after discouragement (1 Kings 19:15–18).

• For us, this reminds us to keep speaking biblical truth even when it’s met with hostility (Acts 5:29).

and gave it to the scribe Baruch son of Neriah

Jeremiah entrusts the fresh scroll to Baruch, his loyal assistant.

• Baruch’s faithfulness is spotlighted elsewhere (Jeremiah 32:12; 45:1–5). He stays with Jeremiah through opposition, illustrating the value of godly partnership (Ecclesiastes 4:9–12; Philippians 2:20–22).

• Scripture often highlights the ordinary servants God uses—scribes, messengers, tentmakers—to advance His word (Ezra 7:6; 2 Timothy 4:11–13).

• Baruch’s dependable character contrasts Jehoiakim’s arrogance, underscoring the blessing of humble service.

and at Jeremiah’s dictation he wrote on it all the words of the scroll

The phrase captures the process of divine inspiration at work.

• Jeremiah speaks; Baruch writes. The ultimate Author is God (2 Peter 1:21; 2 Timothy 3:16).

• The Lord can transmit His exact message through human lips and pens without error (Exodus 34:27; Revelation 1:11, 19).

• Accuracy matters: “all the words” signals a complete, faithful reproduction, challenging any notion that Scripture is merely approximate.

that Jehoiakim king of Judah had burned in the fire

Jehoiakim’s act was a deliberate rejection of God’s call to repent (Jeremiah 36:25–26).

• Burning the scroll symbolized burning the covenant, echoing earlier kings’ disregard for the Law (2 Chronicles 36:5, 12–13).

• God answers contempt not with silence but with further revelation—and impending judgment (Jeremiah 36:30–31).

• Modern parallels: cultures or individuals may dismiss Scripture, yet God’s truth remains intact and will judge them (John 12:48; Acts 7:51–52).

And many similar words were added to them

Far from being curtailed, the prophetic message expands.

• God not only restores what was lost but adds “many similar words”—likely further warnings and promises (Jeremiah 30:2; 31:31–34).

• Divine severity and mercy intertwine: additional words reinforce both judgment on sin and hope for the remnant (Jeremiah 29:10–14).

• The episode foreshadows the completeness of the canon: what God intends to reveal, He reveals in full despite opposition (Revelation 22:6).

• Practical takeaway: attempts to edit or suppress Scripture often result in even clearer witness from God (Philippians 1:12–14).

summary

Jeremiah 36:32 shows that God’s word endures, expands, and accomplishes His purpose despite human resistance. Jeremiah’s swift obedience, Baruch’s faithful service, and the Lord’s decisive response to Jehoiakim together teach us that Scripture is indestructible, fully inspired, and ever relevant. Troubles may burn pages, but they cannot extinguish the truth the pages carry.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(32) And there were added besides unto them many like words.--The passage is interesting as showing, as it were, the genesis of the present volume of the prophet's writings. The discourse delivered in the Temple court was, in modern phrase, revised and enlarged, dictated to Baruch as before, and in this shape has probably come down to us in Jeremiah 25.
