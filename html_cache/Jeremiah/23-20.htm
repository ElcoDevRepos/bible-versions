Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The anger of the LORD will not turn back
This phrase emphasizes the certainty and inevitability of God's judgment. In the context of Jeremiah, the prophet is addressing the false prophets and corrupt leaders of Judah who have led the people astray. The anger of the LORD is a recurring theme in the Old Testament, often associated with divine justice and righteousness. It reflects God's response to sin and rebellion, as seen in passages like Deuteronomy 29:27-28 and Isaiah 5:25. The idea of God's anger not turning back until His purposes are fulfilled underscores His sovereignty and the seriousness of His covenant with Israel.

until He has fully accomplished the purposes of His heart
This part of the verse highlights God's intentionality and determination. The "purposes of His heart" refer to God's divine plan and will, which are ultimately for the good of His people, even if they involve judgment. This aligns with the broader biblical narrative where God's plans are always fulfilled, as seen in Isaiah 46:10 and Romans 8:28. The phrase suggests that God's actions are not arbitrary but are part of a larger redemptive plan, which includes both judgment and restoration.

In the days to come you will understand this clearly
This prophetic statement points to a future time when the people will recognize and comprehend God's actions and purposes. It implies that current events may be confusing or misunderstood, but clarity will come with time. This future understanding is a common theme in prophetic literature, where immediate events are often seen as part of a larger eschatological framework. The phrase can be connected to the New Testament, where the full revelation of God's plan is seen in Jesus Christ, as in Ephesians 1:9-10. It also suggests a future hope and the eventual vindication of God's justice and mercy.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A major prophet in the Old Testament, known for his prophecies concerning the judgment and restoration of Israel. He is often called the "weeping prophet" due to his deep sorrow over the sins of his people.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who is portrayed as just and righteous, executing judgment on His people for their disobedience but also promising restoration.

3. Judah
The southern kingdom of Israel, which was facing impending judgment due to its persistent idolatry and rebellion against God.

4. False Prophets
Individuals in Jeremiah's time who falsely claimed to speak for God, leading the people astray with messages of peace when judgment was imminent.

5. Babylonian Exile
The event of Judah's captivity and exile to Babylon, which was a fulfillment of God's judgment as prophesied by Jeremiah.
Teaching Points
Understanding God's Judgment
God's anger is not arbitrary but is directed towards fulfilling His righteous purposes. Believers should seek to understand God's character and His reasons for judgment.

The Certainty of God's Plans
Just as God's purposes were accomplished in Jeremiah's time, His plans for the world and for individuals are certain and will come to pass.

The Role of Prophets
True prophets speak God's truth, even when it is difficult. Believers should discern and heed messages that align with Scripture.

Hope in Future Understanding
While God's actions may be difficult to comprehend now, there is a promise of future clarity. Trust in God's wisdom and timing is essential.

Repentance and Restoration
God's judgment is often a call to repentance. Believers should examine their lives and turn back to God, trusting in His promise of restoration.

What Does Jeremiah 23:20 Mean
The anger of the LORD

“The anger of the LORD…” (Jeremiah 23:20a)

• God’s wrath is always righteous—never capricious. It responds to sin, here especially the deception of false prophets (Jeremiah 23:9-18; cf. Romans 1:18; Nahum 1:2).

• Scripture shows that divine anger is an expression of holiness: He defends His name and His people (Isaiah 42:8; Deuteronomy 32:21-22).

• For believers today, this reminds us that grace never nullifies God’s moral standards (Hebrews 12:28-29).

will not turn back

“…will not turn back…” (Jeremiah 23:20a)

• The phrase underscores resolve: God does not abandon judgment once it has begun (Ezekiel 7:8-9; Lamentations 3:37-38).

• His constancy reassures us that He keeps every word, whether promise or warning (Numbers 23:19; Revelation 15:3).

• Practical takeaway: sin cannot be managed by delay or denial; repentance is the only safe response (Proverbs 28:13).

until He has fully accomplished the purposes of His heart

“…until He has fully accomplished the purposes of His heart.” (Jeremiah 23:20a)

• God’s plans are comprehensive; nothing is left half-finished (Isaiah 46:10; Philippians 1:6).

• “Purposes” here include purging corruption from Judah and restoring a remnant (Jeremiah 24:5-7).

• The cross references highlight that His redemptive and disciplinary goals are intertwined (Hebrews 12:10-11; Ephesians 1:11).

• Application: even severe providences serve a benevolent, sovereign design for His people’s good and His glory (Romans 8:28).

In the days to come

“In the days to come…” (Jeremiah 23:20b)

• A prophetic time marker: near-term fulfillment in Judah’s exile and long-term echoes in the Messiah’s reign (Jeremiah 30:24; Hosea 3:5).

• It reminds us that history is linear, moving toward appointed endpoints set by God (Acts 17:31).

• Encouragement: what seems delayed to us is perfectly timed by Him (2 Peter 3:9).

you will understand this clearly

“…you will understand this clearly.” (Jeremiah 23:20b)

• Hindsight would reveal both the justice and mercy in God’s actions (Jeremiah 31:18-19).

• Revelation of truth is ultimately a gift from God (Proverbs 2:6; John 16:13).

• For believers, the Spirit illuminates Scripture and history so that we discern God’s hand and grow in reverent trust (1 Corinthians 2:12-13).

summary

Jeremiah 23:20 assures us that God’s holy anger is real, purposeful, and unwavering until His redemptive objectives are met. Though judgment may seem harsh, it is guided by a heart committed to justice, restoration, and ultimate clarity for His people. Recognizing this steadies our faith: God finishes what He starts, times events perfectly, and eventually makes His ways plain to all who seek Him in humility and obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) Shall not return . . .--i.e., shall not turn back from its purpose. Men should look back on it in the "latter days"--literally, the end of the days (Genesis 49:1; Numbers 24:14; Deuteronomy 4:30; Deuteronomy 31:29), i.e., in the then distant future of the exile and the return--and should see that it had done its work both of chastisement and discipline. (Comp. Ezekiel 14:22-23.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - The anger of the Lord. The prophet's interpretation of the image. It is the judicial anger of Jehovah, personified as Divine manifestations so often are (hence "shall not return"). The form of the verse reminds us of Isaiah 55:11. In the latter days; rather, in future days, as Dr. Henderson rightly renders. It seems better to restrict the term "latter days" to the Messianic period ("the coming age," Matthew 12:32), to which, in fact, it is often applied (e.g. Isaiah 2:2; Hosea 3:5). The phrase in itself simply means "in the sequel of the days," i.e. in the future; its Messianic reference, when this exists, is inferred solely from the context. In the passage before us, and in Deuteronomy 4:30, 30:29, there can be no intention of pointing to the Messianic age. Precisely the same phrase occurs in an Assyrian inscription, where its meaning is clear from the context (aria akhrat yumi irib, "For a sequel of days - i.e., for a future time - I deposited"). In the present case it is no distant period to which the prophet refers, for he continues, Ye shall consider it, etc., or rather, ye shall understand it clearly, viz. that the calamities which will have come upon you are the Divine judgment upon your sins.
