At the beginning of the reign of Zedekiah son of Josiah king of Judah
Zedekiah, the last king of Judah, reigned from 597 to 586 BC. He was placed on the throne by Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon after the exile of Jehoiachin. Zedekiah was a son of Josiah, a king known for his religious reforms and dedication to the LORD. The beginning of Zedekiah's reign marks a tumultuous period in Judah's history, characterized by political instability and looming Babylonian threat. This context is crucial as it sets the stage for Jeremiah's prophetic ministry, which often warned of impending judgment due to the nation's disobedience.

this word came to Jeremiah from the LORD
Jeremiah, a prophet called by God, served during the final years of Judah before the Babylonian exile. The phrase "this word came" indicates divine revelation, emphasizing that Jeremiah's messages were not his own but were directly from the LORD. This underscores the authority and authenticity of his prophecies. Throughout the book, Jeremiah is portrayed as a reluctant yet faithful messenger, often facing persecution for delivering God's warnings. The phrase also highlights the continuity of God's communication with His people, even in times of rebellion and impending judgment.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Leadership
God appoints leaders and uses them to fulfill His purposes, even when they are unaware or disobedient. Believers should trust in God's ultimate control over political and spiritual leaders.

The Importance of Obedience
Zedekiah's reign serves as a cautionary tale about the consequences of disobedience to God's commands. Obedience to God is crucial for personal and communal blessing.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Jeremiah are God's mouthpieces, delivering messages that may be unpopular but are vital for spiritual health. We should heed God's word, even when it challenges us.

Historical Context and Its Lessons
Understanding the historical context of biblical events helps us see the relevance of God's word in our lives today. History teaches us about the faithfulness of God and the dangers of straying from His path.

Faithfulness Amidst Opposition
Jeremiah's perseverance in delivering God's message despite opposition encourages believers to remain faithful in their own callings, trusting in God's support and vindication.

What Does Jeremiah 27:1 Mean
At the beginning

• Marks a precise moment—Jeremiah wants us to picture the very start of a new reign.

• Early days often shape the direction of a king’s rule (cf. 1 Kings 12:1–15 for Rehoboam’s first decisions).

• Jeremiah’s timely message shows God’s concern to speak before policies harden.

of the reign of Zedekiah

• Zedekiah ruled 597–586 BC, installed by Nebuchadnezzar (2 Kings 24:17).

• His throne was politically fragile, under Babylonian oversight (Jeremiah 27:3).

• God addresses him at a crossroads: submit to Babylon and live, rebel and perish (Jeremiah 27:8, 12).

son of Josiah king of Judah

• The reference ties Zedekiah to a godly heritage—Josiah had led sweeping reforms (2 Kings 23:25).

• Highlights contrast: a righteous father, yet a son who “did evil in the sight of the LORD” (2 Chronicles 36:12).

• Reminds readers that lineage alone cannot guarantee faithfulness (Ezekiel 18:5–20).

this word came to Jeremiah

• Underscores prophetic authority; Jeremiah is not offering personal opinion.

• Continues the pattern begun in his call: “The word of the LORD came to me” (Jeremiah 1:4; cf. 25:1–3).

• God’s Word interrupts national life, delivering warning and hope.

from the LORD

• Final phrase grounds everything in divine origin—YHWH speaks, nations must heed (Jeremiah 10:10).

• Affirms Scripture’s reliability: what follows is historically anchored and spiritually binding (2 Peter 1:21).

• Sets the stage for chapters 27–29, where God directs international events.

summary

Jeremiah 27:1 pinpoints the moment—right as Zedekiah ascends Judah’s throne—when God breaks in with clear instruction. The verse links a fragile political transition, a king with a mixed legacy, and a prophet bearing God’s authoritative word. It reminds us that every new season of leadership is under the Lord’s scrutiny and guided by His unchanging truth.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
XXVII.

(1) In the beginning of the reign of Jehoiakim.--The mention of the name of Zedekiah as king of Judah in Jeremiah 27:3 shows that the Hebrew text has here perpetuated an error, due probably to the transcriber or first editor of the collected prophecies. We have to think, accordingly, of the state of things which followed on the death of Jehoiakim, and the deposition and exile of Jehoiachin. The tone of the prophecy seems to indicate a time about the middle of Zedekiah's reign. His position was that of a tributary sovereign, subject to Nebuchadnezzar. He and the neighbouring kings, who were in a like position, had not quite renounced the hope of throwing off the yoke, and asserting their independence.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - In the beginning of the reign of Jehoiakim. The Syriao substitutes for, "Jehoiakim" "Zedekiah," to bring the passage into conformity with Jeremiah 28:1, where the fourth year of the reign of Zedekiah is expressly mentioned. But is this emendation sufficient? Can the fourth year be called the beginning of the reign of Zedekiah," When that reign lasted altogether only eleven years? Is it not probable that the transcriber has inadvertently copied the heading of Jeremiah 26, which corresponds verbally with Jeremiah 27:1, except that "unto Jeremiah" is wanting?
