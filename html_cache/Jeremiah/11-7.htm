Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
For from the time I brought your fathers out of the land of Egypt
This phrase refers to the Exodus, a foundational event in Israel's history when God delivered the Israelites from slavery in Egypt. This event is recorded in the book of Exodus and is central to Jewish identity and faith. It signifies God's power and faithfulness in fulfilling His promises. The Exodus is often seen as a type of Christ's deliverance of believers from the bondage of sin, as referenced in the New Testament (e.g., 1 Corinthians 10:1-4).

until today
This indicates the continuity of God's relationship and communication with Israel. It underscores the persistent nature of God's covenantal faithfulness despite Israel's repeated disobedience. The phrase "until today" suggests a historical context that spans centuries, highlighting the long-suffering patience of God.

I strongly warned them again and again
This reflects God's persistent efforts to guide and correct His people through prophets and leaders. The Hebrew word for "warned" can also imply admonishing or instructing, emphasizing God's desire for Israel to follow His ways. This persistence is seen throughout the Old Testament, where God sends prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and others to call Israel back to obedience.

saying, ‘Obey My voice.’
This command encapsulates the essence of the covenant relationship between God and Israel. Obedience to God's voice is a recurring theme in the Bible, signifying submission to His will and commandments. This call to obedience is echoed in the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-5) and is central to the teachings of Jesus, who emphasized love and obedience to God as the greatest commandments (Matthew 22:37-40).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Jeremiah
A prophet called by God to deliver messages of warning and hope to the people of Judah. His ministry was marked by his deep commitment to God's word and his sorrow over the people's disobedience.

2. The Fathers/Ancestors
Refers to the Israelites who were delivered from Egypt. They were the recipients of God's covenant and commandments, which they were repeatedly warned to obey.

3. Egypt
The land from which God delivered the Israelites, symbolizing bondage and oppression. The exodus from Egypt is a foundational event in Israel's history, representing God's power and faithfulness.

4. God
The sovereign Lord who delivered Israel from Egypt and established a covenant with them, requiring their obedience and loyalty.

5. The Exodus
The event of God delivering the Israelites from slavery in Egypt, which is a central account in the Old Testament, demonstrating God's saving power and faithfulness to His promises.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Obedience
God’s repeated warnings to the Israelites underscore the critical nature of obedience in our relationship with Him. Obedience is not just about following rules but aligning our lives with God’s will.

God’s Faithfulness and Patience
Despite Israel's repeated failures, God continued to warn and call them back to Himself. This reflects His patience and desire for His people to return to Him.

Historical Lessons for Today
The history of Israel serves as a cautionary tale for believers today. We are called to learn from their mistakes and remain steadfast in our commitment to God.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Jeremiah were instrumental in delivering God’s messages. Today, we have the Scriptures and the Holy Spirit to guide us in understanding and applying God’s word.

Covenant Relationship
Just as God established a covenant with Israel, He has established a new covenant with believers through Jesus Christ. This relationship calls for our obedience and faithfulness.

What Does Jeremiah 11:7 Mean
For from the time I brought your fathers out of the land of Egypt

God anchors His warning in the historical act of the Exodus. That deliverance was not just about freedom from slavery; it inaugurated a covenant relationship built on His faithfulness and Israel’s obedience.

• In Exodus 19:4-5, the Lord reminds the people, “You have seen… how I carried you on eagles’ wings… Now if you will indeed obey My voice and keep My covenant, you will be My treasured possession.”

• Deuteronomy 7:9 links the Exodus to God’s enduring commitment: “Know therefore that the LORD your God is God, the faithful God who keeps His covenant of loving devotion for a thousand generations with those who love Him and keep His commandments.”

Every generation, including Jeremiah’s, is expected to remember that defining rescue and respond with covenant loyalty.

until today

The phrase underscores God’s patience across centuries. From the wilderness wanderings through the judges and kings, the call to obedience never changed.

• Psalm 95:7-8 pleads, “Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts,” showing that “today” is always the right moment to respond.

• Hebrews 3:15 echoes the same urgency for New-Covenant believers.

God’s mercy stretches over time, but it is not indefinite. Jeremiah’s audience stands at a “today” that demands decision.

I strongly warned them again and again

The repetition reveals both the seriousness of disobedience and the relentless grace of God. He did not issue a single command and walk away; He repeatedly sent prophets to call the nation back.

• 2 Chronicles 36:15-16 notes, “The LORD, the God of their fathers, sent word to them through His messengers again and again… but they mocked God’s messengers.”

• In Jeremiah 7:25, the Lord says, “From the day your fathers came out of Egypt until this day, I have sent you all My servants the prophets, again and again.”

God’s persistence magnifies Israel’s guilt but also His compassion; He warns because He wants restoration, not judgment.

saying, ‘Obey My voice.’

The divine demand is crystal clear: listen and submit. Obedience is personal—“My voice”—not a generic moral code.

• Deuteronomy 30:20 frames life itself as “loving the LORD your God, obeying His voice, and holding fast to Him.”

• Jesus reaffirms the same principle in John 10:27: “My sheep listen to My voice; I know them, and they follow Me.”

Scripture presents obedience as the natural response of redeemed people to their Redeemer; anything less is rebellion.

summary

Jeremiah 11:7 compresses Israel’s whole covenant story into one urgent sentence. The God who rescued His people from Egypt has, from that moment until Jeremiah’s day, patiently and persistently called them to a single response: “Obey My voice.” History proves His faithfulness; the ever-present “today” highlights the immediacy of decision; His repeated warnings display mercy; and the call to obedience reveals the heartbeat of covenant life. The verse reminds every generation that redemption leads to relationship, and relationship demands responsive obedience.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) Rising early.--The phrase in its spiritual meaning, as applied to Jehovah, is almost peculiar to Jeremiah, and is used by him twelve times. In its literal sense, or as denoting only ordinary activity, it is found often, e.g., Genesis 20:8; Proverbs 27:14. (See Note on Jeremiah 7:13.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 7, 8. - A condensation of Jeremiah 7:23-26. Imagination; rather, stubbornness (see on Jeremiah 3:17). I will bring; rather, I brought. All the words. "Word" sometimes means "thing spoken of;" here, for instance, the curses specified in Deuteronomy 28.
