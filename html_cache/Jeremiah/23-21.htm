Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I did not send these prophets
This phrase highlights the issue of false prophets in ancient Israel. Throughout the Old Testament, God often sent prophets to deliver His messages to the people. However, during Jeremiah's time, many individuals claimed to be prophets without being commissioned by God. This reflects a broader theme in the Bible where true prophets are distinguished by their divine calling, as seen in the stories of Moses (Exodus 3:10) and Isaiah (Isaiah 6:8). The false prophets in Jeremiah's time were misleading the people, often promoting messages of peace and prosperity contrary to God's warnings of impending judgment.

yet they have run with their message
The imagery of "running" suggests urgency and eagerness. These false prophets were quick to spread their own messages, which were not from God. This eagerness can be contrasted with the true prophets who often hesitated or were reluctant to deliver God's difficult messages, such as Jonah (Jonah 1:3) and Jeremiah himself (Jeremiah 1:6). The false prophets' messages were likely more appealing to the people, as they often promised peace and security (Jeremiah 6:14), which were contrary to the warnings of judgment that God was actually delivering through His true prophets.

I did not speak to them
This phrase underscores the divine authority behind true prophecy. In the biblical context, a prophet's legitimacy is based on whether God has spoken to them. The false prophets lacked this divine communication, making their messages void of true spiritual authority. This is a recurring theme in the Bible, where God emphasizes that His words are the source of truth and guidance (Deuteronomy 18:18-22). The absence of God's voice in these false prophets' messages highlights their deception and the danger they posed to the community.

yet they have prophesied
Despite not receiving a word from God, these individuals still claimed to speak on His behalf. This act of false prophecy is condemned throughout Scripture, as it leads people away from God's true intentions and plans. In the New Testament, Jesus warns against false prophets who come in sheep's clothing but are inwardly ravenous wolves (Matthew 7:15). The persistence of false prophecy throughout biblical history serves as a warning to discern the true voice of God amidst many competing claims. The false prophets' actions in Jeremiah's time were not only misleading but also a direct challenge to God's authority and truth.

Teaching Points
Discernment in Leadership
Believers must exercise discernment in identifying true spiritual leaders. Not everyone who claims to speak for God is genuinely sent by Him.

The Authority of God's Word
The Bible is the ultimate authority for truth. Any message or teaching must align with Scripture to be considered valid.

The Danger of False Prophecy
False prophets can lead people astray, causing spiritual harm. It is crucial to be vigilant and grounded in biblical truth.

Accountability in Ministry
Those in positions of spiritual leadership are accountable to God for their words and actions. They must ensure they are truly called and sent by Him.

The Role of the Holy Spirit
The Holy Spirit guides believers into all truth. Relying on the Spirit's guidance helps in discerning true messages from God.

What Does Jeremiah 23:21 Mean
I did not send these prophets

“ ‘I did not send these prophets…’ ” (Jeremiah 23:21a)

• God alone authorizes true messengers. From Moses to Paul, every genuine servant is first called by the Lord (Isaiah 6:8–9; Acts 13:2–4).

• When the Lord says He did not send them, He exposes their lack of divine commission—an offense similar to the prophets who “prophesy lies in My name” (Jeremiah 14:14).

• The warning reminds us that ministry begins with God’s initiative, not human ambition (John 20:21).

yet they have run with their message

“ ‘…yet they have run with their message…’ ” (Jeremiah 23:21b)

• The false prophets showed zeal, “running” as eager couriers, but speed cannot substitute for authenticity.

• Like the teachers who “tickle ears” in 2 Timothy 4:3–4, they spread comfortable predictions of peace (Jeremiah 23:17).

• Zeal apart from truth misleads God’s people; Paul saw this in Israel’s “zeal for God, but not in accordance with knowledge” (Romans 10:2).

• Modern parallels arise whenever charisma outruns Scripture, prompting us to weigh every message against the written Word (1 Thessalonians 5:21).

I did not speak to them

“ ‘I did not speak to them…’ ” (Jeremiah 23:21c)

• God’s voice is unmistakable when He truly speaks (Numbers 12:6; Hebrews 1:1–2).

• These prophets lacked any divine revelation, yet presumed to fill the silence with their own imaginations (Ezekiel 13:6–7).

• The phrase underscores the sufficiency of Scripture: what God has spoken stands; what He has not spoken must never be added (Deuteronomy 4:2; 2 Peter 1:21).

• The church safeguards itself by remaining in what is written (2 Timothy 3:16–17) rather than chasing unfounded messages.

yet they have prophesied

“ ‘…yet they have prophesied.’ ” (Jeremiah 23:21d)

• Even without God’s word, the false prophets boldly declared “Thus says the LORD,” mirroring Deuteronomy 18:20’s warning against speaking presumptuously.

• Their prophecies soothed rather than convicted, leaving sin unchallenged (Lamentations 2:14).

• Jesus later cautioned, “Beware of false prophets” (Matthew 7:15), and John urged believers to “test the spirits” (1 John 4:1).

• The ultimate test remains faithfulness to Christ and His revealed truth (Revelation 19:10).

summary

Jeremiah 23:21 exposes a sobering pattern: people can claim God’s authority, rush into ministry, and speak confident words—yet lack any sending, speech, or sanction from the Lord. Authentic servants are called, empowered, and given a message that aligns with Scripture. The verse urges us to discern carefully, remain anchored in God’s written Word, and follow only those whose commission, content, and character unmistakably bear the mark of the One who truly sends.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Yet they ran.--The image is that of messengers who rush eagerly, as from the king's council-chamber, on their self-appointed mission, without waiting for the command of the Master in whose name they profess to come. (Comp. the question, "Who will go for us?" in Isaiah 6:8.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 21, 22. - In vers. 17-20 Jeremiah has shown that these cannot be true prophets, because their message is diametrically opposed to the true revelation. He now proves it from the absence of any moral effect from their preaching.
