Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Turn to me
This phrase is a plea for God's attention and intervention. In the biblical context, turning to God signifies repentance and seeking His presence. It echoes the call for divine attention found in other Psalms, such as Psalm 86:16. The act of turning is also a common theme in the prophetic books, where God calls His people to turn back to Him (e.g., Jeremiah 3:12). This reflects the covenant relationship between God and His people, where God promises to respond to those who seek Him earnestly.

and be gracious
The request for grace highlights the psalmist's reliance on God's unmerited favor. Grace is a central theme throughout Scripture, emphasizing God's kindness and mercy towards humanity. In the Old Testament, grace is often associated with God's covenantal love (hesed) and His willingness to forgive and restore. This plea aligns with the New Testament understanding of grace, as seen in Ephesians 2:8-9, where salvation is described as a gift from God. The psalmist's appeal for grace underscores human dependence on divine benevolence.

for I am lonely
Loneliness in the biblical context can refer to both physical isolation and spiritual desolation. The psalmist's expression of loneliness may reflect the experience of being abandoned or misunderstood by others, a common theme in the life of David, who faced betrayal and isolation. This condition also points to the human need for community and relationship, which is fulfilled in the body of Christ, as seen in the New Testament (Hebrews 10:24-25). The psalmist's loneliness can be seen as a type of Christ, who experienced ultimate loneliness on the cross (Matthew 27:46).

and afflicted
Affliction refers to suffering or distress, often seen as a result of external pressures or internal struggles. In the historical context, affliction could arise from enemies, personal sin, or divine discipline. The psalmist's affliction may mirror the trials faced by the Israelites, such as oppression by foreign nations or the consequences of disobedience. This phrase connects to the broader biblical narrative of God as a deliverer of the afflicted, as seen in Exodus 3:7-8. It also foreshadows the suffering servant motif in Isaiah 53, pointing to Jesus Christ, who bore affliction for humanity's redemption.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 25, David is expressing a personal plea to God. Known as a man after God's own heart, David often turned to God in times of distress.

2. God
The recipient of David's plea. In this verse, God is seen as a compassionate and gracious being who can provide comfort and relief.

3. Loneliness and Affliction
These are the conditions David is experiencing. They represent not just physical isolation but also emotional and spiritual distress.
Teaching Points
God's Compassionate Nature
God is inherently gracious and compassionate. David's plea is based on his understanding of God's character, which is consistent throughout Scripture.

The Reality of Loneliness
Even those close to God, like David, experience loneliness and affliction. It's a common human experience that should drive us to seek God's presence.

Prayer as a Response to Affliction
David models turning to God in prayer during times of distress. This is a practical step for believers facing similar situations.

God's Readiness to Respond
The verse implies that God is willing to turn towards us when we call upon Him, highlighting His readiness to engage with our struggles.

Seeking God's Grace
The request for God to be gracious underscores the importance of seeking divine grace in our lives, recognizing our dependence on Him.

What Does Psalm 25:16 Mean
Turn to me

• David looks heavenward, convinced that God truly sees and hears. “Turn, O LORD, and deliver me” (Psalm 6:4) echoes the same appeal: an urgent request for God’s face, not His back.

• Scripture pictures God’s attentive nearness: “The eyes of the LORD are on the righteous” (Psalm 34:15).

• When believers call, they expect response because relationship is covenantal (Psalm 18:6; James 4:8).

and be gracious

• Grace is undeserved favor; David counts on God’s character, not his own merit. “Remember, O LORD, Your compassion and loving devotion” (Psalm 25:6).

• The plea parallels “Have mercy on me, O God, according to Your loving devotion” (Psalm 51:1).

• Hebrews 4:16 invites every believer to “approach the throne of grace with confidence,” anchoring this Old-Testament cry in New-Testament promise.

for I am lonely

• Isolation hurts more than physical hardship. David was often cut off—from Saul’s court, from family, from loyal friends (1 Samuel 23:14-15).

• God meets the solitary: “God settles the lonely in families” (Psalm 68:6).

• Paul felt the same abandonment: “At my first defense no one came to my support… but the Lord stood by me” (2 Timothy 4:16-17).

and afflicted

• Affliction is pressure—external and internal. “Many are the afflictions of the righteous, but the LORD delivers him from them all” (Psalm 34:19).

• Jesus prepared His followers: “In the world you will have tribulation. But take courage; I have overcome the world” (John 16:33).

• Affliction drives trust deeper; suffering saints learn to say, “It was good for me to be afflicted so that I might learn Your statutes” (Psalm 119:71).

summary

Psalm 25:16 is a fourfold cry: look at me, pour out grace, cure my aloneness, and lift my burdens. The verse teaches that believers can speak candidly to a responsive God, confident of His gaze, His mercy, His companionship, and His deliverance.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - Turn thee unto me, and have mercy upon me. The approach of peril is regarded as a sign that God has "turned away his face." He is besought, therefore, to turn towards one who needs his aid. For I am desolate and afflicted (comp. vers. 17, 18). The affliction evidently comes from enemies, either foreign or domestic (vers. 2, 19); but its nature is not further indicated.
