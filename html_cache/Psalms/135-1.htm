Hallelujah!
The term "Hallelujah" is a transliteration of the Hebrew phrase "Hallelu Yah," meaning "Praise the LORD." It is a call to worship and an expression of joy and gratitude. This exclamation is used frequently in the Psalms and is a reminder of the importance of worship in the life of the believer. The use of "Hallelujah" connects this psalm to the broader tradition of Hebrew worship, which emphasizes the communal and individual response to God's greatness and goodness.

Praise the name of the LORD.
"Praise the name of the LORD" emphasizes the significance of God's name, which in Hebrew culture represents His character and essence. The name of the LORD, often rendered as Yahweh, is considered sacred and is a reminder of God's covenant relationship with His people. This phrase calls believers to honor and revere God for who He is. In biblical times, names held great power and meaning, and to praise God's name is to acknowledge His sovereignty, holiness, and faithfulness. This connects to the commandment in Exodus 20:7 about not taking the LORD's name in vain, highlighting the reverence due to God.

Give praise, O servants of the LORD,
This phrase addresses the "servants of the LORD," which traditionally refers to the priests and Levites who ministered in the temple. However, it can also be understood more broadly to include all who serve God. The call to praise is not limited to a specific group but extends to all who are in a covenant relationship with God. This reflects the biblical theme that all believers are called to be a "royal priesthood" (1 Peter 2:9), serving and worshiping God in their daily lives. Historically, the temple was the center of worship in Israel, and the servants of the LORD played a crucial role in leading the people in worship and maintaining the sacred rituals. This phrase encourages all believers to engage in the act of worship, recognizing their role as servants of the Most High.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The central figure of the Psalm, Yahweh is the covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal and unchanging nature.

2. Servants of the LORD
This refers to those who serve God, including the priests and Levites in the temple, as well as all believers who are called to worship and serve Him.

3. The Temple
While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the context of the Psalm suggests the temple as a place of worship where God's name is praised.
Teaching Points
The Call to Praise
The Psalm begins with a call to "Hallelujah," which means "Praise the LORD." This is a reminder that praise is not just an option but a command for God's people.

The Significance of God's Name
Praising the "name of the LORD" emphasizes the importance of God's character and attributes. His name represents His identity and His deeds.

Role of Servants
As "servants of the LORD," believers are called to a life of worship and service. This is a reminder of our identity and purpose in Christ.

Worship as a Community
The collective call to praise suggests the importance of corporate worship. Gathering with other believers to praise God strengthens our faith and glorifies Him.

What Does Psalm 135:1 Mean
Hallelujah!

• The psalmist opens with an unmistakable command to worship—an exclamation that calls every reader to lift heartfelt praise now, not later (Psalm 150:6; Revelation 19:1).

• Because every word of Scripture is true and binding, this call carries divine authority; praising God is never optional (Psalm 119:160).

• The shout reminds us that worship begins with a conscious, joyful decision to honor the LORD, regardless of circumstance (Psalm 34:1).

Praise the name of the LORD

• Praising His “name” means celebrating every attribute He has revealed—His holiness (Psalm 99:3), power (Psalm 29:2), mercy (Psalm 103:8), and faithfulness (Exodus 34:5-7).

• Ascribing glory to His name centers our worship on who He is, not merely on what He does for us (Psalm 96:8).

• Because Scripture is accurate in all it states, every title and description of God gives us solid ground for continual praise (Proverbs 18:10).

Give praise, O servants of the LORD

• The invitation narrows to those who belong to Him—Levites in the temple (Deuteronomy 10:8), angels (Psalm 103:21), and, through Christ, all believers today (1 Peter 2:9).

• Service and praise are inseparable; genuine ministry flows from a heart that treasures its Master (Psalm 100:2).

• The verse makes praise both a privilege and a duty for God’s people, affirming our identity and purpose (Revelation 1:6).

• Because God’s Word is infallible, our calling as His servants stands secure forever (Isaiah 40:8).

summary

Psalm 135:1 issues a threefold summons: a universal “Hallelujah,” an invitation to exalt the LORD’s incomparable name, and a direct charge to His servants. Each phrase rests on the certainty of God’s Word and His unchanging worthiness. Answering this call aligns us with our created purpose—joyful, wholehearted, obedient praise to the living God.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) The psalm opens with an adaptation and expansion (comp. Psalm 116:19) of Psalm 134:1. As there, the priestly class is addressed. Some, however, think that the addition, "courts of the house of our God," as well as Psalm 135:19, make the application to all these standing in covenant relation to Jehovah. This is possible, but not proved by the evidence adduced.

Pulpit Commentary
