I lift up my hands to Your commandments
This phrase signifies an act of worship and surrender. In biblical times, lifting hands was a common posture in prayer and worship, symbolizing openness and receptivity to God (1 Timothy 2:8). It reflects a deep reverence and submission to God's authority. The psalmist's gesture indicates a heartfelt devotion and a desire to honor God's laws. This act can be seen as a type of Christ, who perfectly fulfilled the law and demonstrated complete obedience to the Father (Matthew 5:17).

which I love
The psalmist expresses a deep affection for God's commandments, highlighting a personal relationship with the divine law. This love is not merely intellectual but involves the whole being, suggesting that the commandments are not burdensome but a source of joy and life (1 John 5:3). The love for God's law is a recurring theme in Psalm 119, emphasizing the psalmist's commitment and delight in following God's ways. This love can be seen as a reflection of Jesus' own love for the Father and His will (John 14:31).

and I meditate on Your statutes
Meditation here involves a deep, reflective consideration of God's statutes, going beyond mere reading to internalizing and applying them. In the historical context, meditation was a common practice among the Israelites, often involving recitation and memorization of Scripture (Joshua 1:8). This practice is essential for spiritual growth and understanding, as it allows the believer to align their thoughts and actions with God's will. The psalmist's meditation on God's statutes can be seen as a precursor to the New Testament call to let the word of Christ dwell richly within believers (Colossians 3:16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep love and reverence for God's law.

2. God's Commandments
The divine laws and instructions given by God, which the psalmist loves and meditates upon.

3. Meditation
A spiritual practice of reflecting deeply on God's statutes, indicating a personal and intimate engagement with the Word.
Teaching Points
Love for God's Word
The psalmist's love for God's commandments is evident. As believers, we are called to cultivate a deep affection for Scripture, seeing it as a source of life and guidance.

Physical Expression of Worship
"I lift up my hands" signifies a physical act of worship and surrender. Consider how physical expressions can enhance our spiritual devotion and connection to God.

Meditation as a Spiritual Discipline
Meditation on God's statutes is a vital practice for spiritual growth. It involves focused reflection and application of God's Word in our daily lives.

Commitment to Obedience
The psalmist's love for the commandments is not just emotional but also practical, leading to a commitment to obey God's laws. Our love for God should manifest in our actions and decisions.

What Does Psalm 119:48 Mean
I lift up my hands

- In Scripture, raising hands is a visible act of worship, surrender, and dependence. “So I will bless You as long as I live; in Your name I will lift up my hands” (Psalm 63:4).

- This posture signals that the psalmist approaches God openly, withholding nothing. It is praise, but also pleading—“Hear the sound of my cry for mercy when I lift up my hands toward Your holy sanctuary” (Psalm 28:2).

- The verse joins body and soul; genuine faith never stays hidden inside.

to Your commandments

- The hands are lifted specifically “to” God’s commandments—His revealed will in written form. The psalmist treats the commands as precious gifts, not grim obligations (Psalm 19:7-8).

- By lifting hands toward the word, he acknowledges its authority and sufficiency. Jesus later affirms the same heart: “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments” (John 14:15).

- Practical takeaway: worship that ignores God’s directives is empty; true devotion gravitates toward what He has spoken.

which I love

- Love moves the action. “Oh, how I love Your law! I meditate on it all day long” (Psalm 119:97).

- Obedience without affection becomes drudgery; affection without obedience rings hollow. The psalmist holds both together, echoing “For this is love for God: to keep His commandments; and His commandments are not burdensome” (1 John 5:3).

- Notice the personal tone—“Your” commandments. Relationship, not mere rule-keeping, lies at the center.

and I meditate on Your statutes

- Meditation means intentional, repeated reflection. “This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you shall meditate on it day and night” (Joshua 1:8).

- The psalmist doesn’t glance at Scripture; he lingers, letting it shape thoughts, desires, and decisions. “His delight is in the law of the LORD, and on His law he meditates day and night” (Psalm 1:2).

- Simple pattern:

• Love the word.

• Lift up holy hands in worship.

• Linger over every line until it saturates life.

summary

Psalm 119:48 pictures whole-person devotion. The psalmist raises his hands—body engaged—in worship. He directs that worship toward God’s commandments—truth embraced. Love fuels the response—heart engaged. Meditation deepens it—mind engaged. Taken together, the verse invites believers to a life where affection, submission, and continual reflection on God’s word flow seamlessly into wholehearted worship.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(48) My hands.--See Psalm 28:2. The expression here is elliptical: "I will lift my hands in prayer for power to observe Thy commands."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 48. - My hands also will I lift up unto thy commandments, which I have loved. In a sort of qualified worship (comp. Genesis 14:22; Psalm 28:2; Psalm 134:2; Psalm 141:2; Lamentations 3:41). And I will meditate in thy statutes. Almost a repetition of ver. 15a.
