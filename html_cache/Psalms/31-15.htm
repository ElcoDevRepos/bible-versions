My times are in Your hands;
This phrase reflects a deep trust in God's sovereignty and control over the events of life. The psalmist acknowledges that every aspect of his life, including its duration and the events that unfold, is under God's authority. This echoes the biblical theme found in Ecclesiastes 3:1-8, where there is a time for every purpose under heaven. The phrase also connects to the idea of God's providence, as seen in Job 14:5, where the days of a person's life are determined by God. The use of "hands" symbolizes power and care, suggesting that the psalmist feels secure in God's protective grasp.

deliver me from my enemies
The plea for deliverance from enemies is a common theme in the Psalms, reflecting the historical context of David's life, who faced numerous adversaries, including King Saul and foreign nations. This request for deliverance is not just a desire for physical safety but also a call for divine justice against those who oppose God's anointed. The concept of God as a deliverer is central to the Old Testament, as seen in the Exodus narrative where God delivers Israel from Egypt (Exodus 14:30). This phrase also foreshadows the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ, who delivers believers from sin and spiritual enemies (Colossians 1:13).

and from those who pursue me.
The mention of pursuers indicates a sense of urgency and danger, as the psalmist is actively being chased by those who wish him harm. This reflects the historical reality of David's life, where he was often on the run from Saul and other adversaries. The cultural context of ancient Israel involved frequent conflicts and the need for refuge, which is why cities of refuge were established (Numbers 35:11-15). Spiritually, this pursuit can be seen as a metaphor for the relentless nature of sin and temptation, from which believers seek God's protection. The imagery of pursuit also points to the New Testament, where believers are encouraged to flee from sin and pursue righteousness (1 Timothy 6:11).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 31, David was the second king of Israel, known for his deep relationship with God and his psalms that express a wide range of human emotions and faith.

2. Enemies
In the context of this psalm, David refers to those who oppose him, possibly including King Saul, who pursued him, and other adversaries throughout his life.

3. God
The central figure in whom David places his trust, acknowledging His sovereignty over time and circumstances.
Teaching Points
Trust in God's Sovereignty
Recognize that God is in control of all aspects of our lives, including the timing of events. This trust can bring peace amidst uncertainty.

Prayer for Deliverance
Like David, we should actively seek God's help and deliverance from our adversaries, whether they are people, circumstances, or spiritual battles.

Patience in God's Timing
Understanding that our times are in God's hands encourages patience and faith as we wait for His perfect timing in our lives.

Confidence in God's Protection
Knowing that God is aware of our struggles and has the power to deliver us should bolster our confidence and reduce fear.

Reflection on God's Faithfulness
Reflect on past instances of God's faithfulness as a source of encouragement and strength for current challenges.

What Does Psalm 31:15 Mean
My times are in Your hands

• “My times are in Your hands” declares that every season, circumstance, and heartbeat is overseen by the Lord, not by chance or human control (Psalm 139:16).

• David’s confidence rests in God’s absolute sovereignty—“The LORD has established His throne in heaven, and His kingdom rules over all” (Psalm 103:19).

• This truth frees us from anxiety (Matthew 6:31-34) and presumptuous planning (James 4:13-15), calling us to humble dependence on God’s providence.

• Because the Lord alone “holds in His hand the life of every creature and the breath of all mankind” (Job 12:10), He is both able and willing to guide the moments of our lives (Proverbs 3:5-6).

• Knowing our times are God-kept encourages:

– Patience when waiting (Psalm 27:14)

– Courage when facing uncertainty (Isaiah 41:10)

– Gratitude for every new morning (Lamentations 3:22-23)

deliver me from my enemies

• David moves from trust to plea: “Deliver me from my enemies”. The God who controls time also controls outcomes (Psalm 18:17).

• Scripture shows repeated rescues—Daniel from lions (Daniel 6:22), Peter from prison (Acts 12:11)—underscoring that deliverance is not theoretical but historical and personal.

• The Lord’s deliverance is comprehensive: He “rescued me from my powerful enemy” (Psalm 18:17), yet ultimately He delivers from sin and death through Christ (Colossians 1:13-14).

• Practical outworking:

– Run first to God, not to human schemes (Psalm 20:7).

– Expect God’s answer in His timing, whether by removing the threat (2 Timothy 4:18) or sustaining amid it (2 Corinthians 12:9).

– Respond to deliverance with worship (Psalm 34:1-4).

and from those who pursue me

• “And from those who pursue me” highlights relentless opposition. David knew real pursuers (1 Samuel 23:26); believers today face both visible foes and spiritual forces (Ephesians 6:12).

• God promises protection that matches the pursuit: “He will cover you with His feathers” (Psalm 91:4) and “You are my hiding place” (Psalm 32:7).

• Even prolonged chasing cannot outrun divine care: “When my spirit grows faint within me, it is You who know my way” (Psalm 142:3).

• Action steps while being pursued:

– Take refuge in God’s character—“The name of the LORD is a strong tower” (Proverbs 18:10).

– Keep doing right, entrusting yourself to the faithful Creator (1 Peter 4:19).

– Remember that persecution links us to Christ’s path (John 15:18-20).

summary

Psalm 31:15 blends unwavering trust with urgent petition. Because every moment is held securely in God’s hands, we can confidently ask Him to rescue us from enemies and relentless pressures. The verse invites us to rest in God’s sovereignty, call on His deliverance, and walk forward in faith, knowing that the One who authors our times also safeguards our lives.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(15) My times are in thy hand--i.e., the vicissitudes of human life (LXX. and Vulg. have "my destinies") are under Divine control, so that the machinations of the foe cannot prevail against one whom God intends to deliver. For the expression comp. 1Chronicles 29:30, "the times that went over him," Isaiah 33:6.

The sense of security in this trusting phrase may be contrasted with the feeling of danger in another Hebrew phrase, "my soul is continually in my hand," Psalm 119:109.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 15. - My times are in thy hand. "My times," i.e. "all the varied events, happy or sad, which make up the parti-coloured web of life" (Kay). Not one of them but is shaped by thee and ordered by thee. Deliver me from the hand of mine enemies, and from them that persecute me (camp. vers. 1, 2, 4). The great need under existing circumstances was deliverance. Absalom was looked for daily to "pass over Jordan, and all the men of Israel with him" (2 Samuel 17:24). A battle was imminent. If the day went against David, and his army was defeated, he would necessarily fall into the hands of his "enemies" and "persecutors," in which case he could not hope that they would spare his life (2 Samuel 17:2, 12).
