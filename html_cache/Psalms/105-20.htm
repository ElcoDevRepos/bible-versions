Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The king sent and released him;
This phrase refers to the moment when Pharaoh, the king of Egypt, sent for Joseph and released him from prison. Joseph had been unjustly imprisoned after being falsely accused by Potiphar's wife. The king's action signifies a divine intervention in Joseph's life, fulfilling God's plan for him. This release is a pivotal moment in Joseph's story, as it transitions him from a prisoner to a position of power. The king's decision to release Joseph can be seen as a fulfillment of God's promise to Joseph, as revealed in his earlier dreams (Genesis 37:5-11). This act of release is also a type of Christ, who was sent by God to release humanity from the bondage of sin.

the ruler of peoples set him free.
Pharaoh, as the ruler of Egypt, was one of the most powerful leaders of the ancient world, often referred to as the ruler of peoples due to Egypt's influence and control over various nations. By setting Joseph free, Pharaoh not only acknowledges Joseph's innocence but also recognizes the wisdom and divine insight Joseph possesses, as demonstrated by his ability to interpret dreams (Genesis 41:14-16). This act of setting Joseph free is symbolic of God's sovereignty and His ability to elevate His chosen ones to positions of influence and authority. It also prefigures the ultimate liberation that Christ offers to all believers, freeing them from spiritual captivity and granting them a place in His eternal kingdom.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The King
Refers to Pharaoh, the ruler of Egypt, who had the authority to release Joseph from prison.

2. Joseph
A Hebrew man who was sold into slavery by his brothers, falsely accused, and imprisoned in Egypt. He was later released and elevated to a position of power.

3. Egypt
The land where Joseph was taken as a slave and where he rose to prominence after his release from prison.

4. The Ruler of Peoples
Another reference to Pharaoh, emphasizing his authority over a vast and diverse population.

5. Release from Prison
The event where Joseph was freed from his unjust imprisonment, marking a pivotal moment in his life and God's plan for Israel.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Timing
God's timing is perfect, even when we face trials. Joseph's release was orchestrated by God at the right moment to fulfill His purposes.

Faithfulness in Trials
Joseph remained faithful to God despite his circumstances. We are called to trust God and remain faithful, knowing He is in control.

Divine Promotion
God can elevate us from the lowest points to positions of influence for His glory. Our current situation does not define our future in God's plan.

Forgiveness and Reconciliation
Joseph's account ultimately leads to reconciliation with his family. We should seek to forgive and reconcile with others, as God works through our relationships.

Providence and Purpose
God has a purpose for our lives, even when we cannot see it. Joseph's journey shows that God uses our experiences to prepare us for His plans.

What Does Psalm 105:20 Mean
The king sent

• Within the flow of Psalm 105, this line recalls the moment when Pharaoh learned of Joseph’s God-given wisdom (Genesis 41:14).

• The initiative comes from the throne, underscoring God’s sovereignty working through earthly authority, just as Proverbs 21:1 affirms that “the king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD.”

• God’s covenant faithfulness is on display: He had foretold Joseph’s rise (Genesis 37:5-11), and now He moves the king to act at the exact appointed time (Psalm 105:17, 19).

and released him

• Joseph’s years of unjust imprisonment end instantly; chains fall off because the word of the king carries absolute power (Ecclesiastes 8:4).

• The verse highlights complete deliverance, echoing other moments when God’s people are suddenly freed—Peter from Herod’s prison (Acts 12:7-10) and the Israelites from Egypt (Exodus 12:31-32).

• By pairing God’s faithfulness with the king’s decree, the psalmist shows that divine purpose is never thwarted by human injustice (Romans 8:28).

the ruler of peoples

• Pharaoh is described not merely as a local monarch but as one who governs nations, stressing the magnitude of Joseph’s promotion.

• This phrase reminds us that God can steer the most influential leaders for His redemptive plan (Daniel 2:21; Isaiah 45:1-4).

• It foreshadows Christ, the true Ruler over all peoples (Revelation 1:5), who will likewise exalt the humble (Philippians 2:9-11).

set him free

• Freedom here is more than release from prison; it is installation into purpose—Joseph becomes administrator over Egypt (Genesis 41:41-44).

• The transformation from slave to statesman mirrors the believer’s transfer “from the dominion of darkness into the kingdom of the Son” (Colossians 1:13).

• Joseph’s freedom becomes the means of preserving many lives (Genesis 50:20), illustrating how personal deliverance often serves a wider redemptive scope.

summary

Psalm 105:20 captures the pivotal turn in Joseph’s story: God moves a world ruler to act, breaks Joseph’s bonds, and positions him for God-ordained influence. The verse testifies that no circumstance is beyond God’s reach; at the right moment He sends, releases, rules, and frees—always keeping His promises and advancing His saving plan for His people.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - The king sent and loosed him (see Genesis 41:14). Even the ruler of the people. Pharaoh, ruler of the Egyptians. Let him go free; i.e. made him a free man, instead of a prisoner and a slave.
