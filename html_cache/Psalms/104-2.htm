He wraps Himself in light as with a garment;
This phrase emphasizes God's majesty and purity. Light in the Bible often symbolizes holiness, truth, and the divine presence (1 John 1:5). The imagery of God clothing Himself in light suggests His unapproachable holiness and glory, reminiscent of the transfiguration of Jesus (Matthew 17:2) where His face shone like the sun. The idea of God being wrapped in light also connects to the creation narrative, where God said, "Let there be light" (Genesis 1:3), indicating His sovereign power over creation. In the ancient Near Eastern context, light was often associated with divine beings, setting God apart as the ultimate source of light and life. This imagery can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is described as the "light of the world" (John 8:12), bringing spiritual illumination and salvation.

He stretches out the heavens like a tent,
This phrase reflects God's creative power and sovereignty over the universe. The imagery of stretching out the heavens like a tent suggests the vastness and order of creation, akin to the ancient practice of setting up a tent, which was a common activity in nomadic cultures. This metaphor is also found in other scriptures, such as Isaiah 40:22, emphasizing God's authority and the ease with which He governs the cosmos. The heavens, in biblical terms, often refer to the sky and the celestial realm, highlighting God's transcendence and immanence. This phrase can also be seen as a foreshadowing of the new creation, where God will establish a new heaven and a new earth (Revelation 21:1), demonstrating His ongoing creative work and ultimate plan for redemption.

Teaching Points
God's Majesty and Sovereignty
The imagery of God wrapping Himself in light and stretching out the heavens highlights His majesty and sovereign power over all creation.

The Symbolism of Light
Light symbolizes purity, holiness, and revelation. As God wraps Himself in light, it signifies His perfect holiness and the revelation of His character to humanity.

Creation as a Reflection of God's Glory
The heavens, stretched out like a tent, reflect God's creative power and glory. Observing creation can lead us to worship and acknowledge God's greatness.

God's Immanence and Transcendence
While God is transcendent, beyond our full comprehension, He is also immanent, involved in His creation, as seen in the detailed care of stretching out the heavens.

What Does Psalm 104:2 Mean
He wraps Himself in light as with a garment

- The picture is of the Lord clothing Himself, not in fabric, but in the very radiance of His own glory. Light is not something He steps into; it is what naturally emanates from His being.

- 1 John 1:5 says, “God is light, and in Him there is no darkness at all,” echoing the idea that light is an essential attribute of His nature.

- Paul affirms in 1 Timothy 6:16, “He alone is immortal and dwells in unapproachable light,” stressing both God’s majesty and the separation His holiness creates.

- Moments when that light briefly broke through human sight—Exodus 34:29, Matthew 17:2, Acts 9:3—underscore the literal brightness that surrounds Him.

- Revelation 21:23 foretells a future where “the glory of God illuminates the city,” reminding us that His light will one day replace every earthly source of illumination.

He stretches out the heavens like a tent

- The psalmist shifts from God’s personal radiance to His cosmic craftsmanship. “Stretches out” presents creation as an ongoing act of power, holding the universe in place much like a shepherd pitching a tent to shelter his flock.

- Isaiah 40:22 mirrors the wording: “He stretches out the heavens like a curtain, and spreads them out like a tent to dwell in,” affirming that the vast skies exist because God intentionally arranged them.

- Job 9:8 and Jeremiah 10:12 repeat the same imagery, tying the heavens’ expanse to His wisdom and strength.

- Genesis 1:1 lays the foundation—“In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth”—and Psalm 19:1 reminds us that those heavens “declare the glory of God,” continuously bearing witness to their Maker.

- Like a tent’s fabric, the universe depends on its poles and stakes; if He withdrew His sustaining hand, the cosmos would collapse. Every sunrise, galaxy, and star testifies to His ongoing care.

summary

Psalm 104:2 invites us to see the Lord both as Light itself and as the cosmic Architect. He is personally glorious—clothed in radiant purity—and universally sovereign, spreading the heavens overhead with ease. Together these pictures call us to worship Him with awe, confidence, and delight, knowing that the same God who illuminates all creation also upholds it moment by moment.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) Who coverest.--Perhaps better with the participles of the original retained:

Putting on light as a robe;

Spreading the heavens as a curtain.

The psalmist does not think of the formation of light as of a single past act, but as a continued glorious operation of Divine power and splendour. Not only is light as to the modern poet,

"Nature's resplendent robe,

Without whose vesting beauty all were wrapt

In unessential gloom,"

but it is the dress of Divinity, the "ethereal woof" that God Himself is for ever weaving for His own wear.

Curtain.--Especially of a tent (see Song of Solomon 1:5, &c.), the tremulous movement of its folds being expressed in the Hebrew word. Different explanations have been given of the figure. Some see an allusion to the curtains of the Tabernacle (Exodus 26, 27). The associations of this ritual were dear to a religious Hebrew, and he may well have had in his mind the rich folds of the curtain of the Holy of Holies. So a modern poet speaks of

"The arras-folds, that variegate . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - Who coverest thyself with light as with a garment. Light was the first thing created (Genesis 1:3), before either the heaven (Genesis 1:6-8) or the earth (Genesis 1:9, 10). In light God, the invisible, as it were, enshrouds himself, making it the image of his hidden glory. Who stretchest out the heavens like a curtain; or, "a canopy" (comp. Isaiah 40:22; Isaiah 42:5; Isaiah 44:25). The metaphor is taken from the stretching out or "spreading out" of a tent (see Isaiah 40:22).
