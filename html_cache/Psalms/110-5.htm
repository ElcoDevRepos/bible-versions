The Lord is at Your right hand;
This phrase indicates a position of power and authority. In ancient Near Eastern culture, the right hand was a symbol of strength and honor. The "Lord" here refers to God, who is depicted as supporting and empowering the king, traditionally understood as the Messiah. This imagery is consistent with other biblical passages where God is described as being at the right hand of His people, providing protection and victory (Psalm 16:8, Psalm 121:5). Theologically, this can be seen as a foreshadowing of Christ's exaltation, as He is seated at the right hand of God (Mark 16:19, Hebrews 1:3).

He will crush kings in the day of His wrath.
This phrase speaks to divine judgment and the ultimate victory over earthly powers. The imagery of crushing kings suggests a decisive and overwhelming defeat, which is a common theme in prophetic literature (Isaiah 24:21, Revelation 19:15). The "day of His wrath" refers to a time of judgment, often associated with the Day of the Lord, a future event when God will execute justice and establish His kingdom (Zephaniah 1:14-18, Joel 2:1-11). This can be seen as a messianic prophecy, pointing to Christ's return when He will judge the nations and establish His eternal reign (Matthew 25:31-46, Revelation 19:11-21).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Lord (Yahweh)
In this context, "The Lord" refers to God, who is depicted as a divine warrior and judge.

2. Your Right Hand
This phrase signifies a position of power and authority. In ancient times, the right hand was associated with strength and honor.

3. Kings
These are earthly rulers who stand in opposition to God's anointed. They symbolize human authority and power that is subject to God's ultimate judgment.

4. Day of His Wrath
This refers to a future time of divine judgment when God will execute justice against His enemies.

5. Messianic Expectation
The psalm is often interpreted as a Messianic prophecy, pointing to Jesus Christ as the ultimate fulfillment of God's promise to defeat His enemies.
Teaching Points
Divine Authority and Judgment
God's authority is supreme, and His judgment is inevitable. Believers should live in reverence and obedience to His will, recognizing His ultimate power over all earthly authorities.

Christ as the Fulfillment
Jesus Christ is the fulfillment of the Messianic prophecy in Psalm 110. As believers, we can find assurance in His victory over sin and death, and His role as our eternal King.

The Right Hand of Power
The imagery of the right hand signifies strength and honor. As followers of Christ, we are called to rely on His strength and seek His guidance in our daily lives.

Living in Light of Future Judgment
Understanding the reality of God's future judgment should motivate us to share the gospel and live lives that reflect His righteousness and justice.

Hope in God's Justice
In a world where injustice often prevails, believers can find hope in the promise that God will one day set all things right and establish His perfect justice.

What Does Psalm 110:5 Mean
The Lord

Psalm 110 opens by distinguishing between “the LORD” (Yahweh) and “my Lord” (the Messiah). In verse 5, the focus shifts back to the Messiah and calls Him “The Lord,” underscoring His full authority. Peter applies this verse to Jesus in Acts 2:34-36, affirming that the risen Christ is the sovereign Lord. Jesus Himself references Psalm 110 in Matthew 22:41-45 to demonstrate His superiority to David. Hebrews 1:13 quotes the psalm again to show that no angel shares this place of honor. All of Scripture agrees that the “Lord” here is Jesus, the promised King-Priest who reigns forever (Psalm 2:6-9; Revelation 19:16).

is at Your right hand

To be at the right hand is to hold the supreme place of honor and power.

• Mark 16:19 records that, after His resurrection, Jesus “was taken up into heaven and sat down at the right hand of God.”

• Ephesians 1:20-22 explains that the Father “seated Him at His right hand in the heavenly realms, far above all rule and authority.”

• Colossians 3:1 invites believers to “seek the things that are above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God.”

This position shows:

• Complete approval—Father and Son in perfect unity.

• Unlimited authority—every power is beneath His feet.

• Constant intercession—He pleads for His people even as He rules (Romans 8:34).

He will crush kings

The language is military and decisive. The Messiah is not merely a spiritual figure; He is a warrior-King who will dismantle every human power that opposes Him. Psalm 2:9 says He will “break them with an iron scepter.” Isaiah 11:4 foretells that “with the breath of His lips He will slay the wicked.” Daniel 2:44 promises a kingdom that “will crush all those kingdoms and bring them to an end.” Revelation 19:11-16 pictures Jesus returning to “strike down the nations.” 1 Corinthians 15:24-25 says He will reign “until He has put all His enemies under His feet.” The crushing is literal, future, and certain.

in the day of His wrath

This is the climactic “day of the LORD,” a fixed point in history when divine patience gives way to righteous judgment (Zephaniah 1:14-18). Romans 2:5 warns that unrepentant hearts are “storing up wrath for yourself in the day of wrath.” Revelation 6:15-17 shows kings and generals hiding in terror, crying, “Fall on us … hide us from the face of Him who sits on the throne and from the wrath of the Lamb.” Yet for believers, 1 Thessalonians 1:10 assures that Jesus “rescues us from the coming wrath.” His wrath is not capricious anger but holy justice against sin.

summary

Psalm 110:5 presents the exalted Jesus seated at the Father’s right hand, fully endorsed and empowered. From that place of honor He will personally shatter every rebellious ruler when His long-foretold day of wrath arrives. This verse calls us to worship the reigning Christ, rest in His present intercession, and live ready for His certain, righteous, and triumphant return.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) The Lord at thy right hand.--We are naturally tempted to understand this as still of the king whom the first verse placed at Jehovah's right hand. But the word for Lord here is Adonai, which is nowhere else used except of God. Moreover, God throughout has as yet appeared as the active agent. It is He who stretched out the sceptre and conferred the office of priest; and hitherto the king has been the person addressed. It is therefore necessary still to consider him as addressed, and suppose that the change of position of Jehovah from the king's right hand to his left is simply due to the usage of the language. To sit at the right hand was an emblem of honour, to stand at the right hand was a figure of protecting might (Psalm 16:8; Psalm 109:31); and the imagery of a battle into which the song now plunges caused the change of expression.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - The Lord at thy right hand shall strike through kings in the day of his wrath. "Adonai" here is certainly Jehovah (Cheyne). He stands at Messiah's right hand (comp. Psalm 16:8; Psalm 121:5) to protect and defend him, and give him victory in the battle. Ver. 5 carries on the description of Messiah's triumph begun in ver. 3. The kings to be "struck through" are those that resist the progress of the gospel - Herod Agrippa, Galerius, Julian, and the like.
