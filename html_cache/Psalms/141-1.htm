A Psalm of David.
This introduction identifies the author as David, the second king of Israel, known for his deep relationship with God and his role as a psalmist. David's life, marked by both triumphs and trials, provides a rich backdrop for understanding his psalms. His experiences as a shepherd, warrior, and king inform his writings, often reflecting a heart seeking after God. The psalms attributed to David are integral to the worship practices of ancient Israel and continue to be central in Christian liturgy.

I call upon You, O LORD;
David's use of "LORD" (YHWH) signifies a personal and covenantal relationship with God. This invocation reflects a deep trust and reliance on God's character and promises. The act of calling upon the Lord is a recurring theme in the Psalms, emphasizing prayer as a vital means of communication with God. It echoes the practice of seeking divine intervention and guidance, as seen throughout the Old Testament, such as in the lives of the patriarchs and prophets.

come quickly to me.
This plea for urgency highlights David's immediate need for God's presence and assistance. It reflects the human experience of desperation and the desire for swift divine intervention. The phrase can be connected to other biblical instances where God's timely response is sought, such as in the cries of the Israelites in Egypt or the prayers of the prophets. It underscores the belief in God's ability to act promptly in times of distress.

Hear my voice when I call to You.
David's request for God to hear his voice underscores the personal nature of his prayer. It reflects a confidence that God is attentive to the cries of His people. This assurance is rooted in the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God promises to listen to His people's prayers. The phrase resonates with other scriptural affirmations of God's attentiveness, such as in 1 John 5:14, which speaks of the confidence believers have in approaching God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of this psalm, David is expressing his urgent plea to God. Known as a man after God's own heart, David often turned to God in times of distress.

2. LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people. David is calling upon Yahweh, highlighting a personal and covenantal relationship.

3. Prayer
The act of calling upon God, which is central to this verse. David's prayer is marked by urgency and a deep desire for God's immediate presence and intervention.
Teaching Points
Urgency in Prayer
David's plea for God to "come quickly" teaches us the importance of urgency in our prayers, especially in times of distress. We should not hesitate to bring our immediate needs before God.

Confidence in God's Response
David's call to God reflects a deep confidence that God hears and responds. We are encouraged to pray with the assurance that God listens to His children.

Personal Relationship with God
The use of "O LORD" signifies a personal relationship. Our prayers should stem from a personal connection with God, recognizing Him as our covenant-keeping Lord.

Consistency in Prayer
David's example shows the importance of consistently turning to God in prayer, regardless of circumstances. We should cultivate a habit of seeking God regularly.

What Does Psalm 141:1 Mean
A Psalm of David

• The heading is part of the inspired text, identifying the writer as Israel’s shepherd-king. The same superscription appears in other prayers of deliverance, such as “A Psalm of David, when he fled from Absalom” (Psalm 3:1).

• By naming David, the Spirit roots the psalm in real history (2 Samuel 22 parallels many of his songs) and reminds us that God’s faithfulness spans a lifetime—pastures, caves, thrones alike.

• Acts 4:25 affirms that David spoke “by the Holy Spirit,” so every line that follows carries divine authority and reliability.

• David’s example encourages us: “In my distress I called upon the LORD…He heard my voice” (Psalm 18:6). The God who answered him still answers today (Hebrews 13:8).

I call upon You, O LORD

• David’s first move is prayer, not self-reliance. “The righteous cry out, and the LORD hears” (Psalm 34:17).

• The personal name “LORD” (Yahweh) highlights covenant relationship; David knows exactly whom he is addressing, just as believers “call on the name of the Lord” for salvation (Romans 10:13).

• Calling implies faith: “‘Call to Me and I will answer you’” (Jeremiah 33:3). It also implies humility—David is king, yet he bows before the King of kings.

• When trouble presses, we follow the same pattern: “I call to God Most High, to God who fulfills His purpose for me” (Psalm 57:2).

come quickly to me

• The plea is urgent. David does not merely hope God will act; he asks Him to act now. “Make haste, O God, to deliver me! Hurry, O LORD, to help me!” (Psalm 70:1).

• Urgency is not impatience but confidence that God is able: “I tell you, He will promptly carry out justice for them” (Luke 18:8).

• Such cries invite us to approach “the throne of grace…to find grace to help in time of need” (Hebrews 4:16).

• God’s swift intervention in previous trials fuels trust for the present one (Psalm 22:19; 38:22).

Hear my voice when I call to You

• David expects to be heard. “Answer me when I call, O God of my righteousness” (Psalm 4:1).

• Hearing in Scripture is more than passive listening; it implies action. “The LORD hears and delivers them from all their troubles” (Psalm 34:17).

• New-covenant believers share the same assurance: “If we ask anything according to His will, He hears us” (1 John 5:14).

• Notice the repetition of “call.” Persistence is commended throughout Scripture—“I have called on You, for You will answer me” (Psalm 17:6).

summary

David teaches us to run first and fast to the Lord. The heading grounds the prayer in real, Spirit-inspired history; the opening cry models personal, covenantal faith; the urgent “come quickly” shows confident dependence; the request to be heard rests on God’s sure promise to listen and act. In every season, we can echo this verse—calling, pleading, and trusting the God who always hears His people.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - Lord, I cry unto thee; make haste unto me. The need is pressing and urgent. God is therefore entreated to "hasten" (comp. Psalm 22:19; Psalm 31:2; Psalm 38:22; Psalm 40:17, etc.). Give ear unto my voice, when I cry unto thee (comp. Psalm 102:2).
