They devoured every plant in their land
This phrase refers to the plague of locusts that God sent upon Egypt as one of the ten plagues described in the Book of Exodus (Exodus 10:12-15). The locusts were a divine judgment against Pharaoh and the Egyptians for their refusal to release the Israelites from slavery. In the ancient Near East, locust swarms were a well-known and feared natural disaster, capable of devastating crops and leading to famine. The use of locusts as a plague highlights God's control over nature and His ability to use it to fulfill His purposes. This event is a demonstration of God's power and a reminder of the consequences of hardening one's heart against His commands.

and consumed the produce of their soil.
The destruction of the produce of the soil signifies the complete devastation of Egypt's agricultural resources. Agriculture was the backbone of the Egyptian economy, and the Nile River's annual flooding made the land particularly fertile. The locusts consuming the produce would have led to severe economic and social consequences, emphasizing the severity of God's judgment. This phrase also connects to the broader biblical theme of God's provision and the consequences of disobedience. In contrast, the Israelites, who were obedient to God's instructions, were spared from the plagues, illustrating the protective nature of God's covenant with His people. This event foreshadows the ultimate deliverance of the Israelites from Egypt, a type of Christ's deliverance of believers from sin.

Persons / Places / Events

3. Pharaoh
The ruler of Egypt who hardened his heart against God and refused to let the Israelites go, prompting the plagues.

4. The Plague of Locusts
One of the ten plagues that God sent upon Egypt, which devastated the land by consuming all vegetation.

5. The Israelites
God's chosen people who were enslaved in Egypt and were the beneficiaries of God's deliverance through the plagues.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Judgment
The plague of locusts demonstrates God's control over nature and His ability to use it as an instrument of judgment. Believers should recognize God's sovereignty and respond with reverence and obedience.

The Consequences of Hardness of Heart
Pharaoh's refusal to heed God's warnings led to severe consequences for Egypt. This serves as a warning against hardening our hearts to God's voice and commands.

God's Faithfulness to His People
Despite the devastation in Egypt, God was working to deliver His people. This reminds us of God's faithfulness and His commitment to fulfill His promises.

The Importance of Repentance
The plagues were opportunities for Pharaoh to repent and turn to God. Similarly, we are called to examine our lives and repent of any disobedience.

Trust in God's Deliverance
Just as God delivered the Israelites from Egypt, He is able to deliver us from our own trials and challenges. We are encouraged to trust in His power and timing.

What Does Psalm 105:35 Mean
They devoured

The verse opens with an image of utter consumption. “They” points back to the swarming locusts God sent on Egypt (Exodus 10:12–14). Picture a dark cloud settling over fields, leaves disappearing in seconds. Scripture treats this as a literal historical event, one of the ten plagues demonstrating the Lord’s absolute power over nature, Pharaoh, and Egypt’s gods (Exodus 10:1–2; Psalm 78:46). It reminds us:

• Judgment can fall swiftly and irresistibly.

• God’s word about judgment is never exaggerated—what He says happens exactly as stated (Numbers 23:19).

every plant in their land

The devastation was total; nothing green escaped. Psalm 105 emphasizes “every” to underline completeness. Comparable language appears in Joel 1:4, where successive waves of locusts leave no vegetation, and Isaiah 37:24 speaks of an enemy boasting he “dug wells and drank waters,” yet God alone can strip a land bare in moments (Psalm 147:17–18). Key takeaways:

• God’s judgments leave no hiding place (Amos 9:2–3).

• The land of Egypt, renowned for fertility, discovered its apparent security meant nothing against the Lord (Genesis 13:10).

and consumed

The repetition—devoured, then consumed—intensifies the scene. In Exodus 10:15 the says, “They devoured all the vegetation of the land… until nothing green remained.” The double statement stresses how thoroughly God dismantled Egypt’s food supply:

• Repetition in Scripture drives home certainty (Genesis 41:32).

• When God moves in discipline, He leaves no doubt about who acted (Psalm 76:7–9).

the produce of their soil

Not only were growing plants lost; stored crops and forthcoming harvests were gone. Egypt’s breadbasket became barren (Deuteronomy 28:38–39 illustrates a similar curse). By striking “the produce of their soil,” God exposed the futility of trusting in earthly abundance (Luke 12:16–21). For Israel, watching these events affirmed:

• The covenant Lord could strip the mightiest empire yet preserve His people (Psalm 105:26, 37).

• Redemption often comes through judgments that topple idols (Exodus 12:12).

summary

Psalm 105:35 recounts the locust plague as a literal, total destruction of Egypt’s vegetation and stored food, showcasing God’s supreme authority, faithfulness to His covenant with Israel, and ability to judge sin decisively. The verse calls readers to stand in awe of His power, trust His promises, and forsake any reliance on earthly security.
