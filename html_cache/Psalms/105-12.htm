When they were few in number
This phrase refers to the early patriarchs of Israel, such as Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who were the forefathers of the Israelite nation. At this time, the family of Abraham was small, emphasizing their vulnerability and dependence on God's promises. This small beginning is significant in biblical history, as it highlights God's ability to fulfill His promises despite seemingly insurmountable odds. The promise to Abraham in Genesis 12:2 that his descendants would become a great nation underscores the miraculous growth from a small family to a populous nation.

Few indeed
The repetition of "few" emphasizes the insignificance of their numbers in the context of the larger populations around them. This serves to highlight God's providence and protection over them. It also reflects the theme of God choosing the weak and insignificant to accomplish His purposes, as seen throughout Scripture (1 Corinthians 1:27-29). The smallness of their numbers is a testament to God's faithfulness in preserving and multiplying them despite their initial insignificance.

And strangers in the land
The patriarchs were sojourners in Canaan, living as foreigners in a land that was promised to them but not yet possessed. This status as "strangers" is a recurring theme in the lives of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who lived in tents and moved frequently (Hebrews 11:9). It underscores the transient nature of their existence and their reliance on God's promise of a future inheritance. This concept of being strangers and pilgrims is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are described as aliens and strangers in the world (1 Peter 2:11), pointing to the ultimate fulfillment of God's promises in a heavenly kingdom.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Abraham and Sarah
The patriarch and matriarch of the Israelites, who were called by God to leave their homeland and journey to a land He would show them. They were the beginning of God's covenant people, often few in number and strangers in foreign lands.

2. Canaan
The land promised by God to Abraham and his descendants. At the time of Abraham, it was inhabited by various tribes, making Abraham and his family strangers in this land.

3. Isaac and Jacob
The son and grandson of Abraham, who continued to live as sojourners in the land of Canaan, maintaining the promise given to Abraham.

4. Egypt
A place where the Israelites would later become numerous but also strangers and slaves, highlighting their journey from few to many.

5. The Covenant
God's promise to Abraham to make his descendants a great nation, despite their small beginnings and status as strangers.
Teaching Points
God's Faithfulness in Small Beginnings
God often starts with small beginnings to fulfill His grand purposes. Just as He began with a small family to create a great nation, He can use our seemingly insignificant efforts for His glory.

Living as Strangers
As believers, we are called to live as strangers and pilgrims in this world, looking forward to our eternal home. Our identity is not rooted in earthly status but in our relationship with God.

Trust in God's Promises
Despite circumstances that seem contrary, we are to trust in God's promises. His faithfulness to Abraham and his descendants assures us of His faithfulness to us.

The Power of Obedience
Abraham's obedience to God's call, despite being few and strangers, is a model for us. Obedience to God can lead to blessings beyond our imagination.

Community and Growth
The growth of the Israelites from a small family to a nation reminds us of the importance of community and the potential for growth when we are united in God's purpose.

What Does Psalm 105:12 Mean
When they were few in number

Psalm 105 is rehearsing God’s faithfulness to the patriarchs. Verse 12 opens with the reminder that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob began as a very small family unit. Genesis 12:1-3 shows only Abraham and Sarah stepping out in faith; by Genesis 15:5-6, the promise of countless descendants is contrasted with the single, childless couple. God keeps count of every individual in His covenant family, as seen again in Deuteronomy 7:7 where Israel is told, “The LORD set His affection on you and chose you, because you were the fewest of all peoples.” Though tiny, they were never insignificant to Him.

Key takeaways:

• God delights in starting great works with small beginnings.

• Numerical weakness magnifies divine strength (cf. Judges 7:2-7).

• Each believer today can rest in the truth that God values even the “few” (Matthew 18:20).

few indeed

The repetition underscores vulnerability. Genesis 46:27 counts only seventy members when Jacob’s clan enters Egypt. Humanly speaking, such a handful could be erased by famine, conflict, or assimilation. Yet the very next verses in Psalm 105 (vv. 13-15) highlight how God “allowed no one to oppress them; He rebuked kings on their behalf.” He guards the fragile until His purposes ripen (Exodus 1:7 speaks of explosive growth after the period of being “few indeed”).

Points to notice:

• Double emphasis shows God’s people have no strength apart from Him (2 Corinthians 12:9-10).

• History validates that His covenant promises never depend on majority status.

• Encouragement for modern believers: apparent smallness is not defeat but stage-setting for God’s glory (Zechariah 4:10).

and strangers in the land

Abraham lived in tents “as a stranger in a foreign country” (Hebrews 11:9). Psalm 105 reminds readers that the patriarchs possessed no legal claim to Canaan at first; Genesis 23 records Abraham negotiating just to buy a burial plot. Still, God’s promise in Genesis 17:8—“the whole land of Canaan… I will give as an everlasting possession”—stood firm. Being “strangers” heightens God’s protective care, evidenced when He warns pagan kings in Genesis 12:17 and 20:3 not to touch Sarah, and later when He shields Israel in Egypt (Psalm 105:37).

Living as pilgrims:

• God’s people often occupy earthly spaces without permanent status (1 Peter 2:11).

• Their true security lies not in citizenship papers but in covenant promises (Philippians 3:20).

• God actively defends His sojourners until He hands them the inheritance prepared for them (Joshua 21:43-45).

summary

Verse 12 spotlights the humble, exposed beginnings of the covenant family—tiny in number, doubly emphasized as vulnerable, and resident aliens in foreign territory. Every phrase magnifies the Lord’s power to protect, multiply, and eventually settle His people exactly as He vowed. What seemed fragile was in fact firmly held by the unbreakable promises of God, inviting believers today to trust Him with every weakness, minority status, and season of sojourning.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - When they were but a few men in number; literally, when they were men of number; i.e. when they could be easily counted. A few scores at the utmost, or, with their entire households, a few hundreds (Genesis 14:14; Genesis 33:1). Yea, very few, and strangers in it; i.e. "in the land of Canaan" (comp. Exodus 6:4).
