Before I was afflicted
This phrase suggests a time of ease or comfort before experiencing hardship. In biblical context, affliction often serves as a tool for correction or growth. The Israelites frequently experienced affliction as a result of straying from God's commandments, as seen in the cycles of sin and redemption in the Book of Judges. Affliction can be seen as a form of divine discipline, intended to bring about repentance and a return to God. Hebrews 12:6 states, "For the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives," highlighting the purpose of affliction in the believer's life.

I went astray
Going astray implies a deviation from God's path or commandments. This is a common theme throughout Scripture, where God's people often turn away from His laws and suffer the consequences. The parable of the lost sheep in Luke 15:3-7 illustrates God's desire to bring back those who have gone astray. The concept of straying is also linked to the human condition of sinfulness, as seen in Isaiah 53:6, "All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned—every one—to his own way."

but now I keep Your word
This phrase indicates a transformation or change in behavior following affliction. Keeping God's word is a central theme in the Bible, emphasizing obedience and adherence to His commandments. The psalmist's commitment to keeping God's word reflects a heart that has been corrected and realigned with God's will. This transformation can be seen as a type of repentance, where the individual turns away from sin and towards God. Jesus Christ, in John 14:15, says, "If you love me, you will keep my commandments," underscoring the importance of obedience as an expression of love and faithfulness to God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep love for God's law and a desire for spiritual growth.

2. God
The divine being to whom the psalmist is speaking, seeking guidance, correction, and understanding.

3. Affliction
The period of suffering or hardship that the psalmist refers to, which serves as a turning point in his spiritual journey.

4. Israel
The nation often seen as the collective recipient of God's laws and teachings, frequently experiencing cycles of straying and returning to God.

5. The Word of God
The divine instructions, commandments, and teachings that the psalmist commits to following after experiencing affliction.
Teaching Points
The Purpose of Affliction
Affliction serves as a divine tool for correction and realignment with God's will. It is not merely punitive but redemptive, guiding us back to the path of righteousness.

The Role of God's Word
The Word of God is a steadfast guide that provides wisdom and direction. Post-affliction, the psalmist's commitment to keeping God's word highlights its importance in maintaining spiritual discipline.

Spiritual Growth through Trials
Trials and afflictions are opportunities for spiritual growth. They refine our character and deepen our relationship with God, leading to a more profound obedience and understanding of His will.

The Importance of Obedience
Obedience to God's word is a response to His love and discipline. It is through obedience that we experience the fullness of God's promises and blessings.

Reflection and Repentance
Times of affliction should lead to self-reflection and repentance. Recognizing our straying and returning to God is a crucial step in our spiritual journey.

What Does Psalm 119:67 Mean
Before I was afflicted

• The psalmist remembers a season of hardship—not as random pain, but as God-permitted discipline. Hebrews 12:6 reminds us, “For the Lord disciplines the one He loves.”

• Affliction often exposes self-reliance and redirects the heart. Deuteronomy 8:2 says God allowed Israel to hunger “to humble you and test you, to know what was in your heart.”

• Psalm 119:71 echoes the same lesson: “It was good for me to be afflicted, that I might learn Your statutes.” Instead of viewing trouble as punishment alone, Scripture frames it as a tutor leading toward holiness.

I went astray

• Before the hardship, the psalmist confesses wandering. Isaiah 53:6 admits, “We all like sheep have gone astray; each one has turned to his own way.”

• Going astray isn’t merely getting lost; it is willful drift—choosing self-designed paths over God’s. Proverbs 14:12 warns, “There is a way that seems right to a man, but its end is the way of death.”

• The verse owns personal responsibility: “I went astray,” not “circumstances led me.” Genuine repentance begins by naming the wrong turn.

But now

• Two small words signal a turning point. Affliction did not end in bitterness; it produced change. 2 Corinthians 5:17 describes such moments: “If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; the old has passed away; behold, the new has come.”

• The contrast emphasizes God’s restoring power. Psalm 30:5 assures, “Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning.” Sorrow becomes the doorway to spiritual renewal.

I keep Your word

• The outcome of the journey is obedience, not merely relief. John 14:15 records Jesus’ words, “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments.”

• Obedience is active and ongoing—“I keep,” not “I kept.” The experience forged a continual resolve to live by Scripture.

• Psalm 1:2 paints the blessed person whose “delight is in the law of the LORD, and on His law he meditates day and night.” Affliction moved the psalmist from casual acquaintance with God’s Word to joyful, disciplined allegiance.

summary

Affliction served as God’s gracious wake-up call. Before hardship, the psalmist wandered; through hardship, he was redirected; after hardship, he embraced consistent obedience. Psalm 119:67 assures us that God can transform suffering into steadfast devotion, turning wandering hearts into Word-keeping hearts.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(67) That there is allusion here to the Babylonian exile, and its moral and religious effect on the nation, there can be little doubt.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 67. - Before I was afflicted I went astray. "Sweet are the uses of adversity." The psalmist feels end confesses that the afflictions, which he has suffered (see comment on ver. 65), have been good for him. They have made him less apt to "go astray" than he was (comp. ver. 71). But now have I kept thy Word (comp. vers. 51, 56, 87, etc.).
