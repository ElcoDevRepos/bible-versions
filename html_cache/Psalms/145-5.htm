the glorious splendor of Your majesty
This phrase emphasizes the overwhelming beauty and magnificence of God's divine nature. The term "glorious splendor" suggests a radiance that is both awe-inspiring and beyond human comprehension. In biblical context, God's majesty is often associated with His kingship and authority over creation, as seen in passages like Psalm 93:1, which declares, "The LORD reigns, He is robed in majesty." The imagery of splendor and majesty is also reminiscent of the visions of God in Isaiah 6:1-4 and Revelation 4, where God's throne is depicted with grandeur and holiness. Historically, the concept of majesty would resonate with ancient audiences familiar with the opulence of earthly kings, yet God's majesty surpasses all. This phrase invites believers to recognize and revere God's supreme power and beauty.

And I will meditate on Your wondrous works
Meditation in this context refers to deep, reflective thought and contemplation on God's actions and deeds. The "wondrous works" of God include His creation, providence, and acts of salvation throughout history. This aligns with passages like Psalm 77:12, "I will consider all Your works and meditate on all Your mighty deeds." The practice of meditation is rooted in the Jewish tradition of pondering God's law and works, as seen in Joshua 1:8. Culturally, meditation was a way to internalize and understand divine truths. Theologically, this phrase encourages believers to focus on God's past faithfulness and miraculous interventions, fostering trust and gratitude. It also points to the ultimate wondrous work in the person of Jesus Christ, whose life, death, and resurrection are central to Christian faith.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 145, King David, is known for his deep relationship with God and his ability to express his devotion through psalms and songs. This psalm is a hymn of praise, highlighting God's majesty and works.

2. God's Majesty
The central focus of this verse is the majesty of God, which refers to His supreme greatness, authority, and splendor.

3. Wondrous Works
This refers to the miraculous and awe-inspiring acts of God throughout history, including creation, deliverance, and ongoing providence.
Teaching Points
Recognizing God's Majesty
Reflect on the greatness of God in your daily life. Consider how His majesty is evident in creation and in the details of your personal experiences.

Proclaiming His Works
Make it a practice to share testimonies of God's wondrous works in your life with others. This can be a powerful witness to His active presence in the world.

Cultivating Awe and Reverence
Develop a heart of worship by regularly meditating on God's attributes and actions. Allow this to deepen your reverence and awe for Him.

Engaging in Praise
Use the Psalms as a guide for your own expressions of praise. Let David's words inspire you to articulate your own thoughts about God's splendor and works.

Living in Gratitude
Let the recognition of God's majesty and works lead you to a lifestyle of gratitude. Acknowledge His hand in both the big and small aspects of your life.

What Does Psalm 145:5 Mean
The glorious splendor of Your majesty

“On the glorious splendor of Your majesty…” (Psalm 145:5)

• David’s words invite us to pause and see God as He truly is—radiant, unrivaled, and magnificent, like the dazzling throne scene of Isaiah 6:1–3 or the brilliant description in Revelation 4:2–3.

• Glory and splendor point to qualities that belong to God alone; 1 Chronicles 29:11 echoes, “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness, the power and the glory.”

• Majesty emphasizes His sovereign rule (Psalm 93:1–2) and His overwhelming beauty (Psalm 96:6).

• When believers behold this splendor, awe naturally follows—just as Moses removed his sandals before the burning bush (Exodus 3:5) and Peter fell at Jesus’ knees after the miraculous catch (Luke 5:8).

• Seeing God’s grandeur strengthens trust. If His glory fills heaven and earth (Psalm 8:1), no earthly threat can shake those who belong to Him (Psalm 46:1–3).

And I will meditate on Your wondrous works

“…and on Your wondrous works, I will meditate.” (Psalm 145:5)

• Meditation means lingering thought, turning God’s deeds over in the mind with gratitude, like Mary “treasuring up all these things” (Luke 2:19).

• Scripture often pairs remembering with faith: “I will remember the works of the LORD; yes, I will remember Your wonders of old. I will reflect on all You have done” (Psalm 77:11–12).

• God’s “wondrous works” include creation (Psalm 19:1), redemption from Egypt (Exodus 15:13), sustaining providence (Nehemiah 9:19–21), and the saving cross of Christ (Romans 5:8).

• Practical ways to meditate:

– Read passages like Psalm 136 aloud, noting each repeated “His love endures forever.”

– Keep a journal of answered prayers, echoing 1 Samuel 7:12’s “Thus far the LORD has helped us.”

– Share testimonies in community, fulfilling Psalm 145:4: “One generation shall declare Your works to the next.”

• Such meditation fuels worship (Psalm 103:1–5), renews hope (Lamentations 3:21–23), and guards against forgetfulness (Deuteronomy 4:9).

summary

Psalm 145:5 calls believers first to gaze on God’s limitless majesty, then to dwell thoughtfully on His mighty deeds. By fixing our hearts on who He is and what He has done, we cultivate reverence, gratitude, and steadfast faith.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) I will speak.--Or, perhaps, sing. The verb is often rendered meditate (Psalm 77:12; Psalm 119:15, &c.):

Thy wondrous works.--Rather, as in Psalm 105:27 (see Note; comp. Psalm 65:3), the details of thy wonders. In psalms like 105, 106, &c, is the detailed fulfilment of this purpose.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - I will speak of the glorious honor of thy majesty, and of thy wondrous works (comp. Psalm 26:7; Psalm 71:17). It was the duty of every faithful Israelite to set forth God's majesty, and to "declare his works with rejoicing" (Psalm 117:2). David proclaims himself ready to perform this duty. Then, he thinks, others will join in.
