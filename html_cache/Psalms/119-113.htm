The double-minded I despise
This phrase highlights a strong aversion to those who are inconsistent or wavering in their faith and commitment. In biblical context, double-mindedness is often associated with instability and lack of faith, as seen in James 1:8, which describes a double-minded person as unstable in all their ways. The psalmist's disdain for double-mindedness reflects a desire for purity and sincerity in one's relationship with God. Historically, the Israelites were often tempted to waver between worshiping Yahweh and following pagan practices, leading to a call for wholehearted devotion. This phrase underscores the importance of a single-minded focus on God, rejecting any form of spiritual compromise.

but Your law I love
The psalmist expresses a deep affection for God's law, which is a central theme throughout Psalm 119. This love for the law signifies more than just adherence to rules; it reflects a profound appreciation for God's guidance and wisdom. The law, or Torah, was given to the Israelites as a covenantal guide for living in a way that pleases God. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, the law was not merely legalistic but was seen as a source of life and blessing. This love for the law is echoed in other scriptures, such as Psalm 1:2, where the righteous delight in the law of the Lord. The psalmist's love for the law can also be seen as a type of Christ, who perfectly fulfilled the law and embodied its principles. Jesus, in Matthew 5:17, affirmed that He came not to abolish the law but to fulfill it, demonstrating the ultimate expression of love for God's commandments.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep love for God's law and a disdain for those who are double-minded.

2. Double-minded
Refers to individuals who are indecisive or inconsistent in their faith and commitment to God, often swaying between belief and unbelief.

3. God's Law
The Torah or the teachings and commandments given by God, which the psalmist loves and adheres to as a guide for righteous living.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Double-mindedness
Double-mindedness leads to instability and a lack of true commitment to God. As believers, we are called to be steadfast in our faith and not waver between worldly desires and spiritual devotion.

Loving God's Law
The psalmist's love for God's law serves as a model for us. We should cultivate a deep appreciation and love for Scripture, allowing it to guide our thoughts, actions, and decisions.

Commitment to Righteousness
Our commitment to God's law should be unwavering. This involves daily reading, meditation, and application of Scripture in our lives, ensuring that our actions align with God's will.

Discernment in Relationships
The psalmist's hatred for the double-minded can also be a call for discernment in our relationships. We should surround ourselves with those who encourage and strengthen our faith, rather than those who lead us astray.

Consistency in Faith
Consistency in our faith journey is crucial. We should strive to be consistent in our prayer life, worship, and obedience to God's commands, reflecting a single-minded devotion to Him.

What Does Psalm 119:113 Mean
The double-minded

• David is speaking of people whose hearts run in two directions at once—professing reverence for God yet chasing the world’s values (James 1:8, “A double-minded man is unstable in all his ways,”).

• Their wavering mirrors the Israel of Elijah’s day: “How long will you waver between two opinions?” (1 Kings 18:21).

• Such divided loyalty offends God, for He calls for wholehearted devotion (Matthew 6:24; Revelation 3:15-16).

I despise

• “Despise” here is not spiteful dislike but a righteous rejection of duplicity. David refuses to admire or imitate it (Proverbs 8:13, “To fear the LORD is to hate evil,”).

• Hatred of double-mindedness safeguards us from compromise: “I will set no vile thing before my eyes… I hate the deeds of faithless men” (Psalm 101:3).

• By turning away from divided hearts, we walk the narrow path that leads to life (Matthew 7:13-14).

but Your law

• The stark contrast “but” shifts focus from human inconsistency to divine certainty. God’s law is fixed, pure, and trustworthy (Psalm 19:7-9).

• Scripture provides the solid footing the double-minded lack: “Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path” (Psalm 119:105).

• Meditating on that law day and night fuels obedience and prosperity of soul (Joshua 1:8; Psalm 1:2-3).

I love

• Love for God’s Word is more than duty; it is heartfelt delight: “Oh, how I love Your law! All day long it is my meditation” (Psalm 119:97).

• This affection draws us to continual communion with the Author, echoing Paul: “I delight in the law of God in my inner being” (Romans 7:22).

• Love replaces lukewarmness with wholehearted surrender, making obedience a joy rather than a burden (1 John 5:3).

summary

Psalm 119:113 sets up a vivid contrast: the psalmist decisively rejects the instability of the double-minded and wholeheartedly embraces the stability of God’s unfailing Word. By despising divided allegiance and loving divine instruction, we anchor ourselves in truth, walk securely, and reflect a single-hearted devotion that honors the Lord.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
SAMECH.

(113) I hate vain thoughts.--Rather, I hate men who halt between two opinions, following 1Kings 18:21, where the cognate noun from the same root, to divide, appears. Probably we are to think of those among the Jews who were for political reasons favourably inclined towards foreign customs and ideas, and who would not throw in their lot frankly and courageously with the national party.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 113. - I hate vain thoughts; rather, unstable or double-minded men (Kay, Cheyne, Revised Version); i.e. "those who are undecided in religion" (Cheyne). But thy Law do I love (comp. vers. 97, 119, 127, 159, 163). There is nothing "unstable" or ',double-minded" in thy Law. It is clear direct, unmistakable.
