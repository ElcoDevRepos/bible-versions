I will lift the cup of salvation
This phrase signifies an act of worship and thanksgiving. In ancient Israel, lifting a cup was a symbolic gesture often associated with offering a drink offering to God, as seen in various Old Testament practices. The "cup of salvation" can be understood as a metaphor for the deliverance and blessings received from God. This imagery is reminiscent of the Passover meal, where a cup of wine is lifted in remembrance of God's deliverance from Egypt. The cup also foreshadows the Last Supper, where Jesus speaks of the cup as the new covenant in His blood (Luke 22:20), symbolizing the ultimate salvation through His sacrifice. The act of lifting the cup is a public declaration of gratitude and acknowledgment of God's saving power.

and call on the name of the LORD
Calling on the name of the LORD is a recurring theme in the Psalms and throughout Scripture, signifying prayer, worship, and reliance on God. This phrase emphasizes the personal relationship between the believer and God, highlighting trust and dependence on His character and promises. In the Old Testament, calling on God's name was often associated with invoking His presence and power, as seen in Genesis 4:26 and 1 Kings 18:24. It reflects a heart posture of humility and faith, recognizing God's sovereignty and willingness to save. This act of calling on the LORD is also prophetic, pointing to the New Testament teaching that salvation is found in no one else, for "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved" (Romans 10:13).

What Does Psalm 116:13 Mean
I will lift

- The statement is an act of deliberate, personal resolve. The writer is not waiting to feel moved; he chooses to act (Psalm 34:1; Romans 12:1).

- “Lift” carries the idea of raising something high in open acknowledgment, just as priests lifted offerings before God (Leviticus 7:30).

- By placing “I will” first, the psalmist shows gratitude is expressed through obedience, echoing the previous verse’s question, “How can I repay the LORD for all His goodness to me?” (Psalm 116:12).

the cup of salvation

- In the Old Testament a “cup” can picture one’s portion in life—either judgment (Isaiah 51:17) or blessing (Psalm 23:5). Here it is blessing: the overflowing experience of being rescued.

- The cup may also allude to the thank offering shared after deliverance (Leviticus 7:11-15). The psalmist publicly drinks from it, celebrating God’s saving work.

- This foreshadows the Last Supper, where Jesus “took a cup” declaring the new covenant of salvation through His blood (Luke 22:20). The cup that overflows for us cost Him everything.

and call

- Salvation does not end the conversation—it begins it. Having lifted the cup, the psalmist continues in prayerful dependence.

- “Call” shows ongoing relationship, not a one-time cry. Believers are invited to “Call to Me and I will answer you” (Jeremiah 33:3) and are assured that “everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved” (Romans 10:13).

- The order matters: thanksgiving first, then fresh petitions. Gratitude fuels faith for today’s needs.

on the name of the LORD

- In Scripture, the “name” represents God’s revealed character. To call on His name is to trust His person and promises (Exodus 34:5-7).

- The psalmist does not appeal to his own merit but to the covenant name, “the LORD,” recalling steadfast love and faithfulness.

- God’s name is a refuge: “The name of the LORD is a strong tower; the righteous run to it and are safe” (Proverbs 18:10).

summary

The verse records a believer’s grateful response to deliverance. He lifts high the overflowing “cup of salvation,” celebrating God’s rescue, and he keeps the conversation alive by calling on the trustworthy name of the LORD. Thanksgiving over past grace inspires confident prayer for present and future needs, all grounded in God’s unchanging character.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) I will take.--Or, lift up.

Cup of salvation.--The drink offering or oblation which accompanied festival celebrations (Numbers 29:19, &c). Others think of the Passover cup mentioned Matthew 26:27, when this psalm as part of the Hallel was sung. Others, again, take the figurative sense of cup--i.e., portion, lot, as in Psalm 16:5.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - I will take the cup of salvation. It has been usual to explain this of actual participation in the contents of a cup offered at a sacrificial meal, and then passed round to the worshippers. But there is no clear evidence of any such usage, except in connection with the Passover, which cannot here be in question. Hengstenberg there fore proposes to regard the phrase as a mere metaphor, like the "cup of trembling" (Isaiah 51:17, 22), and understands the psalmist to mean that he will gladly and thankfully receive God's mercy vouchsafed to him, and thus show his gratitude for it. And call upon the Name of the Lord (comp. vers. 4 and 17).
