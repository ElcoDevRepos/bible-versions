He piles up the waters of the sea;
This phrase evokes imagery of God's sovereign control over creation, reminiscent of the parting of the Red Sea in Exodus 14:21-22, where God made a path through the waters for the Israelites. It highlights God's power over the natural world, a theme consistent throughout Scripture, such as in Job 38:8-11, where God sets boundaries for the sea. The act of piling up waters can also symbolize God's ability to bring order out of chaos, a concept seen in the creation narrative of Genesis 1:2, where the Spirit of God hovers over the waters. This imagery is a reminder of God's omnipotence and His ability to intervene miraculously in human history.

He puts the depths into storehouses.
This phrase suggests God's control over the deep, often associated with chaos and the unknown in ancient Near Eastern thought. The "depths" or "tehom" in Hebrew, can refer to the primordial waters present before creation, as seen in Genesis 1:2. By placing these depths into storehouses, God demonstrates His authority to contain and manage even the most formidable aspects of creation. This concept is echoed in Proverbs 8:27-29, where wisdom describes God setting limits for the sea. The imagery of storehouses also appears in Job 38:22, where God stores snow and hail, further emphasizing His meticulous governance over creation. This control over the depths can be seen as a type of Christ, who calms the storm in Mark 4:39, showing His divine authority over nature.

What Does Psalm 33:7 Mean
He piles up the waters of the sea

• The verse paints a vivid image of God stacking or heaping the ocean’s waters, underscoring His absolute control over creation (Genesis 1:9–10; Job 38:8–11).

• This is not mere poetry but a literal reminder that the seas do only what their Creator allows. When He split the Red Sea, “the waters were divided” and “the Israelites went through on dry ground” (Exodus 14:21-22)—a real, historical event that echoes the same power described here.

• God’s ability to restrain or release water affirms His sovereignty in judgment and mercy. The global Flood (Genesis 7:11-12) and Jesus calming the storm (Mark 4:39) both showcase the same authority hinted at in this line.

• For believers, this means Earth’s most intimidating forces are already in His hands, inviting trust rather than fear (Psalm 46:1-3).

He puts the depths into storehouses

• The “depths” (the hidden reservoirs beneath and within the sea) are depicted as commodities God can store and dispense at will, like a farmer managing barns (Psalm 135:6-7).

• Scripture affirms literal subterranean waters held in reserve: “He binds up the waters in His thick clouds” (Job 26:8), and “He gathers the waters of the sea into jars” (Psalm 33:7, earlier wording). These images reinforce that nothing escapes His oversight.

• When the earth dries after the Flood, “the fountains of the deep... were closed” (Genesis 8:2). God’s “storehouses” had served their purpose and were shut, proving this text’s truth in history.

• Future judgment will again draw from these reserves: Revelation 8:8-9 shows part of the sea affected by divine command, reminding us that the same storerooms can reopen.

summary

Psalm 33:7 teaches that God literally governs the oceans, commanding where water piles up and where it is hidden. The verse invites awe and confidence: the seas we cannot master are already mastered by Him. Believers can rest in His sovereign, purposeful care over both the visible waves and the unseen depths, knowing He is faithful to use His power for His glory and our ultimate good.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) As an heap.--The image explains itself (so we speak of waves "mountains high ") without reference to the passage either of the Red Sea or the Jordan. Still less is there a comparison to heaps of corn, some think, since storehouses in the next clause are not necessarily barns, but reservoirs. But the LXX., Vulg., and all ancient interpreters read nod ("a skin"), instead of ned ("a heap"), and make the reference to the rain, the clouds being considered as bottles. With this comp. Job 38:37.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 7. - He gathereth the waters of the sea together as an heap. An allusion to Genesis 1:8, but with a glance also at Exodus 15:8 and Joshua 3:13-16; as if the original gathering, and continued retention, of the sea in one convex mass were as great a proof of omnipotence as the miracles related in those passages. Nes (נֵס), "a heap" occurs only in the places cited, here, and in Psalm 78:13. He layeth up the depth in storehouses; literally, the deeps. The waters of the great deep are regarded as stored up by the Almighty in the hugo cavities of the ocean bed for his own use, to be employed at some time or other in carrying out his purposes (comp. Genesis 7:11 and Job 38:22, 23).
