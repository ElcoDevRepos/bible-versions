when He performed His signs in Egypt
This phrase refers to the miraculous events God enacted through Moses to demonstrate His power and authority over Pharaoh and the gods of Egypt. These signs, known as the ten plagues, were divine judgments against Egypt's idolatry and oppression of the Israelites. Each plague targeted specific Egyptian deities, showcasing the Lord's supremacy. The plagues included turning the Nile to blood, frogs, gnats, flies, livestock disease, boils, hail, locusts, darkness, and the death of the firstborn. These acts were not only punitive but also redemptive, as they led to the liberation of the Israelites, prefiguring Christ's redemptive work to free humanity from sin.

and His wonders in the fields of Zoan
Zoan, also known as Tanis, was an ancient city in the northeastern Nile Delta, serving as a significant administrative center during the time of the Exodus. The mention of Zoan highlights the historical and geographical context of the plagues, grounding these miraculous events in a real location known for its political importance. The wonders performed in Zoan were a direct challenge to the heart of Egyptian power and culture. This phrase emphasizes God's intervention in human history, demonstrating His ability to work within specific cultural and historical contexts to achieve His purposes. The wonders in Zoan also foreshadow the greater wonders of Christ's ministry, where He performed miracles to reveal God's kingdom and authority.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty and Power
God's ability to perform signs and wonders in Egypt demonstrates His supreme power over all creation and human authorities.

Faithfulness in Deliverance
The signs in Egypt were part of God's plan to deliver His people, showing His faithfulness to His promises.

Remembering God's Works
Just as the Israelites were called to remember God's acts in Egypt, we are encouraged to recall and trust in God's past faithfulness in our lives.

God's Judgment and Mercy
The plagues were both acts of judgment against Egypt and acts of mercy towards Israel, highlighting God's dual nature of justice and grace.

Witness to the Nations
The wonders in Egypt served as a testimony to the surrounding nations of God's power and His special relationship with Israel.

What Does Psalm 78:43 Mean
He performed His signs in Egypt

- The psalmist recalls the ten plagues (Exodus 7–12), each a visible “sign” that exposed Egypt’s idols and authenticated the LORD’s supremacy (Exodus 12:12; Deuteronomy 4:34).

- These signs were not random acts of power; they were purposeful interventions to pry Israel from bondage (Psalm 105:26–36; Acts 7:36).

- Notice the personal pronoun: “He.” God Himself acted. The same God who later sent His Son (John 3:16) is the One who once turned the Nile to blood (Exodus 7:20).

- For believers today, the historical accuracy of these signs anchors our faith. If God literally judged Egypt, He will literally fulfill every promise of deliverance for His people (2 Corinthians 1:20).

- The text presses us to remember and retell God’s mighty deeds so the next generation will set its hope in Him (Psalm 78:6–7).

His wonders in the fields of Zoan

- “Zoan” (Numbers 13:22) was a prominent Egyptian city in the Nile delta, a power center where Pharaoh displayed his might. God chose that very arena to display superior wonders (Psalm 105:27).

- By specifying “fields,” the psalmist widens the stage—from palace halls to everyday life. Everywhere the plagues struck, God’s wonders were unmistakable (Exodus 8:16–24).

- The contrast is sharp: fertile fields turned to wastelands under hail and locusts (Exodus 9:23–25; 10:13–15). What Egypt relied on for sustenance, God disrupted to show that He alone sustains (Psalm 104:27–30).

- These wonders served a covenant purpose: to establish Israel as God’s redeemed people and to proclaim His name among the nations (Exodus 9:16; Romans 9:17).

- For Christians, Zoan’s fields remind us that God often works in the ordinary places of life, confronting idols and proving His sufficiency (Philippians 4:19).

summary

Psalm 78:43 celebrates God’s literal, historical plagues—signs in Egypt, wonders in Zoan—that freed Israel and revealed His unmatched authority. Remembering these events strengthens faith, fuels obedience, and assures us that the God who once shattered Egypt’s power still intervenes today with the same covenant-keeping might.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 43. - How he had wrought his signs in Egypt. The point just touched in ver. 12 is now taken up and expanded, with the object of showing to the Israelites of the writer's day what cause they had for thankfulness to God in the past and for trust in him for the future. And his wonders in the field of Zoan. "The field of Zoan" (sochet Zoan) is said to be mentioned in an Egyptian inscription (Zeitschrift fur Aegyptische Sprache for the year 1872, p. 16).
