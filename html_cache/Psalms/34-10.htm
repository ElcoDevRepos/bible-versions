Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Young lions go lacking and hungry
In the ancient Near East, lions were symbols of strength and power. The reference to "young lions" suggests creatures in their prime, full of energy and potential. Despite their natural prowess, they can still experience lack and hunger. This imagery highlights the limitations of relying solely on one's strength and abilities. In a broader biblical context, this can be seen as a reminder of the futility of human efforts without divine support. The metaphor of lions lacking despite their might contrasts with the provision that comes from God, emphasizing the insufficiency of worldly power.

but those who seek the LORD
Seeking the LORD implies a relationship characterized by trust, obedience, and worship. In the historical context of Israel, seeking God was often associated with following His commandments and engaging in prayer and worship at the temple. This phrase underscores the importance of prioritizing a relationship with God over reliance on personal strength or worldly resources. Biblically, seeking God is a recurring theme, as seen in passages like Jeremiah 29:13, which promises that those who seek God with all their heart will find Him. This pursuit of God is not passive but involves active engagement with His word and presence.

lack no good thing
This promise of provision is a recurring theme in Scripture, reflecting God's faithfulness to those who are devoted to Him. The "good thing" refers to what is truly beneficial and necessary for one's well-being, aligning with God's will. This does not imply a life free from challenges but assures that God provides what is needed for spiritual and often physical sustenance. Theologically, this can be connected to Jesus' teaching in Matthew 6:33, where He instructs His followers to seek first the kingdom of God, with the assurance that all necessary things will be added to them. This phrase encapsulates the idea that true fulfillment and provision come from God, not from human effort or worldly resources.

Teaching Points
Dependence on God Over Self-Sufficiency
The imagery of young lions highlights the futility of relying solely on one's strength. True provision comes from God.

The Promise of Provision
God promises that those who seek Him will lack no good thing. This is a call to trust in His timing and wisdom for what is truly good.

Active Seeking of God
Seeking the LORD is an active pursuit, involving prayer, study of Scripture, and obedience. It is not passive but requires intentionality.

Contentment in God's Provision
Understanding that "no good thing" may not always align with our desires, but with what God knows is best for us.

Faith in Times of Lack
Even when circumstances seem dire, faith in God's provision is crucial. This faith is built through past experiences of His faithfulness.

What Does Psalm 34:10 Mean
Young lions go lacking and hungry

• “Young lions may grow weak and hungry”. The image is of the strongest, fastest creatures in their prime—symbols of self-reliance at its peak (see Job 4:10–11; Psalm 104:21).

• Even these vigorous hunters sometimes come up empty. Their natural power offers no guarantee of provision.

• The verse reminds us that human strength or resources, no matter how impressive, are ultimately limited. Proverbs 21:31 notes, “A horse is prepared for the day of battle, but victory is of the LORD.” Our own “lion-like” abilities cannot secure lasting security.

But those who seek the LORD

• “But those who seek the LORD...” shifts the focus from self-reliance to God-reliance. Psalm 34:4 highlights the psalmist’s testimony: “I sought the LORD, and He answered me.”

• Seeking the Lord speaks of earnest, continual dependence—drawn out in Jeremiah 29:13: “You will seek Me and find Me when you search for Me with all your heart.”

• Jesus echoes this priority in Matthew 6:33, “Seek first the kingdom of God and His righteousness, and all these things will be added unto you.”

Lack no good thing

• “...lack no good thing.” God pledges comprehensive care for those who pursue Him. Psalm 84:11 assures, “No good thing does He withhold from those who walk uprightly.”

• “Good” is defined by God’s wisdom, not human wish lists. Romans 8:32 underscores this: if God did not spare His own Son, “how will He not also, along with Him, graciously give us all things?”

• Philippians 4:19 reinforces, “My God will supply all your needs according to His riches in glory in Christ Jesus.”

summary

Psalm 34:10 contrasts two ways of living. Even the strongest who trust in themselves face need, but those who make the Lord their pursuit experience His unfailing provision. God does not promise indulgence in every desire, yet He guarantees every truly good thing necessary for life and godliness to those who seek Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(10) Young lions.--See Note, Psalm 17:12. The young lion is the emblem of power and self-resource. Yet these sometimes lack, but the earnest seekers after Divine truth and righteousness never. Instead of "lions," the LXX. and Vulgate have "the rich."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 10. - The young lions do lack, and suffer hunger. Some suppose the "young lions" here to represent the proud and violent, as in Job 4:10. But it is simpler to take the present passage literally. In God's animal creation even the strongest suffer want for a time, and have no remedy; his human creatures need never be in want, since they that seek the Lord shall not want any good thing. It is open to them to "seek the Lord" at any time.
