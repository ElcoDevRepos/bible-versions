May the glory of the LORD endure forever;
This phrase emphasizes the eternal nature of God's glory, which is a central theme throughout the Bible. The glory of the LORD refers to His majesty, power, and divine presence. In the Old Testament, God's glory is often associated with His presence in the tabernacle and the temple (Exodus 40:34-35, 1 Kings 8:10-11). The enduring nature of God's glory is a reminder of His unchanging character and eternal sovereignty, as seen in passages like Isaiah 40:8, which states that the word of God stands forever. This phrase also reflects the creation narrative, where God's glory is revealed through His works (Genesis 1). Theologically, it points to the ultimate fulfillment of God's glory in the new creation, as described in Revelation 21:23, where the glory of God illuminates the New Jerusalem.

may the LORD rejoice in His works.
This part of the verse highlights God's delight in His creation. The concept of God rejoicing in His works is rooted in the creation account, where God saw everything He had made and declared it "very good" (Genesis 1:31). This joy reflects the relationship between the Creator and His creation, emphasizing that God's works are not only functional but also bring Him pleasure. Theologically, this can be connected to the idea that creation reveals God's attributes, as stated in Romans 1:20. The rejoicing of the LORD in His works also foreshadows the restoration of creation, where God will once again take delight in a renewed world, as prophesied in Isaiah 65:17-19. This phrase can be seen as a call for believers to also find joy in God's creation and to steward it responsibly, reflecting God's own delight and care.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The LORD (Yahweh)
The central figure in this verse, representing the eternal and sovereign God of Israel, whose glory and works are the focus of the psalmist's praise.

2. The Works of the LORD
Refers to the entirety of creation and the ongoing acts of God in the world, which are a testament to His power and majesty.
Teaching Points
The Enduring Glory of God
God's glory is not temporary or fleeting; it is eternal. As believers, we are called to recognize and celebrate this enduring glory in our daily lives.

God's Rejoicing in His Creation
The LORD takes joy in His works, which includes all of creation. This should encourage us to appreciate and steward the world around us, recognizing it as a reflection of God's creative power.

Our Response to God's Glory
As recipients of God's grace and witnesses to His works, our response should be one of worship and gratitude, acknowledging His sovereignty and majesty.

The Call to Reflect God's Glory
As image-bearers of God, we are called to reflect His glory in our actions and interactions, living in a way that honors Him and points others to His greatness.

The Assurance of God's Sovereignty
In a world that often seems chaotic, this verse reassures us of God's control and His delight in His creation, providing us with peace and confidence in His plans.

What Does Psalm 104:31 Mean
May the glory of the LORD endure forever

- The psalmist lifts his eyes to the radiance that belongs uniquely to God, echoing earlier declarations like “The heavens declare the glory of God” (Psalm 19:1) and Moses’ longing to see that glory in Exodus 33:18.

- “Glory” speaks of God’s visible splendor and His moral perfection; it is the weight of His character on display in creation, history, and redemption. Isaiah 6:3 reminds us that “the whole earth is full of His glory,” while Revelation 4:11 shows that glory resounds eternally around His throne.

- By praying that this glory “endure forever,” the writer affirms God’s unchanging nature (Psalm 90:2) and confidently looks ahead to the new creation where His glory will fill everything without interruption (Habakkuk 2:14).

- For believers, this line invites wholehearted worship and steadfast trust:

• No cultural shift can diminish His radiance.

• No momentary trouble can threaten His eternal splendor (2 Corinthians 4:17).

• In every season we can join the angelic cry of Revelation 5:13, “To Him be blessing and honor and glory and power forever and ever!”

May the LORD rejoice in His works

- From Genesis 1:31—“God saw all that He had made, and it was very good”—Scripture portrays the Creator taking pleasure in what His hands have fashioned. Psalm 104 as a whole rehearses those works: towering mountains, creatures of the deep, the cycles of day and night.

- The prayer is that God would continue to delight in the handiwork that displays His wisdom (Proverbs 8:30-31) and power (Psalm 111:2-4).

- God’s rejoicing is not distant or mechanical; Zephaniah 3:17 pictures Him “rejoicing over you with singing.” His works include not only the cosmos but also His people, “created in Christ Jesus for good works” (Ephesians 2:10).

- This line calls us to join His joy:

• Celebrate creation rather than exploiting it (Psalm 24:1).

• Mirror His delight by living lives that please Him (Colossians 1:10).

• Trust that He who began a good work in us will carry it on to completion (Philippians 1:6), ensuring His rejoicing continues.

Summary

Psalm 104:31 is a two-fold prayer: that God’s unchanging splendor would shine without end, and that He would forever find pleasure in all He has made. Together these requests frame our worship—lifting our gaze to His eternal majesty and inviting us to participate in His ongoing joy over creation and redemption.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) The Lord shall rejoice.--The poet still follows Genesis in representing God as looking on His finished work with pleasure, but he says nothing of a sabbath. But it is possible that the thought of the sabbath hymns of praise led him to join man with the Divine Being in celebrating the glory and perfection of creation.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - The glory of the Lord shall endure forever; rather, let the glory of the Lord, etc. The psalmist prays that there may be no further interruption of the glorious course of nature besides the Deluge, which has come into his thoughts in connection with the destruction of animal life (ver. 29). Henceforward he trusts and prays that the Lord shall rejoice in his works, and not again repent him that he has made them (Genesis 6:7).
