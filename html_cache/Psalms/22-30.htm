Posterity will serve Him;
This phrase suggests a future generation that will continue to worship and serve God. The term "posterity" indicates descendants or future generations, emphasizing the enduring nature of faith and worship. In biblical context, this reflects the covenantal promise God made to Abraham that his descendants would be numerous and blessed (Genesis 17:7). The idea of serving God is a recurring theme throughout the Bible, highlighting the importance of obedience and devotion to God’s commandments (Deuteronomy 6:13). This phrase also points to the fulfillment of God's promises through the lineage of David, ultimately culminating in the coming of Jesus Christ, who is served by believers across generations.

they will declare the Lord to a new generation.
This part of the verse emphasizes the responsibility of each generation to pass on the knowledge and worship of God to the next. The act of declaring the Lord involves teaching, proclaiming, and living out the truths of God’s word. This is reminiscent of the Shema in Deuteronomy 6:4-9, where the Israelites are instructed to teach God's commandments to their children. The cultural context of ancient Israel placed a strong emphasis on oral tradition and the transmission of faith through storytelling and teaching. This phrase also has prophetic undertones, as it points to the spread of the Gospel message through the disciples and the early church, reaching new generations and fulfilling the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). The continuity of faith across generations is a testament to the enduring power and truth of the Gospel, as seen in the growth of Christianity throughout history.

2. Future Generations
The "posterity" mentioned refers to the descendants and future generations who will continue to serve and proclaim the Lord.

3. The Lord
Central to the verse, the Lord is the one whom the future generations will serve and declare.

4. Messianic Prophecy
Psalm 22 is often seen as a prophetic psalm pointing to the suffering and eventual triumph of the Messiah, Jesus Christ.

5. Israel
The original audience of the psalm, representing the people of God who are called to pass down their faith.
Teaching Points
Generational Faithfulness
Emphasize the importance of passing down faith to future generations. Parents and church communities have a responsibility to teach and model a life of service to the Lord.

Prophetic Fulfillment
Recognize Psalm 22 as a messianic prophecy that finds fulfillment in Jesus Christ. This understanding deepens our appreciation for the continuity of God's plan through history.

Evangelism and Discipleship
Encourage believers to actively declare the Lord to others, fulfilling the call to evangelism and discipleship as seen in the Great Commission.

Covenant Relationship
Reflect on the covenant relationship God establishes with His people, which includes promises to future generations. This should inspire confidence and hope in God's faithfulness.

Cultural Relevance
Consider how to effectively communicate the gospel to a new generation in today's cultural context, ensuring that the message remains relevant and impactful.

What Does Psalm 22:30 Mean
Posterity

Psalm 22:30 opens, “Posterity will serve Him.” The word “posterity” directs our attention to future descendants, the yet-to-be-born who will one day take their place in God’s unfolding story.

• The promise is intergenerational—echoing Genesis 17:7 where God pledges an “everlasting covenant” with Abraham’s descendants, and Psalm 102:18, “Let this be written for a future generation, that a people not yet created may praise the LORD.”

• It reassures us that the work God begins is never confined to one era; Acts 2:39 reminds us, “The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off.”

• Because Scripture is accurate and certain, we can expect new believers to rise in every age until Christ returns (Revelation 7:9).

Will serve Him

Service here is active allegiance, not mere acknowledgment.

• Psalm 72:11 foretells, “All kings will bow down to Him and all nations will serve Him,” showing that service is worshipful submission.

• Isaiah 45:23 reinforces that every knee will bow, and Romans 12:1 calls believers to present their bodies “as a living sacrifice.”

• The verse assures us Jesus’ redemptive work (prophesied throughout Psalm 22) will produce tangible obedience in future followers.

They will declare the Lord

Service overflows into proclamation.

• Psalm 145:4 celebrates, “One generation shall declare Your works to the next,” linking declaration to praise.

• 1 Peter 2:9 says we are saved “so that you may proclaim the excellencies of Him who called you.”

• Testimony is central: what the Lord has done is too good to keep silent (Mark 5:19).

To a new generation

The news is intentionally passed along.

• Deuteronomy 6:6-7 commands parents to teach God’s words “diligently to your children.”

• Psalm 78:4 pledges, “We will not hide them from their children; we will tell the next generation the praiseworthy deeds of the LORD.”

• The church therefore looks forward, not just inward, investing in youth, missions, and discipleship so that every new cohort hears the gospel (2 Timothy 2:2).

summary

Psalm 22:30 guarantees a line of believers yet unborn who will worship, obey, and boldly speak of the Lord to those who follow them. The verse is a stirring reminder that God’s redemptive plan extends well beyond us—every generation will meet Christ because previous generations faithfully serve Him and pass the story on.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(30) A seed . . .--Better, Posterity shall serve Him. About Jehovah it shall be told to the (coming) generation. The article makes for this interpretation. Others, as in Psalm 87:6, understand a reference to the census; but the parallelism is against this reference. The next verse repeats the same thought in another form.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 30. - A seed shall serve him. The Church is founded on a rock, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it. So long as the world endures, Christ shall always have worshippers - a "seed" which will "serve" him. It shall be accounted to the Lord for a generation. If we accept this rendering, we must understand that the seed of the first set of worshippers shall be the Lord's people for one generation, the seed of the next for another, and so on. But it is suggested that the true meaning is, "This shall be told of the Lord to generation after generation" (so Hengstenberg, Kay, Alexander, and our Revisers).
