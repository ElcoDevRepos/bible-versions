Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
The LORD is near to the brokenhearted;
This phrase emphasizes God's proximity and compassion towards those who are suffering emotionally. In the biblical context, the "brokenhearted" refers to individuals experiencing deep sorrow or distress. The Hebrew word for "brokenhearted" can imply a shattering or crushing of the heart, indicating profound grief. This concept is echoed in other scriptures, such as Isaiah 61:1, where the Messiah is prophesied to "bind up the brokenhearted," highlighting a messianic role of healing and restoration. The nearness of the LORD suggests His intimate involvement and readiness to provide comfort and support. This is consistent with the character of God as depicted throughout the Bible, where He is portrayed as a refuge and strength, especially in times of trouble (Psalm 46:1).

He saves the contrite in spirit.
The term "contrite" refers to a deep sense of remorse and humility before God. In the cultural and historical context of ancient Israel, contrition was often associated with repentance and a turning back to God. The "spirit" here denotes the inner being or the essence of a person. This phrase underscores the biblical theme of salvation being extended to those who recognize their need for God and approach Him with humility. The connection to other scriptures, such as Isaiah 57:15, where God dwells with the contrite and lowly in spirit, reinforces the idea that God values a humble heart. This humility and recognition of one's need for divine intervention are also seen in the teachings of Jesus, who blessed the poor in spirit (Matthew 5:3), indicating that the kingdom of heaven belongs to those who are spiritually humble. The saving action of God towards the contrite is a type of Christ's redemptive work, where He offers salvation to those who come to Him in repentance and faith.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 34, who wrote this psalm after being delivered from a difficult situation with Abimelech. David's life experiences provide a backdrop for understanding the depth of his reliance on God.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people. In this verse, Yahweh is depicted as compassionate and attentive to those in distress.

3. The Brokenhearted
Individuals who are experiencing deep sorrow or distress. The term "brokenhearted" in Hebrew (nishberei lev) conveys a sense of being crushed or shattered emotionally.

4. The Contrite in Spirit
Those who are humble and repentant. The Hebrew word for "contrite" (dakka) suggests a sense of being crushed or humbled, often in recognition of one's own sinfulness.

5. Abimelech
The Philistine king before whom David feigned madness to escape. This historical context highlights God's deliverance and presence in times of trouble.
Teaching Points
God's Nearness in Suffering
God is not distant in our times of sorrow. He is intimately present and attentive to our pain, offering comfort and healing.

The Value of a Contrite Heart
A humble and repentant spirit is precious to God. Recognizing our need for Him opens the door to His saving grace.

Encouragement in Trials
In moments of deep distress, we can find hope in God's promise to be near. Our brokenness is not the end but a place where God meets us with His love.

Repentance and Restoration
True repentance involves a broken and contrite heart, leading to restoration and salvation. God delights in lifting up those who humbly seek Him.

Community Support
As believers, we are called to reflect God's compassion by supporting and comforting those who are brokenhearted within our communities.

What Does Psalm 34:18 Mean
The LORD is near

• The verse opens with a present-tense certainty: “The LORD is near.” His nearness is literal, personal, and ongoing.

• Scripture consistently depicts God as drawing close to His people—“The LORD is near to all who call on Him” (Psalm 145:18); “He is not far from each one of us” (Acts 17:27).

• This promise flows from His covenant faithfulness. As in Deuteronomy 4:7, no other “gods” stay so accessible when their people call.

• Our response is encouraged: “Draw near to God, and He will draw near to you” (James 4:8). His nearness is both comfort and call.

to the brokenhearted

• God’s closeness is especially directed toward “the brokenhearted”—those crushed by loss, failure, or sorrow.

• He does not distance Himself from pain; He willingly steps into it. “He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds” (Psalm 147:3).

• The Messiah’s mission includes binding “up the brokenhearted” (Isaiah 61:1), fulfilled by Christ who still ministers to every wounded soul.

• Even mourning becomes a context for divine comfort: “Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted” (Matthew 5:4).

He saves

• God’s nearness is purposeful; He comes to act. “He saves”—a verb of deliverance that spans temporal rescues and eternal redemption.

• Salvation is God’s work alone: “I, yes I, am the LORD, and there is no Savior but Me” (Isaiah 43:11).

• The promise is wide: “Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved” (Romans 10:13).

• David’s testimony in Psalm 18:2 (“The LORD is my rock… the horn of my salvation”) shows this rescue is both personal and powerful.

the contrite in spirit

• “Contrite” conveys humble repentance—an inner posture that admits need and turns to God.

• The high and holy One dwells “with him who is contrite and lowly in spirit” (Isaiah 57:15), reviving what is crushed.

• God esteems “those with a humble and contrite spirit, who tremble at My word” (Isaiah 66:2).

• Jesus highlighted such humility in the tax collector who cried, “God, have mercy on me, a sinner!” (Luke 18:13).

• A broken and contrite heart is never despised (Psalm 51:17); instead, it becomes the very place where God’s saving grace is experienced.

summary

Psalm 34:18 offers a four-fold assurance: the Lord is truly present, He intentionally draws near to life’s deepest hurts, He actively rescues, and He does so for those who come in humble repentance. Every hurting, repentant believer can rest in this unwavering promise of God’s nearness and saving power.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 18. - The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart; and sayeth such as be of a contrite spirit. On the value in God's sight of a broken and contrite heart, see Psalm 2:17; and on his mercy towards the truly contrite, see Psalm 147:3; Isaiah 57:15; 69:2. He "is nigh" to such persons, he "dwells with" them, "looks to them, .... revives their heart, .... heals" them, "saves" them.
