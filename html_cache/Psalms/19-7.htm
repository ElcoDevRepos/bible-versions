The Law of the LORD is perfect
The term "Law" refers to the Torah, encompassing God's instructions and commandments given to Israel. "Perfect" indicates completeness and flawlessness, suggesting that God's law lacks nothing and is fully sufficient for guiding life. In biblical context, the Law was given to Moses on Mount Sinai, serving as a covenant between God and His people. It reflects God's holy character and His desire for His people to live in a way that mirrors His righteousness. The perfection of the Law is echoed in Psalm 119:96, which speaks of the boundless nature of God's commandments.

Reviving the soul
The Law's purpose is not merely legalistic but life-giving. "Reviving" implies restoration and renewal, suggesting that adherence to God's commandments brings spiritual vitality and refreshment. This aligns with the biblical theme of God's word as a source of life, as seen in Deuteronomy 8:3, where it is stated that man lives by every word that comes from the mouth of the LORD. The soul, representing the inner being, finds its true life and purpose through God's perfect law.

The testimony of the LORD is trustworthy
"Testimony" refers to God's revealed truth and His covenantal promises. It is "trustworthy," meaning reliable and faithful, reflecting God's unchanging nature. In the historical context, God's testimonies were often inscribed on stone tablets, symbolizing their permanence and dependability. This trustworthiness is a foundation for faith, as seen in 2 Timothy 3:16, which affirms that all Scripture is God-breathed and useful for teaching and guidance.

Making wise the simple
The "simple" are those who are naive or inexperienced, lacking wisdom. God's testimony imparts wisdom, transforming the simple into those who understand and live according to divine truth. This transformation is a recurring biblical theme, as seen in Proverbs 1:4, which states that wisdom gives prudence to the simple. The wisdom imparted by God's word is not merely intellectual but practical, guiding believers in righteous living and decision-making.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 19, David was the second king of Israel and a man after God's own heart. His life and writings reflect a deep relationship with God and a profound understanding of His laws.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God used in the Old Testament, emphasizing His eternal, self-existent nature and His relationship with His people.

3. The Law (Torah)
Refers to the teachings and instructions given by God, particularly the first five books of the Bible. It is seen as God's revealed will for His people.

4. The Testimony
This term often refers to the decrees or statutes of God, which bear witness to His character and His expectations for His people.

5. The Simple
In Hebrew, the word "simple" (pethi) refers to those who are naive or inexperienced, highlighting the transformative power of God's Word to impart wisdom.
Teaching Points
The Perfection of God's Law
God's law is described as "perfect," meaning it is complete and without flaw. It is a reflection of His perfect nature and serves as a reliable guide for life.

Reviving the Soul
The Hebrew word for "reviving" (meshivah) implies restoration and renewal. Engaging with God's Word has the power to refresh and restore our spiritual vitality.

Trustworthiness of God's Testimony
God's decrees are "trustworthy," meaning they are reliable and true. In a world full of uncertainty, believers can find stability and assurance in His Word.

Wisdom for the Simple
God's Word is accessible to all, including the "simple." It provides wisdom and understanding, equipping believers to navigate life's complexities with discernment.

Practical Application of God's Word
Believers are encouraged to not only read but also apply God's Word in their daily lives, allowing it to transform their thoughts, actions, and decisions.

What Does Psalm 19:7 Mean
The law of the LORD is perfect

• “Law” points to every instruction God has given—His commands, principles, and revealed will (Psalm 119:1).

• Because it comes from the flawless character of God (Proverbs 30:5; 2 Timothy 3:16–17), it is complete, lacking nothing, and incapable of error.

• When we embrace Scripture as literally true and wholly sufficient, we find solid ground amid shifting cultural ideas (Isaiah 55:11).

• The perfection of God’s law invites full obedience; we never need to supplement it with human tradition (Mark 7:8–9).

reviving the soul

• God’s Word breathes life where weariness, sin, or sorrow have taken hold (Psalm 23:3; 119:25, 50).

• “Reviving” speaks of restoration—turning back, refreshing, renewing.

– It convicts (Hebrews 4:12)

– It comforts (Romans 15:4)

– It corrects and redirects (Psalm 119:9)

• The same Scriptures that bring new birth (1 Peter 1:23) also sustain daily spiritual vitality.

the testimony of the LORD is trustworthy

• “Testimony” highlights God’s own witness about Himself—His character, works, and promises (Psalm 93:5).

• Because God cannot lie (Titus 1:2), His testimony is entirely dependable (2 Samuel 7:28; Psalm 119:86).

• Every historical fact, prophetic promise, and doctrinal statement in Scripture is rock-solid reality.

• Trustworthy testimony builds unshakable confidence, freeing us from doubt and fear (John 17:17).

making wise the simple

• “Simple” does not mean unintelligent; it describes the inexperienced, those easily swayed (Proverbs 1:4).

• God’s Word imparts practical skill for living:

– It trains discernment (Hebrews 5:14)

– It guides choices (Psalm 119:105)

– It equips for every good work (2 Timothy 3:15)

• Wisdom begins with humility before God’s authority (Proverbs 9:10; James 1:5). The Bible takes a teachable heart and shapes it into a discerning, steadfast follower of Christ.

summary

Psalm 19:7 celebrates the matchless quality and transforming power of God’s written Word. Because Scripture is perfect and trustworthy, it can revive the most depleted soul and turn the simplest learner into a wise disciple. When we receive the Bible as literally true and entirely sufficient, we encounter the living God who restores, guides, and matures us for His glory.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) The law.--The ear catches even in the English the change of rhythm, which is as marked as the change of subject. Instead of the free lyric movement of the preceding verse, we come suddenly upon the most finished specimen of didactic poetry in regular metre, exhibiting a perfect balance of expression as well as of thought, so perfect in the original, that in Psalm 19:7-9 the number of words is the same in each clause. In each clause, too, the Law, under one or another of its many names and aspects, is praised, first for its essential character, then for its results.

The law . . . . the testimony.--These are collective terms embracing, under different regards, the whole body of statutes and precepts in the Jewish code. The law, torah, means in its primary use "instruction," and therefore is used of prophecy (Isaiah 1:10; Isaiah 8:16), but here undoubtedly bears its common and more limited sense. Testimony, from a root meaning "to repeat," suggests the solemn earnestness and insistence of the Divine commands.

The description "perfect" and "sure" suggests the lofty ideal prescribed by the Law, and the reliance which the Hebrew might place upon it as a rule of conduct. The word "simple" is generally used in a bad sense, but here has its primary meaning, "open," "ingenuous," "impressible," easily led either towards folly or wisdom.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 7-11. - The transition from the glories of the material universe to the "law of the Lord" is abrupt and startling. Some go so far as to say that there is no connection at all between the first and second parts of the psalm. But it is the law and order that pervades the material universe which constitutes its main glory; and the analogy between God's physical laws and his moral laws is evident, and generally admitted (see the great work of Bishop Butler, part 1.). Verse 7. - The Law of the Lord is perfect. Whatsoever proceeds from God is perfect in its kind; his "Law" especially - the rule of life to his rational creatures. That salvation is not by the Law is not the fault of the Law, but of man, who cannot keep it. "The Law" itself "is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good" (Romans 7:12). Converting (rather, as in the margin, restoring) the soul. The word employed, meshibah, is used of restoring from disorder and decay (Psalm 80:19), from sorrow and affliction (Ruth 4:15), from death (1 Kings 17:21, 22). The Law, by instructing men, restores them from moral blindness to the light which is theirs by nature (Romans 1:19), and, as a further consequence, in many cases, restores them from sin to righteousness. The testimony of the Lord is sure. 'Eduth - the word translated "testimony" - is employed especially of the Decalogue (Exodus 25:16, 21, 22, 26; Numbers 9:15; 17:23; 18:2, etc.); but may be regarded as sue of the many synonyms under which the whole Law may be spoken of (see Psalm 119:2, 14, 22, 24, 88, etc.). The Law is "sure" - i.e. fixed, firm, stable - in comparison with the fleeting, shifting, unstable judgments of human reason. Making wise the simple; i.e. enlightening their moral judgment.
