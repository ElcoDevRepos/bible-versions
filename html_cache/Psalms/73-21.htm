When my heart was grieved
This phrase reflects a deep emotional turmoil and spiritual struggle. The psalmist, Asaph, is expressing a moment of intense inner conflict and sorrow. In the broader context of Psalm 73, Asaph grapples with the prosperity of the wicked and the suffering of the righteous, which causes him to question God's justice. This grief is not just emotional but also spiritual, as it challenges his faith and understanding of God's ways. The heart, in biblical terms, often represents the center of one's being, including emotions, will, and intellect. This phrase can be connected to other scriptures where the heart is central to one's relationship with God, such as Proverbs 4:23, which advises guarding the heart as it is the wellspring of life.

and I was pierced within
The imagery of being "pierced" suggests a deep, penetrating pain, akin to being wounded. This internal piercing can be understood as a conviction or realization that cuts to the core of one's being. In the context of Psalm 73, this piercing may represent the moment of clarity or awakening when Asaph recognizes his own envy and bitterness towards the prosperity of the wicked. This realization is painful but necessary for spiritual growth and repentance. The concept of being pierced can also be seen in the New Testament, where the Word of God is described as sharper than any double-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and spirit (Hebrews 4:12). This connection highlights the transformative power of divine truth in revealing and correcting the inner thoughts and attitudes of the heart.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Asaph
The author of Psalm 73, Asaph was a prominent Levite singer and seer in David's court. He is known for his honest and heartfelt psalms that often explore the tension between the prosperity of the wicked and the suffering of the righteous.

2. The Sanctuary of God
While not directly mentioned in verse 21, the sanctuary plays a crucial role in the broader context of Psalm 73. It is where Asaph gains understanding and perspective on his struggles.

3. The Wicked
Throughout Psalm 73, Asaph contrasts his own struggles with the apparent prosperity of the wicked, which initially causes him distress and confusion.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Inner Turmoil
Asaph's experience reminds us that believers can experience deep emotional and spiritual struggles. It's important to acknowledge these feelings rather than suppress them.

The Role of Conviction
The phrase "pierced within" suggests a moment of conviction or realization. Allowing God's truth to penetrate our hearts can lead to transformation and understanding.

Seeking God's Presence for Clarity
Asaph's turning point comes when he enters the sanctuary of God. In times of confusion, seeking God's presence through prayer, worship, and scripture can provide clarity and peace.

The Danger of Envy
Envy of the wicked's prosperity can lead to spiritual discontent. Focusing on God's eternal promises helps realign our perspective.

The Importance of Community
Asaph's insights likely came through communal worship and teaching. Engaging with a faith community can offer support and wisdom during times of doubt.

What Does Psalm 73:21 Mean
When my heart was grieved

• Asaph has just confessed that he “was envious of the arrogant” (Psalm 73:3); here he admits the inner cost of that envy.

• “Grieved” pictures the choking sadness that settles when we realize our attitude has drifted from God’s truth (Psalm 32:3-4; Proverbs 14:30).

• The grief comes “when” he recognizes the contrast between temporary prosperity of the wicked (vv. 4-12) and God’s eternal justice (vv. 17-20).

• The Spirit often uses conviction to turn us back (2 Corinthians 7:10). The pain itself is evidence of a living relationship with the Lord (Hebrews 12:6-8).

and I was pierced within

• “Pierced” moves the image from sorrow to sharp conviction, like an arrow striking the conscience (Hebrews 4:12).

• This piercing exposes hidden motives—envy, doubt, self-pity—so they can be confessed (Psalm 139:23-24).

• Similar language appears when listeners were “pierced to the heart” after Peter’s sermon (Acts 2:37), showing that conviction is meant to lead to repentance and fresh obedience.

• God does not leave us wounded; immediately after this verse Asaph declares, “You hold my right hand” (Psalm 73:23), proving that conviction is only the doorway to restored fellowship.

summary

Psalm 73:21 captures the turning point in Asaph’s struggle: first the dull ache of spiritual grief, then the sharp stab of conviction. God allows both feelings to expose sin, redirect desire, and draw His people back into confident communion with Him.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Grieved.--Literally, grew sour; or, as we say, "was soured."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - Thus my heart was grieved; literally, for my heart was grieved, or "was soured." The "for" refers to a suppressed phrase of self-condemnation, "But at the time I did not see all this - the solution did not present itself to me." I was too full of grief and bitterness to consider the matter calmly and dispassionately. And I was pricked in my reins; i.e. "a pang of passionate discontent had pierced my inmost being" (Cheyne).
