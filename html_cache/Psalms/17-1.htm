A prayer of David.
This phrase introduces the psalm as a personal prayer from David, the shepherd who became king of Israel. David's life was marked by moments of deep reliance on God, often expressed through prayer. This context is crucial, as David frequently faced adversaries and sought divine intervention. The title "A prayer of David" indicates a heartfelt communication with God, setting the tone for the entire psalm.

Hear, O LORD, my righteous plea;
David appeals to the LORD, using the covenant name of God, which signifies a personal and relational aspect. The term "righteous plea" suggests that David believes his request aligns with God's will and justice. In the biblical context, righteousness is often associated with living in accordance with God's laws and commands. David's confidence in the righteousness of his plea reflects his understanding of God's character as just and fair.

listen to my cry.
The word "cry" conveys a sense of urgency and desperation. In the ancient Near Eastern culture, crying out to a deity was a common practice in times of distress. David's cry is not just a vocal expression but a deep, emotional appeal for God's attention and intervention. This phrase emphasizes the sincerity and intensity of David's prayer.

Give ear to my prayer—
This phrase is a poetic way of asking God to be attentive. The repetition of requests for God to hear underscores the earnestness of David's appeal. In the Hebrew tradition, prayer was a vital means of communication with God, and asking God to "give ear" implies a desire for a personal and attentive response from the divine.

it comes from lips free of deceit.
David asserts the integrity of his prayer, claiming that it is spoken with honesty and truthfulness. In biblical times, integrity and truth were highly valued, especially in one's relationship with God. This claim of being "free of deceit" suggests that David approaches God with a clear conscience, which is a recurring theme in the Psalms. It also foreshadows the New Testament emphasis on purity of heart and sincerity in worship, as seen in Jesus' teachings.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of this psalm, David is often seen as a man after God's own heart. He frequently turned to God in times of distress, seeking divine intervention and justice.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people. David appeals to Yahweh as the ultimate judge and protector.

3. Prayer
The act of communicating with God, which in this context is a plea for justice and vindication. David's prayer is characterized by sincerity and honesty.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Righteousness in Prayer
David's plea is described as "righteous," indicating that the moral and spiritual condition of the petitioner matters. We should strive to live lives of integrity, ensuring our prayers are sincere and free from deceit.

God's Attentiveness to Our Cries
Just as David confidently asks God to "give ear" to his prayer, we can trust that God is attentive to our cries, especially when we approach Him with honesty and humility.

The Role of Integrity in Our Relationship with God
David emphasizes that his prayer comes from "lips free of deceit." This teaches us the value of maintaining integrity in our speech and actions, which strengthens our relationship with God.

Confidence in God's Justice
David's appeal to God as the ultimate judge encourages us to place our trust in God's justice, especially when facing false accusations or injustice.

What Does Psalm 17:1 Mean
A prayer of David

David labels the psalm a prayer, signaling intentional, personal communion with God, much like in Psalm 86:1 and 1 Chronicles 17:16. By presenting the psalm as prayer, he teaches that every believer may confidently take concerns straight to the throne, knowing, as Hebrews 4:16 affirms, that we approach a throne of grace.

Hear, O LORD, my righteous plea

• “Hear” shows urgency; David believes God both listens and responds (Psalm 34:4, 15).

• He calls his request “righteous.” The plea is in line with God’s moral standard, echoing 1 John 5:14—confidence that petitions made according to God’s will are heard.

• The covenant name “LORD” (YHWH) reminds us that the same faithful God who delivered Israel in Exodus 3:15 is still attentive.

Listen to my cry

• “Cry” conveys deep emotion, illustrating that God welcomes raw honesty, just as in Psalm 61:1 or 142:1.

• This points to a relationship, not ritual; Romans 8:15 assures believers they may cry “Abba, Father.”

• The parallelism reinforces that repetition in prayer is acceptable when it springs from genuine dependence (Matthew 26:44).

Give ear to my prayer—it comes from lips free of deceit

• David appeals to integrity; he is not manipulating God (Psalm 26:1–3).

• Lips “free of deceit” imply a heart aligned with truth, resonating with Psalm 51:6 and Ephesians 4:25.

• The statement anticipates the New Testament emphasis that effective prayer is tied to righteousness (James 5:16).

summary

Psalm 17:1 portrays David urgently yet confidently approaching the covenant-keeping LORD, convinced that God hears petitions rooted in righteousness and integrity. The verse models sincere, truthful, wholehearted prayer, assuring believers that when motives and speech align with God’s truth, their cries reach attentive ears.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) Hear the right.--Or (see margin), justice. Some ancient versions read, "Hear, Lord of righteousness." Others make it concrete: "Hear me, the righteous; "but the Authorised Version has the true sense.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - Hear the right, O Lord (comp. Psalm 9:4). Here and elsewhere the psalmist assumes that right is on his side, and that he is persecuted unjustly. Unless he had been convinced of this, he could not have called on God to vindicate him. The narrative in 1 Samuel 18. - 27, fully justifies his conviction. Attend unto my cry (comp. Psalm 4:1; Psalm 5:2; Psalm 61:1). Rinnah, the word translated "cry" here (and in Psalm 61:1) is a strong term: it means "shout," "outcry" - most often, though not here, "a shout of joy." Give ear unto my prayer, that goeth not out of feigned lips; rather, feigning lips, or guileful lips - lips, i.e., that speak falsehood knowingly.
