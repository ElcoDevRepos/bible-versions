O offspring of His servant Abraham
This phrase highlights the covenant relationship between God and Abraham, emphasizing the promise made to Abraham and his descendants. Abraham is often referred to as God's servant, indicating his obedience and faithfulness. The Abrahamic Covenant, found in Genesis 12:1-3, 15:1-21, and 17:1-14, is foundational to understanding Israel's identity and God's plan for redemption. This covenant included promises of land, descendants, and blessing, which are central themes throughout the Bible. The term "offspring" connects to the promise of numerous descendants, as seen in Genesis 22:17. Abraham's role as a patriarch is crucial, as he is considered the father of faith (Romans 4:11-12), and his faith is a model for believers.

O sons of Jacob
Jacob, also known as Israel, is the grandson of Abraham and the father of the twelve tribes of Israel. This phrase underscores the continuity of God's covenant through the generations. Jacob's life, marked by struggles and divine encounters, is pivotal in the narrative of Israel's formation. The transformation of Jacob's name to Israel (Genesis 32:28) signifies a new identity and mission for his descendants. The "sons of Jacob" refers to the twelve tribes, each playing a unique role in Israel's history. This lineage is significant in biblical prophecy and the unfolding of God's redemptive plan, as seen in the blessings Jacob gives to his sons in Genesis 49.

His chosen ones
This phrase emphasizes the concept of election, where God selects individuals or groups for a specific purpose. Israel is often referred to as God's chosen people, a theme that runs throughout the Old Testament (Deuteronomy 7:6-8). This choice is not based on merit but on God's sovereign will and love. The idea of being chosen carries responsibilities, including living according to God's laws and being a light to the nations (Isaiah 42:6). In the New Testament, this concept is expanded to include all believers in Christ, who are described as a chosen people (1 Peter 2:9). The chosen status of Israel foreshadows the spiritual election of the Church, highlighting God's plan to bring salvation to all nations through Jesus Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Abraham
Known as the father of faith, Abraham is a central figure in the Bible. God made a covenant with him, promising that he would be the father of many nations. His faith and obedience are foundational to understanding God's relationship with His people.

2. Jacob
The grandson of Abraham, Jacob was later named Israel. He is the father of the twelve tribes of Israel. His life is marked by struggles and encounters with God, which shaped the destiny of the Israelites.

3. The Chosen Ones
This term refers to the descendants of Abraham and Jacob, the Israelites, who were chosen by God to be His people. This choice is rooted in God's covenantal promises and His sovereign will.
Teaching Points
Understanding Our Spiritual Heritage
As believers, we are part of a spiritual lineage that traces back to Abraham. This heritage is not just a matter of ancestry but of faith and promise.

The Importance of Being Chosen
Being chosen by God is a profound privilege and responsibility. It calls us to live in a way that reflects God's purposes and character.

Faith and Obedience
Like Abraham and Jacob, our journey with God requires faith and obedience. These are key to experiencing God's promises and blessings.

Covenantal Relationship
God's relationship with His people is based on covenant. Understanding this helps us grasp the depth of His commitment and love for us.

Identity in Christ
Our identity as believers is rooted in being part of God's chosen people, which transcends ethnic and cultural boundaries through faith in Christ.

What Does Psalm 105:6 Mean
O offspring of His servant Abraham

- The psalmist calls the readers to remember they stem from “His servant Abraham,” reminding them of God’s covenant friendship with the patriarch (Genesis 12:1-3; Genesis 15:5-6).

- “Servant” highlights Abraham’s obedient trust; Scripture celebrates that faith as righteousness (Romans 4:3).

- By addressing the audience as Abraham’s descendants, the verse pulls them into the promises of land, blessing, and global influence first given in Genesis 22:17-18.

- Believers today share in that spiritual lineage through faith in Christ (Galatians 3:29), so these covenant assurances resonate beyond ethnic Israel.

O sons of Jacob

- Switching from “Abraham” to “Jacob” narrows the focus from the patriarch of promise to the twelve-tribe nation God birthed (Genesis 35:10-12).

- Jacob experienced God’s faithful care despite failures, illustrating divine perseverance with His people (Hosea 12:3-6).

- The phrase also recalls God’s deliverance from Egypt, a major theme of Psalm 105 (Exodus 1:7; Exodus 3:6), underscoring that the same God who preserved Jacob’s household still watches over their descendants.

His chosen ones

- The title “chosen” crystallizes why Abraham’s seed and Jacob’s sons matter: God set His love on them, not because of their size or merit but by sovereign grace (Deuteronomy 7:6-8).

- Being chosen carries privileges—protection, guidance, and purpose (Isaiah 43:1-4)—and responsibilities—holiness and witness (1 Peter 2:9).

- This identity assures believers that God initiates and sustains the relationship (Ephesians 1:4-5). In Psalm 105 it fuels gratitude for every act of salvation recounted in the psalm.

summary

Psalm 105:6 strings three titles—offspring of Abraham, sons of Jacob, chosen ones—to remind God’s people of their covenant roots, their national story, and their elect status. Each phrase anchors them in God’s unbreakable promises, steady leadership, and gracious purpose. Remembering who they are fuels worship and trust today just as it did when the psalm was first sung.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - O ye seed of Abraham his servant; i.e. "his faithful and obedient follower" (see below, ver. 42; and comp. Genesis 26:24; Galatians 3:9). Ye children of Jacob his chosen; rather, his chosen ones. The word is in the plural, and must be referred, not to "Jacob," but to "children."
