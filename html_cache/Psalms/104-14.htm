He makes the grass grow for the livestock
This phrase highlights God's providential care for His creation, emphasizing His role as the sustainer of life. In the ancient Near East, livestock was a crucial part of daily life, providing food, clothing, and labor. The growth of grass is a simple yet profound example of God's provision, as it sustains the animals that are vital to human survival. This reflects the creation narrative in Genesis, where God provides for all living creatures. The imagery of God making grass grow can also be seen as a metaphor for spiritual nourishment, as seen in Psalm 23:2, where God leads His people to green pastures.

and provides crops for man to cultivate
This phrase underscores the partnership between divine provision and human responsibility. While God provides the resources, humans are called to work and cultivate the land, reflecting the mandate given to Adam in Genesis 2:15 to "work it and take care of it." This partnership is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, illustrating the balance between divine sovereignty and human agency. The cultivation of crops is also a reminder of the Fall, where labor became toilsome (Genesis 3:17-19), yet it remains a means through which God blesses humanity.

bringing forth food from the earth:
This final phrase emphasizes the result of God's provision and human effort: the production of food. In the biblical context, food is not only a physical necessity but also a symbol of God's blessing and faithfulness. The earth's ability to produce food is a testament to God's ongoing care and the fulfillment of His promise to sustain life. This concept is echoed in the Lord's Prayer, where Jesus teaches His followers to ask for "daily bread" (Matthew 6:11), signifying reliance on God's provision. Additionally, the imagery of bringing forth food can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the "bread of life" (John 6:35), providing spiritual sustenance to all who believe.

Teaching Points
God's Provision
Recognize that God is the ultimate provider of all our needs, both physical and spiritual. Trust in His continuous care and provision.

Human Responsibility
Understand that while God provides, humans have a role in cultivating and stewarding the earth. Engage in responsible and sustainable practices.

Interdependence of Creation
Acknowledge the interconnectedness of all creation. Livestock, crops, and humans are part of a divine ecosystem where each has a role.

Gratitude and Worship
Cultivate a heart of gratitude for God's provision. Let this gratitude lead to worship and praise for His faithfulness.

Faith in God's Timing
Trust in God's timing and methods of provision, even when circumstances seem uncertain. His ways are perfect and purposeful.

What Does Psalm 104:14 Mean
He makes the grass grow for the livestock

• God is personally and continually involved in caring for His creatures; the verb “makes” shows active, ongoing provision.

• Psalm 147:8-9 echoes this reality: “He covers the sky with clouds; He prepares rain for the earth; He makes grass grow on the hills. He provides food for the animals…”

• Job 38:41, Psalm 145:15-16, and Matthew 6:26 remind us that animals ultimately depend on the Lord, not chance or human effort.

• Because He supplies pasture, ranchers and shepherds can labor with confidence, knowing the land is under the Creator’s watchful eye.

and provides crops for man to cultivate

• The same God who feeds livestock also gives people seed, soil, and favorable seasons. Genesis 1:29; 2:15; and 8:22 establish this pattern from creation onward.

• Divine provision joins human responsibility: “I planted, Apollos watered, but God made it grow” (1 Corinthians 3:6).

• Farming illustrates stewardship. Passages like Proverbs 12:11 and Isaiah 28:24-26 show that diligent work is expected, yet success still hinges on the Lord who “provides.”

• Recognizing God’s hand in every harvest fosters gratitude, combats pride, and encourages generous sharing (2 Corinthians 9:10-11).

bringing forth food from the earth

• The culmination of God’s care is edible blessing—bread, fruit, vegetables, meat, milk—life-sustaining gifts that literally rise out of soil He formed (Genesis 3:19).

• Acts 14:17 celebrates this same truth: “He has shown kindness by giving you rain from heaven and crops in their seasons; He fills your hearts with food and gladness.”

• Each meal is tangible evidence of His faithfulness (James 1:17). Even the petition “Give us this day our daily bread” (Matthew 6:11) rests on Psalm 104:14’s promise.

• Seeing food as God-given transforms eating into worship and reinforces our dependence on His unchanging character.

summary

Psalm 104:14 portrays the Lord as the generous, hands-on Provider for every living thing. He grows pasture for animals, supplies seed and conditions for human labor, and ultimately places nourishing food on every table. Recognizing His active role invites trust, gratitude, diligent stewardship, and joyful praise.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) For the service of man--i.e., for his use (so Gesenius). But some deny this meaning to the Hebrew, which properly means "labour" or "office." (In 1Chronicles 27:26; Nehemiah 10:37, it means "agriculture," "tillage.") Hence they render, "And herbs for man's labour in bringing them forth from the earth," alluding to his task of cultivating the soil. Standing by itself the clause would indeed naturally require this sense, but the parallelism is against it, and in 1Chronicles 26:30, "service of a king," we have a near approach to the meaning "use."

That he may.--Better, bringing food out of the earth, taking the verb as gerund instead of infinitive absolute.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - He causeth the grass to grow for the cattle. The results of God's careful arrangements are now spoken cf. In the first place, grass - fodder of every kind - is provided for the beasts on which man's life so greatly depends - a boon both to man and beast, of inestimable value. Next, there is brought forth herb for the service of man - i.e. for his direct service - vegetables and fruits for his food; spicy shrubs for his delectation; flax, papyrus, saffron, aloes, etc., for his use. That he may bring forth food out of the earth. That man himself may by his labour, by the cultivation of the natural products, obtain from the earth the food suitable to him.
