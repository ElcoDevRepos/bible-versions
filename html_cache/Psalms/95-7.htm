For He is our God
This phrase emphasizes the covenant relationship between God and His people. In the Old Testament, God established a covenant with Israel, declaring Himself as their God (Exodus 6:7). This relationship is characterized by God's faithfulness and the people's worship and obedience. The declaration "our God" signifies a personal and communal bond, reflecting the intimate relationship believers have with God through faith.

and we are the people of His pasture
The imagery of a pasture suggests provision, care, and sustenance. In ancient Israel, shepherding was a common occupation, and the metaphor of God as a shepherd is prevalent throughout Scripture (Psalm 23:1, Ezekiel 34:31). This phrase indicates that believers are under God's protection and guidance, much like sheep are cared for by a shepherd. It underscores the idea of belonging and being nurtured by God.

the sheep under His care
Sheep are often used in the Bible to symbolize God's people, highlighting their need for guidance and protection. This phrase reinforces the shepherd-sheep relationship, where God is the shepherd who leads, protects, and provides for His flock (John 10:11-14). It also implies trust and dependence on God, as sheep rely on their shepherd for safety and sustenance.

Today, if you hear His voice
This phrase serves as an urgent call to obedience and responsiveness to God's guidance. The word "Today" emphasizes the immediacy and importance of responding to God without delay. Hearing God's voice implies being attentive to His word and commands, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:4-5. This call to action is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are encouraged to listen and respond to God's voice through the Holy Spirit (Hebrews 3:7-8).

What Does Psalm 95:7 Mean
For He is our God

– The verse opens with a confident declaration of belonging. God is not distant; He binds Himself to people in covenant love (Exodus 20:2; Jeremiah 31:33).

– Because He is “our” God, worship flows naturally (Psalm 95:6 just before this verse).

– His identity shapes ours: when we know who He is, we find out who we are (Psalm 100:3).

and we are the people of His pasture

– The “pasture” speaks of a place He has prepared, supplied, and fenced in for safety (Ezekiel 34:14).

– Belonging to His pasture means:

• Provision—green grass and still waters (Psalm 23:2).

• Identity—a marked, gathered community rather than wandering individuals (Deuteronomy 7:6).

• Purpose—living daily under His oversight, bringing Him glory (Isaiah 43:21).

the sheep under His care

– Sheep depend entirely on the shepherd for guidance, protection, and rescue (John 10:11-14).

– Under His care:

• Guidance—He leads along righteous paths (Psalm 23:3).

• Protection—no snatching from His hand (John 10:28-29).

• Rescue—He carries the injured and seeks the lost (Isaiah 40:11; Luke 15:4-7).

– The phrase underscores tenderness and commitment; His watchfulness is constant and personal (Psalm 121:4).

Today, if you hear His voice

– “Today” presses urgency; response to God is never left for tomorrow (2 Corinthians 6:2).

– Hearing implies obedience, not mere awareness (Deuteronomy 30:20; James 1:22).

– The writer of Hebrews repeats this line to urge believers to trust and obey without delay (Hebrews 3:7-15).

– Deliberate, soft-hearted listening positions believers to receive ongoing rest and direction from the Shepherd (John 10:27).

summary

Psalm 95:7 invites wholehearted recognition of the Shepherd-King. He declares Himself “our God,” situates us in His pasture, tends us as cherished sheep, and calls for immediate, obedient listening. The verse weaves identity, security, provision, and prompt response into one beautiful snapshot of life under His loving rule.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(7) To-day if . . .--In joining this clause with Psalm 95:8-9 the Authorised Version follows the LXX. The Masoretic text connects it with the preceding part of the verse, and there seems no good reason for departing from that arrangement. Indeed, the change from the third person, "his voice," to the first, "tempted me," in the same sentence is intolerable even in Hebrew poetry. Nor is there any necessity to suppose the loss of a line. Render: "For He is our God, and we are the people of his pasture, the sheep of his hand. Today would that ye would hearken to his voice." The Oriental custom of leading flocks by the voice is doubtless alluded to, as in John 10:4. Notice the resemblance in Psalm 95:6-7 to Psalm 100:3-4.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 7a. - For he is our God. A second, and a more urgent, reason for worshipping God. Not only is he a "great God" (ver. 3), but he is also "our God" - our own God - brought into the closest personal relationship with us. And we are the people of his pasture, and the sheep of his hand (comp. Psalm 74:1; Psalm 79:13; Psalm 80:1, etc.). We are led by him, tended by him, fed by him, folded by him. We owe everything to his shepherding. Verses 7b-11. - The warning against waywardness. This is delivered in four, or rather four and a half, verses, and commences with the words, "Today if ye will hear his voice." Verse 7b. - Today. This word, standing prominently forward as it does, is a startling call, intimating that the time is come for a momentous decision. If ye will hear his voice. God is crying to his people - will they hear, or will they forbear? If the former, all will go well; if the latter, than assuredly they shall not enter into his rest. The "voice" intended proceeds to give the warning of vers. 8-11.
