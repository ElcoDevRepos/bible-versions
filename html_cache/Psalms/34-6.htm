This poor man called out
The phrase "this poor man" refers to David, who is traditionally understood to be the author of this psalm. At the time of writing, David was likely reflecting on his experiences of distress and need, possibly during his flight from Saul or his time among the Philistines. The term "poor" here can denote not only material poverty but also a state of humility and desperation. In biblical context, calling out to God is a common expression of prayer and supplication, seen throughout the Psalms and other scriptures, such as in Psalm 18:6 and Psalm 120:1, where the faithful cry out to God in times of need.

and the LORD heard him
The assurance that "the LORD heard him" emphasizes God's attentiveness to the cries of His people. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where God promises to be responsive to those who seek Him earnestly. The idea of God hearing prayers is a recurring theme in the Bible, as seen in 1 John 5:14-15, which assures believers that God hears and answers prayers according to His will. This phrase underscores God's faithfulness and His role as a divine protector and provider.

He saved him from all his troubles
The deliverance mentioned here is both physical and spiritual. Historically, David experienced numerous instances of divine intervention, such as his escape from King Saul and his victory over Goliath. Theologically, this deliverance can be seen as a type of salvation that points to the ultimate deliverance through Jesus Christ, who saves believers from sin and eternal separation from God. This phrase also connects to other scriptures that speak of God's saving power, such as Psalm 107:6 and 2 Samuel 22:1, where God is depicted as a rescuer from distress and danger.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 34, David wrote this psalm after pretending to be insane before Abimelech, who drove him away. This context highlights David's reliance on God during times of distress.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His faithfulness and personal relationship with His people. In this verse, Yahweh is the one who hears and saves.

3. The Poor Man
Represents anyone in a state of humility or need, acknowledging their dependence on God. This term can be understood both literally and spiritually.

4. Troubles
Refers to the various adversities and challenges faced by believers. In the context of David's life, it includes threats to his life and well-being.

5. Abimelech
The Philistine king before whom David feigned madness. This event is a backdrop to the psalm, illustrating God's deliverance in dire circumstances.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God
Recognize that true deliverance comes from God alone. In times of trouble, our first response should be to call out to Him, acknowledging our need for His intervention.

God's Faithfulness
Trust in the LORD's faithfulness to hear and respond to our cries. His past deliverances, as seen in David's life, assure us of His continued care.

Humility in Prayer
Approach God with a humble heart, like the "poor man" in the psalm. Acknowledge our limitations and God's sovereignty in every situation.

Encouragement in Trials
Find encouragement in knowing that God is attentive to our struggles. Share testimonies of His deliverance to strengthen others in their faith.

What Does Psalm 34:6 Mean
This poor man

David looks back on his flight from Saul (1 Samuel 21–22) and calls himself “this poor man.” He is not complaining about finances; he is confessing dependence.

• Psalm 40:17 says, “Yet I am poor and needy; may the Lord think of me.”

• Psalm 86:1 echoes the same posture: “Incline Your ear, O LORD, and answer me, for I am poor and needy.”

• Jesus highlights this heart-attitude in Matthew 5:3: “Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.”

By confessing poverty of spirit, David teaches that the starting point of rescue is admitting need.

called out

Need moved David to action—he prayed.

• “In my distress I called upon the LORD; I cried to my God for help” (Psalm 18:6).

• Jeremiah 33:3 invites every believer: “Call to Me, and I will answer you and show you great and unsearchable things you do not know.”

• Hebrews 4:16 encourages, “Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence.”

Prayer is never a last resort; it is faith’s first reflex.

and the LORD heard him

God’s hearing is personal and attentive.

• Psalm 34:15 in the same psalm assures, “The eyes of the LORD are on the righteous, and His ears are inclined to their cry.”

• Psalm 116:1-2: “He has heard my voice … because He has inclined His ear to me, I will call on Him as long as I live.”

• 1 Peter 3:12 repeats the promise for New-Covenant believers: “The eyes of the Lord are on the righteous and His ears are open to their prayer.”

The Creator stoops to listen; no cry of His child is ignored.

He saved him from all his troubles

The hearing led to tangible help. “All” includes every danger David faced—physical, emotional, spiritual.

• Psalm 34:17 restates it: “The righteous cry out, and the LORD hears; He delivers them from all their troubles.”

• 2 Timothy 4:18 affirms the same pattern in the New Testament: “The Lord will rescue me from every evil deed and will bring me safely into His heavenly kingdom.”

• Isaiah 46:4 adds God’s lifelong commitment: “I will carry you and I will save you.”

Deliverance may come through escape, endurance, or ultimate vindication, but God never abandons His own.

summary

Psalm 34:6 paints a simple yet profound sequence: recognize need, pray, trust God’s attentive ear, and experience His comprehensive rescue. David’s testimony becomes a template for every believer who feels poor, overwhelmed, and unheard—call out, for the Lord listens and saves.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - This poor man cried, and the Lord heard him, and saved him out of all his troubles. Almost a repetition of ver. 4, but in the third person instead of the first. The "poor man" intended is David him* self, not an ideal poor man. Otherwise the demonstrative "this" (זֶה) would not have been employed.
