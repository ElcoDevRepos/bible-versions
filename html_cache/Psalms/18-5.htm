The cords of Sheol entangled me;
In ancient Hebrew thought, "Sheol" refers to the realm of the dead, a shadowy place where the departed souls reside. The imagery of "cords" suggests being bound or trapped, indicating a sense of inescapable danger or distress. This phrase reflects the psalmist's feeling of being overwhelmed by mortal peril, akin to being ensnared by death itself. The use of "cords" can also symbolize the spiritual and emotional struggles that bind individuals, drawing a parallel to the New Testament's depiction of sin as a binding force (Romans 6:16). The psalmist's experience can be seen as a type of Christ's own descent into death and His victory over it, as referenced in Acts 2:31, where Peter speaks of Christ not being abandoned to Hades.

the snares of death confronted me.
"Snares" are traps set for capturing prey, emphasizing the sudden and unexpected nature of the threat. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, death was often personified as an active force, seeking to ensnare the living. This phrase highlights the immediacy and inevitability of death, a common theme throughout the Psalms. The confrontation with death can be seen as a foreshadowing of Christ's own confrontation with death on the cross, where He ultimately triumphed over it (1 Corinthians 15:55-57). The psalmist's deliverance from these snares prefigures the resurrection, offering hope of victory over death for all believers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 18, David is expressing his gratitude to God for deliverance from his enemies, including King Saul. This psalm is a song of thanksgiving and praise for God's protection and deliverance.

2. Sheol
In Hebrew thought, Sheol is the realm of the dead, a place of darkness and separation from the living. It represents the grave or the pit, often associated with despair and hopelessness.

3. Death
Personified in this verse as having snares, death is depicted as an active force seeking to capture and destroy. It represents the ultimate enemy and the finality of life without divine intervention.
Teaching Points
Understanding Spiritual Warfare
The imagery of cords and snares reminds us of the spiritual battles we face. Just as David felt entangled by death, believers today encounter spiritual forces that seek to ensnare and destroy. Recognizing these battles is the first step in seeking God's deliverance.

God's Deliverance from Despair
David's experience teaches us that no situation is beyond God's reach. Even when we feel trapped by circumstances that seem as final as death, God is able to deliver and restore us.

The Reality of Death and Hope in Christ
While death is a reality for all, Christians have hope through Jesus Christ, who conquered death. This verse can remind us of the victory we have in Christ, who frees us from the fear of death and the power of Sheol.

Prayer in Times of Distress
Like David, we should turn to God in prayer when faced with overwhelming challenges. Prayer is a powerful tool for seeking God's intervention and experiencing His peace amidst turmoil.

What Does Psalm 18:5 Mean
The cords of Sheol entangled me

“The cords of Sheol entangled me”

• David describes an experience so grave that he felt the realm of the dead tightening around him, as if ropes were already binding him for burial.

• Sheol is not merely poetic imagery; it is the literal place of the departed (Genesis 37:35). Feeling its cords signals that David believed death was moments away.

• Similar cries appear in Psalm 116:3, “The cords of death encompassed me; the torrents of chaos overwhelmed me,” and in Jonah 2:5 when the prophet says, “The waters engulfed me to the neck; the deep surrounded me.” Both passages show God-fearing people sensing they have crossed the boundary into death’s territory.

• In 2 Samuel 22:6 (the historical parallel to Psalm 18), David recounts the same scene, rooting this language in an actual life-threatening event, probably during Saul’s relentless pursuit (1 Samuel 23:14).

• The verse affirms that even God’s anointed can face circumstances that feel terminal. Yet David records the feeling so that believers recognize God’s salvation as clearly as they feel death’s grip.

the snares of death confronted me

“The snares of death confronted me”

• The picture shifts from cords to snares—traps set to ambush prey. David isn’t merely caught; he is being hunted.

• Psalm 140:5 echoes this danger: “The proud have hidden a snare for me and cords; they have spread out a net by the path.” Such verses reveal the deliberate, strategic nature of evil.

• Satan is called the one who holds “the power of death” (Hebrews 2:14). When David sees snares, he is discerning the enemy’s hand behind human foes.

• God often allows exposure to life-and-death traps so His rescue shines brighter. Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 1:8-10 that he “despaired even of life…so that we would not trust in ourselves but in God who raises the dead.”

• Just as a bird escapes a fowler’s snare (Psalm 124:7), David will later declare, “He rescued me because He delighted in me” (Psalm 18:19). The confrontation ends not in defeat but in deliverance that magnifies God’s glory.

summary

Psalm 18:5 paints a vivid, literal scene: David senses death’s cords coiling and its traps snapping shut. He is not exaggerating; he is recording the precise terror that preceded God’s dramatic salvation. For believers, these lines validate the reality of deadly trials while pointing to the greater reality of God’s power to break every cord and spring every snare.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Hell.--Heb., sheol. (See Note on Psalm 6:5.)

Prevented--i.e., suddenly seized upon. The poet seems to feel the cords already tightening on his limbe. He is not dead yet, but like to them who go down to sheol. This verse has one verbal difference from Samuel.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - The sorrows of hell compassed me about; literally, the cords of Sheol, or Hades. Death and Hell are, both of them, personified, and made to join in the chase. The ensnaring nets are drawn nearer and nearer; at last the toils close in, the last cast is made, and the prey is taken. The snares of death prevented me; or, came upon me (Revised Version) - "took me by surprise" (Kay).
