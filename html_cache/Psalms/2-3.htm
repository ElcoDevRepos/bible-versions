Let us break Their chains
This phrase reflects the rebellious nature of humanity against divine authority. In the biblical context, "chains" symbolize the perceived restrictions or limitations imposed by God's laws and decrees. Historically, this rebellion can be seen in the actions of nations and leaders who resist God's sovereignty, as illustrated in the broader context of Psalm 2, which speaks of kings and rulers plotting against the Lord and His Anointed. Theologically, this rebellion is rooted in the sin nature of humanity, which seeks autonomy apart from God. This phrase also connects to the broader biblical narrative of human rebellion, such as the Tower of Babel (Genesis 11:1-9), where humanity sought to establish its own authority apart from God.

and cast away Their cords.
"Cords" here further emphasize the idea of divine guidance and boundaries that humanity seeks to reject. In ancient times, cords were used to bind or secure, symbolizing control or restraint. The act of casting them away signifies a deliberate rejection of God's rule and an attempt to live independently of His will. This mirrors the spiritual rebellion seen throughout Scripture, where individuals and nations turn away from God's commandments. Theologically, this reflects the ongoing spiritual battle between human will and divine authority. In the New Testament, this rebellion is countered by the call to submit to Christ's lordship, as seen in passages like Romans 10:3, where Paul speaks of the need to submit to God's righteousness rather than establishing one's own. This phrase also foreshadows the ultimate victory of Christ, who will establish His kingdom despite human resistance, as prophesied in Revelation 19:11-16.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Nations and Kings
The psalm speaks of the nations and their rulers who are in rebellion against God and His Anointed One. They represent the collective human authority that opposes divine rule.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The sovereign God who is the ultimate authority and whose decrees are unchangeable. He is the one against whom the nations are rebelling.

3. The Anointed One (Messiah)
Refers to the Messiah, who Christians understand to be Jesus Christ. He is the King established by God to rule over the nations.

4. Zion
The holy hill where God has set His King. It symbolizes God's chosen place and His divine rule.

5. The Rebellion
The event of the nations conspiring against God and His Anointed, seeking to reject divine authority.
Teaching Points
Rebellion Against God
The desire to "break Their chains" reflects humanity's inherent rebellion against God's authority. This rebellion is rooted in pride and a desire for autonomy.

Divine Sovereignty
Despite human rebellion, God's sovereignty remains unchallenged. Believers can find comfort in knowing that God's plans and purposes will prevail.

True Freedom
The world's concept of freedom often involves casting off divine authority, but true freedom is found in submission to God and His Anointed.

Christ's Authority
As Christians, we recognize Jesus as the Anointed One. Our response should be one of submission and allegiance to His lordship.

Call to Repentance
The psalm serves as a warning to those in rebellion, urging them to repent and submit to God's rule before it's too late.

What Does Psalm 2:3 Mean
Let us break Their chains

• The speakers are the “kings of the earth and rulers” from Psalm 2:2, openly plotting against the LORD and His Anointed.

• “Chains” evokes the image of God’s moral law and sovereign authority restraining evil (Psalm 119:45; Romans 13:1–2). They call God’s guidelines restrictive rather than protective.

• This deliberate choice to shatter divine restraint mirrors the defiance in Exodus 5:2, where Pharaoh asked, “Who is the LORD, that I should obey His voice?”

• Human history repeats the pattern: “Everyone did what was right in his own eyes” (Judges 21:25) whenever God’s rule is rejected.

• Acts 4:25–27 cites Psalm 2 to show that the same insurrection surfaced at the crucifixion of Jesus as earthly powers tried to silence God’s Messiah.

and cast away Their cords

• “Cords” picture the softer ties of covenant love and daily guidance. Breaking chains is open rebellion; throwing off cords is the refusal of even gentle leading (Hosea 11:4).

• Sinners long for autonomy, yet true freedom is found only in submission to Christ: “If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed” (John 8:36).

• Rejecting God’s cords results in slavery to sin (John 8:34) and eventual judgment (Psalm 2:5,12).

• Proverbs 1:29–31 warns that despising counsel brings disastrous consequences—exactly what these rulers invite by discarding every bond with God.

summary

Psalm 2:3 captures humanity’s rallying cry against divine authority: “Let us break Their chains and cast away Their cords.” The verse exposes the heart that labels God’s righteous limits as oppressive and His loving guidance as unwanted restraint. Such rebellion is futile—God still reigns, Christ is enthroned, and genuine liberty exists only under His lordship.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Let us break.--The whispered purpose now breaks out into loud menace, and we hear their defiance pass along the ranks of the rebels.

Cords.--The LXX. and Vulg. have "yoke," which is in keeping with the metaphor of a restive animal. (Comp. Isaiah 58:6; Isaiah 10:27.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Let us break their bands asunder. Wicked men always feel God's rule and his Law to be restraints. They chafe at them, fret against them, and, in the last resort - so far as their will goes - wholly throw them off. And cast away their cords from us. "Bands" and "cords" are the fetters that restrain prisoners. The rebels determine to burst them, and assert their absolute freedom.
