With my lips
This phrase emphasizes the importance of verbal confession and the spoken word in the practice of faith. In biblical times, oral tradition was a primary means of transmitting knowledge and teachings. The act of speaking aloud was seen as a way to internalize and affirm one's beliefs. This aligns with the Jewish Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-9), which commands the faithful to speak of God's commandments.

I proclaim
Proclamation here suggests a public declaration, indicating that the psalmist is not only internalizing God's word but also sharing it with others. This reflects the biblical mandate to be a witness to God's truth, as seen in the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20). The act of proclaiming is both a personal commitment and a communal responsibility.

all the judgments
The term "judgments" refers to God's decrees and laws, which are seen as just and righteous. In the context of the Old Testament, these judgments are part of the Torah, the first five books of the Bible, which contain the laws given to Moses. The psalmist's commitment to proclaiming "all" the judgments underscores the comprehensive nature of God's law and the believer's duty to uphold it in its entirety.

of Your mouth
This phrase attributes the source of the judgments directly to God, emphasizing divine authority. The imagery of God's mouth suggests that His words are living and active, as seen in Hebrews 4:12, which describes the word of God as "sharper than any double-edged sword." This highlights the belief that Scripture is God-breathed (2 Timothy 3:16) and carries His authority and power.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, though the exact authorship is uncertain. The psalmist is a devout follower of God, expressing a deep love and reverence for God's law.

2. God
The central figure in the psalm, whose laws, decrees, and judgments are the focus of the psalmist's meditation and proclamation.

3. Judgments
Refers to God's decrees or decisions, which are seen as righteous and just. The psalmist commits to proclaiming these judgments, indicating their importance and truth.
Teaching Points
The Power of Proclamation
The psalmist highlights the importance of using our words to declare God's truth. As believers, we are called to speak God's Word boldly and faithfully, recognizing the power of our testimony.

Internalizing God's Word
To proclaim God's judgments effectively, we must first internalize His Word. This involves regular study, meditation, and memorization of Scripture, allowing it to transform our hearts and minds.

Aligning Speech with Faith
Our speech should reflect our faith and commitment to God's truth. We must be mindful of our words, ensuring they align with the teachings and values of Scripture.

Witnessing Through Words
Proclaiming God's judgments is a form of witnessing. By sharing God's truth with others, we fulfill the Great Commission and encourage others to seek and understand God's will.

What Does Psalm 119:13 Mean
With my lips

The psalmist begins with something wonderfully tangible—his own lips. Genuine devotion isn’t silent; it moves from the heart to audible expression.

• Lips shaped by praise (Psalm 34:1) remind us that confession and worship belong together.

• Speaking truth guards us from double-mindedness (James 3:9-10).

• What we verbalize reveals what fills our hearts (Luke 6:45), so the psalmist wants every word to align with God’s Word.

I proclaim

To proclaim is more than casual conversation; it’s deliberate, public declaration.

• David models this in Psalm 40:9-10, announcing God’s righteousness “in the great assembly.”

• The Great Commission (Mark 16:15) echoes the same calling—truth heard in private must be voiced in public.

• Proclamation turns personal conviction into shared blessing, encouraging fellow believers (Colossians 3:16) and warning the lost (Ezekiel 3:17).

all the judgments

He doesn’t cherry-pick pleasant passages; he speaks every verdict, promise, warning, and comfort.

• God’s judgments are wholly righteous (Psalm 19:9) and eternally relevant (Psalm 119:160).

• Accepting all of them guards us from selective obedience (Acts 20:27, Paul declaring “the whole counsel of God”).

• Declaring “all” cultivates integrity—our choices, values, and counsel remain consistent with the entirety of Scripture.

of Your mouth

The source matters. These judgments aren’t human opinion but divine utterance.

• Life itself depends on “every word that proceeds from the mouth of the LORD” (Deuteronomy 8:3).

• God’s spoken Word accomplishes His purpose unfailingly (Isaiah 55:11).

• Because the Author is perfect (Psalm 12:6), His words carry absolute authority; proclaiming them is an act of submission and trust.

summary

Psalm 119:13 shows a believer joyfully voicing God’s complete, authoritative Word. With lips devoted to praise, the psalmist boldly proclaims every divine ruling, acknowledging that each one flows from the flawless mouth of God. Our calling is the same: speak Scripture openly, fully, and confidently, allowing its truth to shape both our words and our witness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) With my lips.--He has not kept his hidden treasure to himself, but, like the good householder of the Gospels, has brought out things new and old.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - With my lips have I declared all the judgments of thy mouth. "Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh." The "word" hid in the psalmist's heart (ver. 11) could not but rise to his lips on fit occasion, and be set forth before the people for their edification - more especially as there was an express command binding upon all Israelites to teach the Law to their children and dependants (see particularly Deuteronomy 6:7).
