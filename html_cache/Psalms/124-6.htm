Blessed be the LORD
This phrase is a call to worship and gratitude, acknowledging God's sovereignty and benevolence. In the Hebrew tradition, blessing the LORD is an act of recognizing His supreme authority and goodness. This echoes the frequent calls to bless the LORD found throughout the Psalms (e.g., Psalm 103:1-2). It reflects a deep-seated tradition of praise that is central to the worship practices of ancient Israel. The act of blessing God is not just verbal but involves a heart posture of reverence and thankfulness.

who has not given us
This part of the verse emphasizes God's protective role over His people. The imagery suggests a divine intervention where God actively prevents harm from befalling His followers. This is consistent with the biblical narrative of God as a protector, as seen in the Exodus story where God delivers the Israelites from Egyptian bondage (Exodus 14). It underscores the belief in God's providence and His ability to control circumstances for the benefit of His people.

as prey to their teeth
The metaphor of being prey to teeth suggests a violent and destructive threat, likely from enemies or oppressors. In the ancient Near Eastern context, this imagery would resonate with the audience familiar with the dangers posed by wild animals. It also reflects the historical reality of Israel's frequent conflicts with surrounding nations. The phrase can be connected to the New Testament depiction of Satan as a roaring lion seeking whom he may devour (1 Peter 5:8), highlighting the spiritual warfare believers face. This imagery also points to the ultimate deliverance found in Jesus Christ, who is seen as the Good Shepherd protecting His flock from spiritual harm (John 10:11-15).

Teaching Points
God’s Sovereign Protection
God is actively involved in the lives of His people, providing protection from both physical and spiritual dangers.

Recognition of Divine Intervention
It is important to acknowledge and give thanks for God’s deliverance in our lives, recognizing His hand in our safety and well-being.

Trust in God’s Faithfulness
Just as God delivered Israel, He remains faithful to His promises and will deliver us from our adversaries.

Spiritual Warfare Awareness
Believers must be aware of the spiritual battles they face and rely on God’s strength and protection.

Praise as a Response to Deliverance
Our response to God’s deliverance should be one of praise and thanksgiving, acknowledging His power and mercy.

What Does Psalm 124:6 Mean
Blessed be the LORD

• The psalmist starts with worship, not worry. Before mentioning enemies, David places God at the center: “Bless the LORD, O my soul” (Psalm 103:1).

• Praising first reminds us the battle is already under God’s control, echoing “Blessed be the name of the LORD now and forevermore” (Psalm 113:2).

• New-Testament believers follow the same pattern: “Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who has blessed us in Christ” (Ephesians 1:3). The reflex of a rescued heart is gratitude.

who has not given us

• David credits God alone for Israel’s survival; it was never luck or military genius. “The horse is prepared for the day of battle, but victory rests with the LORD” (Proverbs 21:31).

• God’s sovereignty means He actively withholds His people from destruction (John 10:28, “no one will snatch them out of My hand”).

• Notice the past-tense certainty. Just as Joshua could say, “Not one of all the LORD’s good promises to Israel failed” (Joshua 21:45), we look back at the cross and empty tomb as completed rescue missions.

as prey

• “Prey” paints a vivid picture of helplessness. Israel was cornered, but God stepped in. Psalm 44:22 admits, “For Your sake we face death all day long; we are considered as sheep to be slaughtered.”

• The church also recognizes real spiritual predators: “Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion seeking someone to devour” (1 Peter 5:8).

• The phrase reminds us God does more than soothe fears—He keeps the enemy from ever getting a bite.

to their teeth

• Teeth symbolize an enemy’s power to destroy (Psalm 57:4, “Their teeth are spears and arrows”). God broke that power: “My God sent His angel and shut the lions’ mouths” (Daniel 6:22).

• Ultimately Christ fulfills this deliverance: He disarmed the rulers and authorities, “triumphing over them by the cross” (Colossians 2:15).

• Because God intervenes, believers can sing with David, “He rescues me from my powerful enemy” (Psalm 18:17).

summary

Psalm 124:6 celebrates a concrete rescue. David blesses the LORD for personally preventing His people from becoming easy pickings for brutal foes. The verse invites believers today to thank God first, trust His sovereign hold, remember our helplessness apart from Him, and rest in the broken teeth of every enemy through Christ’s decisive victory.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - Blessed be the Lord, who hath not given us as a prey to their teeth. We are not devoured - we are not "swallowed up" - thanks to the interposition of the merciful and gracious Lord, to whom therefore praise and blessing are due.
