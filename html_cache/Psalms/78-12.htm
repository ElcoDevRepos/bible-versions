He worked wonders before their fathers
This phrase refers to the miraculous acts God performed in the presence of the Israelites' ancestors. These wonders include the ten plagues, the parting of the Red Sea, and the provision of manna and quail. These acts demonstrated God's power and faithfulness, serving as a testament to His covenant with Israel. The wonders were meant to instill faith and obedience in the Israelites, reminding them of God's sovereignty and care. This theme of divine intervention is echoed throughout the Old Testament, particularly in the narratives of Exodus and Deuteronomy.

in the land of Egypt
Egypt is a significant location in biblical history, representing both a place of refuge and oppression for the Israelites. It was in Egypt that the Israelites grew into a great nation, yet it was also where they experienced severe bondage. The land of Egypt serves as a backdrop for God's deliverance, highlighting His ability to rescue His people from seemingly insurmountable circumstances. The plagues and the Exodus are central events that underscore God's supremacy over the gods of Egypt and His commitment to His promises.

in the region of Zoan
Zoan, also known as Tanis, was an ancient city in the northeastern Nile Delta. It was a prominent city during the time of the Exodus and served as a royal residence for Pharaohs. Archaeological findings suggest that Zoan was a center of political and religious activity, making it a fitting location for God's wonders to be displayed. The mention of Zoan emphasizes the historical and geographical reality of the biblical narrative, grounding the miraculous events in a specific and identifiable location. This reference also connects to other scriptures, such as Isaiah 19:11 and Ezekiel 30:14, which mention Zoan in the context of God's judgment and power.

What Does Psalm 78:12 Mean
He worked wonders

- The psalmist points first to God’s unmistakable acts of power: “He worked wonders.” These are not poetic exaggerations but real, historical miracles that altered nature and toppled Egypt’s pride (Exodus 3:20; 7:3; Psalm 105:5, 27; Acts 7:36).

- Ten specific wonders stand out:

1. Water to blood

2. Frogs

3. Gnats

4. Flies

5. Livestock disease

6. Boils

7. Hail

8. Locusts

9. Darkness

10. Death of the firstborn

- Each sign revealed God’s supremacy over Egypt’s idols and validated Moses’ message of deliverance (Exodus 12:12).

Before their fathers

- “Before their fathers” highlights eyewitness testimony. An entire generation saw these miracles with their own eyes (Exodus 10:1-2; Deuteronomy 29:2-3).

- The psalm invites later generations to trust God by remembering what earlier generations experienced (Psalm 78:3-4).

- Because the events were public, Israel’s faith rests on verifiable history, not private visions.

In the land of Egypt

- Egypt was the superpower that enslaved Israel (Exodus 1:13-14). God chose that setting so His power would be displayed against the world’s strongest empire (Exodus 9:16).

- Lessons from the location:

• Deliverance—God rescues His people from bondage (Exodus 6:6-7).

• Judgment—He confronts sin and oppression head-on (Exodus 12:12).

• Evangelism—News of these acts spread far beyond Israel (Joshua 2:9-11).

- Remembering Egypt keeps believers humble and grateful, never forgetting what God saved them from (Deuteronomy 6:21-23).

In the region of Zoan

- Zoan (also called Tanis) sat in the Nile Delta, a political and religious hub where Pharaoh held court (Numbers 13:22).

- By pinpointing Zoan, the psalm underscores that the miracles happened at the heart of Egypt’s authority, not in some remote corner (Isaiah 19:11; 30:4).

- God’s wonders turned the “fields of Zoan” into a stage for His glory (Psalm 78:43).

- The detail assures us these events are geographically anchored, confirming Scripture’s historical precision.

summary

Psalm 78:12 reminds us that God performed literal, mighty miracles—publicly witnessed by Israel’s ancestors, on Egyptian soil, in Pharaoh’s own backyard of Zoan. These wonders prove His unmatched power, His faithfulness to deliver, and His desire that each generation confidently trust and proclaim His acts.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) Field of Zoan.--See Numbers 13:22. It is the classical "Tanis," merely a corruption of Tsoan, i.e., low country (LXX. and Vulgate). Tanis is situated on the east bank of what was formerly called the Tanitic branch of the Nile. Between it and Pelusium, about thirty miles to the east, stretched a rich plain known as "the marshes," or "the pastures," or "the field" of Zoan.

The psalm now turns to the adventures in the wilderness, postponing the marvels in Egypt till Psalm 78:43.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - Marvellous things did he in the sight of their fathers, in the land of Egypt, in the field of Zoan. The miracles of Egypt are, perhaps, the most striking series in Jewish history. A more particular account of them is given below (vers. 44-53). They were wrought "in the field of Zoan," i.e. in the rich flat tract east and south of the city of Zoan, the Greek Tanis, now San. (On this place, see Mr. Reginald Peele's 'Cities of Egypt,' pp. 64-88.) This fact could not have been gathered from Exodus, but must have come to the writer from the tradition of which he speaks in ver. 3.
