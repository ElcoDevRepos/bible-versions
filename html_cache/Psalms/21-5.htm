Great is his glory in Your salvation;
This phrase highlights the divine origin of the king's glory, emphasizing that it is not self-derived but a result of God's intervention and deliverance. In the historical context, the king of Israel was often seen as God's anointed, and his victories were attributed to divine favor. The term "salvation" here can be understood as deliverance from enemies, a common theme in the Psalms. This reflects the covenant relationship between God and the Davidic king, where the king's success is a testament to God's faithfulness. Theologically, this can be seen as a type of Christ, whose ultimate glory is revealed through His work of salvation for humanity. The glory of the king prefigures the greater glory of Jesus, who is exalted through His resurrection and ascension.

You bestow on him splendor and majesty.
This phrase indicates that the king's honor and dignity are gifts from God, not achievements of his own making. In the ancient Near Eastern context, kings were often seen as representatives of divine authority, and their splendor was a reflection of the deity they served. The words "splendor" and "majesty" suggest a royal dignity that is both visible and awe-inspiring, pointing to the king's elevated status among the nations. Biblically, this can be connected to the promises made to David in 2 Samuel 7, where God assures him of an enduring dynasty. Prophetically, this foreshadows the ultimate fulfillment in Jesus Christ, who embodies divine majesty and whose kingdom is eternal. The bestowal of such attributes on the king serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and the honor He grants to those who serve Him faithfully.

Recognize that true glory and honor come from God alone. Human achievements are ultimately gifts from Him.

Role of Salvation
Understand that salvation is not just deliverance from enemies but also a means through which God bestows glory and majesty.

Reflecting God’s Majesty
As believers, we are called to reflect God’s splendor and majesty in our lives, pointing others to His greatness.

Gratitude for God’s Gifts
Cultivate a heart of gratitude for the splendor and majesty God bestows upon us, acknowledging His role in our successes.

Christ as the Fulfillment

What Does Psalm 21:5 Mean
Great is his glory

“Great is his glory” (Psalm 21:5a) reminds us that the king’s honor is unmistakable and public. The phrase points first to David, whose victories (2 Samuel 8:6) made his name renowned, but it ultimately foreshadows Christ, “crowned with glory and honor” (Hebrews 2:9).

• Glory is not self-generated; it reflects God’s own glory shining through a servant who trusts Him (Psalm 3:3; 1 Corinthians 10:31).

• Because this glory is rooted in God, it is secure—unlike worldly fame that fades (1 Peter 1:24-25).

• The believer shares in this reflected glory: “Those who look to Him are radiant” (Psalm 34:5).

in Your salvation

The king’s greatness rests “in Your salvation” (Psalm 21:5b). Every triumph flows from God’s rescue, whether battlefield deliverance (Psalm 20:6) or eternal redemption (Isaiah 12:2).

• Salvation replaces fear with confidence: “The battle belongs to the LORD” (1 Samuel 17:47).

• It safeguards the future: “He will not let your foot slip” (Psalm 121:3).

• For us, Christ’s cross is the ultimate “salvation” that grounds our identity (Romans 1:16).

You bestow on him

“You bestow” underscores that God is the active Giver. David’s throne and Jesus’ exaltation alike are gifts, not rewards earned.

• “Every good and perfect gift is from above” (James 1:17).

• God’s initiative is seen in His covenant with David: “I took you from the pasture... and I will make your name great” (2 Samuel 7:8-9).

• Our lives mirror this pattern: “What do you have that you did not receive?” (1 Corinthians 4:7).

splendor and majesty

God grants “splendor and majesty” as royal regalia (Psalm 21:5c). For David, that meant a respected throne (1 Chron 29:25). For Christ, it is cosmic kingship: “On His robe and on His thigh He has a name written: KING OF KINGS” (Revelation 19:16).

• These qualities signify authority, beauty, and holy awe (Psalm 96:6).

• Believers are promised a share: “You have made them a little lower than the angels and crowned them with glory and honor” (Psalm 8:5; cf. Revelation 3:21).

• Such majesty calls for humble worship, not self-exaltation (Psalm 29:2).

summary

Psalm 21:5 celebrates a God-given glory that rests on rescue, flows from the Giver, and shines with royal splendor. David experienced it in part; Jesus embodies it fully; and all who trust Him are invited to reflect it—to the praise of the One who bestows honor that never fades.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - His glory is great in thy salvation. David' s glory exceeds that of all other living men, through the "salvation" which God vouchsafes him. That salvation is partly temporal, consisting in deliverance from his foes; partly of an unearthly and spiritual character, arising out of his relationship to the coming Messiah. It is from the latter point of view, rather than the former, that it is said, Honour and majesty hast thou laid upon him.
