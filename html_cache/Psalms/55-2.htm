Attend to me and answer me.
This plea reflects the psalmist's urgent need for God's attention and intervention. In the context of ancient Israel, prayer was a vital means of communication with God, often seeking His guidance and deliverance. The psalmist's request for God to "attend" and "answer" underscores a deep reliance on divine responsiveness, a theme echoed throughout the Psalms (e.g., Psalm 4:1, Psalm 17:6). This phrase also highlights the personal relationship between the psalmist and God, emphasizing trust in God's willingness to listen and respond. The call for God to "answer" can be seen as a precursor to the New Testament assurance found in 1 John 5:14-15, where believers are encouraged that God hears and answers prayers according to His will.

I am restless in my complaint,
The psalmist's restlessness indicates a state of inner turmoil and anxiety. This reflects the human condition of distress when faced with overwhelming circumstances. The term "complaint" here is not merely a grievance but a heartfelt lament, a common feature in the Psalms where the psalmist pours out their soul before God (e.g., Psalm 142:2). This expression of restlessness can be connected to the broader biblical narrative of human suffering and the quest for divine justice and peace. It also foreshadows the New Testament's call to cast all anxieties on God, as seen in 1 Peter 5:7, emphasizing God's care for His people.

and distraught.
The word "distraught" conveys a sense of being overwhelmed and in despair. This emotional state is often depicted in the Psalms as a result of external threats or internal struggles. Historically, the psalmist may have been facing betrayal or persecution, common themes in Davidic psalms, which resonate with the experiences of many biblical figures, including Jesus Christ. The feeling of being "distraught" can be linked to Jesus' own anguish in the Garden of Gethsemane (Matthew 26:38), where He experienced deep sorrow and distress. This connection highlights the typology of Christ as the ultimate sufferer who identifies with human pain and offers hope through His redemptive work.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 55, David is expressing his deep distress and seeking God's attention and intervention.

2. Jerusalem
While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, the context of David's life and many of his psalms often relate to his experiences in and around Jerusalem.

3. Enemies
The psalm reflects David's struggles with adversaries, which could include personal enemies or broader threats to his kingship.

4. God
The central figure to whom David directs his plea, seeking divine attention and response.

5. Prayer
The act of communicating with God, which is the primary action David is engaged in within this verse.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Honest Prayer
David's raw and honest expression of his feelings teaches us that we can bring our true emotions and struggles before God without fear.

God's Attentiveness
This verse reassures us that God is attentive to our cries and desires to respond to our needs, encouraging us to seek Him earnestly.

Dealing with Distress
Like David, we can find solace in turning to God during times of distress, trusting that He hears and will provide comfort and guidance.

The Power of Lament
Lamenting is a biblical way to process pain and seek God's intervention, reminding us that it's okay to express sorrow and seek help.

Faith in God's Response
Even when feeling restless and distraught, maintaining faith that God will answer is crucial for spiritual resilience.

What Does Psalm 55:2 Mean
Attend to me

David opens with an urgent plea, the kind a child makes when tugging a father’s sleeve. He is confident that the Sovereign LORD “hears the cry of the righteous” (Psalm 34:15-17) and that “before they call I will answer” (Isaiah 65:24). Yet he still voices the request because relationship with God is personal, not mechanical. We, too, are invited to speak plainly—just as Hannah poured out her heart (1 Samuel 1:15) and as Jesus encouraged in Matthew 7:7-8.

Why it matters today:

• God welcomes honest, immediate access.

• Petition is not presumption; it is obedience rooted in His fatherly care (Philippians 4:6-7).

• Feeling overlooked? Scripture assures, “Cast your burden upon the LORD and He will sustain you” (Psalm 55:22), the very chapter’s later promise.

and answer me

David doesn’t merely want sympathetic hearing; he longs for divine intervention. The prayer is two-fold: “listen” and “respond.” Echoes of this double request appear in Psalm 143:1 — “hear my prayer…answer me in Your faithfulness.” Elijah on Carmel expected such an answer (1 Kings 18:37), and so can we because “the prayer of the righteous is powerful and effective” (James 5:16).

Practical takeaways:

• Expectancy is not arrogance; it is faith in God’s covenant promises (Hebrews 11:6).

• Answers may be yes, no, or wait, but God never ignores His children (Luke 18:7-8).

• As David later records, assurance often comes first inwardly—“God has heard” (Psalm 6:9)—before circumstances shift outwardly.

I am restless in my complaint

Restlessness pictures a heart pacing the floor. The word “complaint” here describes lament, not grumbling. Like Job (Job 23:2) and Jeremiah (Lamentations 3:1-3), David wrestles honestly with pain. Scripture legitimizes lament; it is faith expressing itself through struggle, not unbelief. Paul echoes this tension: “We were under great pressure…so that we despaired of life itself” (2 Corinthians 1:8-9), yet that very pressure drove trust deeper.

Healthy ways to handle holy restlessness:

• Bring the issue to God first, not last (Psalm 62:8).

• Frame feelings with truth: God’s character remains steady even when emotions do not (Malachi 3:6).

• Share burdens within the believing community—David addressed the choir master with this psalm, inviting collective support (Galatians 6:2).

and distraught

The word paints one tossed about by a storm—an interior chaos. David’s distress springs from betrayal (vv. 12-14) and external threats (vv. 3-4). Yet even turmoil is interpreted in light of God’s nearness: “He will redeem my soul in peace from the battle waged against me” (v. 18). Jesus echoes the same assurance: “Let not your hearts be troubled…My peace I give you” (John 14:1, 27).

Steps toward calm amid distress:

• Recall past deliverances (Psalm 77:11-12).

• Speak promises aloud—“When my heart is overwhelmed, lead me to the rock that is higher than I” (Psalm 61:2).

• Choose worship while waiting; Paul and Silas sang at midnight before chains fell (Acts 16:25-26).

summary

Psalm 55:2 models a transparent, faith-filled conversation with God. David pleads for attention, expects an answer, admits restlessness, and confesses distress. Such honesty does not distance him from the LORD; it draws him closer, where ultimate peace is found. We are invited to do the same—bring every agitation to the One who listens, responds, steadies the restless heart, and replaces turmoil with His unshakable peace.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) I mourn.--A verb found in this form only in three other passages, always with the idea of restlessness--e.g., Genesis 27:40, of the roving life of a Bedouin; Jeremiah 2:31, of moral restlessness; Hosea 12:1, of political instability. Here it may either indicate that bodily restlessness which often serves as an outlet of grief:

"Hard mechanic exercise,

Like dull narcotics, numbing pain,"

or the distracted state of the mind itself.

And make a noise.--Better, and must roar, the form of the verb expressing the compulsion which the sufferer feels to give vent to his feelings in groans and murmurs. (See Note on Psalm 42:5.) . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - Attend unto me, and hear me. A very special need is indicated by these four petitions to be heard (vers. 1, 2). I mourn in my complaint, and make a noise; rather, I wander in my musing, and moan aloud. "I wander," i.e. "from one sad thought to another" (Kay); and, unable to constrain myself, I give vent to meanings. Orientals are given to open displays of their grief (Herod., 8:99; AEschylus, 'Persae,' passim).
