For He has not despised or detested the torment of the afflicted.
This phrase emphasizes God's compassion and attentiveness to those who suffer. In the biblical context, the "afflicted" often refers to those who are oppressed or in distress, a theme prevalent throughout the Psalms. The use of "despised" and "detested" indicates a strong rejection, which God does not exhibit towards the suffering. This reflects God's character as one who is close to the brokenhearted (Psalm 34:18). Historically, the afflicted could be seen as the marginalized in society, such as the poor or the sick, whom God consistently defends. This phrase also prophetically points to Jesus Christ, who, during His crucifixion, was not abandoned by God despite His suffering, fulfilling the messianic prophecy of the Psalm.

He has not hidden His face from him,
In ancient Near Eastern culture, to "hide one's face" was a metaphor for rejection or disfavor. God's assurance of not hiding His face signifies His continual presence and favor. This is a reassurance of God's faithfulness, contrasting with human tendencies to turn away from those in distress. Biblically, God's face represents His presence and blessing (Numbers 6:24-26). Theologically, this phrase underscores the belief that God is always present, even in times of deep suffering, as seen in the life of Jesus, who, despite feeling forsaken on the cross, was ultimately in the presence of the Father.

but has attended to his cry for help.
This phrase highlights God's responsiveness to prayer and cries for deliverance. The act of attending implies active listening and intervention. Throughout scripture, God is portrayed as one who hears and responds to the cries of His people (Exodus 3:7). This is a recurring theme in the Psalms, where the psalmist often cries out to God in times of trouble and experiences His deliverance. In the New Testament, Jesus exemplifies this divine attribute by responding to those who call out to Him in faith, such as the blind men in Matthew 20:30-34. This phrase assures believers of God's readiness to respond to their pleas, reinforcing His role as a compassionate and attentive Father.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Afflicted
This term refers to those who are suffering or in distress. In the context of Psalm 22, it is often seen as a prophetic reference to Jesus Christ, who experienced profound suffering.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His faithfulness and presence with His people, especially in times of distress.

3. The Psalmist (David)
Traditionally attributed to King David, this psalm reflects his personal experiences of suffering and deliverance, while also pointing prophetically to the Messiah.

4. The Cross
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the entire psalm is often seen as a foreshadowing of the crucifixion of Jesus, highlighting His suffering and ultimate vindication.

5. The Cry for Help
Represents the prayers and supplications of those in distress, emphasizing the importance of turning to God in times of need.
Teaching Points
God's Presence in Suffering
God does not abandon those who are afflicted. He is present and attentive to their cries, offering comfort and hope.

The Importance of Prayer
In times of distress, turning to God in prayer is crucial. He listens and responds to the cries of His people.

Prophetic Fulfillment
Psalm 22 is a powerful example of how Old Testament scriptures point to the life and work of Jesus Christ, affirming the unity and divine inspiration of the Bible.

Encouragement for the Afflicted
Believers can find solace in knowing that God does not despise their suffering but is compassionate and responsive.

Faithfulness of God
This verse reassures us of God's unwavering faithfulness, encouraging us to trust Him even when circumstances seem dire.

What Does Psalm 22:24 Mean
He has not despised or detested the torment of the afflicted

Psalm 22 opens with David’s heart-wrenching cry, “My God, My God, why have You forsaken me?” (v. 1). Yet by verse 24 the tone changes. Here David states with confidence that God never looks at suffering with contempt.

• Think of Psalm 34:18—“The LORD is near to the brokenhearted; He saves the contrite in spirit.”

• Isaiah 53:4 declares that the Suffering Servant “took on our infirmities,” showing how seriously God regards human pain.

• Jesus identifies completely with the afflicted (Hebrews 4:15), proving that heaven’s regard for suffering is personal, not detached.

When we hurt, the enemy whispers that God must be disgusted with our weakness. This phrase shatters that lie. The Almighty’s eyes soften—never harden—when His children ache.

He has not hidden His face from him

To “hide the face” is biblical language for withdrawing favor (see Numbers 6:24-26). David insists that the faithful sufferer has not been abandoned.

• Job wrestled with the fear of a hidden face (Job 13:24) yet ultimately confessed, “My Redeemer lives” (Job 19:25).

• Even when Christ quoted Psalm 22:1 from the cross (Matthew 27:46), the Father’s plan remained intact; the resurrection proved He had not truly turned away (Acts 2:24-27).

So, in seasons when God seems silent, this verse reminds us that divine presence persists behind the veil of circumstance.

But has attended to his cry for help

God not only notices but actively responds. The Hebrew idea is leaning in to listen.

• Psalm 34:15 echoes, “The eyes of the LORD are on the righteous, and His ears are inclined to their cry.”

• In Exodus 3:7, God says, “I have surely seen the affliction of My people...and I have heard their cry.”

• Jesus’ own prayers “were heard because of His reverence” (Hebrews 5:7), guaranteeing that our cries in Him reach the same attentive Father.

Practical implications:

- Bring every pain to God honestly; He welcomes it.

- Expect real intervention—whether comfort, wisdom, or deliverance—because He promises to act (1 Peter 5:7).

- Encourage others by reminding them that their groans register in heaven.

summary

Psalm 22:24 assures believers that God never scorns suffering, never withdraws His favor, and always bends down to hear. In Christ this promise is sealed: the Man of Sorrows proves once for all that the afflicted are precious to God, His face remains turned toward them, and their cries set heaven in motion.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - For he hath not despised nor abhorred the affliction of the afflicted. The Father might seem by his passivity to disregard his Son's affliction; but it was not really so. Every pang was marked, every suffering sympathized with. And the reward received from the Father was proportionate (see Isaiah 53:12, "Therefore will I divide him a portion with the great, and he shall divide the spoil with the strong; because he hath poured out his soul unto death;" and Philippians 2:8-11, "He became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross; wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a Name which is above every name: that at the Name of Jesus every knee should bow, of things in heaven, and things in earth, and things under the earth; and that every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father"). Neither hath he hid his face from him; but when he cried unto him, he heard. There was no real turning away, no real forsaking. Every cry was heard, and the cries were answered at the fitting moment.
