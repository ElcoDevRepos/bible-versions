The Daughter of Tyre will come with a gift;
This phrase refers to the city of Tyre, a significant Phoenician port city known for its wealth and trade. In biblical times, Tyre was a powerful and prosperous city-state, often associated with commerce and luxury. The "Daughter of Tyre" symbolizes the people or representatives of Tyre, possibly indicating a diplomatic or political gesture. The act of bringing a gift suggests a tribute or offering, which was a common practice in ancient Near Eastern cultures to show respect or allegiance. This can be seen as a fulfillment of the prophecy in Isaiah 23, where Tyre's wealth and influence are acknowledged. Theologically, this gesture can be seen as a type of the nations coming to honor the Messiah, reflecting the broader biblical theme of Gentiles recognizing the sovereignty of God's anointed.

men of wealth will seek your favor.
This part of the verse highlights the influence and honor bestowed upon the subject, likely a king or a figure of great importance. In the context of Psalm 45, which is a royal wedding psalm, this could refer to the king's bride, symbolizing the church or the people of God. The mention of "men of wealth" indicates that those with power and resources recognize the authority and significance of the one they seek to honor. This aligns with the biblical theme of kings and nations acknowledging the reign of God's chosen one, as seen in passages like Isaiah 60:5-6, where the wealth of nations is brought to Zion. In a Christological interpretation, this can be seen as a foreshadowing of the homage paid to Jesus Christ by the wise men (Magi) in Matthew 2, who brought gifts and sought His favor, recognizing His kingship and divine mission.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Daughter of Tyre
Tyre was a significant Phoenician city known for its wealth and commerce. The "Daughter of Tyre" symbolizes the people or representatives of this city, often seen as a metaphor for wealth and worldly influence.

2. Men of Wealth
This phrase refers to influential and affluent individuals who seek favor and blessings, often representing those who recognize the value of aligning with God's anointed.

3. The King
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of Psalm 45 is a royal wedding song, often interpreted as a Messianic psalm pointing to Christ as the ultimate King.
Teaching Points
Recognition of God's Anointed
Just as the Daughter of Tyre and men of wealth sought favor, we are called to recognize and honor Christ as the ultimate King in our lives.

The Influence of Wealth
Wealth and influence should be used to honor God and support His purposes, rather than for selfish gain.

The Universality of Christ's Kingdom
The inclusion of Tyre and wealthy men signifies that Christ's kingdom is open to all, regardless of background or status.

Generosity as Worship
Bringing gifts to honor the King reflects a heart of worship and generosity, which we should emulate in our own lives.

Seeking Favor with God
True favor comes from aligning our lives with God's will and purposes, rather than seeking worldly approval.

What Does Psalm 45:12 Mean
The Daughter of Tyre

“The Daughter of Tyre” points first to the rich Phoenician city that often interacted with Israel. King Hiram of Tyre supplied cedar for David and Solomon (1 Kings 5:1–11), illustrating how Gentile powers honored God’s anointed. The psalm lifts this historic reality into a prophetic scene: the Messianic King will be acknowledged not only by Israel but by the nations (Isaiah 23:17–18; Acts 21:3–6).

• Tyre’s daughter represents the whole Gentile world—cultured, influential, yet outside the covenant—drawn to honor the King they once ignored (Psalm 87:4).

• Just as Israel’s brides were escorted by companions (Psalm 45:14), so the royal Bridegroom’s court is enlarged by Gentile allies (Ephesians 2:12–13).

Will Come with a Gift

“The Daughter of Tyre will come with a gift”. Tribute is the natural response to a king whose rule is righteous and universal (Psalm 72:10–11).

• Gifts acknowledge sovereignty. Hiram’s cedar acknowledged David; gold and frankincense acknowledged the infant Jesus (Matthew 2:11).

• Prophetic echoes: “They will bring gold and frankincense and proclaim the praises of the LORD” (Isaiah 60:6).

• In Christ’s millennial and eternal reign, nations bring their glory into His city (Revelation 21:24).

Men of Wealth

“Men of wealth” highlights the highest strata of society. The psalm envisions the influential leveraging their resources for the King’s pleasure (Psalm 68:29).

• Earthly power submits to heavenly authority (Isaiah 60:3, 11).

• God’s kingdom agenda redirects wealth from self-promotion to worship and service (Luke 19:8; 1 Timothy 6:17–19).

Will Seek Your Favor

These wealthy leaders “will seek your favor.” Their status cannot be bartered; favor is sought, not presumed.

• The phrase mirrors Psalm 2:10–12, where kings are urged to “kiss the Son.”

• True greatness is found in humble approach to Christ (John 12:20–23).

• Ultimately every knee will bow (Philippians 2:9–11), yet the picture here is voluntary, joyful allegiance—the nations gladly enthroned beneath His rule.

summary

Psalm 45:12 paints a vivid scene of Gentile nobility streaming to honor God’s Anointed. Historically foreshadowed in David’s alliances, literally fulfilled in Christ’s first advent, and awaiting its fullest expression in His future reign, the verse assures us that even the wealth and influence of the world will be harnessed for the glory of the King. The Daughter of Tyre, gifts in hand, and the richest of earth, eager for His smile, all proclaim one truth: Jesus is worthy of universal homage, and His kingdom will draw every nation, resource, and heart to His feet.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) And the daughter of Tyre--i.e., Tyre itself and the Tyrians. (See Note Psalm 9:14.) Render,

The Tyrians with a gift entreat thy favour,

The rich ones of the people.

The objection that Tyre was never subject to Israel is not conclusive, since the gifts may be complimentary presents, such as Hiram sent to Solomon, not tribute. (See next Note.)

Entreat thy favour.--Literally, stroke thy face (comp. Job 11:19, Proverbs 19:6); or since the root-idea is one of polishing or making bright, we may render "makes thy face bright or joyful," i.e., with pleasure at the splendid gifts.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - And the daughter of Tyro shall be there with a gift. Heathen nations shall be attracted to Christ and his Church, shall bring their offerings, and make submission, and humbly sue for favour. Tyro is taken as a type of heathen states and cities generally (comp. Isaiah 49:18-23; Isaiah 56:6-8; Isaiah 60:3-14). Even the rich among the people shall intreat thy favour. (On the wealth of Tyre, see Isaiah 23:2-8; Ezekiel 26:12, 16; Ezekiel 27:3-33; Ezekiel 28:13, etc.)
