He waters the mountains from His chambers;
This phrase highlights God's sovereignty and provision over creation. The "chambers" refer to the heavens, indicating that God controls the natural processes from His dwelling place. In biblical context, this reflects the ancient Near Eastern understanding of the cosmos, where the heavens were seen as a divine realm. The imagery of watering the mountains suggests God's care for even the most remote and elevated parts of the earth, ensuring that life is sustained. This can be connected to other scriptures such as Job 38:25-27, where God is depicted as the one who provides rain to satisfy the desolate wasteland. Theologically, this demonstrates God's omnipotence and His intimate involvement with creation, a theme that runs throughout the Psalms.

the earth is satisfied by the fruit of His works.
This part of the verse emphasizes the abundance and sufficiency of God's provision. The "fruit of His works" refers to the results of God's creative and sustaining actions, which bring nourishment and fulfillment to the earth. This can be seen as a reflection of the creation narrative in Genesis, where God declares His creation "very good" (Genesis 1:31). The satisfaction of the earth can also be linked to the idea of God's covenant faithfulness, as seen in passages like Deuteronomy 11:13-15, where God promises to provide rain for the land in response to Israel's obedience. In a broader theological sense, this phrase can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate fulfillment of God's provision, bringing spiritual satisfaction and life to humanity (John 6:35).

What Does Psalm 104:13 Mean
He waters the mountains

Psalm 104:13 opens with the vivid statement, “He waters the mountains …”. What a picture of the Lord’s active care!

• Mountains stand where human irrigation fails, yet God’s rain reaches even those heights (Job 38:26–27; Jeremiah 14:22).

• The verse affirms that weather patterns are not random but directed by the Creator who “sends rain on the earth” (Acts 14:17).

• Because He literally waters elevated places, we can trust Him to meet every need that seems out of reach.

from His chambers

“… from His chambers …” shifts our focus to the source.

• “Chambers” points to the heavenly dwellings from which God rules (Psalm 33:13–15).

• Scripture often calls heaven God’s storehouse of waters: “At the sound of His voice He makes the clouds rise from the ends of the earth” (Jeremiah 10:13).

• He is not distant; the One who “rides in the highest heavens” (Psalm 68:33) personally releases life-giving rain.

the earth is satisfied

Next we read, “… the earth is satisfied …”. Satisfaction is the natural result of God’s provision.

• The land drinks in the showers and responds with abundance (Psalm 65:9–13).

• Creation’s full contentment mirrors God’s generous heart: “You open Your hand and satisfy the desire of every living thing” (Psalm 145:16).

• When He meets physical needs so completely, we are reminded He will also fulfill every spiritual need (Matthew 6:30, 33).

by the fruit of His works

Finally, the psalm says, “… by the fruit of His works”.

• The “fruit” is everything produced because God acted—streams, crops, forests, and all living creatures (Genesis 1:11–12).

• Nothing can boast of self-sufficiency; all flourishing springs from “every good and perfect gift from above” (James 1:17).

• The phrase underscores that God’s handiwork never runs dry: He continuously sustains the ecosystem (Acts 14:17) and, by extension, His people.

summary

Psalm 104:13 paints a seamless chain: God releases water from His heavenly chambers, the mountains receive it, and the whole earth becomes abundantly satisfied through what His hands have produced. The verse invites us to marvel at His precise, personal governance of creation and to rest in the assurance that the same Lord who waters remote peaks is fully able to provide for every aspect of our lives.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Chambers--i.e., of cloud, as in Psalm 104:3.

Thy works.--If we go by the parallelism, this means the "rain," here called God's works, as in Psalm 65:9 (see Note), his "river." Others prefer to see a general reference to the operations of nature which produce fruit.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - He (i.e. God) watereth the hills from his chambers (comp. ver. 3). The mountains themselves, even their highest tops, are not left dry. Where springs cannot reach, rain falls from God's "chambers" in the sky, and spreads equal refreshment. The earth is satisfied with the fruit of thy works. The whole earth - mountains, hills, plains, valleys - is thus "satisfied," i.e. sufficiently supplied with water, by the means which God has elaborated.
