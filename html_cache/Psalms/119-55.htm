In the night
The phrase "in the night" often symbolizes times of trouble, reflection, or solitude in the Bible. Nighttime can be a period of vulnerability and introspection, as seen in other scriptures like Psalm 63:6, where David meditates on God during the night watches. It suggests a time when distractions are minimal, allowing for deeper contemplation and prayer. Biblically, night can also represent spiritual darkness, yet it is during these times that the psalmist chooses to focus on God.

O LORD
The use of "O LORD" refers to the covenant name of God, Yahweh, emphasizing a personal and relational aspect of God. This name is significant as it denotes God's eternal presence and faithfulness to His people. The invocation of God's name in prayer or meditation highlights the psalmist's reliance on God's character and promises. It is a reminder of the covenant relationship established with Israel, as seen in Exodus 3:14-15, where God reveals His name to Moses.

I remember Your name
Remembering God's name involves recalling His attributes, deeds, and promises. In biblical times, a name was more than a label; it represented the essence and character of a person. Remembering God's name is an act of worship and trust, acknowledging His sovereignty and faithfulness. This practice is echoed in other scriptures, such as Psalm 77:11, where the psalmist remembers the works of the LORD. It is a deliberate act of focusing on God's nature and past faithfulness to inspire present obedience and trust.

that I may keep Your law
The purpose of remembering God's name is to inspire obedience to His law. The law, or Torah, represents God's instructions and commandments given to His people. Keeping the law is a response to God's covenant love and a way to live in harmony with His will. This reflects the biblical theme of obedience as a response to God's grace, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:4-9, where the Israelites are commanded to love the LORD and keep His commandments. The psalmist's desire to keep the law underscores a commitment to live a life pleasing to God, rooted in a deep relationship with Him.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 119, traditionally believed to be King David, who expresses a deep devotion to God's law and a desire to live in accordance with it.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal presence and faithfulness to His people.

3. The Night
Symbolic of times of trouble, reflection, or solitude, where the psalmist finds an opportunity to meditate on God's name and law.
Teaching Points
The Importance of God's Name
Remembering God's name signifies acknowledging His character and attributes. It is a call to recognize His sovereignty and faithfulness in our lives.

Meditation in Solitude
The night represents a time of quiet and reflection. Believers are encouraged to use moments of solitude to focus on God's word and His promises.

Obedience to God's Law
The psalmist's remembrance of God's name is directly linked to his desire to keep God's law. This highlights the connection between knowing God and living according to His commandments.

Consistency in Devotion
The practice of remembering God "in the night" suggests a consistent and ongoing relationship with Him, not limited to specific times or circumstances.

Spiritual Vigilance
In times of darkness or difficulty, recalling God's name and His law can provide guidance and reassurance, helping believers remain steadfast in their faith.

What Does Psalm 119:55 Mean
In the night

The psalmist sets the scene in darkness—both literal nighttime and the figurative “midnight” moments of life. Scripture often highlights how God meets His people when the rest of the world is quiet (Psalm 63:6; Acts 16:25). Night can be:

• A time of solitude, free from daytime distractions, when hearts grow still enough to hear God’s whisper (Psalm 42:8).

• A season of trial, yet also of worship—think of Jesus praying all night on the mountain (Luke 6:12).

By choosing to focus on the Lord during the night, the writer models vigilance and expectancy rather than anxiety.

O LORD

Addressing God by His covenant name (rendered “LORD”) anchors the verse in a personal relationship. The God who introduced Himself as “I AM” (Exodus 3:14-15) is eternally present and faithful. Invoking this name reminds us:

• His promises never lapse (Psalm 135:13).

• His character is steady, even when circumstances shift with every passing hour.

I remember Your name

Remembering here is more than mental recall; it is deliberate meditation that stirs affection and obedience. Other passages echo this practice: “When I remember You on my bed” (Psalm 63:6) and “a scroll of remembrance was written” for those who honored His name (Malachi 3:16). Practical ways to “remember” include:

• Rehearsing God’s attributes—His holiness, mercy, and power (Psalm 20:7).

• Speaking testimonies aloud, as Paul and Silas did in song (Acts 16:25).

• Filling the night hours with Scripture, allowing truth to reshape thought patterns (Joshua 1:8).

that I may keep Your law

The goal of nighttime remembrance is daytime obedience. Scripture is always pushing us from contemplation to action: “I have hidden Your word in my heart that I might not sin against You” (Psalm 119:11). Jesus echoed this rhythm: “Whoever has My commands and keeps them is the one who loves Me” (John 14:21). We keep the law when we:

• Align choices with God’s revealed will (James 1:22-25).

• Depend on His Spirit to empower faithful living (Galatians 5:16-18).

• Let worship fuel obedience, so duty flows from delight.

summary

Psalm 119:55 shows a believer leveraging the quiet of night to fix heart and mind on the covenant-keeping LORD. Remembering His name in those still hours cultivates love that overflows into resolute obedience the next day. Darkness becomes a classroom for devotion, and the result is a life that treasures and keeps God’s law.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 55. - I have remembered thy Name, O Lord, in the night (comp. Psalm 63:6; Psalm 149:5; and Job 35:10). And have kept thy Law. The night is the time when wicked men do their most wicked deeds (Job 24:14-16). I keep thy Law both night and day.
