For he will deliver the needy who cry out
This phrase highlights the king's role as a deliverer, reflecting the ideal of a righteous ruler who acts on behalf of the vulnerable. In the context of ancient Israel, kings were expected to uphold justice and protect those who could not protect themselves. This aligns with the broader biblical theme of God's concern for the poor and oppressed, as seen in passages like Isaiah 61:1, where the Messiah is described as bringing good news to the poor. The phrase also foreshadows the ministry of Jesus Christ, who frequently reached out to the marginalized and responded to their cries for help, as seen in the Gospels (e.g., Mark 10:46-52).

and the afflicted who have no helper
This part of the verse emphasizes the plight of those who are not only needy but also without any support or advocate. In the ancient Near Eastern context, being without a helper meant extreme vulnerability, as social structures were heavily reliant on family and community support. The king's responsibility to aid such individuals is a reflection of God's own character, as seen in Psalm 68:5, where God is described as a "father to the fatherless" and a "defender of widows." This also points to the messianic expectation of a ruler who embodies divine justice and compassion, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who is depicted as the ultimate helper and advocate for humanity (Hebrews 7:25).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Solomon
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 72, Solomon was the son of King David and known for his wisdom and wealth. This psalm is often seen as a prayer for his reign or a messianic prophecy.

2. The Needy and Afflicted
These are the individuals mentioned in the verse who are in distress and require deliverance. They symbolize those who are marginalized or oppressed in society.

3. The King
In the context of the psalm, the king is the one who delivers the needy. This can be seen as a reference to Solomon, but also prophetically to the Messiah, Jesus Christ, who fulfills this role perfectly.

4. Israel
The nation of Israel is the primary audience of the psalm, representing God's chosen people who often found themselves in need of divine intervention.

5. Messianic Prophecy
The psalm is often interpreted as a prophecy about the coming Messiah, who will establish a kingdom characterized by justice and righteousness.
Teaching Points
God's Heart for the Needy
God consistently shows His concern for the marginalized and oppressed. As followers of Christ, we are called to reflect His heart by actively seeking to help those in need.

The Role of Leadership
Leaders, whether in the church or society, have a responsibility to protect and deliver those who are vulnerable. This psalm challenges leaders to prioritize justice and compassion.

Messianic Fulfillment in Christ
Jesus Christ is the ultimate fulfillment of this psalm, as He delivers us from spiritual poverty and affliction. Believers can find hope and assurance in His redemptive work.

Prayer and Dependence on God
The needy cry out to God, demonstrating a posture of dependence. We are encouraged to bring our needs and the needs of others before God in prayer, trusting in His deliverance.

What Does Psalm 72:12 Mean
For He Will Deliver

• The verse begins with confidence: “For He will deliver.” The certainty rests on God’s proven character—He keeps His word (Numbers 23:19) and delights in rescuing His own (Psalm 34:17; 2 Samuel 22:2).

• “Deliver” underscores action, not mere sympathy. The Lord steps in, as He did at the Red Sea (Exodus 14:13-14) and in David’s battles (Psalm 18:17).

• This assurance threads through Scripture: “Call upon Me in the day of trouble; I will deliver you” (Psalm 50:15).

The Needy

• “The needy” points to those lacking basic resources or protection. God consistently identifies with them (Proverbs 19:17).

• When Solomon (the psalm’s author) prays for a king who champions the poor, he mirrors God’s own heart (Psalm 113:7-8; Isaiah 41:17).

• Jesus embodies this concern, feeding crowds (Mark 6:34-44) and announcing good news to the poor (Luke 4:18).

Who Cry Out

• The needy are not silent; they “cry out.” Scripture shows a pattern: distress voiced to God moves His hand (Exodus 2:23-25; Psalm 34:6).

• Honest prayer is invited: “Cast your burden upon the LORD and He will sustain you” (Psalm 55:22).

• The King in Psalm 72 responds instantly, echoing Christ’s readiness in the Gospels—touching lepers (Mark 1:40-42) and calming storms at a plea (Matthew 8:25-26).

And The Afflicted

• “Afflicted” widens the circle to include those oppressed, wounded, or downtrodden (Psalm 9:12).

• God never overlooks suffering: “He has not despised the affliction of the afflicted” (Psalm 22:24).

• The Servant-King foretold in Isaiah 61:1 “binds up the broken-hearted,” a prophecy Jesus claims for Himself (Luke 4:21).

Who Have No Helper

• Helplessness magnifies divine mercy. When no human advocate exists, God becomes the Defender (Psalm 10:14; Job 29:12).

• The psalm envisions a reign where no one is left isolated. That ideal finds ultimate fulfillment in Christ, who promises, “I will not leave you as orphans” (John 14:18).

• Believers are called to mirror this aid (James 1:27), joining God’s mission to the defenseless.

summary

Psalm 72:12 paints a portrait of the perfect King whose heart beats for the vulnerable. He decisively rescues, listens to desperate prayers, embraces the wounded, and stands in the gap when no earthly helper is found. Anchored in God’s unchanging character and ultimately realized in Jesus, this promise assures every believer that no cry for mercy goes unheard.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) For he shall deliver.--Here the verb must be present, "for he delivereth" giving the reason of the wide sway asked for this monarch. The prayer is based on the justice and beneficence of his reign ("to him that hath shall be given"), in which the weak and poor find their lives safe from violence, and their property protected against fraud. The verse is almost word for word the same as Job 29:12.

Poor.--Rather, afflicted.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - For he shall deliver the needy when he crieth; the poor also, and him that hath no helper (comp. ver. 4); rather, and the poor who has no helper. Two classes of persons are spoken of, not three (comp. Job 29:12).
