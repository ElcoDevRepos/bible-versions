A Maskil of Asaph.
The term "Maskil" refers to a type of psalm that is meant to impart wisdom or instruction. Asaph, a prominent Levite musician appointed by King David, is credited with composing this psalm. Asaph's role in the temple worship highlights the importance of music and poetry in conveying spiritual truths. The use of "Maskil" suggests that this psalm is intended to teach and provide insight, possibly reflecting on Israel's history and God's faithfulness.

Give ear, O my people, to my instruction;
This phrase is a call for attention, urging the people of Israel to listen carefully. The phrase "my people" indicates a communal address, emphasizing the collective identity of the Israelites as God's chosen people. The term "instruction" can be understood as teaching or law, reminiscent of the Torah, which is central to Jewish life and worship. This call to heed instruction is a recurring theme in wisdom literature, such as Proverbs, where listening to wisdom is equated with life and blessing.

listen to the words of my mouth.
The repetition of the call to listen underscores the importance of the message being delivered. The "words of my mouth" suggests a direct communication from God through the psalmist, akin to the prophetic tradition where God's messages are conveyed through human voices. This phrase also echoes the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4), a foundational declaration of faith in Judaism, which begins with a call to hear. The emphasis on listening is a reminder of the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where obedience to God's word is paramount.

Persons / Places / Events

The author of Psalm 78, Asaph was a prominent Levite musician appointed by King David to lead worship in the tabernacle. He is known for his role in composing several psalms.

The primary audience of the psalm, representing God's chosen people who are called to listen and learn from their history and God's instructions.

The psalm begins with a call to heed instruction, emphasizing the importance of teaching and learning from past experiences and divine guidance.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Listening
Listening is a fundamental aspect of spiritual growth. Just as the Israelites were called to listen to God's instruction, we too must be attentive to His Word in our daily lives.

Learning from History
Psalm 78 serves as a reminder to learn from the past. Reflecting on the history of God's people helps us understand His faithfulness and the consequences of disobedience.

Passing Down Wisdom
The psalm underscores the responsibility of passing down God's teachings to future generations. We are called to be diligent in teaching our children and others about God's works and commandments.

Active Engagement with Scripture
Engaging actively with Scripture involves more than just reading; it requires listening, understanding, and applying God's Word to our lives.

What Does Psalm 78:1 Mean
A Maskil of Asaph.

- This short superscription tells us three things:

• The psalm is meant to be a “Maskil,” a thoughtful, instructive composition designed to impart wisdom, much like Psalm 32 or 89.

• Asaph, a chief musician appointed by David (1 Chron 15:17, 19; 2 Chron 29:30), is its author or compiler, signaling both prophetic authority and musical excellence.

• The opening signals that what follows is not mere poetry but Spirit-breathed teaching (2 Timothy 3:16). Just as Moses used song to teach Israel (Deuteronomy 31:19), Asaph will employ melody and story to impress truth on the heart.

Give ear, O my people, to my instruction;

- “Give ear” is a covenant summons—God speaks, His people must listen (Deuteronomy 6:4; Isaiah 51:4).

- “My people” reminds Israel of their chosen status (Exodus 19:5-6), heightening both privilege and responsibility.

- “Instruction” (torah, teaching) is not optional advice; it carries the full weight of divine command (Psalm 19:7).

- Practical implications:

• Listening precedes obedience (James 1:22).

• The call is corporate: families, tribes, generations together hear and obey (Psalm 78:5-6).

• Neglecting the Word invites the very forgetfulness and rebellion this psalm will recount (Judges 2:10).

listen to the words of my mouth.

- The parallel line intensifies the appeal: active, sustained attention is required (Proverbs 4:20-22).

- “Words of my mouth” points to direct, personal communication from the Lord—fresh, living speech (Isaiah 55:11; Hebrews 4:12).

- Throughout Scripture, blessing follows those who hear and keep God’s words (Luke 11:28; Revelation 1:3).

- Everyday application:

• Set aside distractions and lean in—ears, heart, and will.

• Expect God to speak through recorded history, turning past events into present counsel (1 Corinthians 10:11).

• Let His words shape family conversations, teaching the next generation diligently (Deuteronomy 6:7).

summary

Psalm 78:1 opens with a musical heading that signals thoughtful instruction from Asaph. God then calls His covenant people to tune in: “Give ear… listen….” The verse establishes the urgent tone for the entire psalm—if Israel will heed God’s authoritative, life-giving words, they will walk in covenant faithfulness; if they ignore them, they will repeat the failures of their forefathers. The same call reaches us today: cherish Scripture as the living voice of God, listen attentively, and obey wholeheartedly.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 1-8. - The introduction calls special attention to the teaching that is about to be put forth, which it declares to be traditional (ver. 3), and, further, to be the sort of instruction which God had especially commanded to be given to his people by their teachers (vers. 5, 6) for their edification (vers. 7, 8). Verse 1. - Give ear, O my people, to my law; rather, to my teaching. Hat-torah - torah with the article - is "the Law;" but torah alone is any teaching or instruction. Incline your ears to the words of my mouth. Dr. Kay regards the words of ver. 1 as "God's own words,"

(1) on account of the expression, "O my people;" and

(2) on account of "my Law." But "my people" is not inappropriate in the mouth of a psalmist, and occurs in Psalm 59:11 and Psalms 144:2. It "indicates the love in which the effort of the psalmist originated" (Hengstenberg). And "law," as already observed, is not the proper, or at any rate not the only, meaning of torah.
