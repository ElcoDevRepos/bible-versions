Let them praise
This phrase is a call to worship, inviting all people to acknowledge and honor God. In the context of ancient Israel, praise was often expressed through song, dance, and sacrifices. The Psalms frequently emphasize the importance of communal worship, reflecting the cultural and religious practices of the time. This call to praise is not limited to Israel but extends to all nations, highlighting the universal sovereignty of God.

Your great and awesome name
The "name" of God in Hebrew culture represents His character and essence. The use of "great and awesome" underscores God's majesty and power. In biblical times, names held significant meaning, often reflecting the nature or destiny of a person. God's name is revered and feared, as seen in Exodus 3:14 where God reveals Himself as "I AM WHO I AM," indicating His eternal and self-existent nature. The reverence for God's name is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, emphasizing His holiness and authority.

He is holy!
The holiness of God is a central theme in the Bible, signifying His purity, separateness, and moral perfection. In the context of the Old Testament, holiness is associated with God's covenant relationship with Israel, where He sets them apart as His chosen people. The declaration of God's holiness is a reminder of His transcendence and the call for His people to be holy as He is holy (Leviticus 19:2). This phrase also points to the ultimate fulfillment of holiness in Jesus Christ, who embodies the perfect holiness of God and calls His followers to live holy lives.

Persons / Places / Events
1. God (Yahweh)
The central figure in this verse, whose name is described as great and awesome. The focus is on His holiness and the reverence due to Him.

2. Israel
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of Psalm 99 is a call to the people of Israel to worship and acknowledge God's holiness.

3. The Temple in Jerusalem
The place where God's presence was uniquely manifested and where His holiness was particularly revered.
Teaching Points
The Holiness of God
God's holiness is central to His character. It sets Him apart from all creation and demands our reverence and worship.

The Power of God's Name
The name of God is not just a label but a representation of His character and authority. We are called to honor and revere His name in our words and actions.

Worship as a Response to Holiness
True worship arises from recognizing God's holiness. Our praise should be a response to His greatness and majesty.

Living in Awe of God
Acknowledging God's holiness should lead us to live in awe and reverence, influencing our daily decisions and interactions.

Community Worship
The call to praise God's name is communal. As believers, we are encouraged to join together in worship, recognizing His holiness collectively.

What Does Psalm 99:3 Mean
Let them praise

- The psalmist invites every listener—Israel and the nations alike—to celebrate God together. Similar invitations resound in Psalm 67:3, “Let the peoples praise You, O God; let all the peoples praise You”, and again in Revelation 7:9, where a “multitude from every nation” stands before the throne.

- Praise is not optional; it is the fitting response to God’s rule proclaimed in Psalm 99:1-2. When we praise, we align our hearts with the heavenly chorus of Psalm 148 and echo Jesus’ exhortation in Luke 19:40 that even stones would cry out if people were silent.

- Practically, praise reorients us from self-focus to God-focus, strengthens faith like Paul and Silas’ midnight worship in Acts 16:25, and equips us for spiritual battle as in 2 Chronicles 20:21-22.

Your great and awesome name

- “Name” in Scripture stands for God’s revealed character. Exodus 3:14 shows Him as the “I AM,” declaring His eternal, self-existent nature. Solomon admitted in 1 Kings 8:27 that even heaven cannot contain Him, yet His name dwells among His people.

- The psalmist stacks the adjectives—great and awesome—because ordinary language cannot capture God’s magnitude (Psalm 145:3). Moses used the same pairing in Deuteronomy 10:17 to portray the LORD as “the great, the mighty, and the awesome God.”

- God’s name inspires reverence (Proverbs 18:10), worship (Psalm 8:1), and mission, as Jesus taught us to pray, “Hallowed be Your name” (Matthew 6:9). Because His name is great, our lives should bear it well (Colossians 3:17), avoiding any misuse (Exodus 20:7).

He is holy!

- Holiness means God is utterly set apart—morally perfect and infinitely pure. Isaiah 6:3 and Revelation 4:8 frame heaven’s unending song: “Holy, holy, holy.”

- God’s holiness exposes sin (Habakkuk 1:13) but also offers hope, for the same Holy One provided atonement through the cross (Hebrews 10:19-22).

- Believers are called to mirror this holiness: “Be holy, because I am holy” (1 Peter 1:16). The gospel empowers that transformation (Ephesians 1:4; 1 Thessalonians 4:7).

- Holiness fuels worship. When we glimpse God’s purity, we respond like John in Revelation 1:17, falling before Him, and like David in Psalm 51, seeking cleansing.

summary

Psalm 99:3 summons every heart to declare loud praise, because God’s name reveals His unrivaled greatness, and His essence is blinding holiness. Acknowledging these truths lifts our focus from ourselves to the King who reigns forever, calls us to live in reverent obedience, and fills us with joy that the Holy One invites us into His presence through Christ.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) Great and terrible name.--The rabbins see here the mystic tetragrammaton, whose pronunciation was kept so secret.

For it is holy.--This is grammatically possible, but as Psalm 99:5; Psalm 99:9 repeat the expression, evidently as a refrain, and there it needs the masculine, it is better to read here, "Holy is He."

In this way, too, we avoid an awkward construction in the next verse, which should be joined closely with this: Let them praise Thy great and terrible name (saying), "Holy is He, and mighty, a king that loveth justice."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Let them praise thy great and terrible Name. Even the Gentiles, after conversion, will praise the Lord, sing of him, and bless his Name. (On the "greatness" and "terribleness" of God, see Exodus 15:11; Deuteronomy 7:21; Deuteronomy 10:17; Nehemiah 1:5; Nehemiah 4:14; Psalm 68:35, etc.) For it is holy; rather, holy is he. This may be a suggestion to those about to praise God - a putting of words into their mouth; or it may be an almost involuntary outburst of praise on the part of the psalmist.
