He rejected the tent of Joseph
This phrase refers to God's decision to move His favor away from the descendants of Joseph, specifically the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh, who were Joseph's sons. Historically, the tribe of Ephraim held a prominent position among the tribes of Israel, often leading in battles and religious matters. The "tent" symbolizes the dwelling place or the center of worship and leadership. This rejection signifies a shift in divine favor and leadership from Ephraim to another tribe. Theologically, this reflects God's sovereignty in choosing whom He will bless and use for His purposes. The rejection of Ephraim is a reminder of the consequences of disobedience and unfaithfulness, as Ephraim had often been associated with idolatry and rebellion against God (Hosea 4:17).

and refused the tribe of Ephraim.
Ephraim was one of the most influential tribes in the northern kingdom of Israel, often representing the entire northern kingdom. The refusal of Ephraim indicates a significant shift in God's plan for His people. This decision is historically linked to the division of the united monarchy after Solomon's reign, where the northern tribes, led by Ephraim, separated from Judah. Theologically, this refusal can be seen as a fulfillment of God's judgment due to the tribe's persistent idolatry and failure to follow God's commandments. This shift also sets the stage for the rise of the tribe of Judah, from which King David and ultimately Jesus Christ would come, fulfilling the prophecy of the Messiah from the line of David (2 Samuel 7:12-16). The rejection of Ephraim and the choice of Judah highlight the theme of God's redemptive plan through a chosen lineage, culminating in the coming of Christ.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Joseph
One of the twelve sons of Jacob, Joseph was a key figure in the history of Israel. His descendants formed two tribes, Ephraim and Manasseh, which were part of the Northern Kingdom of Israel.

2. Ephraim
A tribe of Israel descended from Joseph's son Ephraim. It was one of the most prominent tribes in the Northern Kingdom and often represented the whole of Israel in biblical texts.

3. Rejection
This refers to God's decision to choose the tribe of Judah over Ephraim for leadership and the establishment of His sanctuary, as seen in the context of Psalm 78.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereign Choice
God's decisions are based on His divine wisdom and purpose, not human expectations. He chose Judah over Ephraim for His own reasons, reminding us to trust in His sovereign will.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Ephraim's rejection serves as a warning about the consequences of disobedience and unfaithfulness. We are called to remain faithful to God to avoid similar outcomes.

The Importance of Spiritual Leadership
The shift from Ephraim to Judah highlights the importance of godly leadership. We should pray for and support leaders who seek to follow God's heart.

God's Faithfulness to His Promises
Despite the rejection of Ephraim, God remained faithful to His covenant with Israel. This assures us of His unwavering faithfulness to His promises.

What Does Psalm 78:67 Mean
He rejected the tent of Joseph

• The “tent” recalls the tabernacle that once rested at Shiloh in Joseph’s territory (Joshua 18:1; Psalm 78:60). Though God had honored this place, persistent unbelief—seen in idolatry, grumbling, and treating the ark as a lucky charm (1 Samuel 4:3-11)—led Him to withdraw His special presence.

• “Rejected” shows decisive divine action. The Lord did not merely step back; He judged covenant unfaithfulness, just as He warned in Deuteronomy 31:16-18.

• By naming Joseph, the verse embraces the northern tribes descended from him (Ephraim and Manasseh). Their privileged position could not shield them from accountability (Amos 3:2).

• The exile of the ark, the fall of Shiloh, and later the Assyrian captivity (2 Kings 17:6-18) all stand as historical markers that God keeps His word—both promises and warnings.

and refused the tribe of Ephraim

• Ephraim, the dominant northern tribe, had enjoyed leadership since Jacob’s blessing (Genesis 48:19). Judges 8:1-3 and 12:1-6 show its pride in that role.

• “Refused” underscores that privilege can be forfeited. Hosea 4:17 laments, “Ephraim is joined to idols; leave him alone!” Persistent rebellion brought God’s refusal.

• Spiritually, Ephraim’s story warns that proximity to sacred things never replaces obedience (Jeremiah 7:12-14). Religiosity without faithfulness invites removal of blessing.

• God’s choice moved southward: “He chose the tribe of Judah” (Psalm 78:68). The shift prepared the way for David’s kingship (1 Samuel 16:1-13) and ultimately the Messiah (Micah 5:2), showing God’s sovereign freedom to reassign roles for His redemptive plan.

summary

Psalm 78:67 teaches that God’s blessings are real yet conditional on covenant faithfulness. He once honored Joseph’s line and Ephraim’s leadership, but unrepentant sin brought rejection and refusal. The verse reminds us that divine privilege is a stewardship, not a guarantee, and that God will relocate His work to fulfill His purposes through obedient people.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 67. - Moreover he refused the tabernacle of Joseph. The "tabernacle of Joseph" is the sanctuary at Shiloh, which was north of Bethel, and thus within the limits of the tribe of Ephraim. When a permanent site was to be assigned to the tabernacle and the ark, God did not choose for them the position of Shiloh, but that of Jerusalem. And chose not the tribe of Ephraim. Ephraim had enjoyed the pre-eminency from the time of the death of Moses (see the comment on ver. 9). By the course of events between Samuel's death and the establishment of the kingdom of David, the pre-eminency had been transferred to Judah, according to the design of the Almighty from the first (see Genesis 49:8-10).
