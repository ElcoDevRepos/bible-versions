I hate those who cling to worthless idols
This phrase reflects a strong rejection of idolatry, which is a recurring theme throughout the Bible. Idolatry was prevalent in the ancient Near East, where nations worshiped gods represented by physical idols. The Hebrew word for "hate" here implies a deep aversion and rejection, aligning with God's commandment against idolatry (Exodus 20:3-5). The term "worthless idols" underscores the futility and emptiness of worshiping anything other than the one true God. This sentiment is echoed in other scriptures, such as Isaiah 44:9-20, which mocks the absurdity of idol worship. The psalmist's stance is a call to purity and faithfulness to God, rejecting the cultural norms of surrounding nations.

but in the LORD I trust
This phrase contrasts the futility of idols with the reliability of the LORD, emphasizing a personal and covenantal relationship with God. Trust in the LORD is a central theme in the Psalms and throughout the Bible, highlighting faith as reliance on God's character and promises. The use of "LORD" (YHWH) signifies the covenant name of God, reminding the reader of His faithfulness and sovereignty. This trust is not just intellectual assent but involves a deep, personal reliance on God, as seen in Proverbs 3:5-6. The psalmist's declaration is a model of faith, pointing to the ultimate trust exemplified by Jesus Christ, who perfectly trusted the Father even unto death (Luke 23:46).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The author of Psalm 31, David is expressing his trust in God amidst adversity. He contrasts his faith with the idolatry of others.

2. Idolaters
Those who "cling to worthless idols," representing people who place their trust in false gods or material things rather than the one true God.

3. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His faithfulness and trustworthiness as opposed to the futility of idols.
Teaching Points
The Futility of Idolatry
Idols, whether physical or metaphorical, are ultimately worthless and cannot provide the security or fulfillment that only God can offer.

Trust in the LORD
True security and peace come from placing our trust in God, who is faithful and sovereign over all circumstances.

Exclusive Devotion
Our relationship with God requires exclusive devotion, rejecting anything that competes for our allegiance.

Discernment in Worship
Believers must be vigilant in identifying modern-day idols in their lives, such as money, power, or status, and turn their hearts fully to God.

The Example of David
David's life demonstrates the importance of trusting God even when surrounded by those who do not share the same faith.

What Does Psalm 31:6 Mean
I hate those who cling to worthless idols

“​I hate those who cling to worthless idols” (Psalm 31:6a)

• David expresses moral revulsion, not personal spite. He rejects the practice, loyalties, and worldview that elevate false gods above the true God (Psalm 97:10; Romans 12:9).

• The word “worthless” underscores the futility of idols—hand-made objects or ideas that cannot save (Isaiah 44:9–20; 1 Samuel 12:21).

• “Cling” pictures stubborn attachment. Idolatry is more than a casual mistake; it’s a deliberate grip on deception (Jeremiah 2:5, 13).

• Hating idolatry aligns the heart with God’s own hatred of false worship (Exodus 20:3–5). Healthy spiritual life includes decisive rejection of anything that competes with the Lord (1 John 5:21).

but in the LORD I trust

“​but in the LORD I trust” (Psalm 31:6b)

• David contrasts two allegiances: idols versus the LORD (Yahweh), the covenant-keeping God (Deuteronomy 6:4–5; Joshua 24:15).

• Trust is personal dependence: resting in God’s character, promises, and power (Psalm 9:10; Proverbs 3:5–6).

• The shift from “they” to “I” highlights individual responsibility. Regardless of cultural trends, each believer must choose wholehearted reliance on the Lord (2 Kings 18:5–6; Hebrews 11:6).

• Trusting God brings protection and deliverance that idols can never provide (Psalm 115:4–11; Jonah 2:8–9).

• Confidence in the LORD fuels worship and joyful obedience (Psalm 31:7–8; Philippians 4:4–7).

summary

David draws a clear line: he utterly rejects the empty security of idols and commits himself to the living God. True spiritual life involves both a decisive break with false worship and an active, personal trust in the LORD who alone is worthy.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Lying vanities.--Literally, breath of lies (Jonah 2:8), undoubtedly idols, as the parallelism in Jeremiah 8:19 shows. It was the term adopted by the Deuteronomist (Deuteronomy 32:21) and apparently brought into use by him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - I have hated them that regard lying vanities. By "lying vanities" are meant idolatrous practices, or possibly such superstitious usages as recourse to witch-craft and divination. (For David's hatred of such persons as are here glanced at, see Psalm 26:5.) But I trust in the Lord. Who is the direct opposite of all "lying vanities," being at once Almighty, and the "God of truth" (ver. 5).
