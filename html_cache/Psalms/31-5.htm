Into Your hands I commit my spirit;
This phrase reflects a deep trust in God, often associated with surrender and reliance on divine protection. It is famously echoed by Jesus on the cross in Luke 23:46, highlighting its messianic significance. The act of committing one's spirit to God signifies a complete entrustment of one's life and soul to the Creator, acknowledging His sovereignty and care. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, hands symbolize power and authority, indicating a transfer of control to God. This phrase also resonates with the Jewish practice of reciting prayers of trust and protection, especially in times of distress.

You have redeemed me,
Redemption in the biblical sense involves deliverance from danger or bondage, often through a price or sacrifice. This phrase acknowledges God's past acts of salvation and deliverance, which are central themes throughout the Old Testament. The concept of redemption is deeply rooted in the Exodus narrative, where God redeems Israel from slavery in Egypt. It also foreshadows the ultimate redemption through Jesus Christ, who is seen as the Redeemer of humanity. The use of the past tense "have redeemed" suggests a confidence in God's faithfulness and His ongoing role as a deliverer.

O LORD, God of truth.
The use of "LORD" (Yahweh) emphasizes the covenantal relationship between God and His people, underscoring His eternal and unchanging nature. "God of truth" highlights God's faithfulness and reliability, contrasting with the deceitfulness of human nature and false gods. In the biblical context, truth is not merely factual accuracy but encompasses faithfulness, reliability, and moral integrity. This phrase reassures believers of God's trustworthy character, encouraging them to rely on His promises. The acknowledgment of God as the "God of truth" aligns with the broader biblical narrative that portrays God as the ultimate source of truth and righteousness.

Teaching Points
Trust in God's Sovereignty
David's commitment of his spirit to God is an act of profound trust. Believers are encouraged to entrust their lives to God's sovereign care, especially in times of distress.

Understanding Redemption
Recognize that redemption is a central theme in the Bible, fulfilled ultimately in Jesus Christ. Reflect on how God has redeemed you personally and how this impacts your daily life.

God of Truth
Emphasize the importance of knowing God as the "God of truth." This knowledge should lead to a life of integrity and faithfulness, mirroring God's character.

Following Christ's Example
Jesus' use of this verse on the cross serves as a model for believers to follow, showing that even in suffering, we can trust in God's plan and purpose.

Living with Eternal Perspective
Like David, believers are called to live with an eternal perspective, committing their spirit to God and trusting in His ultimate redemption and truth.

What Does Psalm 31:5 Mean
Into Your hands

- David opens with a vivid picture of safety: the Almighty’s hands. When Scripture speaks of God’s hands, it highlights His personal involvement and sovereign power (Deuteronomy 33:27).

- For anyone who trusts Him, those hands are a place of refuge (Isaiah 41:10) and security no one can breach (John 10:28-29).

- By addressing God directly—“Your hands”—David reminds us that true protection is never abstract. It is relational, anchored in the character of the One who holds us.

I commit my spirit

- “Commit” is an intentional, conscious act. David is not forced; he willingly entrusts his very life-breath to God.

- Jesus echoed these exact words on the cross (Luke 23:46), modeling perfect trust in the Father even in death. Stephen followed the same pattern when facing martyrdom (Acts 7:59).

- For believers today, committing our spirit is a daily declaration: “I belong to You—my past, present, and future” (2 Timothy 1:12; Galatians 2:20).

You have redeemed me

- Redemption speaks of a price paid to set someone free. David looks back to God’s faithfulness: the Exodus (Exodus 6:6) and countless personal rescues.

- Prophetically, these words anticipate the greater redemption accomplished by Christ’s blood (1 Peter 1:18-19; Ephesians 1:7).

- Because redemption is already accomplished—“You have redeemed me”—we approach God from a place of gratitude, not uncertainty (Colossians 1:13-14; Isaiah 43:1).

O LORD, God of truth

- “LORD” (Yahweh) is God’s covenant name, assuring us He never abandons His own (Exodus 34:6).

- “God of truth” underscores His absolute reliability (Deuteronomy 32:4; Numbers 23:19). What He promises, He performs; what He says, stands forever (Psalm 33:4).

- Jesus embodies this same truth, declaring, “I am the way and the truth and the life” (John 14:6), and Titus 1:2 confirms God “cannot lie.” Our faith rests on a foundation that cannot crumble.

summary

Psalm 31:5 is a confession of total trust. With confidence in God’s powerful hands, we willingly place our lives in His care, assured of redemption already won and anchored in the unchanging truthfulness of the LORD.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) I commit.--Most memorable, even among expressions of the Psalms, as the dying words of our Lord Himself (Luke 23:46), and a long line of Christian worthies. Polycarp, Bernard, Huss, Henry V., Jerome of Prague, Luther, Melancthon, are some of the many who have passed away comforted and upheld by the psalmist's expression of trust. But death was not in his thought, it was in life, amid its troubles and dangers, that he trusted (Hebrew, deposited as a trust) his spirit (r-ach, comp. Isaiah 38:16) to God. But the gift brought to the altar by the seer of old, has been consecrated anew and yet anew.

Lord God of truth.--Comp. 2Chronicles 15:3, where, as here, there is a contrast between Jehovah and idols; but also, as in Deuteronomy 32:4, the "faithful God."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Into thine hand I commit my spirit. Our Lord's adoption of these words, and application of them to himself and his own departure from earth, have given them a special sacredness beyond that which attaches to Scripture generally. At the same time, they have impressed on them a new meaning, since David was not thinking of a final committal of his soul, as distinct from his body, into the hands of the Creator, but only intended solemnly to commit himself, both soul and body, into the Divine keeping, to be preserved from the attacks of his enemies. Thou hast redeemed me, O Lord God of truth; or, thou hast delivered me, O Lord God of truth. It is redemption in the general sense of "deliverance from peril," not redemption from sin, of which the psalmist speaks. David, having frequently experienced such deliverance in the past, is emboldened to expect now another deliverance.
