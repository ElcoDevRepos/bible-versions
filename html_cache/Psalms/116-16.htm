Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Truly, O LORD, I am Your servant;
This phrase emphasizes the psalmist's acknowledgment of his relationship with God as one of servitude and devotion. In the ancient Near Eastern context, a servant was someone who was wholly dedicated to their master's will. This reflects a deep commitment and submission to God's authority. The repetition of "I am Your servant" underscores the psalmist's sincerity and total dedication. Biblically, servanthood is a recurring theme, with figures like Moses, David, and the prophets often described as servants of the Lord. This phrase also prefigures the ultimate Servant, Jesus Christ, who perfectly fulfilled the will of the Father (Philippians 2:7).

I am Your servant, the son of Your maidservant;
The mention of being "the son of Your maidservant" suggests a generational faithfulness and a heritage of serving God. In biblical times, family lineage and heritage were significant, and being the son of a maidservant of the Lord implies a legacy of devotion. This could also indicate humility, as the psalmist identifies with a lowly position, much like Mary, the mother of Jesus, who referred to herself as the Lord's servant (Luke 1:38). This connection to a faithful lineage can be seen in the lives of Timothy, whose faith was influenced by his mother and grandmother (2 Timothy 1:5).

You have broken my bonds.
This phrase speaks to the deliverance and freedom granted by God. In a historical context, bonds could refer to literal chains or metaphorical constraints such as sin, oppression, or distress. The breaking of bonds signifies liberation and redemption, themes central to the biblical narrative. This deliverance is a type of the salvation offered through Jesus Christ, who proclaimed freedom for the captives (Luke 4:18). The imagery of breaking bonds also resonates with the Exodus story, where God delivered the Israelites from slavery in Egypt, symbolizing spiritual liberation for believers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 116, traditionally attributed to King David, though the specific identity is not definitively known. The psalmist expresses deep gratitude and devotion to God.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal, self-existent nature and His relationship with His people.

3. The Maidservant
Refers to the psalmist's mother, indicating a heritage of faith and service to God.

4. Chains
Symbolic of bondage or affliction, from which the psalmist has been delivered by God.
Teaching Points
Identity in Service
As believers, our identity is rooted in being servants of God. This service is not burdensome but a response to His love and deliverance.

Heritage of Faith
The mention of the "son of Your maidservant" highlights the importance of a godly heritage and the influence of faithful parents in nurturing a relationship with God.

Freedom in Christ
True freedom is found in Christ, who breaks the chains of sin and bondage. Our response should be one of gratitude and commitment to serve Him.

Gratitude for Deliverance
Reflect on the ways God has delivered us from our own "chains" and respond with a heart of thanksgiving and service.

Commitment to God
Like the psalmist, we are called to reaffirm our commitment to God, recognizing His lordship and our role as His servants.

What Does Psalm 116:16 Mean
Truly, O LORD,

• The psalmist begins with a wholehearted affirmation—“Truly.” No hesitation, no exaggeration; just plain, honest devotion (see Psalm 86:12, “I will praise You, O Lord my God, with all my heart”).

• Addressing God by His covenant name (“LORD”) signals personal relationship rather than distant formality. Compare Psalm 116:1, “I love the LORD, for He has heard my voice,” and Exodus 3:15, where the same name guarantees steadfast faithfulness.

• Genuine faith always speaks up. Psalm 116:10 says, “I believed, therefore I said,” echoing 2 Corinthians 4:13, where Paul quotes this very verse to describe authentic, spoken-out trust.

I am Your servant;

• Declaring servanthood twice in the verse underscores joyful submission. Psalm 119:125 voices a similar plea: “I am Your servant; give me understanding.”

• Biblical servanthood is covenant privilege, not drudgery. Exodus 21:5–6 describes a bond-servant who chooses lifelong service because he loves his master.

• Jesus models this heart in Philippians 2:7, “taking the very nature of a servant.” When we echo the psalmist, we align with Christ Himself.

I am Your servant, the son of Your maidservant;

• By adding “the son of Your maidservant,” the writer highlights a heritage of faith—a mother who served the LORD before him. This mirrors Timothy’s lineage in 2 Timothy 1:5, where Paul notes the sincere faith first in Lois and Eunice.

• God delights in generational loyalty (Psalm 103:17: “the loving devotion of the LORD is from everlasting to everlasting on those who fear Him, and His righteousness to their children’s children”).

• Mentioning the mother also conveys humility: even with noble lineage, the speaker identifies solely as God’s servant. No entitlement, just gratitude.

You have broken my bonds.

• The psalmist looks back on God’s rescue. Earlier he cried, “The cords of death encompassed me” (Psalm 116:3), but now he testifies, “You have broken my bonds.”

• Such deliverance foreshadows Christ’s liberation. Jesus repeats Isaiah 61:1 in Luke 4:18, proclaiming freedom for captives. John 8:36 assures, “So if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed.”

• God’s intervention turns servants into joyful worshipers (Psalm 107:13–16 tells how He “broke their chains in pieces”). Gratitude fuels continued obedience.

summary

Psalm 116:16 captures a believer’s heartfelt response to God’s saving power: sincere devotion to the covenant LORD, willing servanthood rooted in family faith, and joyful freedom born of divine deliverance. The verse invites every redeemed soul to echo, “Truly, O LORD, I am Your servant… You have broken my bonds.”

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) Thy servant, and the son of Thine handmaid.--Comp. Psalm 86:16. Not only himself but his family were in the covenant, and, as very commonly in the East, the mother is selected for mention instead of the father.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - O Lord, truly I am thy servant; rather, even so, O Lord, for I am thy servant. Entitled, therefore, to thy care and consideration. I am thy servant, and the son of thine handmaid (comp. Psalm 86:16). "Thine handmaid" - the Church; or, if the writer is Hezekiah, "thy handmaid, Abiyah, the daughter of Zechariah," who "had under standing in the vision of God" (2 Chronicles 26:5; 2 Chronicles 29:1). Thou hast loosed my bonds. The "cords of death" (ver. 3) are probably intended.
