May their eyes be darkened so they cannot see
This phrase is a plea for divine judgment against the psalmist's enemies. The imagery of darkened eyes suggests a spiritual blindness, a common biblical metaphor for those who are unable to perceive God's truth. In the context of Psalm 69, David is calling for justice against those who oppose him, reflecting a broader theme of divine retribution found throughout the Psalms. This concept of spiritual blindness is echoed in the New Testament, particularly in Romans 11:8-10, where Paul references this psalm to describe Israel's hardening. The idea of eyes being darkened can also be seen as a type of Christ, who often spoke of those who have eyes but do not see, highlighting the spiritual blindness of those who rejected Him.

and their backs be bent forever
This phrase conveys a sense of perpetual suffering and subjugation. The imagery of bent backs suggests a burden or oppression, possibly alluding to the physical and spiritual consequences of sin. In the historical context, a bent back could symbolize the labor and toil of slavery or servitude, which was a reality for many in ancient times. This imagery is also prophetic, as it points to the ultimate judgment and separation from God for those who persist in rebellion against Him. The concept of a bent back can be connected to the New Testament, where Jesus offers rest to those who are weary and burdened (Matthew 11:28-30), contrasting the freedom found in Christ with the bondage of sin.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 69, David is expressing deep distress and calling for divine intervention against his enemies.

2. Enemies of David
The individuals or groups who oppose David, representing those who oppose God's anointed and, by extension, God Himself.

3. Israel
The nation often seen as the context for David's laments and prayers, representing God's chosen people.

4. Messianic Prophecy
Psalm 69 is often seen as a prophetic psalm, with elements that are later applied to Jesus Christ in the New Testament.

5. Divine Judgment
The event or process by which God enacts justice, often seen in the imprecatory nature of this psalm.
Teaching Points
Spiritual Blindness
This verse highlights the danger of spiritual blindness, a condition where individuals are unable to perceive or understand God's truth. It serves as a warning to remain open and receptive to God's Word.

Consequences of Rejection
The imagery of "backs bent forever" symbolizes the burden and consequences of rejecting God. It reminds believers of the importance of aligning with God's will to avoid spiritual and moral burdens.

Imprecatory Prayer
While difficult, imprecatory prayers like this one remind us of the reality of divine justice. Believers are encouraged to trust in God's righteous judgment rather than seeking personal vengeance.

Call to Repentance
The verse serves as a call to repentance, urging individuals to turn from spiritual blindness and seek God's light and truth.

Hope in Christ
Despite the harshness of the judgment, there is hope in Christ, who offers sight to the blind and rest to the weary. Believers are encouraged to find solace and redemption in Him.

What Does Psalm 69:23 Mean
May their eyes be darkened

• David, under the Spirit’s inspiration, calls down God’s just judgment on those who hate and persecute the righteous (Psalm 69:4).

• “Eyes” symbolize perception. When God withdraws light, the result is real, spiritual blindness. Compare Deuteronomy 28:28–29, where covenant breakers are struck with “blindness” and “grop[e] at noon,” and 2 Kings 6:18, where Elisha prays, “Strike this people with blindness.”

• Isaiah 6:9–10 and John 12:40 show the same principle: a hard-hearted people can be given over to blindness they have chosen.

so they cannot see

• The purpose clause intensifies the plea: judgment is not partial or symbolic; it is effective. They will be unable to discern truth or plot further evil.

• Paul quotes this line in Romans 11:9–10, applying it to Israel’s unbelieving majority: their rejection of Christ results in God allowing their hearts to grow dull “until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in.”

• Proverbs 4:19 observes that “the way of the wicked is like deep darkness; they do not know what makes them stumble,” echoing the inevitable consequence of refusing light.

and their backs be bent forever

• A bent back pictures continual burden, bondage, and humiliation. Leviticus 26:13 contrasts God’s saving intent—“I broke the bars of your yoke and enabled you to walk upright”—with the curse invoked here.

• The permanence of “forever” underscores the seriousness of persistent rebellion. Psalm 129:3–4 describes furrows plowed on the backs of the oppressed; here the oppressors themselves reap that fate.

• Luke 13:11–16 records Jesus straightening a woman “bent over” by Satan; deliverance is possible, yet those who resist the Lord remain bowed beneath judgment.

summary

Psalm 69:23 asks God to impose fitting, lasting consequences on hardened enemies of His kingdom: spiritual blindness replacing sight, and enduring bondage replacing prideful strength. The verse affirms that rejecting divine light invites real, tangible judgment—yet it also highlights the holy consistency of God, who gives every person the fruit of the path they choose.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Their eyes.--The darkened eyes and trembling limbs (comp. Nahum 2:10; Daniel 5:6) are expressive of terror and dismay.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - Let their eyes be darkened, that they see not. This may be taken either literally, "let blindness come upon those who have so misused their eyes;" or metaphorically, "let their understandings, which they have partially blinded, be wholly darkened." And make their loins continually to shake. Deprive them of the strength whereof they have boasted, and which they have misapplied.
