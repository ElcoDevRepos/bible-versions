They band together against the righteous
This phrase highlights the collective opposition faced by the righteous, often seen throughout biblical history. The righteous, those who live according to God's laws, frequently encounter resistance from those who oppose divine principles. This opposition can be seen in the story of Daniel, who faced conspiracies from his peers (Daniel 6:4-9). The phrase also reflects the broader theme of spiritual warfare, where forces of evil unite against God's people. In the New Testament, Jesus warned His followers of persecution (John 15:18-20), indicating that such opposition is a recurring challenge for believers.

and condemn the innocent to death.
This part of the verse underscores the grave injustice faced by the innocent, a theme prevalent in both the Old and New Testaments. The condemnation of the innocent is a direct violation of God's justice, as seen in the wrongful execution of Naboth (1 Kings 21) and the trial of Jesus Christ, who was sinless yet condemned to death (Matthew 27:24-26). This phrase also points to the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus, the innocent Lamb of God, who was condemned to bring salvation to humanity (Isaiah 53:7-8). The cultural context of ancient Israel, where legal systems were often manipulated by the powerful, further illustrates the vulnerability of the innocent. This verse serves as a reminder of God's ultimate justice and the hope of vindication for the righteous.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Righteous
Refers to those who live according to God's laws and seek to follow His will. In the context of the Psalms, the righteous are often those who are oppressed or persecuted by the wicked.

2. The Wicked
Those who oppose God's ways and often seek to harm the righteous. In this verse, they are described as banding together to carry out injustice.

3. The Innocent
Individuals who are blameless or without guilt, yet find themselves condemned by the wicked. This can be seen as a reference to those who suffer unjustly.

4. The Psalmist
Traditionally attributed to David, the psalmist often speaks from a place of personal experience with persecution and injustice.

5. The Assembly of the Wicked
This refers to the collective group of those who plot against the righteous, highlighting the organized nature of their opposition.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Persecution
Believers should not be surprised by opposition or persecution. The Bible consistently teaches that the righteous will face challenges from the wicked.

God's Justice Prevails
While the wicked may seem to triumph temporarily, God's justice will ultimately prevail. Believers can find comfort in knowing that God sees and will act.

Stand Firm in Righteousness
In the face of opposition, Christians are called to stand firm in their faith and continue to live righteously, trusting in God's protection and vindication.

The Power of Unity
Just as the wicked band together, believers are encouraged to unite in prayer, support, and encouragement to withstand trials.

The Call to Intercede
Christians are called to pray for those who are persecuted and to seek justice for the innocent, reflecting God's heart for righteousness.

What Does Psalm 94:21 Mean
They band together

– Scripture plainly states, “They band together” (Psalm 94:21). Wicked people do not usually act alone; sin loves company.

• Psalm 2:1-2 shows the nations “conspiring” together against the LORD and His Anointed.

• Proverbs 1:10-14 pictures sinners inviting others into a shared plot.

• In Matthew 26:3-4, the chief priests and elders “plotted together to arrest Jesus… and kill Him,” a vivid New-Testament echo of this same pattern.

When evil unites, its collective force can look intimidating, yet the Lord who “sits enthroned in heaven laughs” at such conspiracies (Psalm 2:4).

Against the righteous

– The target of the alliance is “the righteous,” those who trust and obey the Lord.

• Psalm 37:12 notes, “The wicked scheme against the righteous and gnash their teeth at them,” but God promises their plans will fail (v.13).

• Jesus warned in John 15:18-19 that the world hates His followers because they “are not of the world.”

• 1 Peter 3:14 reminds us, “Even if you should suffer for what is right, you are blessed.”

Opposition is not evidence of God’s absence; it is confirmation that righteousness and wickedness are fundamentally at odds.

And condemn the innocent to death

– The hostility escalates to legal persecution: they “condemn the innocent to death.”

• Isaiah 5:23 condemns those “who acquit the guilty for a bribe, but deny justice to the innocent.”

• The most striking fulfillment is the trial of Jesus in Matthew 27:22-26, where Pilate, pressured by the crowd, handed over the sinless Son of God to be crucified.

• Stephen’s martyrdom in Acts 7:54-60 shows the cycle continuing in the early church.

God sees every miscarriage of justice, and He promises, “He will bring back on them their iniquity” (Psalm 94:23).

summary

Psalm 94:21 paints a sober, literal picture of wicked alliances, their deliberate targeting of God’s people, and their readiness to pronounce lethal judgments on the innocent. The verse reminds believers that such hostility is nothing new; it threads through Scripture from Genesis to Revelation. Yet every scheme is under the sovereign eye of the Lord, who defends the righteous and will ultimately overturn every unjust verdict.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) They gather--i.e., possibly, They crowd into the courts of law to take part in the unjust condemnation of the just, or more generally, "They attack the life of the righteous." LXX., "they hunt." (Comp. Psalm 35:15.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - They gather themselves together against the soul of the righteous, and condemn the innocent blood (comp. 2 Kings 21:16; 2 Kings 24:4; Psalm 10:8; Proverbs 6:17; Isaiah 1:21; Isaiah 59:3, 7; Jeremiah 7:6; Jeremiah 22:3, 17, etc.). A Messianic allusion is possible, but not necessary.
