You, indeed, have made my days as handbreadths
This phrase emphasizes the brevity and fragility of human life. A "handbreadth" is an ancient unit of measurement, roughly the width of four fingers, symbolizing something very short and limited. In biblical times, this measurement was used to convey the idea of transience and the fleeting nature of life. This concept is echoed in other scriptures, such as Psalm 90:10, which speaks of the shortness of life, and James 4:14, which compares life to a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes. The acknowledgment of God as the one who determines the length of our days underscores His sovereignty and the human need for humility.

and my lifetime as nothing before You
This phrase highlights the insignificance of human life in comparison to the eternal nature of God. In the grand scheme of God's eternal existence, human life is but a moment. This perspective is consistent with the biblical theme of human humility before God, as seen in Job 7:7, where life is described as a breath. The phrase also reflects the biblical understanding of God's omnipotence and the temporal nature of earthly existence, encouraging believers to focus on eternal values rather than temporal achievements.

Truly each man at his best exists as but a breath
Here, the psalmist acknowledges that even at the peak of human strength and achievement, life is still ephemeral. The word "breath" is often used in scripture to denote something fleeting and insubstantial, as seen in Ecclesiastes 1:2, where life is described as "vanity" or "meaningless." This serves as a reminder of the limitations of human power and the futility of relying on worldly accomplishments. It calls believers to place their trust in God, who is eternal and unchanging, rather than in their own abilities or the temporary things of this world.

Selah
The term "Selah" is a musical or liturgical pause, inviting the reader or listener to reflect on the preceding words. It serves as a moment to meditate on the profound truths just expressed, encouraging deeper contemplation of the brevity of life and the eternal nature of God. This pause allows for introspection and a reassessment of one's priorities in light of the eternal perspective provided by the psalm.

What Does Psalm 39:5 Mean
You, indeed, have made my days as handbreadths

David looks at his calendar and realizes God is the One who set it. A “handbreadth” is roughly four inches—a tiny span compared to the sweep of history.

• Psalm 31:15 reminds us, “My times are in Your hands,” showing that every sunrise and sunset is divinely scheduled.

• Job 7:6 pictures life’s shuttle racing on the loom, expressing the same briefness.

• Psalm 90:12 urges, “Teach us to number our days,” because counting them drives us to wise living.

We’re not accidents drifting through time; our short allotment of breaths is lovingly measured by the Creator.

and my lifetime as nothing before You

Stack our whole lifespan—decades, memories, accomplishments—next to God’s eternity, and it barely registers.

• “A thousand years are like a day” to Him (2 Peter 3:8), so even a century seems momentary.

• Isaiah 40:15 says, “the nations are a drop in a bucket,” underscoring how even collective human history is minute in His presence.

• Psalm 102:11-12 contrasts our fading life with God’s enduring throne, reminding us what really lasts.

Because God is eternal, He sees the end from the beginning, and He alone can give permanent significance to our fleeting story.

Truly each man at his best exists as but a breath

Peak health, wealth, status—still vapor.

• James 4:14 asks, “What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes.”

• Isaiah 2:22 warns against trusting mere humans “who have but a breath in their nostrils.”

• 1 Peter 1:24 echoes Isaiah 40:6-8: “All flesh is like grass… but the word of the Lord stands forever.”

Bulleted realities:

– Achievements fade; God’s approval endures.

– Strength wanes; His power remains.

– Fame dims; His name shines.

Our best moment is still a brief exhale compared to His everlasting glory.

Selah

Pause. Let the truth settle. The psalmist invites us to stop scrolling, budgeting, planning long enough to feel the weight of eternity pressing against our brief segment of time. Reflect, recalibrate, worship.

summary

Psalm 39:5 pulls back the curtain on time: God measures our days, our lifetime is negligible beside Him, and even our strongest hour is a breath. Recognizing these facts anchors us in humility, fuels dependence on the Eternal One, and re-orders our priorities around what never ends—His glory and His word.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Handbreadth.--Better, some spans long. The plural without the article having this indefinite sense.

Mine age.--Literally, duration. (See Psalm 17:14.) The LXX. and Vulg. have "substance."

Before thee.--Since in God's sight "one day is as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day." "If nature is below any perception of time, God, at the other extremity of being, is above it. God includes time without being affected by it, and time includes nature, which is unaware of it. He too completely transcends it, his works are too profoundly subject to it, to be otherwise than indifferent to its lapse. But we stand at an intermediate point, and bear affinity with both extremes" (J. Martineau, Hours of Thought).

Verily every man . . .--Better, nothing but breath is every man at his best. (Literally, though standing firm.) Comp.

"Reason thus with life--

If I do lose thee, I do lose a thing

That none but fools would keep; a breath thou art.

SHAKESPEARE: Measure for Measure.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Behold, thou hast made my days as a handbreadth. It seems inconsistent that one who professes to be weary of his life should then complain of life's shortness. But such inconsistency is human. Job does the same (Job 14:1, 2). And mine age is as nothing before thee. The short human existence can scarcely be regarded by God as existence at all; rather, it is mere nothingness. Verily every man living at his best state is but vanity. So our Revisers. But most moderns translate, "Verily every man living was ordained for utter vanity" (comp. Psalm 62:9; Psalm 144:4).
