They will lift you up in their hands
This phrase refers to the protection and care provided by angels, as mentioned earlier in Psalm 91. Angels are often depicted in Scripture as messengers and protectors of God's people (Hebrews 1:14). The imagery of being lifted up suggests a divine intervention that prevents harm. In the context of ancient Near Eastern culture, the idea of being carried or lifted by a superior being was a sign of favor and protection. This phrase also connects to the temptation of Jesus in the wilderness, where Satan quotes this verse to challenge Jesus to test God's protection (Matthew 4:6). Jesus' response highlights the importance of trusting God without putting Him to the test.

so that you will not strike your foot against a stone
This part of the verse emphasizes the completeness of God's protection. In the historical context, walking on rocky paths was common, and stumbling was a frequent danger. The assurance that one will not even strike their foot against a stone signifies a promise of safety from even the smallest harm. This imagery can be seen as a metaphor for avoiding spiritual pitfalls and temptations. Theologically, it points to the care God provides to ensure His people are not led astray or harmed by the trials of life. This protection is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who, though tempted, did not fall into sin, exemplifying perfect reliance on God's provision and care.

What Does Psalm 91:12 Mean
They will lift you up in their hands

– “They” refers to the angels God commands on behalf of those who dwell in the shelter of the Most High (Psalm 91:11).

– Angels are literal, personal beings sent to serve believers (Hebrews 1:14) and to encamp around those who fear the LORD (Psalm 34:7).

– Their ministry is active: they “lift up,” a picture of powerful, tender protection. When Elisha’s servant’s eyes were opened, he saw heavenly forces surrounding them (2 Kings 6:17), illustrating the unseen reality Psalm 91 describes.

– The devil quoted this line to Jesus (Matthew 4:6; Luke 4:10-11), acknowledging that it is a concrete promise, though he twisted its intent. Jesus refused to test God, confirming the promise is received through trust, not presumption.

so that you will not strike your foot

– God’s care extends to the smallest detail—“foot” suggests daily steps, routine moments. Psalm 121:3 affirms, “He will not allow your foot to slip.”

– Protection is comprehensive: “Then you will walk safely in your way” (Proverbs 3:23) and “no evil will conquer you” (Psalm 91:10).

– For the Messiah, this foretold God’s preserving Him until His appointed hour (John 7:30). For believers in Christ, it assures that nothing, even ordinary missteps, escapes the Father’s notice (Matthew 10:30-31).

against a stone

– In ancient paths, an unseen stone could break a traveler’s stride. Here it represents every obstacle, snare, or hostile attack.

– The LORD removes or overrules those hazards: “You will tread on the lion and cobra” (Psalm 91:13), and “You will not be afraid… of the pestilence that stalks in darkness” (Psalm 91:5-6).

– The Stone imagery also points to Christ: though men stumble over Him (Romans 9:32-33; 1 Peter 2:8), the Father guarded His way perfectly until He willingly laid down His life (John 10:18).

– For us, obstacles remain real, yet God’s guardianship means they cannot derail His purposes (Romans 8:28). Even when harm seems to reach us, He carries us through it (Isaiah 43:2).

summary

Psalm 91:12 promises literal angelic protection commissioned by God to guard His people in every step, shielding them from unseen dangers and ensuring His sovereign plan prevails. Ultimately fulfilled in Jesus and continually applied to those who abide in Him, it invites confident, humble trust—not reckless testing—because the Father’s care is both tender and absolute.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(12) In their hands.--Literally, on, as a nurse a child. There is a Spanish proverb, expressive of great love and solicitude: "They carry him on the palms of their hands."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 12. - They shall bear thee up in their hands; rather, upon their hands - lifting thee over difficulties and stumbling blocks. Lest thou dash thy foot against a stone (comp. Proverbs 3:23, 24). Moral impediments are, no doubt, chiefly meant.
