Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Some wandered in desert wastelands
This phrase evokes the imagery of the Israelites' journey through the wilderness after their exodus from Egypt. The desert wastelands symbolize a place of desolation and testing, reflecting the spiritual and physical challenges faced by God's people. The wilderness experience is a recurring theme in Scripture, representing times of trial and reliance on God's provision (Deuteronomy 8:2-3). The wandering also alludes to the human condition of spiritual lostness and the need for divine guidance, as seen in the parable of the lost sheep (Luke 15:4-7).

finding no path
The lack of a path signifies confusion and aimlessness, highlighting the need for divine direction. In biblical times, paths or roads were crucial for travel and trade, and their absence would mean isolation and danger. This imagery is echoed in Proverbs 3:5-6, where trust in the Lord is emphasized for making one's paths straight. The absence of a path also points to the spiritual blindness and lostness of humanity without God's revelation and guidance.

to a city in which to dwell
Cities in biblical times were centers of security, community, and provision. The longing for a city reflects the human desire for stability and belonging. This phrase can be connected to the concept of the "city of God," a place of ultimate refuge and peace, as seen in Revelation 21:2-4, where the New Jerusalem is described. The search for a city also typifies the believer's journey towards the heavenly city, a theme explored in Hebrews 11:10, where Abraham is said to look forward to a city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Wanderers
These are individuals or groups who find themselves lost, both physically and spiritually, in a barren and desolate place. They symbolize those who are searching for meaning, direction, and a place of belonging.

2. Desert Wastelands
This refers to the literal and metaphorical barren places in life. In the Hebrew context, the desert often symbolizes a place of testing, trial, and dependence on God.

3. City to Dwell In
Represents a place of safety, community, and rest. In biblical terms, a city often symbolizes security and provision, contrasting with the desolation of the desert.
Teaching Points
Dependence on God in Desolation
Just as the wanderers in the desert had to rely on God for guidance and provision, we must trust Him in our own times of spiritual desolation.

The Search for Belonging
Our spiritual journey often involves searching for a place of belonging and rest. This search is ultimately fulfilled in Christ and the community of believers.

God's Guidance and Provision
Even when we feel lost, God provides a path and a place for us. We must seek His guidance through prayer and Scripture.

Spiritual Deserts as Growth Opportunities
Times of wandering can be opportunities for spiritual growth and deeper reliance on God. Embrace these times as part of God's refining process.

The Promise of Eternal Rest
Our ultimate hope is in the eternal city, the New Jerusalem, where we will find everlasting rest and fellowship with God.

What Does Psalm 107:4 Mean
Some wandered

“Some wandered in desert wastelands, finding no path to a city in which to dwell.” (Psalm 107:4)

• “Some” highlights that God remembers every subset of His people; none are lost in the crowd (Exodus 13:18).

• Historically, this points to Israel’s literal wilderness journey after the Exodus (Deuteronomy 32:10).

• Spiritually, it pictures anyone who drifts from God’s guidance—believers who let life pull them off course (Luke 15:4).

• The psalm immediately invites readers to trace God’s rescuing hand in their own story, just as He led those wanderers.

In desert wastelands

• The desert is a real, scorching place (Deuteronomy 8:15), but it also stands for seasons that feel barren—when prayers seem to evaporate like heat waves.

• Jeremiah 2:6 recalls how God’s people once trudged through “a land of drought and darkness,” yet He never abandoned them.

• Dry stretches remind us that only the Lord provides living water (Psalm 63:1; John 7:37).

Finding no path

• Without divine direction, every trail circles back to emptiness (Proverbs 14:12).

• Psalm 23:3 celebrates the opposite—“He guides me in paths of righteousness.”

• Isaiah 30:21 promises a voice behind us saying, “This is the way,” yet the wanderers in Psalm 107:4 illustrate what happens when we mute that voice.

• The verse lays bare the ache of confusion and the exhaustion of trial-and-error living.

To a city in which to dwell

• God created His people for community and security, not perpetual drift (Psalm 68:6).

• Abraham looked “for the city with foundations” (Hebrews 11:10-16), and Revelation 21:2 shows the final answer: the New Jerusalem where God dwells with His redeemed.

• Even now, Christ prepares a place (John 14:2), and His church offers a foretaste—a gathered people who find their belonging in Him (Ephesians 2:19-22).

• The line reminds us that every earthly city disappoints, but God’s city satisfies.

summary

Psalm 107:4 records literal wanderers and speaks to all who feel stuck in dry, directionless stretches. God sees the lonely “some,” meets them in the desert, provides the right path, and leads them toward His prepared city. The verse calls us to remember His past faithfulness, admit present need, and trust His sure promise of a permanent home.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(4-9) The wanderers.

(4) They.--It seems more natural to understand the subject of the verb wandered from the preceding clauses, than to supply a general subject, they; but this is by no means a certain interpretation. It depends on the view we take of the poem. (See Introduction.)

A solitary way.--Better, in a desert track. (Comp. Acts 8:26.) There is a grammatical difficulty, but this does not affect the general intention of the verse. Whether it represents an historical fact, or merely draws an imaginary picture, the reference to the dangers of Eastern travel is equally clear and distinct.

City to dwell in.--Literally, city of habitation, as rendered in Psalm 107:7.

"Boundless and bare

The lone and level sands stretch far away."--SHELLEY.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 4-9. - The form is historical, but the intention is to describe a recurrent event. Men from time to time wander - lose their way - either literally, or in the wilderness of life, grow faint and weary, and are ready to perish. But if they cry to God, God gives them aid, succors them, saves them. Then let them praise and thank him. Verse 4. - They wandered in the wilderness in a solitary way; they found no city to dwell in. It is, perhaps, best to divide this verse as was done by the LXX., who attached דרך, "way," to the latter clause. So Cheyne, who translates, "They wandered in the wilderness, yea, in the desert; they found no road to a city of habitation." So also Rosenmüller.
