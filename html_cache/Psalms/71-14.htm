But I will always hope
This phrase reflects a steadfast trust in God, a recurring theme throughout the Psalms. The psalmist's declaration of hope is not a passive wish but an active, confident expectation rooted in God's past faithfulness. In the biblical context, hope is often linked with waiting patiently for God's timing and deliverance, as seen in Lamentations 3:21-24, where hope is tied to God's mercies being new every morning. The psalmist's hope is not based on circumstances but on the unchanging nature of God, which is a central tenet of faith throughout the Old and New Testaments. This hope is also prophetic, pointing to the ultimate hope found in Jesus Christ, who is described as our hope in 1 Timothy 1:1.

and will praise You
Praise is a central act of worship in the Bible, expressing adoration and reverence for God. The psalmist's commitment to praise is a response to God's character and deeds. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, praise was often expressed through music, song, and communal worship, as seen in the practices surrounding the Temple in Jerusalem. This phrase emphasizes the importance of praise as a continual act, not limited to times of prosperity but also in adversity, as exemplified by Paul and Silas singing hymns in prison (Acts 16:25). Praise is both a personal and communal expression, reflecting the psalmist's desire to glorify God in all circumstances.

more and more
The phrase "more and more" indicates an increasing intensity and frequency of praise. It suggests a growing relationship with God, where the psalmist's understanding and appreciation of God's greatness deepen over time. This progression is mirrored in the life of a believer, where spiritual growth leads to a more profound and consistent expression of worship. Theologically, this reflects the concept of sanctification, where believers are continually transformed into the likeness of Christ, as described in 2 Corinthians 3:18. The psalmist's commitment to increasing praise also serves as an encouragement to believers to cultivate a lifestyle of worship that grows richer and more vibrant with each passing day.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Psalmist
The author of Psalm 71 is traditionally believed to be David, though the psalm itself does not specify. The psalmist is an individual who is experiencing distress and is seeking refuge in God.

2. God
The central figure to whom the psalmist directs his hope and praise. God is portrayed as a refuge and source of strength.

3. Enemies
Though not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of the psalm includes adversaries who threaten the psalmist, prompting his reliance on God.

4. Zion
While not explicitly mentioned in this verse, Zion often represents the place of God's dwelling and the center of worship, which is relevant to the theme of praise.

5. Old Age
The psalmist refers to his old age in the broader context of the psalm, emphasizing a lifetime of reliance on God.
Teaching Points
The Nature of Hope
Hope in God is not passive but active, requiring a continual choice to trust in His promises despite circumstances.

The Power of Praise
Praise is a powerful response to adversity. It shifts focus from problems to God's sovereignty and faithfulness.

Perseverance in Faith
The psalmist's commitment to hope and praise "more and more" suggests growth in faith and reliance on God over time.

Aging with Grace
The psalmist's reference to old age in the broader context encourages believers to continue growing in faith and reliance on God throughout their lives.

Witness through Worship
Consistent praise and hope serve as a testimony to others of God's faithfulness and the believer's trust in Him.

What Does Psalm 71:14 Mean
But I

• The psalmist steps forward with a deliberate “I,” setting himself apart from the opponents just mentioned in Psalm 71:13.

• This personal resolve mirrors other declarations such as “But as for me, I trust in You” (Psalm 55:23) and “As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15).

• By using “But,” he draws a line of contrast: no matter what others do or how fierce the trial, his stance before God is fixed.

will always hope

• Hope here is not wishful thinking; it is settled confidence in the unchanging promises of God.

• Scripture presents hope as continuous: “I wait for the LORD; my soul waits, and in His word I put my hope” (Psalm 130:5).

• Because God’s word is entirely true (Psalm 119:160), this hope can be constant—“always.”

• Even Job grasped the same certainty: “Though He slay me, yet will I hope in Him” (Job 13:15).

and will praise You

• Hope naturally pours into worship. When trust is anchored in God, the mouth cannot stay silent: “I will bless the LORD at all times; His praise will always be on my lips” (Psalm 34:1).

• Praise is not limited to sanctuary moments; Hebrews 13:15 urges believers to “continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise.”

• The psalmist links praise directly to God Himself—“You”—reminding us that worship is person-centered, not circumstance-centered.

more and more

• Praise is meant to grow, not plateau. The phrase points to an ever-increasing, lifelong ascent.

• Paul prayed, “that your love may abound more and more in knowledge and every discernment” (Philippians 1:9); the same upward trajectory applies to praise.

• Spiritual maturity is measured by expanding gratitude: “They will still bear fruit in old age; they will stay fresh and green” (Psalm 92:14).

• Practical ways this “more and more” unfolds:

– Recalling fresh mercies each morning (Lamentations 3:22-23).

– Sharing testimonies of God’s faithfulness with the next generation (Psalm 71:18).

– Letting every new trial become a new platform for thanksgiving (2 Corinthians 4:16-17).

summary

Psalm 71:14 captures a determined heart posture: despite opposition, the believer personally chooses to keep trusting God without interruption, letting that hope erupt into praise that only intensifies with time. Grounded in the unfailing truth of Scripture, hope is continual, praise is inevitable, and growth is the lifelong norm.

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 14-24. - Regarding his prayers as heard, and their fulfilment as certain, the psalmist now betakes himself to praise and thanksgiving, He will never cease to hope; he will praise God more and more (ver. 14). He will spend the whole day in telling of God's righteousness and salvation (ver. 15). The mighty acts of the Lord shall form his theme, together with the righteousness of God, and of none other (ver. 16). As God has enabled him to declare his praise in the past (ver. 17), so he trusts to be still upheld and enabled to proclaim the same to the new generation (ver. 18). God's righteousness is "very high," and there is none like him (ver. 19). When he. brings men into trouble, it is only to "turn again and comfort them" (vers. 20, 21). In conclusion, the writer promises that his hymns of praise shall not only be said, but sung, and accompanied with the melody of music (ver. 22). His lips and soul shall both rejoice together (ver. 23); and the praise of God shall employ his tongue without ceasing (ver. 24). Verse 14. - But I will hope continually; literally, but as for me, I will hope, etc. The phrase, "as for me," almost always marks a transition. And will yet praise thee more and more; literally, I will add to all thy praise; i.e. "I will add to all my past praises of thee further praises in the future."
