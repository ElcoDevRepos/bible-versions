Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But I trust in You, O LORD;
This phrase reflects a deep personal reliance on God, a theme prevalent throughout the Psalms. Trust in the LORD is a central tenet of faith, as seen in Proverbs 3:5-6, which encourages believers to trust in the LORD with all their heart. The use of "LORD" in all caps signifies the covenant name of God, Yahweh, emphasizing a personal relationship. Historically, this trust was crucial for the Israelites, who often faced external threats and internal turmoil. The psalmist's declaration of trust is an act of faith amidst adversity, echoing the sentiments of figures like David, who faced numerous trials yet consistently turned to God for refuge and strength.

I say, “You are my God.”
This declaration is a personal affirmation of faith and allegiance to God. It mirrors the covenant language found in Exodus 6:7, where God promises to be the God of the Israelites, and they will be His people. This phrase underscores the personal relationship between the believer and God, highlighting the intimacy and commitment involved. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, where polytheism was prevalent, declaring allegiance to one God was a profound statement of faith. This phrase also foreshadows the New Testament understanding of God as a personal Father, as seen in the teachings of Jesus Christ, who emphasized a personal relationship with God through prayer and obedience.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 31, David is expressing his trust in God amidst adversity.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)

3. Enemies and Adversaries

Teaching Points
Trust in God Amidst Trials
David’s declaration of trust serves as a model for believers facing difficulties. Trusting God involves a conscious decision to rely on His character and promises.

Personal Relationship with God
The phrase "You are my God" indicates a personal and intimate relationship. Believers are encouraged to cultivate a personal connection with God, recognizing Him as their own.

Faith Over Fear
In times of fear and uncertainty, faith in God provides stability and peace. This verse encourages believers to choose faith over fear.

God’s Sovereignty
Acknowledging God as "my God" affirms His sovereignty and control over all circumstances. Believers can find comfort in knowing that God is in control.

Declaration of Faith
Speaking out one’s faith, as David does, can strengthen personal conviction and serve as a testimony to others.

What Does Psalm 31:14 Mean
But

- The word signals a decisive shift. David has detailed betrayal, fear, and relentless opposition (Psalm 31:11–13).

- In Scripture, “but” often marks a turn from human despair to divine hope—see Psalm 73:26 “My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart,” and Ephesians 2:4 “But God, being rich in mercy…”.

- This change of direction reminds us that circumstances never have the last word; God does.

I trust in You

- Trust is personal reliance, not vague optimism. David stakes himself on the character of God, echoing Psalm 56:3 “When I am afraid, I will trust in You.”

- Note the present tense: trust is an ongoing choice, not a one-time event.

- Other believers model the same stance: Daniel in the lions’ den (Daniel 6:23) and Paul aboard the storm-tossed ship (Acts 27:25).

- Practical takeaway: faith is exercised most vividly when every visible prop is removed.

O LORD

- David addresses Yahweh by name, invoking the covenant God who revealed Himself to Moses (Exodus 3:14).

- Calling on the LORD anchors prayer in God’s unchanging faithfulness (Malachi 3:6) and recalls His past acts of rescue (Psalm 136).

- The intimacy here contrasts with the abandonment David feels from people; God remains accessible and attentive.

I say

- Faith finds its voice. Speaking truth aloud reinforces it internally (Romans 10:10 “with the mouth one confesses and is saved”).

- Verbal confession counters fearful thoughts and aligns the heart with God’s promises—see Psalm 91:2 “I will say to the LORD, ‘My refuge and my fortress.’”

- We, too, are invited to articulate God’s sufficiency, shaping our perspective and witness.

You are my God

- The declaration is exclusive and personal: “my” God, not just “the” God.

- By claiming God as his own, David renounces self-reliance and rival saviors—compare Jonah 2:8–9.

- This aligns with the first commandment (Exodus 20:3) and anticipates Thomas’s confession to Jesus, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28).

- Such ownership brings assurance: if the Almighty is mine, nothing can sever His care (Romans 8:38–39).

summary

Psalm 31:14 captures a pivotal moment: in the midst of crushing pressure, David purposefully pivots from dread to dependence. One small word—“but”—opens the door to active, spoken trust in the covenant-keeping LORD. By declaring, “You are my God,” he claims the ultimate security and models the path every believer can follow: acknowledge the crisis, choose to trust, speak that trust, and rest in the personal, unbreakable relationship God offers.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(14) But I.--Emphatic, in contrast to the pretended panic and in spite of the real dangers around him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - But I trusted in thee, O Lord. Having fully represented the miserable condition to which he is reduced (vers. 9-13), David now returns to expressions of trust in God, and to earnest prayer to him (comp. ver. 6). I said, Thou art my God; rather, I have said. In all my sufferings, dangers, and difficulties, I have always clung to thee, and said, "Thou, and thou alone, art, and ever shalt be, my God."
