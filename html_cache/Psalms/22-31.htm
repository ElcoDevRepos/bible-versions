They will come
This phrase suggests a future action, indicating that there will be individuals or groups who will arrive or emerge to fulfill a specific purpose. In the context of Psalm 22, which is often seen as a prophetic psalm, this can be interpreted as the coming of future generations or messengers who will carry forward the message of God's deeds. This aligns with the biblical theme of God's work being declared throughout generations, as seen in passages like Isaiah 59:21.

and proclaim His righteousness
The act of proclaiming God's righteousness involves declaring His just and holy nature. In the biblical context, righteousness is often associated with God's faithfulness to His covenant and His moral perfection. This proclamation is a central theme in the Psalms and throughout Scripture, where God's righteousness is celebrated and made known to others (Psalm 40:10). It also points to the ultimate revelation of God's righteousness through Jesus Christ, who embodies divine justice and mercy.

to a people yet unborn
This phrase emphasizes the continuity of God's message across time, reaching even those who are not yet born. It reflects the biblical principle of passing down faith and knowledge of God to future generations, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:6-7. The idea of a legacy of faith is crucial in Scripture, ensuring that God's works and character are known and revered by all generations.

all that He has done
This encompasses the entirety of God's actions and deeds, which are to be recounted and celebrated. In the context of Psalm 22, which begins with a cry of anguish and ends in triumph, "all that He has done" can be seen as a reference to God's deliverance and faithfulness. This phrase also connects to the broader biblical narrative of God's redemptive work, culminating in the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ, who fulfills the prophecies and promises of the Old Testament.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 22, David is expressing a prophetic vision of future generations acknowledging God's righteousness.

2. Future Generations
The "people yet unborn" refers to those who will come after the current generation, highlighting the enduring nature of God's deeds and righteousness.

3. Proclamation of Righteousness
This event involves the declaration of God's righteous acts, emphasizing the importance of sharing God's works with future generations.
Teaching Points
The Importance of Testimony
Sharing personal and communal experiences of God's righteousness is crucial for the faith development of future generations.

Legacy of Faith
Believers are called to leave a legacy of faith by teaching and proclaiming God's deeds to those who come after them.

God's Righteousness is Eternal
God's righteousness is not limited to one time or place; it transcends generations and is relevant for all people at all times.

Active Participation in God's Story
Each believer has a role in God's ongoing account, contributing to the proclamation of His righteousness through their lives and testimonies.

Hope for Future Generations
The promise that God's righteousness will be proclaimed to future generations provides hope and assurance that His truth will endure.

What Does Psalm 22:31 Mean
They will come

“Posterity will serve Him…” (Psalm 22:30) sets the stage. The closing “They will come” points to a continuing line of believers who respond to God’s saving work:

• This is not theoretical; it is certain—“they will.”

• Acts 2:39 shows the promise reaching “all who are far off.”

• Isaiah 59:21 speaks of God’s word remaining in successive generations.

The verse assures us that the covenant community will not die out; God Himself guarantees the arrival of new worshipers.

Proclaim His righteousness

The core assignment is verbal witness: declaring what God has done and why it matters.

• “Proclaim” echoes Psalm 71:15–16—“My mouth will tell of Your righteousness.”

• Romans 3:25–26 reveals the fullest display of that righteousness at the cross.

• 2 Corinthians 5:21 ties God’s righteousness directly to the saving work of Christ.

The good news is not vague spirituality; it centers on God’s perfect, just character made visible in redemption.

To a people yet unborn

The audience extends beyond the songwriter’s lifetime.

• Psalm 78:6 urges teaching “the children yet to be born.”

• Joel 1:3 commands, “Tell it to your children and their children.”

• From Pentecost to the present, every disciple has been both beneficiary and messenger, forming an unbroken chain of testimony.

God’s plan embraces future generations, proving His faithfulness across centuries.

All that He has done

The final phrase ties the message to concrete acts, not abstract ideas.

• Psalm 66:5 invites, “Come and see the works of God.”

• John 19:30 records the climactic “It is finished,” fulfilling Psalm 22’s earlier prophecy (v. 18, v. 16).

• Revelation 5:9 shows heaven eternally celebrating “all that He has done” through the Lamb.

Believers recount specific historical deeds—creation, covenant, cross, resurrection, and the ongoing gathering of the Church.

summary

Psalm 22:31 closes the prophetic psalm on a note of triumphant continuity. God guarantees a coming people who will keep the story alive, openly declaring His flawless righteousness and completed saving acts. Every generation hears, believes, and passes the torch, ensuring that the victory accomplished at Calvary resounds “to a people yet unborn.”

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(31) They shall come--i.e., the generation just foretold: it shall announce His righteousness to a still younger generation (literally, to a people born) that He wrought. The tale of Jehovah's goodness to Israel would be handed on from age to age,

"His triumphs would be sung

By some yet unmoulded tongue."

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 31. - They shall come, and shall declare his righteousness unto a people that shall be born, that he hath done this. One generation after another shall come, and shall report God's righteousness, as shown forth in Christ, each to its successor - a people yet to be born - telling them that God "has done this;" i.e. effected all that is here sketched out, and so accomplished the work of redemption.
