You are my God, and I will give You thanks.
This declaration of personal relationship with God reflects the covenantal language found throughout the Old Testament, where God identifies Himself as the God of Israel (Exodus 20:2). The psalmist's acknowledgment of God as "my God" signifies a personal and communal bond, echoing the intimate relationship between God and His people. Giving thanks is a central theme in the Psalms, often associated with worship and recognition of God's faithfulness and deliverance (Psalm 100:4). Historically, thanksgiving was expressed through sacrifices and offerings at the temple, a practice rooted in the Levitical laws (Leviticus 7:12-15). This phrase also anticipates the New Testament teaching of giving thanks in all circumstances (1 Thessalonians 5:18), highlighting a continuous thread of gratitude in the believer's life.

You are my God, and I will exalt You.
The repetition of "You are my God" emphasizes the psalmist's devotion and the exclusivity of worship due to God alone, rejecting idolatry and affirming monotheism. Exalting God involves lifting Him up in praise and acknowledging His supreme authority and majesty. This act of exaltation is a response to God's mighty acts and His steadfast love, themes prevalent in the Psalms (Psalm 145:1). The cultural context of ancient Israel included public acts of worship, such as singing and playing instruments, to exalt God during festivals and gatherings (2 Chronicles 5:13). This phrase also points to the ultimate exaltation of Jesus Christ, who is lifted up and glorified (Philippians 2:9-11), serving as a type of Christ in the psalmist's expression of worship.

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Psalmist
The author of Psalm 118, traditionally attributed to King David, who expresses personal devotion and gratitude to God.

2. God (Yahweh)
The central figure in this verse, acknowledged as the personal God of the psalmist, deserving of thanks and exaltation.

3. The Temple
While not directly mentioned in this verse, the context of Psalm 118 suggests a setting of worship, possibly linked to the temple in Jerusalem.

4. Israel
The nation of Israel, often the collective voice in the Psalms, expressing communal worship and thanksgiving to God.

5. The Exodus
An event that underpins much of Israel's worship, reflecting on God's deliverance and faithfulness, which is a recurring theme in the Psalms.
Teaching Points
Personal Relationship with God
The psalmist's declaration, "You are my God," emphasizes a personal relationship with God. Believers are encouraged to cultivate a personal and intimate connection with God, recognizing Him as their own.

Attitude of Gratitude
The commitment to give thanks is a central theme. Christians are called to maintain an attitude of gratitude, acknowledging God's goodness and faithfulness in their lives.

Exaltation in Worship
Exalting God is an act of worship that goes beyond words. It involves lifting God high in our hearts and lives, prioritizing His glory in all we do.

Consistency in Praise
The repetition of "You are my God" and the actions of giving thanks and exalting God suggest consistency. Believers should strive for regular and consistent praise, regardless of circumstances.

Community Worship
While the verse is personal, it is also part of a communal psalm. Christians are encouraged to join together in worship, strengthening their faith and witness as a community.

What Does Psalm 118:28 Mean
You are my God

The psalmist begins with a clear, personal confession of faith: “You are my God” (Psalm 118:28).

•That declaration is covenant language, echoing verses like Exodus 15:2, “The LORD is my strength and my song, and He has become my salvation.”

•It moves beyond acknowledging God’s existence to claiming Him as personal Lord, exactly as Thomas later affirmed, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28).

•Because Scripture is true and literal, the same invitation stands for us: we can—must—make that same personal claim.

I will give You thanks

Gratitude flows naturally from ownership of faith. “I will give You thanks.”

•Psalm 92:1 reminds us, “It is good to give thanks to the LORD.”

•Paul reinforces the same posture: “Give thanks in every circumstance” (1 Thessalonians 5:18).

•Thanksgiving redirects our focus from self to the steadfast love proclaimed throughout Psalm 118, culminating in verse 1, “His loving devotion endures forever.”

You are my God (reaffirmed)

The line repeats, not for redundancy but for emphasis.

•Repetition in Scripture underscores certainty (see Isaiah 6:3; Philippians 4:4).

•It anchors the heart when circumstances shift—much like the psalmist again tells himself, “Why, my soul, are you downcast?… Put your hope in God” (Psalm 42:5).

•The repeated confession fortifies identity: the psalmist belongs to God; God belongs to him (Deuteronomy 26:17).

I will exalt You

Thankfulness turns outward to public praise: “I will exalt You.”

•Exalting means raising God’s name so others see His greatness (Psalm 34:3, “Magnify the LORD with me; let us exalt His name together”).

•It is active and vocal, seen in Isaiah 25:1, “I will exalt You; I will praise Your name, for You have worked wonders.”

•Ultimately, every creature will do the same (Revelation 5:13), but believers choose to start now, proclaiming His unmatched worth.

summary

Psalm 118:28 moves from personal confession to grateful response and on to public exaltation—twice affirming who God is and twice declaring how the believer will respond. The verse models a simple pattern for everyday faith: confess Him as your God, thank Him for His steadfast love, restate the confession to steady your heart, and lift His name high so others can see His glory.
