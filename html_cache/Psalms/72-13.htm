He will take pity on the poor and needy
This phrase highlights the king's compassion, reflecting God's own heart for the marginalized. In biblical context, the poor and needy often symbolize those who are vulnerable and without power. The king's role as a protector and provider is emphasized, aligning with the responsibilities of a righteous ruler as seen in Proverbs 31:8-9. The Hebrew word for "pity" conveys a deep sense of mercy and compassion, suggesting an active response to suffering. This mirrors God's character as described in Psalm 146:7-9, where He upholds the cause of the oppressed and provides for the needy. The king's actions foreshadow the ministry of Jesus Christ, who demonstrated compassion for the poor and needy throughout His earthly ministry (Matthew 9:36, Luke 4:18).

and save the lives of the oppressed
The phrase underscores the king's role as a deliverer, a theme prevalent in the Old Testament where God frequently acts to save His people from oppression (Exodus 3:7-8). The term "oppressed" refers to those who suffer under unjust treatment or hardship, often at the hands of the powerful. This reflects the biblical principle of justice, where the king is expected to act as God's agent in righting wrongs and delivering justice (Isaiah 1:17). The deliverance of the oppressed is a messianic expectation, pointing to Jesus Christ as the ultimate Savior who liberates humanity from spiritual oppression (Luke 4:18-19, John 8:36). This phrase also connects to the eschatological hope found in Revelation 21:4, where God promises to end all forms of suffering and oppression.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Solomon
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 72, Solomon was the son of King David and Bathsheba, known for his wisdom and wealth. This psalm is often seen as a prayer for his reign and a foreshadowing of the Messiah's kingdom.

2. The Poor and Needy
These individuals represent those who are marginalized, vulnerable, and in need of assistance. In the context of the psalm, they are the recipients of the king's compassion and justice.

3. The Oppressed
This group includes those who suffer under injustice and exploitation. The psalm highlights the king's role in delivering and saving them.

4. The King
While the immediate context refers to Solomon, the psalm is also seen as a messianic prophecy pointing to Jesus Christ, the ultimate King who embodies perfect justice and compassion.

5. The Kingdom
The setting of the psalm is a kingdom characterized by righteousness, justice, and peace, reflecting God's ideal for leadership and governance.
Teaching Points
Compassion as a Divine Attribute
God's compassion is a central theme in Scripture, and believers are called to emulate this attribute in their interactions with others.

The Role of Justice in Leadership
Leaders, both secular and spiritual, are tasked with the responsibility to protect and uplift the marginalized, reflecting God's heart for justice.

Messianic Fulfillment in Christ
Jesus Christ perfectly fulfills the role of the compassionate King, offering salvation and hope to all who are oppressed by sin and injustice.

Practical Compassion
Christians are encouraged to actively seek out ways to support and advocate for the poor and needy in their communities.

Prayer for Righteous Leadership
Believers should pray for leaders who embody the values of justice and compassion, as outlined in Psalm 72.

What Does Psalm 72:13 Mean
He will take pity

• The verse opens with active compassion: “take pity.” This is not passive sympathy but deliberate movement toward those in distress.

• God’s heart is consistently shown this way—Psalm 103:13 says, “As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear Him.”

• Christ mirrors this in the Gospels; in Matthew 14:14 He “was moved with compassion for them and healed their sick.” The royal son of Psalm 72 acts in the same spirit.

on the poor and needy

• Scripture links God’s rule with special concern for the vulnerable. Proverbs 19:17: “Kindness to the poor is a loan to the LORD, and He will repay the lender.”

• Psalm 70:5 personalizes it: “But I am poor and needy; hurry to me, O God.” The king in Psalm 72 answers that cry.

• This focus marks genuine, righteous leadership. Isaiah 11:4 foretells a ruler who “will judge the poor with righteousness.”

and save

• Pity moves to action—salvation. God never stops with feeling; He rescues. Psalm 34:6: “This poor man called out, and the LORD heard him; He saved him from all his troubles.”

• The same saving intent motivates Christ’s mission (Luke 19:10: “For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost”).

• In Psalm 72 the king’s deliverance is comprehensive, covering earthly needs and pointing ahead to eternal security in the Messiah.

the lives

• More than temporary relief, the promise reaches to “lives”—the very breath and future of the oppressed.

• Psalm 22:26 links life and hope: “The poor will eat and be satisfied; those who seek the LORD will praise Him—may your hearts live forever!”

• God guards life itself (Psalm 121:7: “The LORD will keep you from all harm; He will watch over your life”).

of the oppressed

• Oppression can be economic, social, or spiritual; whatever its form, the king stands as liberator.

• Psalm 9:9: “The LORD is a refuge for the oppressed, a stronghold in times of trouble.”

• Psalm 146:7 adds that He “upholds the cause of the oppressed.” Psalm 72 portrays those truths enacted through a righteous ruler, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus who breaks every chain (Luke 4:18).

summary

Psalm 72:13 paints a ruler whose heart beats with God’s own compassion: He notices the suffering, is moved by pity, takes decisive action to save, safeguards the very lives of those downtrodden, and ends their oppression. It is a portrait of ideal kingship realized perfectly in Christ—a Savior-King who feels, moves, and delivers, ensuring that the poor, needy, and oppressed find lasting rescue and life in Him.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - He shall spare the poor and needy; or, the weak and needy. And shall save the souls of the needy. He shall not merely deliver them from their cruel oppressors in this life (ver. 4), but also give health and life to their souls.
