He let no man oppress them;
This phrase reflects God's protective nature over His chosen people, Israel, during their journey and sojourn in foreign lands. Historically, this can be seen during the time of the patriarchs, such as Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, when they traveled through territories ruled by various kings. The divine protection ensured that despite being strangers in these lands, they were not subjected to oppression or harm. This protection is a recurring theme in the Old Testament, where God intervenes to safeguard His people, as seen in Genesis 20 when God warns Abimelech in a dream not to harm Abraham or Sarah. This divine intervention underscores God's covenantal faithfulness and His role as a guardian over Israel.

He rebuked kings on their behalf:
This phrase highlights specific instances where God directly intervened to protect His people by confronting and correcting rulers. A notable example is found in Genesis 12:17-20 and Genesis 20:3-7, where God afflicts Pharaoh and Abimelech with plagues and dreams, respectively, to prevent them from wronging Abraham and Sarah. These events demonstrate God's sovereignty over earthly powers and His willingness to act decisively to uphold His promises. The rebuke of kings also foreshadows the ultimate authority of Christ, the King of Kings, who will judge and rule over all nations. This divine rebuke serves as a reminder of God's power to influence and direct the affairs of nations for the sake of His people, ensuring the fulfillment of His divine plan.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty
God is sovereign over all nations and rulers. He has the power to protect His people from any form of oppression.

Divine Protection
Just as God protected the Israelites, He continues to watch over and protect His people today. Believers can trust in His providence and care.

God's Faithfulness
The recounting of God's past actions serves as a reminder of His faithfulness. He keeps His promises and acts on behalf of His people.

Courage in Faith
Knowing that God can rebuke kings and authorities should embolden believers to stand firm in their faith, trusting in His ultimate authority.

Intercession and Advocacy
God acts as an advocate for His people, intervening on their behalf. This encourages believers to seek His intervention in times of trouble.

What Does Psalm 105:14 Mean
He let no man oppress them

• Psalm 105:14 opens with the assurance, “He let no man oppress them”. The psalmist is recounting God’s dealings with Abraham’s family while they were “few in number, … wandering from nation to nation” (Psalm 105:12–13).

• Genesis repeatedly shows the Lord stepping in when danger loomed:

– Genesis 12:17 records how the Lord “plagued Pharaoh” after Abram’s wife Sarai was taken into Pharaoh’s house.

– Genesis 20:6–7 tells of God warning Abimelech in a dream, keeping him from harming Sarah.

– Genesis 26:11 notes that Abimelech “warned all the people, saying, ‘Whoever touches this man or his wife shall surely be put to death.’”

• In each case God’s promise in Genesis 12:3—“I will bless those who bless you, and curse those who curse you”—is fulfilled literally. No human scheme could overturn His covenant; He personally blocked oppression.

• The same protective hand appears later when Jacob travels: “The terror of God fell upon the towns all around them, so that no one pursued them” (Genesis 35:5).

• Psalm 105 therefore magnifies a God who does not merely sympathize with His people’s trials; He actively restrains hostile hands.

He rebuked kings on their behalf

• The verse continues, “He rebuked kings on their behalf”. God’s defense escalates from restraining ordinary men to confronting monarchs.

• Pharaoh in Egypt experiences direct rebuke through plagues (Exodus 7–12). Earlier, Pharaoh in Abram’s day is struck with plagues (Genesis 12:17). Both Pharaohs learn the same lesson: the God of Abraham is not subject to royal decree.

• Abimelech king of Gerar hears God say, “You are as good as dead because of the woman you have taken” (Genesis 20:3). That royal household is silenced before harm can be done.

• Centuries later, Psalm 105’s twin passage in 1 Chronicles 16:21–22 repeats the line to remind Israel that their national history is anchored in a pattern of divine intervention: kings may issue commands, but the Lord overrules.

• This theme continues throughout Scripture:

– “By Me kings reign” (Proverbs 8:15).

– “The king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD” (Proverbs 21:1).

• For believers, the implication is clear: no earthly authority can thwart the purposes God has for His covenant people.

summary

Psalm 105:14 celebrates God’s protective faithfulness to the patriarchs. He personally halted oppression and confronted powerful rulers so His covenant plan could advance. The same Lord still guards His people, proving that promises made are promises kept—no matter who sits on an earthly throne.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 14. - He suffered no man to do them wrong: yea, he reproved kings for their sakes. The reference is to the punishment inflicted on the Pharaoh of Abraham's time (Genesis 12:17), and on Abimelech of Gerar (Genesis 20:3, 7, 18).
