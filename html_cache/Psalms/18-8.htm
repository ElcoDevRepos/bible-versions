Smoke rose from His nostrils
This imagery is often associated with divine anger and power. In ancient Near Eastern culture, smoke and fire were common symbols of a deity's presence and wrath. The depiction of smoke rising from the nostrils suggests a fierce, almost volcanic, expression of God's righteous indignation. This phrase can be connected to other biblical instances where God's presence is accompanied by smoke, such as at Mount Sinai (Exodus 19:18), emphasizing His holiness and the awe-inspiring nature of His presence.

and consuming fire came from His mouth
Fire is frequently used in the Bible to symbolize God's judgment and purification. The consuming fire from God's mouth indicates His ability to destroy evil and purify His people. This can be linked to passages like Deuteronomy 4:24, where God is described as a "consuming fire," highlighting His role as a judge. The imagery also foreshadows the final judgment, where God's word will be the ultimate authority. In a typological sense, this can be seen as a precursor to the words of Christ, who speaks with authority and power (Revelation 19:15).

glowing coals blazed forth
Glowing coals represent the intense heat and energy of God's presence. In the ancient world, coals were a source of light and heat, symbolizing both life and destruction. This imagery can be connected to Isaiah 6:6-7, where a seraphim uses a live coal to purify Isaiah's lips, signifying cleansing and commissioning. The blazing coals here emphasize the transformative power of God's presence, capable of both judgment and renewal. This can also be seen as a type of Christ, who purifies and refines believers through His sacrifice and the work of the Holy Spirit.

What Does Psalm 18:8 Mean
Smoke rose from His nostrils

- David recounts how the LORD visibly intervened; the smoke pictures righteous anger building, not mere metaphor but a concrete manifestation of His presence (Exodus 19:18; Psalm 97:2–3).

- Such smoke stands as a warning to every enemy of God’s people that His patience has limits (Nahum 1:3).

- For the believer, it assures that when trouble engulfs us, the Lord does not remain distant—He moves, and the very atmosphere changes.

Consuming fire came from His mouth

- Scripture repeatedly describes the LORD as “a consuming fire” (Deuteronomy 4:24; Hebrews 12:29). Here that fire proceeds from His mouth, tying His spoken word to unstoppable power (Jeremiah 23:29; Revelation 19:15).

- The image reminds us that creation itself began with God’s voice (Genesis 1), and judgment will be executed by that same voice (2 Thessalonians 1:7–8).

- For the faithful, the fire that destroys the wicked also purifies and refines (Malachi 3:2–3), proving God’s activity is always purposeful.

Glowing coals blazed forth

- Glowing coals signal sustained, intense heat—God’s action is not a flash in the pan but deliberate and thorough (Ezekiel 10:2).

- Coals from the heavenly altar once purified Isaiah’s lips (Isaiah 6:6–7); here they blaze outward, showing the same holiness that cleanses His servants consumes their foes.

- Elijah’s confrontation on Carmel (1 Kings 18:38) and the fire that fell on Ahaziah’s soldiers (2 Kings 1:10) echo this truth: God defends His honor and His people with tangible fire when necessary.

summary

Psalm 18:8 presents the LORD rising in literal, fiery fury to rescue His servant. Smoke manifests His anger, fire from His mouth wields His decisive word, and glowing coals display sustained, purifying judgment. The verse assures believers that the holy God who once descended in fire still personally, powerfully intervenes for those who call on His name.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(8) A smoke.--Now the thunder-cloud forms--smoke, as it were, from the nostrils of God (comp. Psalm 74:1; Deuteronomy 29:20 : the literal rendering is, "there ascended smoke in his nostrils")--and intermittent flashes of lightning dart forth and play about the distant summits, seeming to devour everything in its path. (Comp. the expression lambent flame.")

Coals were kindled by it.--Rather, flaming coals blazed from it.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 8. - There went up a smoke out of his nostrils. Emissions of smoke are a common feature of volcanic disturbances, with which earthquakes are closely connected. The LXX. give, instead of "out of his nostrils," in his anger (ἐν ὀργῇ αὐτοῦ), which is better, since the Hebrew prefix בּ, "in," certainly cannot mean "out of." And fire out of his mouth devoured. Fire-balls are said to have accompanied some earthquakes, as especially that one by which Julian's design of rebuilding Jerusalem was frustrated. Coals were kindled by it. The fire-balls above spoken of are declared to have scorched and burnt the workmen employed by Julian (Amm. Marc., 23. 1).
