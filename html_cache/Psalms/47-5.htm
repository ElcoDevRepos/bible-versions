God has ascended amid shouts of joy
This phrase reflects a scene of divine triumph and celebration. The imagery of God ascending suggests a victorious return, possibly alluding to the Ark of the Covenant being brought to Jerusalem, as seen in 2 Samuel 6:15. The shouts of joy indicate a communal expression of worship and reverence, reminiscent of the Israelites' celebrations during significant religious events. This ascent can also be seen as a type of Christ's ascension into heaven, as described in Acts 1:9, where Jesus is taken up in glory, marking the completion of His earthly mission and the beginning of His reign at the right hand of the Father.

the LORD with the sound of the horn
The use of "the LORD" emphasizes the covenant name of God, Yahweh, highlighting His personal relationship with His people. The sound of the horn, or shofar, was traditionally used in Israel for various purposes, including calling people to worship, signaling the start of festivals, and announcing the coronation of a king (1 Kings 1:39). This sound signifies divine authority and the acknowledgment of God's kingship. The horn's blast is also prophetic, pointing to the future return of Christ, as mentioned in 1 Thessalonians 4:16, where the trumpet of God will sound at the second coming, gathering His people to Himself.

What Does Psalm 47:5 Mean
God has ascended

“God has ascended amid shouts of joy…” (Psalm 47:5)

• The verb “ascended” pictures a triumphal rise to the throne. It echoes the moment when the ark was carried up to Jerusalem (2 Samuel 6:12–15) and anticipates Christ’s bodily ascension (Acts 1:9; Ephesians 4:8).

• Ascension means sovereignty. Psalm 68:18 declares, “You have ascended on high; You have led captives in Your train,” affirming that the Lord rules over all powers.

• For believers, this establishes confidence: the One who has already risen is presently reigning (Colossians 3:1).

Amid shouts of joy

“…amid shouts of joy…”

• The procession is not silent; it is charged with celebration, much like the joyful noise when the ark entered Zion (1 Chronicles 15:28) or when the returning exiles praised God at the rebuilt altar (Ezra 3:11).

• Joy signals recognition: the people see God’s victories as their own (Psalm 98:4; 1 Peter 1:8).

• Corporate praise reminds us that worship is communal. We lift our voices together because His reign benefits all who trust Him (Psalm 95:1–3).

the LORD with the sound of the horn

“…the LORD with the sound of the horn.”

• The shofar (ram’s horn) marked festal gatherings, coronations, and battle victories (Joshua 6:5; 1 Kings 1:39). Its blast here announces that the covenant LORD is King over every nation (Psalm 47:8).

• Trumpet imagery runs through Scripture, from Sinai’s trembling blast (Exodus 19:16) to the final trumpet that heralds Christ’s return (1 Corinthians 15:52; 1 Thessalonians 4:16). The same Lord who once ascended will descend in glory to gather His people.

• Each trumpet call nudges the heart toward readiness—worship now, watchfulness for the future.

summary

Psalm 47:5 paints a royal procession: the sovereign God rises to His rightful throne, greeted by exuberant praise and the triumphant blast of trumpets. The scene ties together past victories, Christ’s ascension, our present worship, and the coming consummation of His kingdom. Because God has ascended, we rejoice loudly, trust confidently, and look expectantly for the day the same Lord returns with an even greater shout.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) Is gone up.--Not, as in Genesis 17:22, Judges 13:20, to heaven, but, as in Psalms 24, to the Temple, as is shown by the public acclaim accompanying the ark to its resting-place after victory. (Comp. 2Chronicles 20:28; Psalm 68:17; Amos 2:2.)

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - God is gone up with a shout; the Lord with the sound of a trumpet. As God "comes down" when he interposes for the relief or deliverance of his people (Psalm 144:5), so after the relief or deliverance is effected, he is viewed as "going up" - returning to his glorious abode, reoccupying his seat in the heaven of heavens, and there remaining until some fresh call is made upon him. If the interposition has been one of a striking and unusual character, if the relief has been great, the deliverance signal, the triumph accorded to his people extraordinary, then he "goes up with a shout" - amid the exulting cries and loud jubilations of rescued Israel. When the occasion is such as to call for a public manifestation of thanksgiving at the house of God (2 Chronicles 20:28), then he "goes up" also "with the sound of the trumpet," which was always sounded by the priests on great occasions of festal joy and gladness (see 2 Samuel 6:15; 2 Kings 11:14; 1 Chronicles 13:8; 1 Chronicles 16:42; 2 Chronicles 5:12; 2 Chronicles 7:6; 2 Chronicles 29:27; Ezra 3:10; Nehemiah 12:35).
