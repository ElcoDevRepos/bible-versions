Let the redeemed of the LORD say so
This phrase calls for a public declaration of God's deliverance. The term "redeemed" refers to those whom God has rescued or bought back, often from slavery or captivity. In the Old Testament, redemption is a key theme, particularly in the context of the Israelites' deliverance from Egypt (Exodus 6:6). The act of saying or proclaiming God's redemption is an act of worship and testimony, encouraging others to recognize God's power and faithfulness. This aligns with the biblical principle of bearing witness to God's works (Isaiah 43:10).

whom He has redeemed from the hand of the enemy
This phrase highlights God's intervention in rescuing His people from adversaries. Historically, this could refer to various instances where Israel was delivered from physical enemies, such as during the Babylonian exile (Jeremiah 31:11). The "hand of the enemy" symbolizes oppression and danger, from which God provides salvation. This redemption is a type of Christ's ultimate redemption of humanity from sin and death (Colossians 1:13-14). The imagery of God's hand is significant, often representing His power and authority (Psalm 89:13).

Persons / Places / Events
1. The Redeemed
Refers to those whom God has delivered or saved, particularly from peril or bondage. In the context of the Old Testament, this often refers to the Israelites who were delivered from Egypt or other captivities.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal nature and faithfulness to His promises. He is the one who redeems His people.

3. The Enemy
Represents any force or power that opposes God's people, whether physical enemies like foreign nations or spiritual adversaries.

4. Redemption
The act of being saved or delivered. In Hebrew, the word "ga'al" is used, which implies a kinsman-redeemer, someone who rescues or buys back.

5. The Hand of the Enemy
Symbolizes control or power that the enemy has over someone, from which God delivers His people.
Teaching Points
Proclaim Your Redemption
As believers, we are called to testify about God's deliverance in our lives. Sharing our redemption account can encourage others and glorify God.

Recognize the Redeemer
Acknowledge that it is the LORD who redeems us. Our salvation and deliverance are acts of His grace and power, not our own efforts.

Understand the Nature of Enemies
Enemies can be both physical and spiritual. Recognize the spiritual battles we face and rely on God's strength for deliverance.

Live as the Redeemed
Our lives should reflect the freedom and gratitude of those who have been redeemed. This involves living in obedience and faithfulness to God.

Encourage Others with Your Testimony
Use your account of redemption to uplift and encourage others who may be facing their own battles or feeling trapped by their "enemies."

What Does Psalm 107:2 Mean
Let the redeemed

• “Redeemed” describes people God has bought back, rescuing them at a cost only He could pay (Exodus 6:6; Titus 2:14).

• The psalmist assumes real, personal experience—only those who know deliverance can truly sing about it (Isaiah 51:11).

of the LORD

• Redemption originates in the LORD, the covenant God who keeps His promises (Psalm 100:3).

• Belonging to Him defines our identity more than past slavery or present circumstances (1 Corinthians 6:19-20).

say so

• Silence is not an option; testimony is commanded.

• God’s people are witnesses—“You are My witnesses,” declares the LORD (Isaiah 43:10).

• New-covenant believers echo the call: “Proclaim the excellencies of Him who called you out of darkness” (1 Peter 2:9).

whom He has redeemed

• The verb repeats to spotlight the Redeemer, not the redeemed.

• “In Him we have redemption through His blood” (Ephesians 1:7).

• Gratitude grows when we rehearse who acted, what it cost, and how complete the rescue is (Hebrews 9:12).

from the hand of the enemy

• For Israel, enemies were literal captors—Egypt, Babylon, hostile nations (Psalm 18:17).

• In Christ, the ultimate enemy is defeated: “He has rescued us from the dominion of darkness” (Colossians 1:13).

• Daily battles persist, yet we stand in victory, “more than conquerors through Him who loved us” (Romans 8:37).

summary

Psalm 107:2 invites everyone God has liberated to speak up. Our stories—rooted in His decisive rescue—become living proof that the LORD still saves, still delivers, and still deserves public praise.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 2. - Let the redeemed of the Lord say so. "The redeemed of the Lord" in this place are those whom the Lord has just delivered out of exile and captivity (comp. Isaiah 44:22-24; Isaiah 51:11; Jeremiah 31:11; Zechariah 10:8, etc.). The writer calls on them to give voice to the thanksgiving of ver. 1. Whom he hath redeemed from the hand of the enemy; i.e. of Babylon.
