But I am a worm and not a man
This phrase reflects a deep sense of humiliation and degradation. The term "worm" in Hebrew is "tola'at," which can refer to a maggot or a crimson worm used for dye. This imagery suggests extreme lowliness and insignificance. In the cultural context of ancient Israel, being likened to a worm would convey utter worthlessness and vulnerability. This expression of profound humility and suffering is often seen as a prophetic foreshadowing of Christ's own humiliation and suffering on the cross, as He took on the sins of humanity and was treated with contempt.

scorned by men
The psalmist expresses being ridiculed and mocked by others. This scorn is not just personal but public, indicating a widespread rejection. In the historical context, public scorn was a severe form of social ostracism, often reserved for those considered cursed or condemned. This phrase connects to the New Testament accounts of Jesus' crucifixion, where He was mocked by soldiers, religious leaders, and passersby (Matthew 27:39-44). The scorn He endured fulfills the prophetic nature of this psalm, highlighting the Messiah's suffering and rejection by humanity.

and despised by the people
The term "despised" indicates a deep-seated contempt and rejection. In biblical times, being despised by the community meant being cut off from social and religious life, often seen as a sign of divine disfavor. This phrase underscores the collective rejection faced by the psalmist, which is mirrored in the life of Jesus Christ. Isaiah 53:3 describes the Messiah as "despised and rejected by men," further linking this psalm to the suffering servant motif. The rejection by "the people" can be seen as representative of Israel's initial rejection of Jesus as the Messiah, as well as humanity's broader rejection of God's anointed one.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 22, David is expressing deep anguish and feelings of abandonment, which prophetically point to the suffering of Christ.

2. Jesus Christ
Psalm 22 is often seen as a messianic psalm, with this verse reflecting the humiliation and suffering Jesus endured, particularly during His crucifixion.

3. The People
Refers to those who scorn and despise, representing both the immediate context of David's adversaries and the broader prophetic fulfillment in the rejection of Christ by the people of His time.
Teaching Points
Understanding Humility
The term "worm" (Hebrew: tola'at) signifies extreme humility and lowliness. As believers, we are called to embrace humility, recognizing our dependence on God.

Enduring Scorn for Righteousness
Just as David and ultimately Christ endured scorn, Christians may face ridicule for their faith. We are encouraged to stand firm, knowing our identity in Christ.

Prophetic Fulfillment
This verse highlights the prophetic nature of the Psalms, showing how Old Testament writings point to the life and mission of Jesus.

Identifying with Christ's Suffering
Reflect on how Christ's suffering and rejection were part of God's redemptive plan, encouraging us to find purpose in our own trials.

Responding to Rejection
When faced with rejection or scorn, believers can look to Christ's example of forgiveness and love, responding with grace and truth.

What Does Psalm 22:6 Mean
But

• The psalmist shifts abruptly from confident prayer to painful lament: “But I am a worm…” (Psalm 22:6).

• This sharp contrast mirrors the experience of Christ on the cross—moving from trusting the Father (Psalm 22:3–5) to feeling utterly forsaken (Matthew 27:46).

• Throughout Scripture, “but” often marks a turning point where faith must confront harsh reality (2 Corinthians 4:8–9; Jonah 2:4).

I am a worm

• The word picture emphasizes deep humiliation and weakness. David, and ultimately Jesus, identifies with the lowest of creatures to show voluntary self-abasement (Philippians 2:7–8).

• Isaiah 41:14 likewise comforts Israel, calling the nation a “worm” yet assuring God’s help—underscoring that God values what the world belittles.

• In the Gospels, Jesus allows Himself to be mocked and beaten, embracing the role of suffering servant (Mark 14:65).

And not a man

• Feelings of being dehumanized surface here. Enemies treat the righteous sufferer as less than human (Isaiah 52:14).

• Jesus fulfilled this when soldiers and bystanders denied Him ordinary dignity (Luke 23:18–21).

• Yet He remained fully God and fully man, choosing to endure such treatment for our redemption (Hebrews 2:9–10).

Scorned by men

• Open ridicule piles onto physical pain. David experienced this from Saul’s courtiers; Jesus faced it from Roman guards (Matthew 27:29–31).

• Proverbs 14:31 warns that mocking the poor insults the Maker, highlighting the seriousness of such contempt.

• Though scorned, the sufferer entrusts justice to God (1 Peter 2:23).

Despised by the people

• Public rejection completes the picture: leaders and crowds unite in disdain (John 19:15).

• Isaiah 53:3 foretells the Messiah would be “despised and rejected”—language echoed here.

• Yet Psalm 22 ultimately turns to global praise (vv. 27–31), proving that temporary rejection cannot thwart God’s plan (Acts 2:23–24).

summary

Psalm 22:6 paints a vivid portrait of the Messiah’s voluntary humiliation: the startling contrast (“But”), the depth of His lowliness (“worm”), the stripping of dignity (“not a man”), the active mockery (“scorned”), and the sweeping rejection (“despised”). Each phrase foreshadows Jesus’ passion, assuring believers that He fully entered our suffering to secure our salvation. What seems like utter defeat becomes the foundation for triumphant praise, inviting us to trust the God who turns scorn into glory.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - But I am a worm, and no man (comp. Job 25:6; Isaiah 41:14). The worm is a symbol of extreme weakness and helplessness - it is naturally despised, derided, trodden upon. A reproach of men, and despised of the people (Comp. Isaiah 49:7; Isaiah 53:3; and for the fulfilment, see Matthew 27:39). How deeply Christ was "despised of the people" appeared most evidently when they expressed their desire that, instead of him, a murderer should be granted to them (Acts 3:14).
