Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I am bent and brought low;
This phrase reflects a state of deep physical and emotional distress. The imagery of being "bent" suggests a posture of defeat or overwhelming burden, often associated with sin or suffering. In biblical times, physical posture was a significant indicator of one's spiritual and emotional state. The psalmist's condition can be compared to the afflictions of Job, who also experienced profound suffering and humiliation (Job 30:16-17). The phrase "brought low" indicates a humbling or abasement, which is a common theme in the Psalms, where the psalmist often cries out to God from a place of humility and need (Psalm 51:17). This condition can also be seen as a type of Christ, who was "brought low" in His incarnation and suffering (Philippians 2:7-8).

all day long I go about mourning.
The continuous nature of the psalmist's mourning suggests an unrelenting sorrow or grief. Mourning in the ancient Near Eastern context often involved wearing sackcloth, ashes, and public displays of lamentation (Genesis 37:34). This phrase indicates a state of perpetual lament, possibly due to personal sin, illness, or external persecution. The psalmist's experience resonates with the lamentations of Jeremiah, who mourned the destruction of Jerusalem (Lamentations 1:1-4). Mourning is also a theme in the Beatitudes, where Jesus blesses those who mourn, promising comfort (Matthew 5:4). This reflects the biblical principle that God is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit (Psalm 34:18).

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
Traditionally attributed as the author of Psalm 38, David is expressing deep personal anguish and repentance. His life, marked by both triumphs and failures, provides a backdrop for understanding the depth of his lament.

2. God
The ultimate recipient of David's lament. David's relationship with God is central to the psalm, as he seeks divine mercy and healing.

3. Israel
The nation of Israel, often represented by its king, is indirectly involved as David's personal struggles often reflect the spiritual state of the nation.
Teaching Points
The Reality of Sin's Consequences
Sin can lead to physical, emotional, and spiritual distress. David's lament is a reminder of the weight of sin and the need for repentance.

The Importance of Lament
Lament is a biblical way to process pain and seek God's intervention. It is an honest expression of our struggles before God.

God's Compassion for the Brokenhearted
Despite our lowly state, God is compassionate and attentive to those who are brokenhearted and contrite.

The Call to Repentance
True repentance involves acknowledging our sin and turning to God for forgiveness and restoration.

Hope in God's Deliverance
Even in the depths of despair, there is hope in God's ability to lift us up and restore us.

What Does Psalm 38:6 Mean
I am bent

- David pictures himself physically hunched over, overwhelmed by the weight of sin’s consequences and God’s chastening hand (Psalm 38:3-4).

- Similar imagery appears in Psalm 34:18, where “The LORD is near to the brokenhearted,” affirming that the Lord draws close when a believer feels crushed.

- Proverbs 14:12 warns that the way of sin “ends in death,” emphasizing that the heaviness David feels is not imaginary but the real burden of guilt.

- Luke 18:13 shows the tax collector “standing at a distance, not even willing to lift his eyes to heaven,” a New Testament echo of the same humbled posture before God.

and brought low

- David confesses that he has been reduced, humbled, and stripped of self-reliance (Psalm 51:17).

- Psalm 147:6 declares, “The LORD sustains the humble,” revealing that God’s purpose in lowering David is restorative, not destructive.

- James 4:6 affirms, “God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble,” showing that being brought low is the pathway to receiving fresh grace.

- Isaiah 57:15 promises God “revives the spirit of the lowly,” assuring readers that this low place is precisely where revival begins.

all day long I go about mourning

- David’s sorrow is continuous, not momentary; sin’s impact lingers until confession and cleansing occur (1 John 1:9).

- Psalm 32:3-4 describes similar nonstop anguish: “For day and night Your hand was heavy upon me.”

- Lamentations 3:19-23 reminds believers that even in relentless mourning, God’s compassions “are new every morning,” holding out hope for renewal.

- Romans 7:24 captures Paul’s cry, “What a wretched man I am!” demonstrating that sincere believers in every age know this ongoing grief over indwelling sin.

summary

Psalm 38:6 portrays the believer bowed down under the real, painful weight of sin, humbled by God’s discipline, and experiencing continual grief until repentance restores fellowship. Yet Scripture consistently pairs this honest confession with God’s sure promise of nearness, grace, and ultimate revival for all who humble themselves before Him.

(6) I am troubled.--Better,

I am made to writhe (see margin),

I am bowed down exceedingly,

All day long I go about squalid.

(See Psalm 35:14, and comp. Isaiah 21:3.) The usual Oriental signs of mourning are alluded to in the last clause.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 6. - I am troubled; literally, bent; which some take physically, and explain as "twisted by violent spasms," others, psychically, as "warped in mind," "driven crazy." I am bowed down greatly; i.e. bowed to earth, crooked, as men are in extreme old age, or by such maladies as lumbago and rheumatism. I go mourning all the day long. My gait is that of a mourner - I stoop and move slowly.
