May those who fear You
This phrase refers to individuals who have a reverent awe and respect for God. In biblical context, "fear" often denotes a deep reverence and commitment to God's commandments. This is seen throughout the Old Testament, such as in Proverbs 1:7, where the fear of the Lord is described as the beginning of knowledge. The fear of God is a foundational aspect of the covenant relationship between God and His people, as seen in Deuteronomy 10:12. It implies a lifestyle of obedience and worship, aligning with the wisdom literature's emphasis on living in accordance with God's will.

turn to me
The psalmist expresses a desire for those who fear God to turn to him, suggesting a community of like-minded believers. This reflects the communal aspect of faith in ancient Israel, where worship and adherence to God's laws were practiced collectively. The phrase can also imply seeking guidance or fellowship, as seen in the New Testament where believers are encouraged to gather and support one another (Hebrews 10:24-25). The psalmist's request may indicate a desire for mutual encouragement and accountability among those who are faithful to God.

those who know Your testimonies
"Testimonies" refers to God's laws and decrees, as given in the Torah. Knowing God's testimonies implies not just intellectual understanding but also living in accordance with them. This is a recurring theme in Psalm 119, which emphasizes the importance of God's word in guiding the believer's life. The historical context of this phrase highlights the centrality of the Torah in Jewish life and worship, as seen in passages like Deuteronomy 6:6-9, where the Israelites are instructed to keep God's commandments at the forefront of their daily lives. This knowledge is not merely academic but transformative, shaping the character and actions of those who follow God.

What Does Psalm 119:79 Mean
May those who fear You

- The psalmist speaks first about a specific group: “those who fear You.” Scripture consistently portrays the fear of the LORD as reverent awe that leads to obedience (Proverbs 1:7: “The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge”).

- Psalm 25:14 tells us, “The LORD confides in those who fear Him,” showing that a respectful, obedient heart enjoys special closeness with God.

- By invoking this group, the writer aligns himself with people who are already serious about honoring God (Psalm 31:19; Malachi 3:16).

Turn to me

- The request is not for personal popularity but for mutually edifying fellowship. Psalm 119:63 affirms, “I am a companion of all who fear You, and of those who keep Your precepts.”

- When believers turn toward one another, they strengthen each other’s walk (Hebrews 10:24–25, “Let us consider how to spur one another on to love and good deeds”).

- Bullet points of what “turn to me” accomplishes:

• Encouragement in trials (2 Corinthians 1:3–4)

• Accountability to stay faithful (Galatians 6:1)

• Shared wisdom drawn from God’s Word (Proverbs 27:17)

Those who know Your testimonies

- The third clause narrows the description further. These God-fearers are also people who “know” God’s testimonies—His revealed words, works, and statutes (Psalm 19:7–8, “The testimony of the LORD is trustworthy, making wise the simple”).

- Knowing in Scripture is relational and experiential (John 17:3). It moves beyond information to wholehearted allegiance (James 1:22).

- Cross references that illustrate this depth of knowledge:

• Deuteronomy 6:6, “These words I command you today are to be upon your hearts.”

• John 14:21, “Whoever has My commandments and keeps them is the one who loves Me.”

• 1 John 2:3, “By this we can be sure that we have come to know Him: if we keep His commandments.”

summary

Psalm 119:79 reveals the psalmist’s longing for fellowship with a distinct community—believers who both revere God and actively live out His Word. He prays that such people will “turn to” him, forming bonds that encourage faithfulness and mutual growth. In practical terms, the verse urges us to seek companionship with those who fear God and cherish His testimonies, so that together we may walk in steadfast obedience and deeper intimacy with the Lord.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 79. - Let those that fear thee turn unto me; or, "return to me;" i.e. recover their confidence in me, when they see that I am not forsaken of thee (see vers. 76, 77), but am the recipient of thy "tender mercies." And those that have known thy testimonies; or, according to another reading, "and let them know thy testimonies;" i.e. "let them learn from my experience to know thy precepts better."
