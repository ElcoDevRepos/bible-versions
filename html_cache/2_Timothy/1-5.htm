I am reminded of your sincere faith
Paul begins by acknowledging Timothy's genuine faith, which is a central theme in his letters. This faith is not superficial but deeply rooted and authentic. The term "sincere" suggests a faith without hypocrisy, aligning with the biblical call for believers to worship in spirit and truth (John 4:24). This phrase highlights the importance of personal faith in the life of a believer, a recurring theme in Paul's epistles.

which first dwelt in your grandmother Lois
The mention of Lois, Timothy's grandmother, underscores the significance of familial influence in spiritual development. In the Jewish tradition, the family played a crucial role in religious education, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:6-7, where parents are instructed to teach God's commandments to their children. Lois's faith is a testament to the generational transmission of belief, emphasizing the impact of a godly heritage.

and your mother Eunice
Eunice, Timothy's mother, is also noted for her faith, which she passed on to her son. This highlights the role of women in the early church and their influence in nurturing faith within the family. Acts 16:1 reveals that Eunice was a Jewish believer married to a Greek, indicating a mixed cultural background. Despite potential challenges, Eunice's faith remained steadfast, illustrating the power of a committed spiritual life.

and I am convinced is in you as well
Paul expresses confidence in Timothy's faith, affirming its continuity from previous generations. This assurance reflects the apostle's close relationship with Timothy, whom he regards as a spiritual son (1 Timothy 1:2). The phrase also serves as an encouragement for Timothy to remain steadfast in his faith amidst trials, echoing the exhortation found in Hebrews 10:23 to hold unswervingly to the hope professed.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Spiritual Heritage
The faith of Lois and Eunice demonstrates the impact of a godly legacy. Parents and grandparents play a crucial role in nurturing faith in the next generation.

Sincere Faith as a Foundation
Timothy's faith is described as sincere, meaning it is genuine and without hypocrisy. Believers are called to cultivate a faith that is authentic and deeply rooted in Christ.

The Role of Mentorship
Paul’s relationship with Timothy highlights the importance of mentorship in the Christian life. Mature believers should invest in the spiritual growth of younger Christians.

Faith in Action
The faith of Lois and Eunice was not passive but active, influencing Timothy's life and ministry. Our faith should be evident in our actions and influence those around us.

Generational Influence
The passage encourages believers to consider the legacy they are leaving. Are we passing down a sincere faith to the next generation?

What Does 2 Timothy 1:5 Mean
I am reminded

Paul begins the verse by telling Timothy, “I am reminded …” (2 Timothy 1:5). The apostle’s memory is stirred every time he prays for his young protégé, just as he has said a verse earlier: “I constantly remember you in my prayers night and day” (2 Timothy 1:3). Throughout his letters Paul often pauses to recall believers with gratitude (Philippians 1:3; Romans 1:9). Here the recollection is deeply personal:

•Paul’s chains make him cherish faithful partners all the more (2 Timothy 2:9).

•Remembering Timothy’s walk with Christ fuels Paul’s own courage to keep proclaiming the gospel (2 Timothy 1:8).

The simple phrase reminds us that Christian leaders thrive on encouraging memories of fellow believers who stay true to Jesus.

of your sincere faith

What stands out in Paul’s mind is “your sincere faith.” In 1 Timothy 1:5, “sincere faith” is linked with love and a pure heart—no room for hypocrisy. The adjective points to faith that is:

•Genuine—no pretense or performance (James 2:17).

•Proven—tested yet standing firm (1 Peter 1:7).

•Visible—expressed in deeds as well as words (1 John 3:18).

Paul is confident Timothy’s trust in Christ is authentic, not derivative or merely intellectual. Such sincerity is a hallmark of every believer made new by the Spirit (2 Corinthians 6:6).

which first dwelt in your grandmother Lois and your mother Eunice

Timothy’s faith story did not begin with him. It “first dwelt in your grandmother Lois and your mother Eunice.” Acts 16:1 notes that Eunice was “a Jewish believer,” and by inference Lois was the same. Their example highlights:

•The home as the primary setting for passing on the faith (Deuteronomy 6:6-7).

•The strategic influence of godly women; Proverbs 22:6 and Psalm 78:4 echo the call to train up the next generation.

•A faith that “dwelt” in them—resided, shaped their decisions, and overflowed in ministry to others.

Lois and Eunice embody the quiet yet powerful ministry of nurturing belief in children long before pastors or missionaries enter the picture.

and I am convinced is in you as well

Paul finishes the thought with a warm affirmation: “and I am convinced is in you as well.” Earlier he had laid hands on Timothy (1 Timothy 4:14), seen his gifts, and watched him persevere through hardship (1 Timothy 1:18-19). His confidence rests on:

•Timothy’s consistent track record (2 Timothy 3:14-15).

•The confirming witness of the Spirit, who assures both Timothy and Paul (2 Corinthians 1:24).

•The observable fruit of ministry that flows from true faith (Philippians 2:12-13).

Such reassurance bolsters Timothy to “fan into flame the gift of God” (2 Timothy 1:6), knowing that the same authentic faith of his mother and grandmother pulses in his own life.

summary

2 Timothy 1:5 shows Paul cherishing the memory of Timothy’s genuine faith, tracing its roots through a godly grandmother and mother, and expressing unwavering confidence that the same living trust now animates Timothy. The verse celebrates intergenerational discipleship, the beauty of authentic, unhypocritical faith, and the encouragement believers draw from remembering how God has worked in one another’s lives.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(5) When I call to remembrance the unfeigned faith that is in thee.--It is for the "unfeigned faith" which he was confident dwelt still in his dearest and best loved companion, whom he had intrusted with the care of the Ephesian church, that he thanked God. (See 2Timothy 1:3.)

It is more than probable that some special instance of this unfeigned faith on the part of the chief pastor of Ephesus had come to the Apostle's knowledge, and cheered that great loving heart of his while he languished in prison.

Which dwelt first in thy grandmother Lois, and thy mother Eunice . . .--We know, in the course of his second missionary journey (Acts 16:1-3) St. Paul was brought into contact with this pious family at Lystra. It has been suggested that Lois, Eunice, and Timothy were kinsfolk of St. Paul, hence his intimacy with the family, and his knowledge of their faith; hence, too, perhaps, his devoted and unbroken friendship for Timothy. We are told (Acts 16:1) that this Eunice was a Jewess, married to a Greek. Lystra is no great distance from Tarsus--whence St. Paul came. The supposition is just possible; but it is only an ingenious thought, there being no data to support it. Of the names--Lois is the same with the more familiar Lais; Eunice is an equivalent of the Latin Victoria.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 5. - Having been reminded of for when I call to remembrance, A.V.; in thee for that in thee, A.V. Unfeigned (ἀνυποκρίτου); as 1 Timothy 1:5 (see also Romans 12:9; 2 Corinthians 6:6; 1 Peter 1:22; James 3:17). Having been reminded, etc. (see preceding note). Thy grandmother Lois. Μάμμη properly corresponds exactly to our word "mamma." In 4 Macc. 16:9, Οὐ μάμμη κληθεῖσα μακαρισθήσομαι, "I shall never be called a happy grandmother," and here (the only place where it is found in the New Testament) it has the sense of "grandmother." It is hardly a real word, and has no place in Stephens' 'Thes.,' except incidentally by comparison with πάππα. It has, however, a classical usage. The proper word for a "grandmother" is τήθη. Lois; a name not found elsewhere, possibly meaning "good," or "excellent," from the same root as λωί'τερος and λώι'στος. This and the following Eunice are examples of the frequent use of Greek or Latin names by Jews. Eunice, we know from Acts 16:1, was a Jewess and a Christian, as it would seem her mother Lois was before her.
