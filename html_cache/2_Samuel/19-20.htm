For your servant knows that I have sinned
This phrase reflects a confession of wrongdoing, which is a recurring theme in the Bible. The acknowledgment of sin is crucial for reconciliation with God and others. In the context of 2 Samuel, this is Shimei speaking, who had previously cursed King David. The act of confessing sin is seen throughout Scripture, such as in Psalm 51, where David himself confesses his sin with Bathsheba. The recognition of sin is the first step toward repentance and forgiveness, aligning with the biblical principle that God is merciful to those who admit their faults.

so here I am today
This indicates a present action and willingness to make amends. It shows a sense of urgency and immediacy in seeking forgiveness. The phrase suggests a change of heart and a desire to rectify past wrongs. In biblical narratives, coming forward to make amends is often seen as a sign of genuine repentance, as seen in the story of Zacchaeus in Luke 19:8, who immediately sought to repay those he had wronged.

as the first of all the house of Joseph
The "house of Joseph" refers to the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh, descendants of Joseph, one of the twelve sons of Jacob. This phrase highlights the significance of Shimei's actions, as he represents a larger group. The tribes of Joseph were significant in Israel's history, often associated with leadership and blessing, as seen in Genesis 48:20. Shimei's position as the first to come forward may indicate leadership or influence within his tribe, emphasizing the importance of his gesture of reconciliation.

to come down to meet my lord the king.
This phrase signifies humility and submission. "To come down" implies leaving one's place of comfort or status to approach someone of higher authority. Meeting "my lord the king" shows respect and acknowledgment of David's rightful position as king. This act of coming down is reminiscent of the humility required to approach God, as seen in the parable of the Prodigal Son in Luke 15:18-20, where the son returns to his father in humility. The phrase also foreshadows the ultimate submission and reconciliation found in Jesus Christ, who is often referred to as the King of Kings in the New Testament.

What Does 2 Samuel 19:20 Mean
For your servant knows that I have sinned

- Shimei owns his guilt with no excuses, echoing David’s own transparency in Psalm 51:3, “For I know my transgressions, and my sin is always before me.”

- Genuine confession is the doorway to mercy (Proverbs 28:13; 1 John 1:9).

- His words fulfill what he refused in 2 Samuel 16:5-13—moving from cursing to contrition.

- Like the prodigal saying, “Father, I have sinned” (Luke 15:21), Shimei models the posture every sinner must take before the true King.

so here I am today

- Repentance is acted upon immediately; Shimei does not wait for a safer moment. Psalm 32:5-6 urges the same haste, and Paul presses, “Now is the day of salvation” (2 Corinthians 6:2).

- Present-tense obedience shows faith is alive (James 2:18).

- Standing in David’s path, he places himself entirely at the king’s mercy—no backup plan, no delay.

as the first of all the house of Joseph

- “House of Joseph” refers to the northern tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh (Genesis 48:20; Judges 1:22). These tribes often led Israel’s internal politics; Shimei’s early arrival signals a public, representative repentance.

- By coming first, he opens a door for the rest to follow, just as Zacchaeus’s swift repentance stirred a household (Luke 19:8-9).

- True repentance has ripple effects; Acts 26:20 notes how Paul called people to “repent and turn to God, performing deeds worthy of repentance.” Shimei is already doing such a deed.

to come down to meet my lord the king

- “Come down” suggests humility—descending from his own territory to David’s presence (compare John 3:30, “He must increase, but I must decrease”).

- Meeting the king rather than avoiding him parallels Hebrews 4:16, “Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence.”

- The title “my lord the king” reaffirms allegiance broken in 2 Samuel 16. What once was rebellion is now reverence, prefiguring every knee bowing to Christ the King (Revelation 17:14; Philippians 2:10-11).

- David’s later pardon of Shimei (2 Samuel 19:23) pictures the gospel pattern: sincere confession met by sovereign mercy.

summary

Shimei’s single sentence moves step-by-step from confession (“I have sinned”) to urgent action (“here I am today”), to exemplary leadership (“first of the house of Joseph”), and finally to surrendered allegiance (“to meet my lord the king”). The verse showcases the heart of repentance: honest admission, immediate approach, influential example, and humble submission to the rightful king—foreshadowing how every sinner must come to Christ for full, royal forgiveness.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(20) The house of Joseph.--Shimei was not strictly of "the house of Joseph," but of Benjamin; and it is plain that Joseph, as the name of the most prominent member, stands for all the tribes outside of Judah. This usage is well recognised at a later time (see 1Chronicles 5:1-2; Amos 5:15), and it has hence been argued that it indicates a late date for the composition of the book; but it is also found in Psalm 80:1-2; Psalm 81:5 (the date of which it would be rash to attempt to fix), in the reign of Solomon, 1Kings 11:28, and probably very early in Judges 1:35. There is no reason why the expression may not have been used at the earliest date when there began to be a certain separation and distinction between Judah and the other tribes, which was soon after the conquest of Canaan. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - The first.., of all the house of Joseph. Shimei, who was a Benjamite, could not have thus claimed to be the representative of the northern tribes, had he remained on the western bank, where "half the people of Israel" were assembled. Strictly, "the house of Joseph" signified the tribe of Ephraim (Judges 1:22, 35; and comp. Psalm 78:67), and in this sense Shimei did not belong to it. But Ephraim claimed a supremacy over all Israel; and one cause of the opposition to David certainly was the transference of the leadership to the tribe of Judah. Even the long reign of Solomon failed to weld the tribes together, and as soon as the reins of power fell into the weak hands of Rehobeam, an Ephraimite. Jeroboam, whom Solomon had made "ruler ever all the charge of the house of Joseph" (1 Kings 11:28), quickly wrested the ten tribes from him. In Amos 5:6 "the house of Joseph" signifies all the northern tribes, for the reason given in 1 Chronicles 5:1, 2; and such is its sense here. And Shimei compressed many powerful arguments in the phrase. For as a Benjamite he offered David the allegiance of the tribe which had given Israel its first king; while, as an Israelite, he professed also to represent the leading house of Ephraim, and all the northern tribes which usually followed its bidding.
