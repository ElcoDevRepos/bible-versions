Was he not more honored than the Three?
This phrase refers to Abishai, the brother of Joab, who was one of David's mighty men. The "Three" are the elite warriors among David's mighty men, known for their extraordinary feats of bravery and skill in battle. Abishai's recognition as being "more honored" suggests his exceptional valor and leadership, which earned him a distinguished reputation. This honor reflects the biblical principle that God exalts those who demonstrate faithfulness and courage. The comparison to the "Three" highlights the high regard in which Abishai was held, despite not being part of this elite group. This can be seen as a reminder of the biblical theme that God often elevates individuals based on their character and actions rather than their official status or title.

And he became their commander,
Abishai's role as a commander signifies his leadership abilities and the trust David placed in him. In the context of ancient Israel, military leadership was crucial for the survival and success of the nation. Abishai's appointment as a commander underscores his strategic acumen and ability to inspire and lead men in battle. This mirrors the biblical theme of God raising leaders who are capable of guiding His people through challenging times. Abishai's leadership can also be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate leader and commander of God's people, guiding them with wisdom and strength.

even though he was not included among the Three.
This phrase emphasizes that Abishai's achievements and leadership were recognized despite not being part of the most elite group of warriors. It highlights the biblical principle that one's worth and contribution are not solely determined by formal titles or positions. Abishai's story is a testament to the idea that God values faithfulness and service over status. This can be connected to the New Testament teaching that in the body of Christ, every member has a unique role and is honored for their contribution, regardless of their position. It also reflects the theme of God's grace, where He often chooses the unlikely or overlooked to fulfill His purposes, as seen throughout Scripture.

Persons / Places / Events

2. The Three
This group refers to the three most elite warriors among David's mighty men. They were renowned for their extraordinary feats and held a special place of honor.

3. David's Mighty Men
A group of elite warriors who served King David with distinction. They were known for their courage and loyalty, and their exploits are detailed in 2 Samuel 23.
Teaching Points
Honor and Leadership
Abishai was honored and became a commander because of his deeds, not just his title. True leadership is earned through actions and character.

God's Recognition
Even if not part of the "elite," God recognizes and honors those who serve faithfully and courageously.

Role of Supportive Leadership
Abishai's role shows the importance of supportive leadership. He was not one of the three, yet he played a crucial role in David's success.

Courage and Loyalty
Abishai's account encourages believers to be courageous and loyal in their service to God and others, trusting that God sees and rewards faithfulness.

What Does 2 Samuel 23:19 Mean
Was he not more honored than the Three?

• Abishai’s exploits had already been listed in 2 Samuel 23:18, where he “lifted his spear against three hundred men and killed them.” Such courage placed him in a league all his own.

• The line in verse 19 spotlights public recognition: Abishai’s reputation surpassed even that of the famed Three (see also 1 Chronicles 11:20: “He was doubly honored above the Three”).

• Honor in Scripture is tied to faithful service, not self-promotion—Proverbs 27:2 reminds, “Let another praise you, and not your own mouth.” Abishai earned esteem by risking his life for God’s anointed king (1 Samuel 26:6–12).

• His distinction underlines a key principle: God notices and records acts of bravery and loyalty, even when others may occupy higher formal ranks (compare David’s own rise in 1 Samuel 18:30).

And he became their commander

• Recognition turned into responsibility. David placed Abishai over the elite warriors, much as he later entrusted him with leading a division against the Ammonites (2 Samuel 10:9–10) and with a third of the army against Absalom (2 Samuel 18:2).

• Scripture often links honor with greater stewardship (Luke 12:48). Abishai’s promotion illustrates that God-given accomplishments equip a servant for broader leadership.

• His command did not diminish the Three; instead, it provided unified direction for the whole corps of mighty men, echoing the orderly structure God values (Exodus 18:21, 1 Corinthians 14:40).

even though he was not included among the Three

• Abishai’s role shows that status and service can differ. Membership in the Three was fixed—Josheb-Basshebeth, Eleazar, and Shammah (2 Samuel 23:8–12)—yet leadership could rest on someone outside that inner circle.

• This separation prevents us from equating titles with effectiveness. The body of Christ likewise assigns differing functions while honoring every part (1 Corinthians 12:18–21).

• Abishai’s story guards against envy: he celebrated his commission without resenting the distinct glory of the Three, modeling Romans 12:10, “Outdo one another in showing honor.”

summary

2 Samuel 23:19 highlights that Abishai’s extraordinary valor earned him unique honor, prompting David to appoint him commander over the mighty men. Though not one of the original Three, his faithfulness and achievements positioned him for leadership. The verse teaches that God-given honor stems from devoted service, that responsibility accompanies recognition, and that meaningful leadership is possible without holding the most prestigious title.
