The rich man
In the context of 2 Samuel 12, the "rich man" represents King David. This parable, told by the prophet Nathan, is a metaphor for David's actions regarding Bathsheba and Uriah. The rich man's wealth symbolizes David's power and resources as king. In biblical times, wealth was often measured by livestock, which were essential for food, clothing, and sacrifices. This imagery highlights the disparity between David's abundance and Uriah's limited resources.

had a great number of sheep and cattle
Sheep and cattle were significant assets in ancient Israel, representing wealth and prosperity. The mention of a "great number" emphasizes the extent of the rich man's resources, contrasting with the poor man's single ewe lamb in the parable. This abundance is symbolic of David's many blessings and privileges as king. In a broader biblical context, sheep often symbolize God's people (Psalm 100:3), and cattle can represent material wealth (Genesis 13:2). The rich man's possession of many sheep and cattle underscores his responsibility to use his resources justly and compassionately, a responsibility David failed to uphold in his dealings with Uriah.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Rich Man
Represents those with abundance and wealth. In the context of Nathan's parable to David, he symbolizes King David himself, who had many blessings and resources.

2. Sheep and Cattle
Symbolize wealth and abundance in ancient times. They are used in the parable to illustrate the rich man's vast resources compared to the poor man's single lamb.

3. Nathan
The prophet who delivers God's message to King David through this parable. He plays a crucial role in confronting David about his sin with Bathsheba.

4. David
The King of Israel, who is being indirectly addressed through Nathan's parable. His actions with Bathsheba and Uriah are the underlying issue being confronted.

5. Parable
A teaching method used by Nathan to reveal a moral truth to David. It serves as a mirror to David's own actions, prompting self-reflection and repentance.
Teaching Points
Wealth and Responsibility
The abundance of resources comes with a responsibility to act justly and compassionately. The rich man's failure to do so is a cautionary tale for those blessed with much.

Self-Reflection and Repentance
Like David, we must be open to recognizing our faults and seeking repentance. Nathan's parable serves as a tool for self-examination.

God's Justice
The parable illustrates that God sees all actions and will hold individuals accountable. It is a reminder of divine justice and the importance of living righteously.

The Power of Parables
Parables are effective in revealing truths and prompting change. They encourage listeners to see themselves in the account and reflect on their own lives.

What Does 2 Samuel 12:2 Mean
The rich man

• In Nathan’s divinely inspired parable, this wealthy landowner (2 Samuel 12:1–4) pictures David himself, whom God had exalted from shepherd to king (2 Samuel 7:8).

• His status underscores the immense privilege David enjoyed—“The LORD gave you the house of Israel and Judah” (2 Samuel 12:8).

• Scripture consistently warns that earthly prosperity can mask spiritual danger (Deuteronomy 8:11–14; Luke 12:16–21), yet it also teaches that every gift comes from God and carries responsibility (1 Chronicles 29:12; 1 Timothy 6:17–18).

• The contrast between this rich man and the poor man in verse 3 sets the stage for exposing injustice, much like the prophets later condemned those who “devour widows’ houses” (Isaiah 10:1–2; Mark 12:40).

had a great number

• Abundance is explicit: he possessed “a great many” animals. The wording highlights a surplus beyond personal need (Genesis 13:2; Deuteronomy 8:13).

• God never condemns the possession of wealth in itself; He condemns hoarding it selfishly (Proverbs 3:27–28; James 5:1–5).

• “To whom much is given, much will be required” (Luke 12:48). The verse invites us to examine whether plenty leads to generosity or to presumption.

• David had received “much”: victory, kingdom, wives (2 Samuel 12:7–8). The problem was not God’s gift but David’s grasping for more (2 Samuel 11:2–4).

of sheep and cattle

• In agrarian Israel, livestock equaled both wealth and the capacity to provide hospitality (Genesis 18:7–8; Job 1:3).

• The rich man’s many animals could easily have supplied the traveler’s meal (2 Samuel 12:4), underscoring his callousness when he seizes the poor man’s lone ewe.

• Nathan’s imagery recalls shepherd motifs throughout Scripture—God as Shepherd who cares sacrificially (Psalm 23:1; John 10:11)—heightening the offense when a “shepherd” exploits rather than protects (Ezekiel 34:2–3).

• Spiritually, the sheep and cattle underline stewardship: possessions are entrusted by God to serve others, not to indulge self (Proverbs 11:24–25; 2 Corinthians 9:8–11).

summary

2 Samuel 12:2 spotlights a materially blessed man who should have used his God-given abundance to bless others. In Nathan’s story the figure exposes David’s heart: privilege had bred presumption. The verse warns that plenty without compassion leads to grave sin, while reminding us that all resources—like the rich man’s flocks—are entrusted by the Lord for righteous, generous stewardship.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(2) There were two men.--The parable is designed to bring out David's indignation against the offender without being so clear as to awaken at first any suspicion of a personal application. It does not allude to the special crimes of David, but to the meanness and selfishness of the transaction--qualities which David was still in a condition to appreciate. For a similar use of parables see 2Samuel 14:2-11; 1Kings 20:35-41.
