When David was told
This phrase indicates a moment of communication and revelation to King David. In the context of ancient Israel, the king was often informed of significant events by messengers or advisors. This reflects the hierarchical structure of the monarchy, where information was crucial for decision-making. David, as a king, was responsible for maintaining justice and order, and being informed was essential for his role.

what Saul’s concubine Rizpah, daughter of Aiah, had done,
Rizpah was a concubine of Saul, Israel's first king, which places her within the royal household, though with a lower status than a wife. Her actions, which involved a public display of mourning and protection of the bodies of her sons, highlight the cultural practices of mourning and the importance of burial rites in ancient Israel. Rizpah's lineage as the daughter of Aiah may indicate her noble or significant family background, which could have added weight to her actions. Her story is a poignant example of maternal devotion and has been seen as a type of intercession, reflecting themes of sacrifice and advocacy. This event connects to the broader narrative of Saul's house and the consequences of his actions, as well as the fulfillment of justice and the restoration of order under David's reign.

Persons / Places / Events
1. David
The King of Israel, known for his heart after God and his leadership. In this context, he is informed about Rizpah's actions.

2. Rizpah
The daughter of Aiah and a concubine of Saul. Her actions of mourning and protecting the bodies of her sons demonstrate her devotion and maternal love.

3. Saul
The first King of Israel, whose actions led to a famine in the land due to his breach of a covenant with the Gibeonites.

4. Gibeonites
A group that had a covenant with Israel, which Saul violated, leading to a famine as divine retribution.

5. Aiah
The father of Rizpah, mentioned to provide her lineage and background.
Teaching Points
The Consequences of Sin
Saul's breach of the covenant with the Gibeonites had long-lasting effects, reminding us that our actions can have far-reaching consequences.

The Power of a Mother's Love
Rizpah's dedication to her sons, even in death, exemplifies the depth of maternal love and commitment.

Justice and Atonement
The need to address past wrongs is crucial for healing and restoration, as seen in David's response to the famine.

Leadership and Responsibility
David's role in addressing the famine highlights the importance of leaders taking responsibility for resolving issues, even those inherited from predecessors.

Faithfulness in Adversity
Rizpah's actions demonstrate faithfulness and perseverance, encouraging us to remain steadfast in difficult times.

What Does 2 Samuel 21:11 Mean
When David was told

– David’s leadership consistently pivots on the reports that reach him (2 Samuel 1:2-12; 4:9-12; 12:18-20).

– Here, the famine has already driven him to seek the LORD (21:1). Now fresh news presses him to finish the work of national repentance.

– A true shepherd-king does not ignore grief among his people; he moves toward it, just as the Good Shepherd later “saw the crowds and had compassion on them” (Matthew 9:36).

what Saul’s concubine

– The mention of “Saul’s concubine” recalls the lingering tension between the old dynasty and the new (2 Samuel 3:7; 16:21-22).

– Concubines in royal households symbolized dynasty and inheritance (2 Samuel 12:8; 1 Kings 2:21-22). By honoring one of Saul’s, David publicly affirms that the former king’s household still matters before God.

– In God’s economy, covenant mercy reaches backward as well as forward; unfinished wrongs must be set right (Numbers 35:33-34).

Rizpah, daughter of Aiah,

– Scripture lingers on her name twice in this chapter (21:8, 10), spotlighting a mother who will not abandon her sons.

– Her vigil mirrors earlier acts of covenant love:

• Jabesh-gilead’s rescue of Saul’s body (1 Samuel 31:11-13).

• Hannah’s yearly journeys for Samuel (1 Samuel 2:18-19).

• Mary standing near her crucified Son (John 19:25).

– Such faithfulness shines whether or not the world notices—and in God’s timing it always reaches the king’s ears.

had done

– Rizpah “took sackcloth and spread it out for herself on a rock… she did not allow the birds of the air… or the beasts of the field” (21:10).

– Her actions preach several truths:

• Respect for the dead accords with God’s law not to leave a body exposed overnight (Deuteronomy 21:22-23).

• Persistent intercession—day and night—moves heaven and earth (Luke 18:1-7).

• Genuine repentance must be matched by tangible righteousness; David responds by retrieving the bones of Saul and Jonathan for honorable burial (21:12-13).

– The result? “After that, God answered prayer for the land” (21:14). Heaven’s drought ends when earthly justice is completed.

summary

2 Samuel 21:11 marks the turning point of the whole episode. A grieving mother’s steadfast love is reported to a godly king, and he acts. David’s response unites mercy with justice, heals a national wound, and opens the way for God to lift the famine. The verse reminds us that God sees every hidden act of faithfulness and often uses the quiet courage of the overlooked to spur leaders toward righteousness and to pour out blessing on the land.
