In love,
The concept of love in the New Testament is often rooted in the Greek word "agape," which signifies a selfless, sacrificial, unconditional love. This type of love is central to Christian teachings and is exemplified by Jesus Christ's sacrifice on the cross. In the context of the early church, love was a binding force that united believers and was a testament to their faith in Christ. The Apostle Paul frequently emphasizes love as a fundamental Christian virtue (1 Corinthians 13, Galatians 5:22).

hold them in highest regard
This phrase underscores the importance of respect and honor within the Christian community, particularly towards leaders and those who labor in ministry. The early church was structured with leaders such as elders and deacons who were responsible for teaching, guiding, and caring for the congregation. Paul instructs believers to esteem these individuals highly, recognizing their dedication and service. This respect is not merely for their position but for their commitment to the work of the Gospel (1 Timothy 5:17).

because of their work.
The work referred to here involves the labor of preaching, teaching, and shepherding the flock. In the early church, leaders often faced persecution and hardship, making their work both challenging and vital for the growth and stability of the Christian community. The work of ministry is seen as a calling from God, and those who undertake it are to be supported and encouraged by the congregation (Ephesians 4:11-12, Hebrews 13:17).

Live in peace with one another.
Peace is a recurring theme in Paul's letters and is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22). The call to live in peace reflects the unity that should characterize the body of Christ. In a historical context, the early church faced external pressures from Roman authorities and internal conflicts among believers. Paul urges the Thessalonians to maintain harmony and resolve disputes amicably, reflecting the peace that Christ brings to His followers (Romans 12:18, Colossians 3:15).

A city in Macedonia where the church to whom Paul is writing is located. It was a significant city in the Roman Empire, known for its diverse population and strategic location.

3. Church Leaders
The individuals within the Thessalonian church who are being referred to in this verse. They are responsible for guiding and shepherding the congregation.

4. Thessalonian Believers
The recipients of the letter, who are being instructed to respect and love their leaders.

5. Peaceful Living
The event or state of being that Paul is urging the Thessalonian believers to pursue within their community.
Teaching Points
Respect for Leadership
Recognize and appreciate the hard work and dedication of church leaders. This respect is not just a duty but an expression of love.

The Role of Love
Love is the foundation for holding leaders in high regard. It is through love that we can genuinely appreciate and support those who lead us.

Pursuit of Peace
Actively seek to live in peace with others in the church. This requires effort, humility, and a willingness to resolve conflicts.

Community Harmony
Understand that the health of the church community depends on mutual respect and peaceful relationships among its members.

Biblical Obedience
Align your actions with biblical teachings by respecting leaders and striving for peace, as these are commands from Scripture.

What Does 1 Thessalonians 5:13 Mean
In love

Paul begins, “to esteem them very highly in love” (1 Thessalonians 5:13). Love sets both the tone and the motive. Rather than grudging respect, the heart response is warm affection. Jesus made love the badge of discipleship (John 13:34-35), and Peter urges, “Above all, love one another deeply” (1 Peter 4:8). When affection governs our attitudes, honor becomes a delight instead of a duty.

Hold them in highest regard

“To esteem them very highly” speaks of a deliberate choice. It’s not casual appreciation but active, intentional honor. Paul uses similar language when he tells believers to “give double honor to those who direct the affairs of the church” (1 Timothy 5:17) and to “honor one another above yourselves” (Romans 12:10). Practical ways include:

• Speaking well of leaders publicly and privately

• Defending them against unfair criticism (Proverbs 31:8-9)

• Sharing material support (Galatians 6:6)

• Imitating their faith-filled example (Hebrews 13:7)

Because of their work

The honor is not personality-based; it’s tied to the God-given task. Pastors and elders “keep watch over your souls as those who will give an account” (Hebrews 13:17). They labor in the Word, prayer, and shepherding so the body matures (Ephesians 4:11-12). Recognizing the weight of that calling stirs gratitude and respectful cooperation. When the flock honors the work, leaders are encouraged, the gospel advances, and Christ is glorified.

Live in peace with one another

After addressing leaders, Paul turns to the congregation as a whole: “Live in peace with one another.” Peace is the atmosphere God desires for His people. Jesus pronounced a blessing on peacemakers (Matthew 5:9), and Paul urges, “If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone” (Romans 12:18). Practical steps include:

• Quick forgiveness (Colossians 3:13)

• Gentle speech that turns away wrath (Proverbs 15:1)

• Refusal to entertain gossip or division (Titus 3:10)

• Pursuing reconciliation before worship (Matthew 5:23-24)

When honor flows upward and peace spreads outward, the church becomes a compelling witness to a watching world (John 17:21).

summary

1 Thessalonians 5:13 calls believers to wrap their leaders in heartfelt love, esteem them because of their God-assigned labor, and cultivate peace within the fellowship. Love motivates, honor expresses, work validates, and peace protects. When the church lives this verse, spiritual leaders thrive, unity deepens, and Christ’s body displays His beauty to all.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(13) Very highly in love.--The original here is difficult; but it seems best, with most good commentators, ancient and modern, to construe "in love" with "esteem," and to make "very highly" (a very enthusiastic word in the Greek) an expletive attached to "in love," implying "hold in a most extraordinary degree of love." The bond which binds the Christian community to their directors is not to be one of "recognition" and obedience only (1Thessalonians 5:12), but of holy affection above all.

For their work's sake.--Our love is to be paid them not for any social or intellectual qualities they may have in themselves; it is the work which they have to do that should attract our sympathy. The original seems to mean that we are to love them, not only because they do such work, but also 'for the sake of their work," i.e., to help it forward.

Be at peace among yourselves.--Discipline to be observed towards equals, as well as superiors.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 13. - And to esteem them very highly in love for their work's sake; that is, both on account of their labors, and especially on account of the dignity of their office, for their work is the work of the Lord. Both love for their persons and respect for their authority are here enjoined. And; to be omitted, as not in the original. Be at peace among yourselves. A new exhortation, entirely independent of the preceding; it is not addressed to the presbyters, but to the members of the Church in general.
