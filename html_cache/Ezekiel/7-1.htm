And the word of the LORD came to me, saying,
This phrase introduces a divine revelation to the prophet Ezekiel, emphasizing the direct communication from God. The phrase "the word of the LORD" is a common biblical expression indicating a message from God, often used by prophets to assert the divine origin of their message. This underscores the authority and authenticity of the prophecy. In the context of Ezekiel, this communication is part of a series of visions and messages given to him during the Babylonian exile, around 593-571 BC. The phrase "came to me" highlights the personal nature of the revelation, indicating that Ezekiel is the chosen vessel for this message. This is consistent with the role of prophets in the Old Testament, who were often called to deliver God's messages to His people, sometimes involving warnings of judgment or calls to repentance. The phrase "saying" introduces the content of the message, which in this chapter, is a pronouncement of impending judgment on Israel due to their persistent idolatry and rebellion against God. This reflects the covenantal relationship between God and Israel, where disobedience leads to divine discipline. The prophetic nature of this message also points forward to the ultimate fulfillment of God's redemptive plan through Jesus Christ, who is the Word made flesh, bringing both judgment and salvation.
Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet and priest during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel is the recipient of God's message. His role is to convey God's warnings and promises to the Israelites.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who communicates directly with Ezekiel, delivering messages of judgment and hope.

3. Israel
The nation to whom the message is directed. At this time, they are experiencing the consequences of their disobedience and idolatry.

4. Babylonian Exile
The historical context in which Ezekiel prophesies. The Israelites are in captivity due to their persistent rebellion against God.

5. Prophetic Revelation
The event of God speaking to Ezekiel, which is a common occurrence in the prophetic books, emphasizing the divine origin of the message.
Teaching Points
The Authority of God's Word
God's word is authoritative and comes directly from Him. As believers, we must recognize the Bible as the ultimate authority in our lives.

The Role of the Prophet
Prophets are chosen by God to deliver His messages. We should respect and heed the teachings of the prophets as recorded in Scripture.

The Consequences of Disobedience
Israel's exile serves as a warning about the consequences of turning away from God. We must examine our lives for areas of disobedience and seek repentance.

God's Communication with His People
God desires to communicate with His people, often through His word. We should be attentive and responsive to His guidance.

Hope Amidst Judgment
Even in messages of judgment, there is hope for restoration. We can trust in God's faithfulness and His plans for redemption.

What Does Ezekiel 7:1 Mean
And

The opening conjunction ties Ezekiel 7:1 to the flow of God’s ongoing conversation with His prophet.

• Just as in Ezekiel 6:1—“The word of the LORD came to me, saying…”—the Lord’s messages arrive in a steady stream, underscoring continuity.

• Scripture often uses “and” to connect successive revelations (Jeremiah 1:11, 13; Amos 7:1, 4, 7), showing that God’s dealings with His people unfold step by step rather than in isolated bursts.

• This reminds us that chapter 7 is not a new idea but the next piece of God’s righteous judgment on a rebellious nation, announced in chapter 6.

the word of the LORD

Everything hinges on the fact that the communication originates with Yahweh Himself.

• He speaks with the same authority that called the universe into being (Genesis 1:3).

• Prophets like Moses (Exodus 4:12) and Isaiah (Isaiah 55:11) delivered words guaranteed to accomplish God’s purposes; Ezekiel now stands in that same line.

• Because it is “the word of the LORD,” the message carries absolute truth, leaving no room for debate or dilution (Psalm 119:89).

came to me

God’s word is personal and direct; it “came” to Ezekiel, not through committee or conjecture.

• The Lord sovereignly selects His messengers (Jeremiah 1:5; Galatians 1:15-16).

• Ezekiel, already exiled in Babylon (Ezekiel 1:1-3), receives divine clarity while far from Jerusalem, proving that God’s reach knows no geographical limits (Psalm 139:7-10).

• The phrase underscores accountability: once the word comes, the prophet must proclaim it (Ezekiel 3:17-18; Acts 20:26-27).

saying

The verb highlights content—God does not merely impress feelings; He articulates distinct words.

• Throughout Scripture, revelation is verbal and specific (Numbers 12:6-8; Hebrews 1:1-2).

• Clear speech leaves Judah without excuse when judgment falls (Ezekiel 33:31-33).

• For today’s reader, the same certainty applies: when God speaks in His written Word, He expects attentive obedience (James 1:22-25).

summary

Ezekiel 7:1 sets the tone for the entire chapter: a fresh installment of God’s unbroken, authoritative, personal, and explicit communication. The Lord continues His dialogue, delivers His infallible word, singles out His servant, and provides a precise message. Knowing this grounds us in confidence that what follows carries divine weight and demands a response of humble obedience.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - The absence of any fresh date, and the fact that it is simply tacked on to the previous chapter by the copulative conjunction, shows that what follows belongs to the same group. The use of the phrase, the word of the Lord came unto me, shows, however, that there was an interval of silence, perhaps of meditation, followed by a fresh influx of inspiration; and, so far as we may judge from the more lyrical character of the chapter, a more intense emotion.
