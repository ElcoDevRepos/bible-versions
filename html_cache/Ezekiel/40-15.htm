Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And the distance from the entrance of the gateway
This phrase refers to the measurement of the temple's outer gate, which is part of Ezekiel's vision of a new temple. The entrance of the gateway signifies the beginning of the sacred space, emphasizing the importance of approaching God with reverence. In ancient Near Eastern architecture, gateways often symbolized transition from the secular to the sacred. This vision of the temple serves as a prophetic symbol of God's future dwelling with His people, reflecting the holiness and order of divine worship.

to the far end of its inner portico
The inner portico represents the inner part of the gateway structure, which would have been a covered area leading into the temple complex. This design reflects the architectural style of ancient temples, where porticos served as transitional spaces. The inner portico's mention highlights the detailed and orderly nature of God's design for worship, emphasizing the importance of preparation and sanctification before entering His presence. This can be seen as a type of Christ, who is the ultimate gateway to God, providing access to the Father through His sacrifice.

was fifty cubits.
A cubit is an ancient measurement roughly equivalent to 18 inches or 45 centimeters, making fifty cubits approximately 75 feet or 22.5 meters. The specific measurement underscores the precision and intentionality in God's design for the temple. The number fifty in biblical numerology often symbolizes deliverance or freedom, as seen in the Year of Jubilee (Leviticus 25), which occurred every fifty years and was a time of liberation and restoration. This measurement may prophetically point to the ultimate deliverance and restoration found in Jesus Christ, who fulfills the law and the prophets, bringing spiritual freedom to believers.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet and priest during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel receives visions from God, including the detailed vision of a new temple in chapters 40-48.

2. The Temple
The vision in Ezekiel 40 describes a future temple, symbolizing God's presence and the restoration of Israel. This temple is not the same as Solomon's or the Second Temple but represents a perfect, divine structure.

3. The Outer Gate
Part of the temple's structure, the outer gate signifies the entrance to the sacred space, emphasizing the separation between the holy and the common.

4. The Inner Portico
This is the inner part of the gate structure, leading further into the temple, symbolizing deeper access to God's presence.

5. The Measurement (Fifty Cubits)
The specific measurement highlights the precision and order of God's design, reflecting His holiness and the importance of approaching Him with reverence.
Teaching Points
God's Holiness and Order
The detailed measurements in Ezekiel's vision remind us of God's holiness and the order He desires in worship. We should approach God with reverence and respect for His divine order.

Symbolism of Access
The outer gate and inner portico symbolize the journey into God's presence. As believers, we are invited to draw near to God through Christ, who is the ultimate gate.

Restoration and Hope
Ezekiel's vision of the temple offers hope for restoration. In times of personal or communal exile, we can trust in God's promise to restore and dwell among His people.

Spiritual Temple
As the church and individual believers are described as God's temple, we are called to live lives that reflect His holiness and order, being mindful of how we build our spiritual lives.

What Does Ezekiel 40:15 Mean
And the distance

• Ezekiel stresses measurement because God values precision. As with the tabernacle (Exodus 25:9; Hebrews 8:5), exact dimensions reveal divine order rather than human guesswork.

• The text reminds us that nothing in worship is accidental. Every span, stone, and stair serves the Lord’s purpose, just as 1 Kings 6:38 records the careful timing of Solomon’s temple.

from the entrance of the gateway

• Gates mark transition—from common ground to holy ground (Psalm 118:19-20; John 10:9).

• In Ezekiel’s vision, the gate faces east (Ezekiel 40:6), the direction of sunrise and resurrection hope (Matthew 28:1).

• The gate’s threshold forms the first step toward fellowship with God, a pattern later fulfilled when Jesus became “the way” into God’s presence.

to the far end of its inner portico

• The inner portico is the sheltered area just inside the gate, akin to a foyer. It offers immediate welcome yet reminds worshipers they have further to go before reaching the sanctuary (Ezekiel 40:9; 42:15).

• This layered approach pictures progressive intimacy—outer court, inner court, temple—mirroring our draw near to God through Christ (Hebrews 10:19-22).

• The portico’s columns and side rooms (Ezekiel 40:10-14) provide order and symmetry, signaling both beauty and boundary.

was fifty cubits

• A cubit is roughly eighteen inches, so fifty cubits equal about seventy-five feet—a substantial span that communicates majesty.

• The number fifty also recalls liberty and restoration in the Year of Jubilee (Leviticus 25:10). While Ezekiel’s measurement is literal, its size whispers freedom for a redeemed people, later celebrated when the Spirit was poured out fifty days after Passover (Acts 2:1).

• By specifying “fifty cubits,” God affirms that He—not man—sets the standard for approach, accessibility, and grace.

summary

Ezekiel 40:15 records an exact measurement to show that every detail of God’s future temple is deliberate. The gate introduces worshipers to holy ground; the inner portico invites them deeper; the fifty-cubit span proclaims both grandeur and anticipated jubilee. Through these dimensions the Lord assures His people that access to Him is meticulously provided, ultimately fulfilled in Christ, who is the true and living gateway into God’s glorious presence.
