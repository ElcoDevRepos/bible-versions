Then the word of the LORD came to me, saying,
This phrase indicates a direct communication from God to the prophet Ezekiel. In the context of the Old Testament, prophets were often the mouthpieces of God, receiving divine revelations to convey to the people. The phrase underscores the authority and divine origin of the message that follows. This is a common introduction in prophetic literature, emphasizing that the message is not of human origin but from God Himself. It reflects the intimate relationship between God and His prophets, as seen in other instances such as Jeremiah 1:4 and Isaiah 38:4. The phrase also sets the stage for the seriousness and importance of the message, as it is not merely a human opinion but a divine directive. This communication often came during times of crisis or significant change, highlighting God's ongoing involvement in the affairs of His people.
Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet and priest during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel is the recipient of God's messages and visions. His role is to convey God's words to the Israelites.

2. The LORD (Yahweh)
The covenant God of Israel, who communicates His will and judgments through His prophets.

3. The Exile
The context of Ezekiel's prophecies is the Babylonian exile, a period of great trial and reflection for the Israelites, who are away from their homeland due to their disobedience.
Teaching Points
The Role of the Prophet
Ezekiel's role as a prophet underscores the importance of being attentive to God's word and faithfully communicating it, regardless of the audience's receptivity.

Divine Communication
The phrase "the word of the LORD came to me" highlights the personal and direct nature of God's communication with His chosen messengers. It reminds us of the importance of seeking and listening to God's voice in our lives.

Responsibility and Accountability
Just as Ezekiel was accountable for delivering God's messages, believers today are called to be faithful in sharing God's truth with others, understanding the weight of this responsibility.

God's Sovereignty in Exile
The context of exile serves as a reminder of God's sovereignty and His purposes, even in difficult circumstances. It encourages believers to trust in God's plan and remain faithful during trials.

Repentance and Restoration
The broader context of Ezekiel's message often includes calls to repentance and promises of restoration, urging believers to turn back to God and find hope in His promises.

What Does Ezekiel 33:23 Mean
Then

• The tiny adverb tells us this moment is tied to what just happened—Ezekiel’s mouth had been opened again after Jerusalem’s fall (Ezekiel 33:22).

• God never speaks at random; He speaks at the right time (2 Samuel 7:4; Habakkuk 2:3).

• The sequence shows His ongoing involvement with a shattered nation—He has not abandoned them even in judgment (Lamentations 3:22-23).

the word of the LORD

• What follows is not Ezekiel’s opinion but God’s flawless, authoritative revelation (Isaiah 40:8; 1 Thessalonians 2:13).

• This phrase appears repeatedly in the prophets to underline the same truth: God’s Word is living, active, and final (Jeremiah 1:4; Hebrews 4:12).

• Because Scripture is entirely accurate, we can read Ezekiel 33:23 with quiet confidence that every syllable is trustworthy.

came to me,

• The initiative is God’s; Ezekiel doesn’t conjure a message—he receives it (Amos 3:7; 2 Peter 1:21).

• “Came” pictures God’s Word moving toward a ready servant. It still does the same for any believer who opens Scripture (Psalm 119:130).

• Personal pronouns matter: “to me” reminds us the Lord knows, calls, and equips individuals for specific tasks (Ezekiel 3:16-17; John 10:3-4).

saying,

• God is about to speak in clear, intelligible sentences; He is not distant or cryptic (Isaiah 45:19; Jeremiah 33:2-3).

• The verb points forward—there is content, correction, and comfort on the way (verses 24-33).

• What He “says” will stand, accomplishing His purpose just as surely as He promised in Isaiah 55:11.

summary

Ezekiel 33:23 is more than a narrative placeholder; it testifies that, even after Jerusalem’s collapse, God still initiates conversation with His people. At the precise moment He chooses, His inerrant Word arrives, targets a willing servant, and delivers an unmistakable message. The verse reassures us that the Lord remains present, purposeful, and vocal, ready to guide anyone who will listen.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Then the word.--There is no reason to doubt that the following prophecy was uttered immediately after the arrival of the fugitive; but there may have been a short interval. None of the prophecies from this point to the close of Ezekiel 39 are dated. Ezekiel 40-48 form one continuous prophecy, which closes the book, and is dated more than twelve years after the present one. We are then to suppose that the prophecies, to Ezekiel 39 inclusive, were uttered at intervals during these twelve years, but we have no means of fixing their dates more exactly.
