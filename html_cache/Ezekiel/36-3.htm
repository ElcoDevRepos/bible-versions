Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
therefore prophesy and declare that this is what the Lord GOD says:
This phrase introduces a divine message delivered through the prophet Ezekiel. Prophecy in the Old Testament often served as a direct communication from God to His people, providing guidance, warning, or comfort. The authority of the message is underscored by the phrase "the Lord GOD says," emphasizing that the words are not Ezekiel's own but are from Yahweh, the covenant-keeping God of Israel. This reflects the role of prophets as God's mouthpieces, a theme consistent throughout the prophetic books.

Because they have made you desolate and have trampled you on every side,
This part of the verse addresses the condition of the land of Israel, which has been left desolate and ravaged by surrounding nations. Historically, this refers to the invasions and destruction brought by foreign powers such as the Babylonians. The imagery of being "trampled on every side" suggests total devastation and humiliation, a common fate for conquered lands in the ancient Near East. This desolation is a consequence of Israel's disobedience and the resulting judgment, as outlined in earlier chapters of Ezekiel.

so that you became a possession of the rest of the nations
The land of Israel, once a symbol of God's promise and blessing, has become a spoil for other nations. This reflects the historical reality of the Babylonian exile, where the Israelites were removed from their land, and it was occupied by others. Theologically, this serves as a reminder of the consequences of breaking the covenant with God, as the land was a central element of the Abrahamic promise. The possession by other nations also foreshadows the eventual restoration and the hope that God will reclaim and restore His people and their land.

and were taken up in slander by the lips of their talkers,
The phrase highlights the ridicule and scorn faced by Israel from other nations. In the ancient world, the defeat of a nation was often seen as the defeat of its gods, leading to mockery and slander. This reflects the cultural context where the honor of a people was closely tied to their land and their deity. The slander also points to the spiritual battle, as the nations' mockery of Israel is ultimately a challenge to the power and faithfulness of Yahweh. This theme of vindication is echoed in other prophetic writings, where God promises to restore His name and His people, as seen in passages like Isaiah 52:5-6.

What Does Ezekiel 36:3 Mean
Therefore prophesy

The Lord’s first word to Ezekiel is a command to speak—not his own opinions, but God’s sure word (Jeremiah 1:7; 2 Peter 1:21). Prophecy here is not guesswork; it is God’s authoritative announcement, carrying the weight of Isaiah 55:11, “so is My word… it will accomplish what I please.”

And declare that this is what the Lord GOD says

By repeating that the message comes from “the Lord GOD,” the text underscores absolute divine authority (Exodus 3:14; Psalm 33:9). Whatever follows is not open to negotiation; it is as certain as Numbers 23:19 reminds us: “God is not a man, that He should lie.”

Because they have made you desolate

The hills and land of Israel have been stripped bare—visible evidence of covenant curses foretold in Leviticus 26:32–33 and Deuteronomy 29:23. Babylon’s campaigns left Jerusalem “a heap of rubble” (2 Kings 25:9–10), fulfilling those earlier warnings to the letter.

• Physical ruin: crops burned, cities leveled (Jeremiah 39:8).

• Spiritual shame: the land once flowing with milk and honey now echoes lamentations (Lamentations 1:4).

And have trampled you on every side

Foreign armies marched back and forth without restraint (Psalm 79:1). Like grapes in a press, the land was crushed “on every side,” a phrase echoed in Luke 21:24 when Jesus predicts Gentile trampling of Jerusalem. The indignity is comprehensive—north, south, east, and west (Isaiah 1:7).

So that you became a possession of the rest of the nations

What rightly belonged to the tribes of Israel (Joshua 13–19) fell into pagan hands (Obadiah 1:13). The Edomites, Ammonites, Philistines, and Babylonians treated the land as spoil (Ezekiel 25). This fulfills Deuteronomy 28:48: “you will serve your enemies whom the LORD will send against you.”

• Occupation means exile: the people are scattered (Ezekiel 36:19).

• Control shifts: foreigners reap harvests meant for covenant heirs (Joel 3:2).

And were taken up in slander by the lips of their talkers

The nations didn’t stop at seizing territory; they mocked God’s people, sneering, “Where is their God?” (Psalm 115:2). Israel became “a byword among the nations” (Jeremiah 24:9). The humiliation is verbal and public—exactly what Psalm 44:13 describes: “You make us a reproach to our neighbors… a laughingstock among the peoples.”

• Slander questions God’s faithfulness (Malachi 3:15).

• Yet even mockery is woven into divine purpose; Romans 2:24 notes, “The name of God is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you,” setting the stage for restoration that will silence every lying tongue (Ezekiel 36:36).

summary

Ezekiel 36:3 captures God’s indictment against the nations for ravaging His land and ridiculing His people. Each phrase traces a progression: command to prophesy, affirmation of divine authority, catalog of destruction, occupation, and ridicule. The verse lays the groundwork for the stunning promises that follow (36:4–15): God will reverse the desolation, reclaim the land from foreign hands, and turn slander into praise. In short, the Lord exposes the full extent of Israel’s humiliation so that His forthcoming restoration will shine all the brighter to every watching nation.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(3) In the lips of talkers, and are an infamy.--A phrase equivalent to a by-word and a reproach. (Comp. Deuteronomy 28:37; 1Kings 9:7, &c.) In the previous clause the words, "have swallowed you up," should rather be "pant for you," the word being taken from the snuffing and panting of wild beasts. It was after this fashion that "the residue of the heathen," all those whom the conquests of Nebuchadnezzar had yet left, panted for the possession of the lands of Israel.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 3. - Therefore. Ewald calls attention to the fivefold repetition of this conjunction, saying, "It repeats itself five times, the reasons [for God's judgments] against these enemies thrusting themselves forward, before the discourse calmly dwells upon the mountains of Israel, of which it is strictly intended to treat." As it were, the prophet's emotion is so strong, and his indignation against Israel's enemies so vehement, that, though he three times in succession begins to prophesy to the mountains of Israel, he on each occasion breaks off before he can get his message told, to expatiate upon the wickedness of Israel's foes. In the prophet's estimation that wickedness was so heinous as to inevitably carry in its bosom appropriate retribution. Because - literally, because and because, or even because, a reduplication for the sake of emphasis, as in Ezekiel 13:10 and Leviticus 26:43 - they have made you desolate, and swallowed you up on every side; literally, wasting of and panting after you (are) round about. Fairbairn, Ewald, and Smend, deriving שַׁמות from נָשַׁם, "to pant," rather than from שָׁמַם, "to lay waste," translate, "because there is snapping and puffing at you round about," which Plumptre thinks "falls in better with the context," since "the prophet's spirit seems to dwell throughout on the derision rather than the desolation to which his country, the mountains of Israel, had been subject." And ye are taken up; literally, ye are made to come, if וַתֵּעֲלוּ be an imperf., niph. of עָלַה, "to go up "(Rosenmüller, Schroder); or, ye are come, if it be imperf., kal of עָלַל, "to press, or go in" (Ewald, Havernick); or, ye are gone up, if it be second pers. kal of עָלַה (Hitzig, Smend). In the lips of talkers; literally, upon the lip of the tongue - the lip being regarded as the instrument or organ with which the tongue speaks. Havernick unnecessarily takes "the tongue" as equivalent to "people" in the parallel clause - a signification לָשׁון has only in Isaiah 66:18; while Kliefoth views it as synonymous with "slander," as in Psalm 140:11, and translates, "upon the lip of slander and of the evil report of the people." Keil sees in "the tongue" a personification for the "tongue-man" or talker of Psalm 140:11; and Gesenius considers the two clauses as tautological.
