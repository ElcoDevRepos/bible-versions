But I had concern for My holy name
This phrase highlights God's deep commitment to His own reputation and character. In the biblical context, God's name represents His nature, authority, and presence. Throughout Scripture, God's name is synonymous with His glory and holiness (Psalm 8:1, Isaiah 42:8). The concern for His holy name underscores the importance of God's reputation among the nations, as His actions are often intended to reveal His holiness and righteousness to the world (Exodus 9:16). This concern is not merely for the sake of His name but also for the sake of His covenant people, as His name is tied to His promises and faithfulness.

which the house of Israel had profaned
The term "profaned" indicates that Israel had treated God's name with disrespect and irreverence. Historically, Israel's idolatry and disobedience led to their exile, which in turn caused God's name to be dishonored among the nations (Ezekiel 20:39). The Israelites were meant to be a light to the nations, reflecting God's holiness and righteousness (Deuteronomy 4:6-8). Instead, their actions led to the opposite effect, bringing shame to God's name. This profaning of God's name is a recurring theme in the prophetic books, where the prophets call Israel to repentance and a return to covenant faithfulness.

among the nations
The phrase "among the nations" indicates the widespread impact of Israel's actions. Israel's exile and dispersion among the nations were a direct result of their covenant unfaithfulness (2 Kings 17:6-23). This dispersion served as a witness to the surrounding nations of both God's judgment and His enduring commitment to His people. The nations were meant to see the distinctiveness of Israel's God through their obedience, but instead, they witnessed the consequences of Israel's rebellion. This context sets the stage for God's redemptive plan to restore His name and His people, ultimately pointing to the inclusion of the Gentiles in the New Covenant (Romans 11:11-12).

to which they had gone
This phrase refers to the physical and spiritual exile of the Israelites. Geographically, the Israelites were scattered to various regions, including Assyria and Babylon, as a result of their disobedience (2 Kings 17:6, 2 Kings 25:11). Spiritually, this dispersion symbolizes a separation from God's presence and blessings. The exile serves as both a punishment and a means of purification, preparing the way for a future restoration. This restoration is ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, who gathers God's people from all nations and restores them to a right relationship with God (John 10:16, Ephesians 2:13-16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel was called by God to deliver messages of judgment and restoration to the people of Israel.

2. House of Israel
Refers to the people of Israel, God's chosen nation, who were in exile due to their disobedience and idolatry.

3. Nations
The foreign lands where the Israelites were scattered during their exile, representing the broader world observing Israel's actions.

4. God's Holy Name
Represents God's character and reputation, which was profaned by Israel's disobedience and idolatry.

5. Profanation
The act of desecrating or disrespecting something sacred, in this case, God's name through Israel's actions.
Teaching Points
God's Concern for His Name
God's actions are often motivated by a desire to uphold His holy name. This underscores the importance of living in a way that honors God and reflects His character.

The Impact of Our Actions
The behavior of God's people can either honor or profane His name. Our lives should be a testimony to God's holiness and love.

Restoration and Hope
Despite Israel's failure, God promises restoration for the sake of His name. This offers hope that God can redeem and restore us, regardless of our past.

Holiness as a Witness
Living a holy life is a powerful witness to the world. Our conduct should draw others to God, not push them away.

Repentance and Renewal
Recognizing where we have profaned God's name calls for repentance and a commitment to renewal in our relationship with Him.

What Does Ezekiel 36:21 Mean
But

The verse opens with a contrasting conjunction that invites us to look at what God is doing in spite of Israel’s unfaithfulness.

• Prior verses describe Israel’s disgrace and dispersion (Ezekiel 36:17–20).

• The “But” signals a decisive shift from judgment to restoration, echoing earlier divine turn-arounds (Psalm 106:44-45; Isaiah 54:7-8).

I had concern

God Himself is moved; His actions are never indifferent or mechanical.

• “I take no pleasure in the death of the wicked” (Ezekiel 33:11).

• His concern reveals covenant loyalty, the same heart seen in Exodus 2:24-25 when He “heard” Israel’s groaning.

for My holy name

The central motive is the sanctity of God’s own reputation.

• God guards His name because it displays His character (Exodus 34:6-7).

• “For the sake of Your name, LORD, forgive my iniquity” (Psalm 25:11) shows this recurring biblical theme.

• Compare Ezekiel 20:9 and Isaiah 48:9-11 where God acts “for My name’s sake.”

which the house of Israel had profaned

Israel’s conduct brought shame on the very name they were meant to honor.

• Profaning occurs when behavior contradicts proclaimed belief (Leviticus 18:21; Romans 2:24).

• Their idols, injustice, and bloodshed (Ezekiel 36:17-18) made God appear powerless or unrighteous to outsiders.

among the nations

The fallout was international; Gentile observers judged God by Israel’s failures.

• “Why should the nations say, ‘Where is their God?’” (Psalm 115:2).

• God’s global purpose from Abraham onward was to bless all nations (Genesis 12:3); Israel’s sin obstructed that witness.

to which they had gone

Exile scattered Israel, but also spread the scandal of God’s name.

• Assyria deported the northern tribes (2 Kings 17:6).

• Babylon carried off Judah (2 Kings 25:11).

• Even in dispersion, God intended to reclaim glory by gathering them back (Ezekiel 36:24; 39:27-28).

summary

Ezekiel 36:21 reveals a God who intervenes, not because His people deserve it, but to vindicate the holiness of His own name. Their sin had tarnished His reputation before a watching world, yet His covenant faithfulness compelled Him to act. By restoring Israel, He would display His righteousness, power, and mercy so that the nations—and Israel itself—would know that He alone is the LORD.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(21) Pity for mine holy name.--The meaning of this has been already explained in the Note on the previous verse; and in the following verses it is emphasised that God would restore His people, not for their sakes, but for His own.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 21. - I had pity for mille holy Name. Havernick, after the LXX., wrongly renders, "I spared (them, i.e. Israel) for my holy Name s sake; but the preposition for or "upon" following the verb usually marks the object upon which the action of the verb terminates (see Ezekiel 16:5). Gesenius translates, "I will be sparing of my holy Name;" i.e. I will care for its honor.
