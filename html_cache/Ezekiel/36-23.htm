I will show the holiness of My great name
This phrase emphasizes God's intention to vindicate His holy name, which is central to His character and reputation. In biblical times, a name represented one's identity and authority. God's name being "great" signifies His supreme power and sovereignty. The holiness of His name reflects His purity, righteousness, and separateness from sin. This declaration is a response to the desecration of His name by His people, Israel, and serves as a promise of restoration and divine intervention. The holiness of God's name is a recurring theme in Scripture, seen in passages like Isaiah 48:11, where God acts for the sake of His name.

which has been profaned among the nations
The profaning of God's name among the nations refers to the actions and behaviors of the Israelites that led to God's name being disrespected and dishonored. This occurred when Israel, chosen to be a light to the nations, failed to uphold God's laws and commandments, leading to their exile. The nations viewed Israel's defeat and exile as a reflection of their God's weakness. This phrase highlights the importance of God's people living in a way that honors Him, as seen in Leviticus 22:32, where God commands His people not to profane His holy name.

the name you have profaned among them
This part of the verse directly addresses the Israelites, holding them accountable for their actions that led to the profanation of God's name. It underscores the covenant relationship between God and Israel, where Israel's disobedience and idolatry brought shame to God's name. The historical context includes Israel's repeated failure to remain faithful to God, leading to their punishment and exile. This serves as a reminder of the responsibility of God's people to uphold His name through their conduct, as seen in Deuteronomy 28:58-59.

Then the nations will know that I am the LORD
This phrase points to the ultimate purpose of God's actions: to reveal His identity and sovereignty to all nations. The acknowledgment of God as the LORD (Yahweh) by the nations signifies a recognition of His supreme authority and power. This is a fulfillment of God's promise to make Himself known to the world, as seen in Exodus 9:16, where God declares His intention to show His power and proclaim His name throughout the earth. It also foreshadows the universal recognition of God in the eschatological future, as prophesied in Isaiah 45:23.

declares the Lord GOD
This declaration emphasizes the authority and certainty of the message. The use of "Lord GOD" (Adonai Yahweh) combines the titles of God's sovereignty and covenant faithfulness. It assures the fulfillment of His promises and the execution of His divine will. This phrase is often used in prophetic literature to underscore the seriousness and divine origin of the message, as seen throughout the book of Ezekiel.

when I show My holiness in you before their eyes
God's holiness will be demonstrated through the transformation and restoration of His people, Israel. This transformation will serve as a testimony to the nations of God's power and faithfulness. The phrase "before their eyes" indicates a public and undeniable display of God's work, leading to the recognition of His holiness. This transformation is a type of the redemptive work of Jesus Christ, who through His life, death, and resurrection, revealed God's holiness and provided a way for humanity to be restored to a right relationship with God. This concept is echoed in the New Testament, where believers are called to be holy as God is holy (1 Peter 1:15-16).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet during the Babylonian exile, tasked with delivering God's messages to the Israelites.

2. Israelites
The people of God who were in exile due to their disobedience and idolatry.

3. Nations
Refers to the Gentile nations surrounding Israel, who witnessed Israel's unfaithfulness and God's subsequent judgment.

4. The LORD (YHWH)
The covenant name of God, emphasizing His eternal, self-existent nature and His relationship with Israel.

5. Babylonian Exile
The period when the Israelites were taken captive by Babylon, serving as a backdrop for Ezekiel's prophecies.
Teaching Points
God's Holiness and Reputation
God's primary concern is the sanctity of His name. Our actions as believers should reflect His holiness and not bring dishonor to His name.

Restoration and Witness
God's plan to restore Israel is not just for their benefit but to demonstrate His holiness to the nations. Our lives should be a testimony to God's transformative power.

Repentance and Renewal
The Israelites' profaning of God's name required repentance. Similarly, we must turn from actions that dishonor God and seek His renewal.

God's Sovereignty
Despite Israel's failures, God remains sovereign and will accomplish His purposes. Trust in God's ability to work through our weaknesses.

Living as a Light
Just as Israel was called to be a light to the nations, Christians are called to live in a way that draws others to God.

What Does Ezekiel 36:23 Mean
I will show the holiness of My great name

• God Himself takes the initiative. Just as He parted the Red Sea to vindicate His glory (Exodus 14:17–18), He now promises to act again so His reputation will shine unmistakably.

• “Holiness” speaks of His utter separateness from sin (Isaiah 6:3). Here He is not merely teaching about holiness; He is putting it on display for all to witness.

• His “great name” recalls passages such as Psalm 115:1—“Not to us, O LORD, not to us, but to Your name be the glory.” The point is that everything God does aims at magnifying who He is.

which has been profaned among the nations

• Israel’s rebellion dragged God’s name through the mud. When the covenant people were exiled, the surrounding nations mocked, assuming Israel’s God was powerless (2 Kings 18:33–35).

• Profaning the name means treating what is sacred as common (Leviticus 22:32). Israel’s disobedience misrepresented God’s character to the watching world.

the name you have profaned among them

• The Lord speaks directly to the exiles: “you.” Their personal accountability is inescapable (Ezekiel 18:30–32).

• Though God will restore them, He makes clear that the restoration is motivated by His own honor, not their merit (Ezekiel 36:22). This keeps grace front and center.

Then the nations will know that I am the LORD

• The repeated purpose clause in Ezekiel—“then they will know that I am the LORD” (over 60 times)—highlights the mission: universal recognition of Yahweh’s sovereignty (Psalm 46:10).

• God’s acts of judgment and restoration both serve this end (Ezekiel 25:17; 39:28). His identity is revealed through real historical events.

declares the Lord GOD

• The phrase stresses absolute certainty. It is the signature of divine authority (Isaiah 40:5; Jeremiah 1:12).

• Because God’s word cannot fail (Numbers 23:19), His promise of vindication stands firm.

when I show My holiness in you before their eyes

• God will transform His people so markedly that observers cannot miss the change (Ezekiel 36:24–27).

• This outward demonstration parallels passages like Deuteronomy 4:6–8, where obedience to God’s statutes leads surrounding nations to marvel.

• Ultimately, God’s holiness on display in His people anticipates the new covenant reality—hearts of flesh, the Spirit within (Jeremiah 31:33; Galatians 2:20). The church today is called to the same purpose: “that you may proclaim the excellencies of Him” (1 Peter 2:9).

summary

Ezekiel 36:23 teaches that God is committed to vindicating His holy name, tarnished by His people’s sin, through decisive, gracious action that transforms them. This restoration, rooted in His unchanging character, compels the nations to recognize that He alone is the LORD.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Before their eyes.--The Hebrew text as it stands has your eyes, as in the margin. Many manuscripts and other authorities have their. Either of them admits of an excellent sense; but the reading your brings out the important truth that God must first be sanctified in the eyes of the people themselves by their repentance and moral reformation, and then, through them and the consequent blessing upon them, He will be sanctified in the eyes of the heathen also.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 23. - I will sanctify my great Name; i.e. the name of my holiness (Deuteronomy 28:58; Psalm 8:1; Malachi 1:11). As Israel's dispersion had caused that Name to be profaned, so Israel's restoration would secure that it should be magnified among the heathen (Ezekiel 38:23), who should learn from this event that their previous ideas of Jehovah, as a feeble and local divinity, had been wrong. The question whether your eyes, as in the Hebrew text, or "their eyes," as in many ancient versions, should be read is debated. The latter reading appears to be demanded by the usus loquendi of Ezekiel (see Ezekiel 20:41; Ezekiel 28:25; Ezekiel 38:16; Ezekiel 39:27), and is adopted by both English versions as well as by interpreters of eminence; but other expositors of equal name adhere to the former reading on the ground that the sanctifying of Jehovah's Name in the eyes of Israel was an indispensable preliminary to its sanctification in the eyes of the heathen. Havernick regards "their eyes" as "an obvious emendation to relieve a difficulty," to which in no case should criticism accord the preference; while Keil gives it the preference, though admitting that "your eyes" can be justified.
