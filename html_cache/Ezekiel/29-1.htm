In the tenth year
This refers to the tenth year of King Jehoiachin's exile, which places the prophecy around 587 BC. This was a tumultuous period for Judah, as the Babylonian siege of Jerusalem was ongoing. The historical context is crucial, as it highlights the impending judgment on Egypt, a nation that had been both an ally and a source of false hope for Israel.

on the twelfth day of the tenth month
The specificity of the date underscores the precision and reliability of prophetic revelation. This exact dating is significant in biblical prophecy, as it provides a historical anchor for the events described. It also reflects the meticulous nature of Ezekiel's record-keeping, which is consistent throughout his ministry.

the word of the LORD came to me, saying
This phrase indicates divine revelation, a common introduction in prophetic literature. It emphasizes that the message is not from Ezekiel himself but from God. The phrase "word of the LORD" is used frequently in the Old Testament to denote a direct communication from God, often carrying authority and urgency. This particular message is part of a series of prophecies against Egypt, highlighting God's sovereignty over all nations.

What Does Ezekiel 29:1 Mean
In the tenth year

• Ezekiel dates this oracle to the tenth year of King Jehoiachin’s exile (Ezekiel 1:2), anchoring it in real history and reminding readers that God rules over specific moments, not vague eras.

• The date falls around 587 BC, only a few months before Jerusalem’s final fall (2 Kings 25:1–2). The Lord speaks while His people are under severe pressure, showing His faithfulness in crisis (Psalm 46:1).

• Other prophecies are likewise time-stamped—see Ezekiel 24:1 and Haggai 1:1—demonstrating that God’s word can be verified.

on the twelfth day of the tenth month

• The precision (the “twelfth day”) underscores that God’s revelations are not random impressions but exact, documented events (Exodus 12:2).

• This same month is highlighted in Jeremiah 52:4 as the moment Babylon besieged Jerusalem, so Judah is experiencing judgment even as Egypt faces its own (Ezekiel 29:19).

• God’s timing weaves together multiple nations’ destinies, proving His sovereignty over the calendar and the map (Daniel 2:21).

the word of the LORD came to me

• Ezekiel’s authority rests not in personal insight but in “the word of the LORD,” echoing his call in Ezekiel 1:3 and Jeremiah 1:4–9.

• Every true prophet speaks when “the word of the LORD” arrives—1 Kings 18:1, Jonah 1:1—affirming verbal, propositional revelation (2 Peter 1:21).

• The phrase assures readers that what follows is wholly reliable, inspired, and without error (2 Timothy 3:16).

saying

• This introduces the exact message about Pharaoh and Egypt that unfolds in verses 2–16. God is about to speak judgment and promise restoration for His own people, illustrating that His words perform what He declares (Isaiah 55:11).

• Similar transitions appear in Genesis 1:3 (“Then God said”), highlighting the creative and decisive power of divine speech (Psalm 33:9).

summary

Ezekiel 29:1 carefully dates and authenticates the coming prophecy against Egypt. The verse affirms that God’s words break into precise historical moments, carry absolute authority, and set the stage for the detailed judgment that follows. By recording the exact year, month, and day, Ezekiel invites us to trust Scripture’s accuracy and to see God’s sovereign hand directing both Israel’s trials and Egypt’s downfall.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(1) In the tenth year, in the tenth month.--This was exactly a year and two days after the investment of Jerusalem by Nebuchadnezzar (Ezekiel 24:1-2; 2Kings 25:1), and about six months before its fall, or seven before its destruction (2Kings 25:3-8). It must have been, therefore, after the time when the siege was temporarily raised by the approach of the Egyptians under Pharaoh-Hophra (Jeremiah 37:5; Jeremiah 37:11), and when Jeremiah prophesied the failure of that attempt (Jeremiah 37:6-10); and probably was just when the news of that relief reached Chaldaea, and gave fresh hope to the exiles of the deliverance of Jerusalem. . . .
Pulpit Commentary
Verse 1. - In the tenth year, etc. The precision with which the dates of the several portions of the prophecy against Egypt are given, here and in Ver. 17; Ezekiel 30:20; Ezekiel 31:1; Ezekiel 32:1, 7, shows that each was called forth by the political events of the time, and has to be studied in connection with them. It will be well, therefore, to begin with a Brief survey of the relations which existed at this period between Judah, Egypt, and Babylon. After the great defeat of Pharaoh-Necho by Nebuchadnezzar at Carchemish ( B.C. 604), on which Jeremiah (46.) dwells fully, he was succeeded in B.C. 594 by his son Psammetik II. the Psammis of Herodotus 2:160, who invaded Ethiopia, and died in B.C. 588, leaving the throne to his son Uah-prahet, the Pharaoh Hophra of Jeremiah 44:30, the Apries of Herod., 2:161. The Greek historian tells us that he attacked Tyre and Zidon, failed in an enterprise against Cyrene, and was deposed by Amasis ( B.C. 569). Zedekiah and his counselors, following in the steps of Hezekiah (Isaiah 30.) and Jehoiakim (Jeremiah 46.), had courted his alliance against the Chaldeans. As Ezekiel had prophesied (Ezekiel 17:11-18), they found that they were once more leaning on a broken reed. We have now come to B.C. 589, when Jerusalem was actually besieged, but was still dreaming of being relieved by an Egyptian army.
