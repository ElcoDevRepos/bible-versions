Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Next he brought me into the inner court through the south gate
The "inner court" refers to the more sacred area of the temple complex, indicating a progression towards holiness. In the context of Ezekiel's vision, this represents a deeper spiritual journey. The "south gate" is one of the three gates leading into the inner court, symbolizing access and entry into a closer relationship with God. The south gate's mention may also have geographical significance, as the south often represents warmth and light in biblical symbolism. This movement into the inner court can be seen as a type of Christ's work, bringing believers into a closer communion with God, as seen in Hebrews 10:19-22, where believers are encouraged to draw near to God with a sincere heart.

and he measured the south gate
The act of measuring signifies God's order and precision, reflecting His sovereignty and the importance of structure in worship. Measuring also implies ownership and the establishment of boundaries, as seen in Revelation 11:1-2, where the temple is measured to signify God's protection and claim over His people. This measurement underscores the idea that every aspect of worship and the temple is under divine scrutiny and care.

it had the same measurements as the others
This phrase emphasizes uniformity and consistency in God's design, suggesting that each gate, and by extension, each aspect of worship, holds equal importance in the eyes of God. The repetition of measurements throughout Ezekiel's vision highlights the perfection and completeness of God's plan. This uniformity can be seen as a reflection of God's unchanging nature, as stated in Malachi 3:6, and the equality of access to God provided through Christ, as noted in Galatians 3:28, where there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for all are one in Christ Jesus.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet and priest during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel is the recipient of the vision described in this passage. His role is to convey God's messages to the Israelites.

2. The Inner Court
This is part of the visionary temple described in Ezekiel's vision. The inner court represents a place of greater holiness and proximity to God's presence compared to the outer court.

3. The South Gate
One of the gates leading into the inner court of the temple. Gates in biblical times often symbolize access and entry into sacred spaces.

4. The Measuring
The act of measuring the temple signifies God's order, precision, and the importance of the structure. It reflects God's attention to detail and the sanctity of His dwelling place.

5. The Vision
This is part of a larger vision given to Ezekiel, detailing a future temple. It symbolizes hope and restoration for the people of Israel.
Teaching Points
God's Order and Precision
The detailed measurements of the temple reflect God's nature as a God of order. In our lives, we should seek to align with His order and design.

Access to God's Presence
The south gate leading to the inner court symbolizes access to deeper fellowship with God. We are invited to draw nearer to Him through Christ.

Hope and Restoration
Ezekiel's vision of the temple offers hope for restoration. In times of personal exile or difficulty, we can trust in God's promise to restore and renew.

Holiness and Reverence
The inner court's sanctity reminds us of the importance of holiness in our lives. We are called to live set apart for God's purposes.

Vision for the Future
Just as Ezekiel's vision pointed to a future hope, we are encouraged to live with an eternal perspective, looking forward to the fulfillment of God's promises.

What Does Ezekiel 40:28 Mean
Next he brought me into the inner court

Ezekiel notes a fresh movement: “Next he brought me…” (Ezekiel 40:28). The guiding angel escorts the prophet from the outer area to the inner court of the future temple.

• The inner court represents a deeper approach to God’s holy presence, much like the step from the Holy Place to the Holy of Holies in Solomon’s temple (1 Kings 6:16).

• Progressive access mirrors the invitation believers have to “draw near with confidence” (Hebrews 10:22).

• It also anticipates the millennial period when redeemed Israel will worship in a restored sanctuary, fulfilling promises such as Ezekiel 37:26-28.

through the south gate

The route matters. In Ezekiel 40:24-26 the south gate of the outer court had already been described; now the same direction provides entry into the inner court.

• Using the south gate shows that every direction of the temple is purposeful and accessible; God’s house welcomes worshippers from all sides, echoing Psalm 100:4, “Enter His gates with thanksgiving.”

• The angel’s orderly tour—east gate (v. 6), north gate (v. 20), south gate (v. 24), then inner court—reflects God’s character of order (1 Corinthians 14:33).

• Cardinal directions in Scripture often carry covenant overtones: the southern approach recalls Judah’s territory, aligning with prophecies like Zechariah 12:10 that center on Jerusalem’s future redemption.

and he measured the south gate

Measurement dominates Ezekiel 40-42. Here it stresses precision and authenticity.

• Measuring asserts that the structure is not symbolic only but physically real; God provides exact specifications, as in Exodus 25:9 for the tabernacle.

• It underscores that holiness has boundaries—nothing enters that defiles, paralleling Revelation 11:1-2 where John is told to “measure the temple of God.”

• Detailed dimensions reassure the exiles that restoration will be complete, echoing Isaiah 44:26, “I will fulfill the words of My servants.”

it had the same measurements as the others

Uniformity among the gates testifies to God’s impartiality and stability.

• Every worshipper approaches on equal terms; Acts 10:34 reminds us that “God shows no partiality.”

• Consistency in design highlights God’s unchanging nature (Malachi 3:6); what He designs once, He sustains.

• Repeated measurements anchor the reader in reliability—just as Hebrews 13:8 affirms Jesus Christ is “the same yesterday and today and forever.”

• The matched dimensions knit the entire complex together, ensuring harmony and peace, foreshadowing the ordered peace of Christ’s millennial reign (Isaiah 9:7).

summary

Ezekiel 40:28 moves the prophet from the outer to the inner court, emphasizing deeper intimacy with God. Entering through the south gate spotlights the inclusiveness and order of divine worship. The act of measuring affirms a literal future temple marked by holiness and precision. Identical dimensions across all gates reveal God’s steadfast, impartial character. Together these details assure believers that God’s promised restoration is certain, orderly, and invites all who will come to Him through the provision He has made.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(28) Brought me to the inner court.--The preposition should be translated into, being the same with that in Ezekiel 40:32. The prophet having entered the inner court by the south gate, this is first described (Ezekiel 40:28-31). This and the other gates of this court are essentially the same, and require the same changes of translation as in the case of the outer gates. The same plan will serve for both, remembering that it must be reversed, the porches of one set of gates facing the porches of the other set; of course the steps led to the porches of the inner gates instead of to the opposite end. The few points of difference between them will be noted as they occur.
