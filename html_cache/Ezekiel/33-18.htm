If a righteous man turns from his righteousness
This phrase highlights the concept of apostasy, where an individual who once followed God's commandments chooses to abandon them. In the context of Ezekiel, this reflects the broader theme of personal responsibility and accountability before God. The Israelites were often warned against turning away from God's laws, as seen in Deuteronomy 30:17-18. The idea of turning away is also echoed in the New Testament, such as in Hebrews 6:4-6, which warns against falling away after having received the knowledge of the truth.

and commits iniquity
Iniquity refers to gross injustice or wickedness, a deviation from what is right. The Hebrew word for iniquity, "avon," implies a twisting or perversion of what is good. This phrase underscores the seriousness of sin and its consequences. The cultural context of ancient Israel placed a strong emphasis on communal and individual adherence to the law, as seen in the covenantal relationship between God and His people. The prophets, including Ezekiel, often called out the iniquities of the people, urging them to repent and return to righteousness.

he will die for it
This statement emphasizes the principle of divine justice, where each person is held accountable for their actions. The death mentioned here can be understood both physically and spiritually, as separation from God. In Ezekiel's time, the concept of retributive justice was prevalent, where actions had direct consequences. This is consistent with the warnings found in other parts of the Old Testament, such as in Proverbs 14:12, which states that the way of sin leads to death. Theologically, this also points to the ultimate consequence of sin, which is spiritual death, as articulated in Romans 6:23. The phrase serves as a sobering reminder of the need for continual faithfulness and repentance.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Perseverance in Righteousness
Righteousness is not a one-time achievement but a continual pursuit. Believers must remain vigilant in their walk with God.

The Consequences of Turning Away
Turning from righteousness to sin has serious consequences, including spiritual death. This serves as a warning to remain steadfast.

The Role of Repentance
While the verse warns of death for iniquity, the broader biblical account offers repentance as a path back to life and restoration with God.

Accountability within the Community
Believers are called to hold each other accountable, encouraging one another to remain faithful and turn back to God when they stray.

The Assurance of Forgiveness
Despite the warning, the Bible assures us that God is faithful to forgive those who repent and seek His mercy.

What Does Ezekiel 33:18 Mean
If a righteous man turns

God is speaking about someone already identified by their righteous walk—one who has been living in obedience (Ezekiel 3:20; 18:24).

• The warning affirms personal responsibility; prior obedience does not grant a lifetime exemption (1 Corinthians 10:12).

• Scripture consistently urges steadfastness: “But My righteous one will live by faith; and if he shrinks back, I will take no pleasure in him” (Hebrews 10:38).

from his righteousness

Turning “from” pictures a conscious pivot away from what is right.

• This is not a stumble but an abandonment, similar to Israel’s frequent departures (Deuteronomy 30:17–18).

• Jesus said, “No one who puts his hand to the plow and then looks back is fit for the kingdom of God” (Luke 9:62).

and commits iniquity

The turn is proven by actions. Sin moves from thought to deed (James 1:14–15).

• Iniquity is lawlessness (1 John 3:4), willful rebellion rather than ignorance.

• Paul contrasts yielding ourselves to God with yielding to sin, warning that the latter makes us its slaves (Romans 6:13, 16).

he will die for it

The consequence is certain and just: “The soul who sins is the one who will die” (Ezekiel 18:20).

• Death here includes temporal judgments—Israel’s exile exemplifies this—and ultimately spiritual death if unrepentant (Romans 6:23; Revelation 21:8).

• The clause underscores God’s impartiality: He “shows no favoritism” (Romans 2:11). What matters is present posture toward Him, not past merits.

summary

Ezekiel 33:18 is a sober reminder that righteousness must be maintained, not merely begun. A person who abandons obedience and embraces sin forfeits the life that righteousness brings. God’s justice is unwavering: the outcome hinges on our ongoing response to Him.
