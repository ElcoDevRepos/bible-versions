Then the word of the LORD came to me, saying,
This phrase introduces a divine revelation to the prophet Ezekiel, emphasizing the direct communication between God and His chosen messenger. In the context of Ezekiel's ministry, this phrase underscores the authority and authenticity of the message that follows. Ezekiel, a priest and prophet during the Babylonian exile, often received visions and messages from God to convey to the Israelites. This particular message is part of a larger allegory involving two eagles and a vine, symbolizing the political alliances and betrayals of Judah. The phrase "the word of the LORD" is a common biblical expression indicating that what follows is not merely human insight but divine instruction. This is consistent with other prophetic books, such as Jeremiah and Isaiah, where God directly communicates His will and plans. The phrase also highlights the role of prophets as intermediaries between God and His people, tasked with delivering messages of warning, judgment, and hope.

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Judgment
God is in control of history and uses nations and events to accomplish His purposes. Understanding this helps us trust in His plan even when circumstances seem dire.

The Role of Prophets
Prophets like Ezekiel are God's messengers, calling people back to faithfulness. We should heed the warnings and teachings of Scripture as God's word to us today.

Consequences of Disobedience
Israel's exile serves as a reminder of the serious consequences of turning away from God. We are called to examine our lives and repent of any disobedience.

Hope in Restoration
Despite judgment, God promises restoration for those who return to Him. This offers hope and encouragement for believers facing trials.

What Does Ezekiel 17:11 Mean
Then

The single word “Then” quietly anchors us in the story line of Ezekiel 17. It signals sequence—God is continuing a conversation already in motion. After Ezekiel presents the allegory of the two eagles and a vine (verses 1-10), the Lord does not leave the meaning up to guesswork; He steps in at that very moment.

• Scripture often uses “Then” to mark a fresh installment of revelation, as in Ezekiel 12:26, “Then the word of the LORD came to me, saying,” and Jeremiah 1:4.

• We are reminded that God’s dealings are orderly; He speaks at the right time to advance His purposes (Ecclesiastes 3:1-8).

the word of the LORD

These five words elevate everything that follows. This is not opinion, tradition, or speculation; it is God’s own speech.

• 2 Timothy 3:16 insists, “All Scripture is God-breathed,” underscoring that what Ezekiel hears is infallible truth.

• Psalm 33:6 declares, “By the word of the LORD the heavens were made,” so the same powerful voice that created now interprets.

• The phrase appears repeatedly in Ezekiel (e.g., 6:1; 13:1) to remind us that prophetic authority rests solely on divine authorship, never on the prophet’s personality.

came to me

God’s word does not float in abstraction; it arrives, targeting a specific messenger.

• Ezekiel is personally singled out, echoing Ezekiel 3:16, “The word of the LORD came to me.”

• This pattern matches Jeremiah 1:2 and Amos 3:7—God entrusts His secrets to chosen servants so that His people can hear and respond.

• It also highlights grace: the holy God stoops to communicate with a human being, ensuring clarity for the exiles in Babylon who desperately need direction and hope.

saying

The verse ends with anticipation. A message is about to be articulated in plain terms.

• Deuteronomy 29:29 divides the hidden from the revealed; here God moves something from hidden to revealed.

• 2 Peter 1:21 notes that “men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit,” so when God is “saying,” the Spirit is actively guiding the prophet’s words.

• By prefacing the explanation of the parable with “saying,” God underscores that what follows (verses 12-21) is an authoritative interpretation, not a human guess.

summary

Ezekiel 17:11 is brief, yet it packs four rich truths: timing (“Then”), authority (“the word of the LORD”), personal delivery (“came to me”), and impending revelation (“saying”). Together they affirm that God intervenes at the right moment, speaks with absolute authority, chooses real people as His mouthpieces, and makes His will unmistakably clear. The verse prepares us to receive the rest of the chapter as God’s own, trustworthy explanation of current events and future hope.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(11) Moreover the word of the Lord.--The form of expression leaves it uncertain whether the explanation of the parable was given at the same time with the parable itself, or whether, as is more probable, a little time was suffered to elapse, during which it should be "a riddle" to the people that they might be the more attentive to its meaning when given to them.
