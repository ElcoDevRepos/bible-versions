and the villages on her mainland will be slain by the sword.
This phrase refers to the impending judgment on Tyre, a prominent Phoenician city-state known for its wealth and maritime prowess. The "villages on her mainland" indicates the surrounding settlements dependent on Tyre, which were vulnerable to attack. Historically, Tyre was divided into two parts: the mainland city and the island city. The mainland was more accessible to invaders, making it susceptible to conquest. The "sword" symbolizes warfare and divine judgment, often used in prophetic literature to denote God's instrument of punishment. This prophecy was fulfilled through various historical sieges, notably by Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon and later by Alexander the Great, who destroyed the mainland settlements.

Then they will know that I am the LORD.
This phrase underscores the purpose of the judgment: to reveal God's sovereignty and authority. Throughout the Old Testament, the recognition of God as LORD is a recurring theme, especially in the context of judgment and deliverance. The phrase "they will know that I am the LORD" is a common refrain in Ezekiel, emphasizing that God's actions, whether in judgment or blessing, are meant to lead people to acknowledge His supremacy. This acknowledgment is not just intellectual but relational, calling for a response of reverence and obedience. The fulfillment of this prophecy against Tyre serves as a testament to God's control over nations and history, reinforcing His identity as the one true God.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Tyre
An ancient and prosperous Phoenician city located on the Mediterranean coast, known for its wealth and strong maritime trade. In Ezekiel's time, Tyre was a powerful city-state that often interacted with Israel.

2. Ezekiel
A prophet of God who ministered to the Israelites during their Babylonian exile. He delivered messages of judgment and hope from God.

3. Nebuchadnezzar
The king of Babylon, who played a significant role in the fulfillment of Ezekiel's prophecies against Tyre. His military campaigns led to the eventual downfall of the city.
Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty Over Nations
Ezekiel 26:6 demonstrates God's control over the rise and fall of nations. Believers can trust that God is sovereign over world events and political powers.

The Consequences of Pride
Tyre's downfall serves as a warning against pride and self-reliance. Christians are reminded to remain humble and dependent on God rather than their own strength or wealth.

Judgment and Redemption
While God's judgment is severe, it is also just. This passage encourages believers to seek repentance and align themselves with God's will to avoid similar judgment.

The Importance of Prophetic Warnings
Prophecies like Ezekiel's serve as warnings and calls to repentance. Christians should heed biblical warnings and allow them to guide their lives.

What Does Ezekiel 26:6 Mean
Villages on her mainland

Tyre was more than a fortified island city; it also claimed coastal settlements spread along the Phoenician shore. God singles out these dependent towns to show that His judgment will reach every corner of Tyre’s influence (Ezekiel 27:8-11). Their inclusion mirrors earlier patterns—when Canaanite “villages and their surrounding settlements” fell with their parent cities (Joshua 19:29-30). The phrase reminds us that sin’s consequences never stay isolated; influence brings accountability (Proverbs 14:34).

Will be slain by the sword

The sword in Ezekiel consistently represents literal warfare (Ezekiel 21:3-4; 29:8). Here it foretells Nebuchadnezzar’s campaign (Ezekiel 26:7-11) and, later, Alexander’s total demolition of the mainland in 332 BC. The language is not metaphor but concrete: soldiers, siege ramps, and bloodshed. God often calls foreign armies “My sword” (Isaiah 10:5; Jeremiah 47:6-7). He wields them to judge unrepentant nations, proving that geopolitical events unfold under His direct governance.

Then they will know

Recognition follows judgment throughout the book (Ezekiel 6:7; 25:7; 30:19). People ignore God’s warnings until His actions break their defenses and illusions. When Tyre’s proud commerce turns to rubble, survivors and onlookers alike will grasp that the God of Israel alone ordains rise and ruin (Ezekiel 27:27-36). Divine purpose is never simple vengeance; it is revelation—pressing truth on hearts hardened by prosperity.

That I am the LORD

The covenant name Yahweh (translated “the LORD”) proclaims self-existence, sovereignty, and moral authority (Exodus 3:14-15; Isaiah 45:5-7). By attaching His name to this prophecy, God assures its certainty: if the coastal villages fall exactly as spoken, Israel—and the world—must acknowledge the Speaker. The collapse of Tyre becomes another milestone in the unfolding testimony that “the Most High rules the kingdoms of men” (Daniel 4:17). What He foretells, He performs; what He performs, He interprets.

summary

Ezekiel 26:6 declares that Tyre’s mainland villages will literally fall by invading swords, demonstrating God’s control over nations. Their destruction forces every witness to recognize Yahweh’s unique sovereignty. Judgment is not random; it is a deliberate revelation of the Lord who alone keeps His word and commands history.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(6) Daughters which are in the field.--Comp. Ezekiel 26:8. A poetic way of describing the dependencies of Tyre upon the mainland.

In Ezekiel 26:7-11 the particular and now impending conquest by Nebuchadnezzar is graphically described, and then, with the change to the plural in Ezekiel 26:12, there seems to be again a looking forward to the long vista of successive devastations.
