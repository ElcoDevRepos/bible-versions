I dispersed them among the nations
This phrase refers to the historical event of the Babylonian exile, where the Israelites were taken from their homeland and scattered among foreign nations. This dispersion was a fulfillment of the warnings given by prophets like Moses (Deuteronomy 28:64) and Jeremiah (Jeremiah 9:16). The dispersion served as both punishment and a means to preserve a remnant of Israel, ensuring the continuation of God's covenant people. It also foreshadows the New Testament concept of the Church being scattered among the nations to spread the Gospel (Acts 8:1).

and they were scattered throughout the lands
The scattering of the Israelites was not just a physical displacement but also a spiritual and cultural challenge. Living among pagan nations, they faced the temptation to assimilate and abandon their distinct identity as God's chosen people. This scattering is reminiscent of the Tower of Babel (Genesis 11:8-9), where God scattered humanity to prevent them from uniting in rebellion against Him. The scattering also highlights God's sovereignty over all nations and His ability to use even judgment to fulfill His purposes.

I judged them according to their ways and deeds
This phrase emphasizes God's justice and righteousness. The Israelites were judged not arbitrarily but based on their actions and behavior, which had deviated from the covenantal laws given to them. This principle of divine retribution is consistent throughout Scripture, as seen in passages like Psalm 62:12 and Romans 2:6, where God repays each person according to their deeds. It underscores the importance of obedience and faithfulness to God's commands. This judgment also points to the ultimate judgment that will be executed by Jesus Christ, who will judge the living and the dead (2 Timothy 4:1).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Ezekiel
A prophet during the Babylonian exile, Ezekiel conveyed God's messages to the Israelites, often focusing on themes of judgment and restoration.

2. Israelites

3. Nations
Refers to the various foreign lands where the Israelites were dispersed as a result of God's judgment.

4. Judgment
God's righteous assessment and action against the Israelites for their unfaithfulness.

5. Exile

Teaching Points
God's Sovereignty in Judgment
God is just and sovereign, and His judgments are based on His people's actions. Understanding this helps us recognize the importance of living in obedience to God's commands.

Consequences of Disobedience
The scattering of Israel serves as a powerful reminder of the consequences of turning away from God. It encourages us to examine our own lives for areas of disobedience.

Hope in Restoration
While judgment is a theme, God's ultimate plan includes restoration. This offers hope and assurance that God’s discipline is meant to bring us back to Him.

Living as Exiles
Just as the Israelites lived in foreign lands, Christians are called to live as "exiles" in the world, maintaining faithfulness to God amidst a secular culture.

Reflecting on Our Ways
The passage challenges us to reflect on our own "ways and deeds" and seek alignment with God's will, knowing that He sees and judges righteously.

What Does Ezekiel 36:19 Mean
I dispersed them among the nations

God Himself initiated the exile. This was not random political upheaval; it was the deliberate act of a holy Lord responding to covenant violation (see Deuteronomy 28:64; 2 Kings 17:20-23). By saying “I dispersed,” the Lord underscores His sovereign right to discipline His people. The scattering to Assyria and Babylon—and later across the Mediterranean world—demonstrated that no border could shield Israel from divine correction. Yet even in judgment, He preserved them as a recognizable people, anticipating future regathering (Jeremiah 31:10).

and they were scattered throughout the lands

The repetition paints the exile as wide-ranging and complete (Leviticus 26:33; Hosea 9:17). Every region that received these refugees became a living billboard of both Israel’s unfaithfulness and God’s faithfulness to His word—He had promised dispersion for disobedience, and He kept that promise. At the same time, scattered communities carried with them the knowledge of the one true God, sowing seeds for future witness (Esther 8:17; Acts 2:5-11).

I judged them according to their ways and deeds

Divine justice is never arbitrary. The exile matched the gravity of Israel’s idolatry, bloodshed, and covenant neglect (Jeremiah 17:10; Romans 2:6). The Lord measured their conduct against His revealed law and rendered a fitting verdict. Yet judgment served a restorative purpose: to purge corruption and bring the people to repentance (Ezekiel 20:43-44; Hebrews 12:10-11). God’s dealings are always righteous, blending severity with the ultimate aim of redemption.

summary

Ezekiel 36:19 shows God acting as both righteous judge and faithful covenant partner. He personally scattered Israel to fulfill His warnings, publicly displaying His holiness while preserving a remnant for future restoration. The verse assures us that the Lord always keeps His word—both in disciplining sin and in preparing the way back to blessing.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 19. - According to their way and according to their doings I judged them. The language hints at a correspondence between the punishment and the crime. As a woman in her separation was not only defiled, but separated from the congregation Leviticus 15:19), so Israel, having defiled both herself and her land, required to be removed from it (Leviticus 18:28). And she was. Jehovah scattered her among the heathen and dispersed her through the countries.
