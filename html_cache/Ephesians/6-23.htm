Peace to the brothers
The term "peace" in this context refers to the Hebrew concept of "shalom," which encompasses not only the absence of conflict but also the presence of wholeness and well-being. This peace is a gift from God, often associated with the reconciliation brought by Christ's sacrifice (Ephesians 2:14-18). The "brothers" refers to fellow believers, emphasizing the familial bond within the body of Christ. This greeting of peace is a common Pauline benediction, reflecting the unity and harmony that should characterize the Christian community (Philippians 4:7).

and love with faith
Love and faith are central themes in Paul's writings. Love, or "agape," is the selfless, sacrificial love that believers are called to exhibit, mirroring Christ's love for the church (Ephesians 5:2). Faith, in this context, is trust and belief in God and His promises. The combination of love with faith suggests that true Christian love is rooted in faith, and faith is expressed through love (Galatians 5:6). This pairing underscores the inseparable nature of these virtues in the life of a believer.

from God the Father
This phrase highlights the source of peace, love, and faith. God the Father is the origin of all spiritual blessings (Ephesians 1:3). The paternal imagery emphasizes God's role as the provider and sustainer of His children. It reflects the intimate relationship believers have with God, who is not distant but actively involved in their lives (James 1:17).

and the Lord Jesus Christ
The inclusion of Jesus Christ alongside God the Father underscores the divine nature and authority of Christ. It affirms the Christian doctrine of the Trinity, where Jesus is co-equal with the Father. The title "Lord" signifies Jesus' sovereignty and His role as the head of the church (Ephesians 1:22). This phrase also points to the mediating work of Christ, through whom believers receive peace, love, and faith (John 14:27). The unity of the Father and the Son in bestowing these blessings reflects the harmony within the Godhead and the comprehensive nature of salvation.

The recipients of the letter, the church in Ephesus was a significant early Christian community located in a major city of the Roman province of Asia (modern-day Turkey).

3. God the Father
The first person of the Holy Trinity, the source of peace and love mentioned in this verse.

4. The Lord Jesus Christ
The second person of the Holy Trinity, through whom believers receive peace and love with faith.

The broader context in which this letter was written, a time of growth and establishment for Christian communities across the Roman Empire.

What Does Ephesians 6:23 Mean
Peace

- This closing blessing mirrors the opening of Ephesians (1:2) and bookends the whole letter with God-given tranquility.

- Peace here is not mere calm but the wholeness Jesus promised: “Peace I leave with you; My peace I give you…” (John 14:27).

- Earlier, Paul described Christ as the believer’s armor in spiritual warfare (Ephesians 6:10-17). Even amid conflict, God’s peace reigns (Colossians 3:15: “Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts…”).

- Because “we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ” (Romans 5:1), we stand reconciled, no longer enemies but family.

to the brothers

- “Brothers” highlights the family bond shared by all who trust Christ, regardless of background (Ephesians 2:19).

- It underscores unity: the blessings Paul pronounces are corporate, meant for every saint in the household of faith.

- The term also reminds readers of mutual responsibility—guarding one another’s hearts as fellow soldiers (6:18).

and love

- Love follows peace because reconciliation overflows in affection. Paul has emphasized love throughout the letter (Ephesians 5:2: “Walk in love, just as Christ loved us…”).

- Biblical love is self-giving, Spirit-produced (Romans 5:5) and the greatest evidence of true discipleship (John 13:34-35).

- This love binds believers together in the same way Christ’s love drew them to God (1 John 4:11).

with faith

- Love is paired “with faith” because genuine affection for God and others springs from trust in Christ (Galatians 5:6: “faith expressing itself through love”).

- Faith here is the ongoing reliance that sustains believers; it is the shield just discussed in 6:16.

- Paul often links these two virtues (Colossians 1:4) to show that neither can thrive in isolation.

from God the Father and the Lord Jesus Christ

- The peace, love, and faith just mentioned are gifts, not achievements. “Every good and perfect gift is from above” (James 1:17).

- Placing the Father and the Son side by side points to their perfect unity in blessing the church (2 Thessalonians 2:16-17).

- Their joint bestowal assures believers that divine resources will never fail; the same God who armed them for battle now equips them with sustaining grace.

summary

Ephesians 6:23 is a Spirit-inspired benediction that seals the letter. Paul confers God-given peace, love, and faith upon the unified family of believers. These blessings flow unceasingly from both the Father and the Son, enabling Christians to live reconciled, love sacrificially, trust continually, and stand firm in every spiritual conflict.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(23) Peace be to the brethren . . .--In the conclusion of the Epistle, as at the beginning, St. Paul gives the double benediction, "Peace and grace be with you all." But it. is impossible not to notice the difference between the generality of the terms here used ("the brethren," and "all who love the Lord Jesus Christ") and the personal "you" of all the other Epistles--a difference which would be inexplicable if this Epistle were addressed to the well-known and loved Church of Ephesus alone.

Peace seems especially dwelt upon in the Epistles of the Captivity, of which the Epistle to Philippi contains (in Ephesians 4:7) the fullest description of the "peace of God which passeth all understanding." It is naturally connected here with love (as in 2Corinthians 13:11; Colossians 3:15-16)--a "love with faith," "making perfect" (as in Galatians 5:6) the faith which St. Paul takes for granted as being in them. For peace is first with God, in the thankful receiving of His mercy; from this naturally arises "love with faith" towards Him; and out of this, again, peace and love towards men, in the conviction that, "if God so loved us, we ought also to love one another" (1John 4:11). All these are gifts from "God the Father and the Lord Jesus Christ." . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verses 23, 24. - CLOSING BENEDICTION. Verse 23. - Peace be to the brethren. There is a double invocation of blessing - to the brethren, and to all that love the Lord. "The brethren" must mean the members of the Church addressed, with special reference to the amalgamation in one body of Jews and Gentiles, or to the one family (Ephesians 3:15) in which they were brethren, Peace is the echo of Ephesians 1:2, and denotes the apostle's desire for the continuance among them of the peace with God to which they had been admitted, as well as the prevalence of peace in every sense of the word. And love with faith. "Love" in the widest sense (Ephesians 3:17, 19) - the love of Christ to them, their love to Christ, and their love to one another; and love is coupled with faith, because faith is the companion of love, they are in the closest relation to each other. Faith in Christ receives him as he is offered, in all his love and goodness; it sees his loving face, and is changed into the same image. From God the Father and the Lord Jesus Christ (comp. Ephesians 1:2).
