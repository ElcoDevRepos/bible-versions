Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
I ask that out of the riches of His glory
This phrase highlights the abundant and limitless nature of God's glory. The "riches of His glory" suggests the vastness and sufficiency of God's resources, which are available to believers. This concept is echoed in Philippians 4:19, where Paul speaks of God supplying every need according to His riches in glory. The glory of God is a recurring theme throughout Scripture, often associated with His majesty, holiness, and the manifestation of His presence, as seen in Exodus 33:18-23 when Moses asks to see God's glory.

He may strengthen you with power
The request for strength and power indicates the need for divine enablement in the believer's life. This power is not of human origin but is a supernatural empowerment that comes from God. In Acts 1:8, Jesus promises the disciples that they will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes upon them, signifying the transformative and empowering work of the Spirit. The strength mentioned here is necessary for living a life that reflects Christ and for enduring trials and challenges.

through His Spirit
The Holy Spirit is the agent of this empowerment, emphasizing the role of the Spirit in the believer's life. The Spirit's work is essential for spiritual growth, understanding, and the ability to live out the Christian faith. In John 14:16-17, Jesus describes the Holy Spirit as the Helper who will be with believers forever, guiding them into all truth. The indwelling of the Spirit is a mark of the New Covenant, as prophesied in Ezekiel 36:26-27, where God promises to put His Spirit within His people.

in your inner being
The focus on the "inner being" underscores the importance of inner transformation rather than mere external conformity. This inner renewal is a theme in Paul's writings, as seen in Romans 12:2, where believers are urged to be transformed by the renewing of their minds. The inner being refers to the heart, mind, and soul, the core of a person's identity and spiritual life. This transformation is necessary for developing Christ-like character and for experiencing the fullness of life in Christ.

What Does Ephesians 3:16 Mean
I ask that

Paul’s words drip with humble dependence. He approaches God as a child making a request, reminding us of Jesus’ invitation in Matthew 7:7–11 to “ask, seek, knock.” Because prayer is rooted in relationship, we confidently bring every need before the Father, knowing He listens (1 John 5:14–15).

out of the riches of His glory

The supply line is limitless. God’s “riches” point to His inexhaustible resources—grace, mercy, wisdom, strength—all flowing from His radiant glory (Philippians 4:19; Romans 11:33–36). We are never praying to a reluctant giver; we are drawing from a vast treasury that can never run dry (Psalm 50:10–12).

He may strengthen you

The request centers on divine reinforcement, not self-help. Like Elijah who outran Ahab’s chariot “in the strength of the LORD” (1 Kings 18:46), believers are empowered beyond natural capacity. God bolsters weary hearts so we “do not lose heart” (2 Corinthians 4:16).

with power

This is no mild boost but the same might that raised Christ from the dead (Ephesians 1:19–20). It is durable power for everyday obedience—resisting temptation (1 Corinthians 10:13), loving difficult people (Colossians 1:11), enduring trials (James 1:12). Spiritual vitality thrives when divine power flows.

through His Spirit

The Holy Spirit is the delivery system. Just as wind fills a sailboat, the Spirit fills and propels us (Acts 1:8). He applies Christ’s finished work, makes Scripture come alive (John 14:26), and produces fruit we could never manufacture on our own (Galatians 5:22–23). Dependence on the Spirit is non-negotiable.

in your inner being

God targets the core—mind, will, emotions. Renewal starts inside, then spills outward (Romans 12:2). While culture fixes on appearances, the Lord transforms the heart (1 Samuel 16:7). An anchored inner life produces steadfastness when circumstances shake (Hebrews 6:19).

summary

Paul prays that believers will be inwardly fortified by the limitless resources of God’s glory, delivered through the Holy Spirit’s mighty power. As we ask, God pours strength into our inner lives, enabling us to live resilient, Christ-honoring lives no matter what comes.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(16) To be strengthened with might by his Spirit in the inner man.--From the Father, as the source of all life and being, St. Paul passes on to the Spirit, "proceeding from the Father," as the giver of life to men. His prayer here, as in Ephesians 1:17, is for the gift of the Spirit, but under some difference of aspect. There the prayer is for illumination, here for strength to grasp the mystery, to be rooted in love, and be filled up to the fulness of God. Accordingly, there the inner man is represented only by the "eyes of the heart;" here (as in Romans 7:22; 2Corinthians 4:16) we hear of the "inner man" in his entirety, including all faculties--intellectual, emotional, moral--which make up his spiritual nature. And St. Paul emphasises this prayer very strikingly by asking that the gift may be "according to the riches of His glory," unlimited as the illimitable glory of the Divine Nature itself. Moreover, a greater closeness of communion is clearly indicated here. For light is a gift from without; strength comes from an indwelling power, making itself perfect in weakness, and continually growing from grace to grace.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 16. - That he would grant you, according to the riches of his glory. The standard or measure of the Divine giving is brought into view. "Riches of his glory" is a more emphatic expression than "glorious riches," though substantially the same in meaning. God's standard of giving is liberal, bountiful, overflowing. An image of the riches of his glory is seen in the starry heavens, which proclaim at once the vast riches and surpassing glory of God. Or in the beautiful appearance of an autumn sunset, where the whole sky is flecked with clouds brightened into a sea of glory. In prayer, it is both useful for ourselves and glorifying to God to recognize his bountifulness - to remember that he gives us a King (2 Samuel 24:23). To be strengthened with might by his Spirit in the inner man. The inner man is the seat of influence, but with us it is the scat of spiritual feebleness. Most men may contrive to order their outward conduct suitably; but who has control of the inner man? Faith, trust, humility, love, patience, and the like graces which belong to the inner man, are what we are weakest in, and what we have least power to make strong. In this very region it is sought that the Ephesians might be strengthened with might by the Spirit. The gift of the Spirit is available for this very purpose for all that ask him.
