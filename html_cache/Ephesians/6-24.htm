Grace to all
The concept of grace is central to Christian theology, representing unmerited favor from God. In the context of Ephesians, Paul often emphasizes grace as a gift that brings salvation (Ephesians 2:8-9). This grace is extended universally to all believers, transcending cultural and ethnic boundaries, which was a significant theme in the early church as it expanded beyond Jewish communities.

who love our Lord Jesus Christ
Love for Jesus is a defining characteristic of true believers. This love is not merely emotional but is demonstrated through obedience and commitment to His teachings (John 14:15). The title "Lord Jesus Christ" underscores His divinity, messianic role, and authority, which were central to the early church's confession of faith (Philippians 2:11).

with an undying love
The phrase "undying love" suggests a love that is eternal and incorruptible. This reflects the biblical call for believers to remain steadfast in their faith and devotion, even amidst trials and persecution (James 1:12). The concept of enduring love is also seen in the covenantal relationship between God and His people, as exemplified in the steadfast love (hesed) described in the Old Testament (Psalm 136).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Paul the Apostle
The author of the letter to the Ephesians, Paul was a key figure in the early Christian church, known for his missionary journeys and theological teachings.

2. Ephesians
The recipients of the letter, the Ephesians were members of the early Christian church in Ephesus, a major city in Asia Minor (modern-day Turkey).

3. Jesus Christ
Central to the verse, Jesus is the Lord and Savior whom believers are called to love with an undying love.

4. The Early Church
The broader context of the letter, the early church was a community of believers navigating their faith in a predominantly non-Christian world.

5. Grace
A key theological concept in Christianity, grace refers to the unmerited favor and love of God towards humanity.
Teaching Points
The Nature of Grace
Grace is a gift from God, not earned by works but given freely to those who love Jesus. It empowers believers to live out their faith.

Undying Love for Christ
The Greek word for "undying" (aphtharsia) implies incorruptibility and immortality. Our love for Christ should be steadfast and enduring, not swayed by life's challenges.

Love as a Mark of True Believers
Genuine love for Jesus is a distinguishing mark of true believers. It is not merely an emotional feeling but a commitment that influences actions and decisions.

Living in Grace
Believers are called to live in the grace of God, allowing it to transform their lives and relationships. This involves extending grace to others as a reflection of the grace received from God.

Community of Love
The church is a community bound by love for Christ. This love should manifest in unity, support, and encouragement among believers.

What Does Ephesians 6:24 Mean
Grace

Grace is God’s unearned favor poured out on believers.

• Ephesians 2:8-9 reminds us, “For it is by grace you have been saved through faith… it is the gift of God.”

• Romans 5:1-2 says we “have gained access by faith into this grace in which we now stand.”

• John 1:16 notes, “From His fullness we have all received grace upon grace.”

Paul ends the letter by reaffirming that everything we need for life and godliness flows from this limitless reservoir of grace.

to all

This blessing is expansive, not selective.

• Acts 10:34 declares that God “shows no favoritism.”

• Romans 10:12-13 highlights that “the same Lord is Lord of all and richly blesses all who call on Him.”

• 1 Timothy 2:3-4 underscores His desire that “all people be saved.”

Paul’s words remind every reader that no believer is outside the reach of divine favor.

who love

Love marks genuine discipleship.

• Jesus states in John 14:15, “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments.”

• 1 John 4:19 affirms, “We love because He first loved us.”

• Mark 12:30 identifies love for God as the greatest commandment.

Grace is pronounced specifically on those whose lives are characterized by this heartfelt devotion.

our Lord Jesus Christ

The blessing centers on a Person, not a program.

• Philippians 2:10-11 proclaims that every knee will bow to Jesus and every tongue confess Him as Lord.

• Colossians 1:15-20 presents Him as the pre-eminent Creator and Redeemer.

• Hebrews 12:2 calls believers to fix their eyes on Jesus, “the author and perfecter of our faith.”

By naming Him, Paul anchors the benediction in the perfect sufficiency of Christ’s work and person.

with an undying love

The phrase points to love that is incorruptible and enduring.

• Romans 8:38-39 assures that nothing “will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus.”

• 1 Corinthians 13:8 declares, “Love never fails.”

• Revelation 2:4 warns against forsaking first love, implying that true love should remain vibrant.

• 1 John 3:18 encourages loving “in action and truth,” evidence of a love that stays alive.

Paul envisions believers whose affection for Christ is not a fleeting emotion but a perpetual, unwavering commitment.

summary

Ephesians 6:24 extends God’s lavish grace to every believer whose life is marked by genuine, persevering love for the Lord Jesus Christ. The verse gathers the core themes of the letter—grace, universality of the gospel, wholehearted devotion, Christ-centeredness, and enduring fidelity—into a single closing blessing, urging us to receive grace and respond with unfading love.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(24) Grace be with all them . . .--The salutation, "Grace be with you," in various forms, is, as St. Paul himself says in 2Thessalonians 3:17, "the token," or characteristic signature, in every one of his Epistles, written with his own hand. It may be noted that it is not found in the Epistles of St. James, St. Peter, St. Jude and St. John, and that it is found in the Epistle to the Hebrews. Here, however, it is at once general and conditional, "to all them who love the Lord Jesus Christ." So in 1Corinthians 16:22, "If any man love not the Lord Jesus Christ, let him be anathema."

In sincerity.--The original is far stronger, "in incorruptibility," a word usually applied to the immortality of heaven (as in Romans 2:7; 1Corinthians 15:42; 1Corinthians 15:50; 1Corinthians 15:53-54; 2Timothy 1:10); only here and in Titus 2:7, applied to human character on earth. Here it evidently means "with a love immortal and imperishable," incapable either of corruption or of decay, a foretaste of the eternal communion in heaven. . . .

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 24. - Grace he with all them that love our Lord Jesus Christ in incorruptibility. As grace was the first word, so it is the last (comp. Ephesians 1:2), not as denoting anything essentially different from the blessings invoked in the preceding verse, but for variety, and in order that the favorite word may be, both here and before, in the place of prominence. The expression is peculiar - love the Lord Jesus Christ ἐν ἀκαθαρσίᾳ. The word denotes, especially in Paul's usage, what is unfading and- permanent. The love that marks genuine Christians is not a passing gleam, like the morning cloud and the early dew, but an abiding emotion. Nowhere can we have a more vivid idea of this incorruptible love than in the closing verses of Romans 8, "I am persuaded that neither death nor life," etc.
