Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
And I prayed to the LORD my God and confessed
Daniel's prayer is a model of humility and repentance. The act of praying and confessing indicates a deep personal relationship with God, acknowledging His sovereignty and the need for divine intervention. This reflects the Jewish practice of prayer and confession, especially during times of distress or national crisis. Daniel's prayer is set during the Babylonian exile, a period when the Israelites were seeking restoration and forgiveness. Confession is a key theme in the Old Testament, seen in Leviticus 26:40-42, where God promises to remember His covenant if His people confess their iniquity.

O, Lord, the great and awesome God
Daniel acknowledges God's majesty and power, using titles that emphasize His transcendence and authority. The terms "great" and "awesome" are often used in the Old Testament to describe God's mighty acts and His ability to inspire reverence and fear. This echoes Deuteronomy 7:21, where God is described as "great and awesome," highlighting His role as a divine warrior and protector of Israel. The use of these titles sets the tone for a prayer of adoration and reverence.

who keeps His covenant of loving devotion
This phrase underscores God's faithfulness and steadfast love, known in Hebrew as "hesed." The covenant refers to the promises made to the patriarchs and the nation of Israel, which God remains committed to despite their unfaithfulness. This reflects the covenantal theology central to the Old Testament, where God's relationship with His people is based on promises and obligations. The idea of a covenant-keeping God is also seen in Nehemiah 1:5, reinforcing the theme of divine faithfulness.

to those who love Him and keep His commandments
This condition highlights the reciprocal nature of the covenant relationship. Love and obedience are central to the covenant, as seen in Deuteronomy 6:5, where loving God with all one's heart, soul, and strength is commanded. Obedience to God's commandments is a demonstration of love and loyalty, and it is through this obedience that the blessings of the covenant are realized. This principle is echoed in the New Testament, where Jesus states in John 14:15, "If you love Me, you will keep My commandments," linking love and obedience as evidence of true discipleship.

Teaching Points
The Importance of Confession
Daniel begins his prayer with confession, recognizing the sins of his people. Confession is a crucial step in restoring our relationship with God.

God's Faithfulness
Despite Israel's unfaithfulness, God remains faithful to His covenant. This encourages believers to trust in God's unchanging nature.

The Role of Intercession
Daniel's prayer is an example of intercessory prayer, where he stands in the gap for his people. Believers are called to intercede for others, especially in times of need.

Loving Devotion and Obedience
The covenant relationship with God involves loving Him and keeping His commandments. Our love for God should be demonstrated through obedience.

The Power of Prayer
Daniel's prayer life is a testament to the power and importance of prayer in seeking God's will and intervention.

What Does Daniel 9:4 Mean
And I prayed to the LORD my God

• Daniel’s first instinct in crisis is personal, direct prayer, showing that communion with God is the believer’s primary resource (Philippians 4:6; 1 Thessalonians 5:17).

• “LORD” (Yahweh) signals covenant relationship; Daniel approaches the same covenant-keeping God who answered in Daniel 2:17-23.

• The phrase “my God” is intimate: faith is never merely national or inherited; it is owned (Psalm 63:1).

and confessed

• Prayer includes honest acknowledgment of sin; revival always begins here (1 John 1:9).

• Confession aligns us with God’s verdict on sin instead of excusing it, as Nehemiah 1:6-7 demonstrates.

• This sets the stage for mercy, just as Psalm 32:5 testifies: “I acknowledged my sin to You… and You forgave the guilt of my sin”.

O, Lord

• The simple cry “O” carries reverence and urgency, echoing Isaiah 6:1-5 where awareness of God’s holiness produces humble address.

• Recognizing God as “Lord” means surrendering to His authority before presenting any petition (Psalm 8:1).

the great and awesome God

• Daniel praises before petitioning, mirroring Psalm 47:2: “How awesome is the LORD Most High, the great King over all the earth”.

• “Awesome” evokes holy fear, not dread, affirming God’s transcendent power (Deuteronomy 7:21).

• Praising God’s greatness lifts faith above circumstances, as Psalm 68:35 states, “You are awesome, O God…”

who keeps His covenant of loving devotion

• God’s steadfast love is covenantal, not fickle; it is anchored in His character (Deuteronomy 7:9).

• The history of Israel proves His faithful love even in discipline (Psalm 103:17-18).

• By recalling the covenant, Daniel appeals to promises God is bound to honor, much like Moses in Exodus 32:13.

to those who love Him and keep His commandments

• Covenant blessings are relational, received by those who respond in love-fueled obedience (Exodus 20:6).

• Love and obedience are inseparable; Jesus restates the pattern: “If you love Me, you will keep My commandments” (John 14:15).

• Obedience is not legalism but grateful allegiance; 1 John 5:3 assures, “His commandments are not burdensome.”

summary

Daniel 9:4 models prayer that begins with worshipful, confession-filled approach to the covenant-keeping God. Daniel reminds us that God’s greatness inspires confidence, His covenant love guarantees faithfulness, and His promises flow to those who love and obey Him. Such prayer aligns the heart with God’s character, preparing us to receive His gracious answers.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 4. - And I prayed unto the Lord my God, and made my confession, and maid, O Lord, the great and dreadful God, keeping the covenant and mercy to them that love him, and to them that keep his commandments. The versions do not call for remark. The first clause is somewhat of a repetition of the end of the previous verse, and may thus be the indication of there having been two recensions; at the same time, the Oriental style allows greater repetition and redundancy than in Western countries would be permitted. There is a reference here to Deuteronomy 7:9, from which the latter clause is quoted verbatim. It is also quoted with equal exactness in Nehemiah 1:5. The chapter in Deuteronomy exhibits God's love for Israel, and hence, as that love is his plea, Daniel appeals to it. We note the evidence of careful acquaintance with preceding Scripture.
