Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
He said to me,
This phrase introduces a divine messenger speaking to Daniel. In the context of Daniel 10, this messenger is often understood to be an angel, possibly Gabriel, who previously appeared to Daniel in earlier visions (Daniel 8:16, 9:21). The role of angels as messengers is consistent with other biblical accounts, such as the angelic visitations to Mary and Joseph in the New Testament (Luke 1:26-38, Matthew 1:20-24).

“Daniel, you are a man who is highly precious.
Daniel is described as "highly precious," indicating his esteemed status before God. This reflects Daniel's faithfulness and dedication to God throughout his life, as seen in his refusal to defile himself with the king's food (Daniel 1:8) and his unwavering commitment to prayer (Daniel 6:10). The term "highly precious" underscores the biblical theme that God values those who are righteous and faithful, as seen in other scriptures like Psalm 116:15.

Consider carefully the words that I am about to say to you.
The instruction to "consider carefully" emphasizes the importance of the message Daniel is about to receive. This reflects the biblical principle of attentiveness to God's word, as seen in Proverbs 4:20-22, where believers are urged to pay attention to wisdom and understanding. The message Daniel receives is part of a larger prophetic revelation concerning future events, highlighting the need for discernment and understanding.

Stand up, for I have now been sent to you.”
The command to "stand up" signifies respect and readiness to receive a message. In biblical times, standing was a posture of attentiveness and reverence, as seen when people stood to hear the reading of the Law (Nehemiah 8:5). The phrase "I have now been sent to you" indicates divine commissioning, similar to other instances where God sends messengers to deliver important revelations, such as the sending of prophets like Isaiah (Isaiah 6:8).

And when he had said this to me, I stood up trembling.
Daniel's response of standing "trembling" reflects the awe and fear often experienced in the presence of the divine or angelic beings. This reaction is consistent with other biblical accounts, such as when the prophet Isaiah encountered God's holiness (Isaiah 6:5) or when the apostle John fell at the feet of the risen Christ (Revelation 1:17). The trembling signifies both reverence and the overwhelming nature of the divine encounter.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Daniel
A prophet and a man of God, known for his unwavering faith and integrity. He is in a period of mourning and prayer when he receives this vision.

2. The Angelic Messenger
Though not named in this verse, the context suggests this is likely Gabriel, who is delivering a message from God to Daniel.

3. Babylon
The setting of Daniel's life and ministry during the Jewish exile. Daniel is in Babylon when he receives this vision.

4. The Vision
Daniel receives a vision that is part of a larger revelation concerning the future of his people and the spiritual battles that are taking place.

5. The Trembling
Daniel's physical reaction to the divine encounter, highlighting the awe and fear that often accompany such experiences.
Teaching Points
God's Esteem for the Faithful
Daniel is called "treasured by God," reminding us that God values those who are faithful and devoted to Him. We should strive to live lives that are pleasing to God, knowing that He sees and values our faithfulness.

Understanding God's Word
The angel instructs Daniel to "understand the words," emphasizing the importance of seeking understanding in our study of Scripture. We should approach God's Word with a desire to comprehend and apply it to our lives.

Courage in the Presence of God
Daniel is told to "stand up," which signifies readiness and courage. In our spiritual walk, we must be prepared to stand firm in faith, even when we feel overwhelmed or fearful.

The Reality of Spiritual Warfare
The delay in the angel's arrival due to spiritual conflict reminds us that there is a spiritual realm that affects our world. We should be vigilant in prayer and aware of the spiritual battles around us.

What Does Daniel 10:11 Mean
He said to me

- The speaker is an angelic messenger, most likely Gabriel as in 8:16 and 9:21.

- Scripture often shows God initiating conversation through His messengers (Judges 6:12; Luke 1:11–13).

- Daniel’s experience reminds us that God still speaks, whether by angelic visitation then or by His Spirit and Word now (Hebrews 1:1-2).

Daniel, you are a man who is highly precious

- The same wording appears in 9:23 and 10:19, underscoring Daniel’s cherished status.

- God esteems those who remain faithful under pressure (1 Samuel 2:30; Revelation 3:8).

- Being “highly precious” is grace, not merit; Daniel’s humility in 9:3–5 shows that.

- Cross reference: “The LORD takes pleasure in those who fear Him” (Psalm 147:11).

Consider carefully the words that I am about to say to you

- The command calls for focused attention, not casual hearing (Proverbs 4:20-22).

- Throughout prophetic Scripture, receiving God’s word demands sober reflection (Ezekiel 2:7-8; Matthew 13:23).

- Daniel’s pattern: he “gave heed to the word and understood the vision” (10:1).

Stand up, for I have now been sent to you

- Standing signifies readiness to receive commission (Ezekiel 2:1-2) and restores dignity to the overwhelmed prophet.

- The phrase “I have now been sent” highlights divine initiative; angels obey God’s direct orders (Psalm 103:20).

- Cross reference: Gabriel to Zechariah—“I was sent to speak to you” (Luke 1:19).

I stood up trembling

- Trembling shows reverent fear, a proper response to holy revelation (Habakkuk 3:16; Acts 9:6).

- God does not dismiss human frailty; moments later He strengthens Daniel (10:18-19).

- The mix of obedience (“I stood up”) and awe (“trembling”) models how believers approach God’s presence (Philippians 2:12-13).

summary

Daniel 10:11 presents a vivid encounter where an angel affirms Daniel’s precious standing, calls him to attentive listening, restores him to his feet, and acknowledges his trembling humanity. The verse teaches that God values the faithful, expects deliberate attention to His word, empowers His servants to stand, and meets them with both authority and compassion.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 11. - And he said unto me, O Daniel, a man greatly beloved, understand the words that I speak unto thee, and stand upright: for unto thee am I dew sent. And when he had spoken this word unto me, I stood trembling. The versions do not afford cause for remark. O Daniel, a man greatly beloved. This is the same term as that applied to Daniel (Daniel 9:23), "man of desires" (which see). Understand the words that I speak unto thee; "have understanding in the words," or better, "matters, which I am speaking or telling to thee." As the language used was one intelligible to Daniel, it was needless to command him to understand the words, but the "matters" communicated by the words might require a special effort of attention to comprehend. Debareem means "matters" as well as "words." And stand upright; "'stand upon thy standing." Gesenius would render this word when it occurs before (Daniel 8:18), "place;" but both here and there the contrast is in the attitude. From being absolutely prone, as in the eighth chapter, or on hands and knees as here, he is to be upright, and, taking his previous attitude into account, this is not merely to stand where he is, and neither approach nor depart. The LXX. renders, τόπου; Theodotion, στάσει; the Vulgate has gradu. For unto thee am I now sent. This assigns a reason for the command to stand upon his feet. In the Assyrian marbles, however lowly the obeisance made to the monarch by any one admitted to his presence, he stands when he receives the monarch's commands. Standing implies attention. And when he had spoken this word unto me, I stood trembling. He obeyed the command, but still trembling took hold of him in the angelic presence.
