But you his son, O Belshazzar
Belshazzar is identified as the son of Nebuchadnezzar, though historically he was the son of Nabonidus. In ancient Near Eastern texts, "son" can also mean "successor" or "descendant." This reflects the biblical practice of using familial terms to denote lineage or succession. Belshazzar was a co-regent with his father Nabonidus, ruling Babylon during his father's absence. This context is crucial for understanding the political and familial dynamics at play.

have not humbled your heart
The concept of humility is central to the biblical narrative, especially in relation to God’s sovereignty. Belshazzar's failure to humble himself is contrasted with Nebuchadnezzar's eventual humility after his period of madness (Daniel 4). Humility before God is a recurring theme in Scripture, seen in figures like Moses (Numbers 12:3) and Jesus (Philippians 2:8). Belshazzar's pride is a direct affront to God, echoing the pride of other biblical figures who faced divine judgment.

even though you knew all this
Belshazzar was aware of the events that transpired with Nebuchadnezzar, including his humbling experience and acknowledgment of God's sovereignty. This phrase underscores the theme of accountability and the importance of learning from the past. The biblical narrative often emphasizes the responsibility of leaders to heed divine warnings, as seen in the stories of Pharaoh (Exodus 7-11) and the kings of Israel and Judah. Belshazzar's knowledge of these events makes his actions more culpable, highlighting the biblical principle that greater knowledge brings greater responsibility (Luke 12:48).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Belshazzar
The last king of Babylon, known for his pride and defiance against God. He is the central figure in this chapter, where he fails to learn from the past mistakes of his predecessor, Nebuchadnezzar.

2. Daniel
A prophet and servant of God who interprets the writing on the wall for Belshazzar. He serves as a voice of truth and judgment in the Babylonian court.

3. Nebuchadnezzar
The former king of Babylon, who was humbled by God due to his pride. His account serves as a warning to Belshazzar.

4. Babylon
The setting of this event, a powerful empire known for its wealth and idolatry, which ultimately falls due to its leaders' arrogance and disobedience to God.

5. The Writing on the Wall
A divine message of judgment against Belshazzar, symbolizing the imminent fall of Babylon due to the king's pride and failure to honor God.
Teaching Points
The Danger of Pride
Belshazzar's account is a stark reminder of how pride can lead to downfall. We must guard our hearts against arrogance and self-reliance.

Learning from the Past
Belshazzar had the opportunity to learn from Nebuchadnezzar's mistakes but chose not to. We should heed the lessons from history and Scripture to avoid repeating the same errors.

The Importance of Humility
Humility is a key virtue in the Christian life. By humbling ourselves before God, we align with His will and receive His grace.

God's Sovereignty and Judgment
God's judgment on Belshazzar demonstrates His sovereignty over nations and leaders. We must recognize God's authority and live in obedience to Him.

The Role of God's Messengers
Daniel's role as a truth-teller in a corrupt environment encourages us to stand firm in our faith and speak God's truth, even when it is unpopular.

What Does Daniel 5:22 Mean
But you his son

– Daniel draws an immediate contrast between Belshazzar and Nebuchadnezzar. Though Belshazzar is called “son,” the term functions as “successor,” placing him in the direct line of responsibility for what happened before (Daniel 5:18–21).

– The phrase reminds us that spiritual heritage is a stewardship. Just as Timothy inherited faith from his mother and grandmother (2 Timothy 1:5), Belshazzar inherited firsthand reports of God’s sovereignty over kings.

– Scripture consistently teaches that lineage never guarantees righteousness; each generation must choose (Ezekiel 18:20, John 1:12–13).

O Belshazzar

– Daniel names the king personally, making the rebuke immediate and intimate—no hiding behind the throne. Nathan did the same when he told David, “You are the man!” (2 Samuel 12:7).

– God’s servants often address rulers by name to underscore divine authority over earthly power (Acts 24:24–25; Isaiah 45:1).

Have not humbled your heart

– Humility before God is non-negotiable. Nebuchadnezzar learned it the hard way (Daniel 4:34–37). Belshazzar refused the lesson.

– Pride sets itself against God (Proverbs 16:18; James 4:6). Humbling the heart is the cure, yet Belshazzar preferred arrogance, symbolized by drinking from the sacred vessels (Daniel 5:3–4).

– The Lord gives clear warnings before judgment; willful pride invites swift consequences (Isaiah 2:11–12).

Even though you knew all this

– Knowledge heightens accountability (Luke 12:47–48). Belshazzar cannot plead ignorance; palace archives and living witnesses recounted Nebuchadnezzar’s seven years of humiliation.

– Romans 1:21 describes the same pattern: “For although they knew God, they neither glorified Him as God nor gave thanks to Him.”

– Deliberate disregard turns knowledge into condemnation rather than salvation (Hebrews 10:26–27).

summary

Daniel 5:22 exposes Belshazzar’s fatal choice. As Nebuchadnezzar’s successor, he inherited undeniable evidence of God’s supremacy. Addressed personally, he is charged with failing to bow his heart. Because he knew the truth and still exalted himself, judgment was certain. The verse stands as a timeless warning: spiritual heritage and information are blessings, but without humble response they become grounds for divine reckoning.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) Though thou knewest.--The whole history of Nebuchadnezzar was known to Belshazzar. He had not, however, learned the moral lesson conveyed by it. He was therefore doubly guilty in the sight of God, because his blasphemy was wilful.
