Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
“Go on your way, Daniel,”
This phrase indicates a directive for Daniel to continue with his life and mission. It suggests a sense of closure for Daniel's immediate concerns about the visions he has received. The instruction to "go on your way" implies trust in God's timing and plan, reflecting a common biblical theme where individuals are encouraged to live faithfully despite not having full understanding of God's purposes. This can be compared to other biblical figures like Abraham, who was called to go to a land he did not know, trusting in God's promise.

“he replied,”
The speaker here is likely the angelic messenger who has been communicating with Daniel throughout the latter chapters of the book. This reflects the common biblical motif of divine messages being delivered through angels, as seen in other instances such as Gabriel's announcements to Mary and Zechariah in the New Testament. The use of an angelic messenger underscores the importance and divine origin of the message.

“for the words are closed up and sealed”
This phrase indicates that the prophecy Daniel received is not meant to be fully understood or revealed at the present time. In ancient Near Eastern cultures, sealing a document was a way to preserve and protect its contents until the appropriate time for its opening. This concept of sealing is also seen in Revelation 5, where a scroll with seven seals is opened only by the Lamb, symbolizing Christ. The sealing of the words suggests that the full understanding of the prophecy is reserved for a future time, emphasizing God's sovereignty over history.

“until the time of the end.”
This phrase points to an eschatological focus, referring to the culmination of history as understood in biblical prophecy. The "time of the end" is a recurring theme in apocalyptic literature, including the book of Daniel and Revelation, where it signifies the period when God's ultimate purposes will be fulfilled. This aligns with the Christian belief in the second coming of Christ and the final establishment of God's kingdom. The phrase encourages believers to remain faithful and vigilant, trusting that God's plan will be revealed in His perfect timing.

Persons / Places / Events
1. Daniel
A prophet and a key figure in the Old Testament, known for his faithfulness to God and his ability to interpret dreams and visions. He is the recipient of the message in this verse.

2. The Angelic Messenger
While not named in this specific verse, the context of Daniel 12 suggests that this is likely the same angelic figure who has been delivering messages to Daniel throughout the latter chapters of the book.

3. The Time of the End
This refers to the eschatological period, a future time when God's ultimate plans for humanity and the world will be fulfilled.
Teaching Points
The Mystery of God's Timing
God's plans are often beyond human understanding and are revealed in His perfect timing. We are called to trust in His sovereignty and wisdom.

Faithfulness in the Present
Like Daniel, we are encouraged to continue faithfully in our walk with God, even when we do not fully understand His plans.

The Importance of Prophecy
Prophecies serve as a reminder of God's control over history and His ultimate victory. They encourage believers to remain steadfast and hopeful.

Sealed Until the Appointed Time
Some aspects of God's plan are intentionally hidden until the right moment. This teaches us patience and reliance on God's revelation.

Living with an Eternal Perspective
Understanding that there is a "time of the end" should influence how we live today, prioritizing eternal values over temporary concerns.

What Does Daniel 12:9 Mean
Go on your way, Daniel

• The angel frees Daniel from further responsibility for unveiling details. Much like Elijah in 1 Kings 19:15–18, Daniel is told to continue his earthly course and trust God’s timing.

• Scripture often urges faithful servants to “go” after receiving revelation—Genesis 12:1; Matthew 28:19—reminding us obedience is lived out, not merely studied.

• Daniel’s role was to record, not resolve, every mystery (Daniel 12:4). His part is finished; God will unfold the rest.

he replied

• The speaker is the same heavenly messenger who has guided Daniel since 10:11. Angelic assurance highlights divine authorship; the message is certain (Hebrews 2:2).

• Replying implies God hears the prophet’s concern (Daniel 12:8) and answers, echoing Jeremiah 33:3 where God promises to reveal “great and unsearchable things” on His terms.

• It underscores a living dialogue, reinforcing that prophecy is relational, not abstract.

for the words are closed up

• “Closed up” pictures a scroll rolled tight, inaccessible until God chooses (Isaiah 29:11).

• This safeguards the prophecy from tampering, as seen when Jeremiah’s scroll is protected even after Jehoiakim burns it (Jeremiah 36:27-28).

• It assures readers that every prediction—rise of Antichrist, resurrection, final judgment—remains intact for its appointed unveiling (Habakkuk 2:3).

and sealed

• A seal in ancient times authenticated and preserved (Esther 8:8). Here it guarantees divine authority over Daniel’s visions.

• Comparable to the sealed scroll in Revelation 5:1-5, which only the Lamb can open, emphasizing Christ’s exclusive right to disclose end-time events.

• The sealing does not hide truth from all generations but reserves full clarity for those living when fulfillment accelerates (Proverbs 25:2).

until the time of the end

• “Time of the end” aligns with earlier references in Daniel 8:17 and 10:14—an era climaxing in tribulation, resurrection, and kingdom consummation (Daniel 12:1-2).

• Jesus echoes this schedule in Matthew 24:15-31, treating Daniel’s prophecy as future and literal.

• 1 Peter 1:10-12 notes that prophets saw pieces, while believers near the end will witness completion, empowered by the Spirit to understand previously sealed truths.

• Thus, increasing global knowledge (Daniel 12:4) and escalating birth-pains (Romans 8:22) will pry the seal open for the final generation.

summary

Daniel 12:9 informs us that God has finished speaking to His servant about end-time events. Daniel is released to live faithfully, while the prophetic scroll is divinely secured until history reaches its climax. The verse guarantees the reliability of the revelation, limits human speculation, and promises that clarity will burst forth precisely when the closing chapter of God’s redemptive plan unfolds.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) Go thy way.--That is, be at peace. Observe that the matter is not explained to Daniel any further. He is assured that the end will most certainly come. Compare another gentle rebuke that was addressed to one who wished to see further than was fitting into the future (John 21:21-22).

Closed up and sealed.--To be explained as in Daniel 12:4. The book is to be carefully preserved till the end of time.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - And he said, Go thy way, Daniel; for the words are closed up and sealed till the time of the end. The Septuagint omits the last clause, and completes this verse from that which succeeds, "And he said, Depart, Daniel; for the commands are veiled and sealed until many shall be tried and shall be sanctified." Theodotion renders, "Come, Daniel, because the words are fenced and sealed till the time of the end." The Peshitta and the Vulgate agree with the Massoretic. Go thy way, Daniel. This is a refusal to grant Daniel's prayer, but in the refusal no condemnation of Daniel is implied. The oracles were sealed until circumstance broke the seal. The purpose of prophecy was not to enable men to write history beforehand. It is to be a sign that, recognized in its fulfilment, may afford evidence of the Divinity of the message or person to whom it referred. Closed up and sealed. This verse gives us the real meaning of these words. Daniel's oracles were not concealed and sealed from being read, but because they were not interpreted they were not understood. For even to Daniel they are "closed up and sealed." Till the time of the end. This is omitted, as may be seen above, from the Septuagint. Although this has a satisfactory meaning, yet it seems better to connect this verse more directly with that which follows.
