Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
But the people did not listen
This phrase highlights the persistent disobedience of the Israelites, a recurring theme throughout the Old Testament. Despite numerous warnings from prophets and the clear instructions given in the Law of Moses, the people often turned away from God. This reflects a pattern seen in passages like Judges 2:17, where the Israelites repeatedly failed to heed divine guidance. The failure to listen is not just a physical act of ignoring but a spiritual rebellion against God's covenant.

and Manasseh led them astray
Manasseh, son of Hezekiah, was one of the most notorious kings of Judah, reigning for 55 years. His leadership marked a significant departure from his father's reforms, as he reintroduced idolatry and pagan practices. This phrase underscores the influence of leadership on the spiritual direction of a nation. Manasseh's actions are detailed in 2 Kings 21:1-7, where he is described as building altars to Baal, worshiping the host of heaven, and even sacrificing his own son. His reign is a stark reminder of the impact of ungodly leadership, as seen in Proverbs 29:2.

so that they did greater evil than the nations
The comparison to the nations that God had previously judged and displaced for their wickedness (such as the Canaanites) is significant. It indicates that Judah's sins under Manasseh's rule were not only comparable but exceeded those of the pagan nations. This is a fulfillment of warnings given in Leviticus 18:24-28, where God cautioned Israel against adopting the detestable practices of the nations they were to dispossess. The phrase serves as a sobering reminder of the potential for God's people to fall into deeper sin than those who do not know Him.

that the LORD had destroyed before the Israelites
This refers to the divine judgment executed upon the Canaanite nations during the conquest of the Promised Land, as recorded in the book of Joshua. The destruction of these nations was due to their abominable practices, which had reached a fullness of iniquity (Genesis 15:16). The mention of the LORD's past actions serves as a historical reminder of God's righteousness and justice. It also foreshadows the impending judgment that would come upon Judah for their transgressions, as prophesied in Jeremiah 25:9-11. This phrase emphasizes the seriousness of turning away from God and the inevitable consequences of such actions.

Teaching Points
The Influence of Leadership
Manasseh's reign demonstrates the powerful influence leaders have over their people. Leaders are called to guide others toward righteousness, not lead them astray.

The Consequences of Idolatry
Idolatry leads to moral and spiritual decay. As believers, we must guard against allowing anything to take the place of God in our lives.

The Importance of Listening to God
The people of Judah did not listen to God’s warnings. We must be attentive to God’s Word and responsive to His guidance.

The Danger of Complacency
Judah's descent into greater evil than the nations before them warns against becoming complacent in our faith and moral standards.

Repentance and Restoration
Despite Manasseh's evil, God’s grace allows for repentance and restoration. We should never underestimate the power of turning back to God.

What Does 2 Kings 21:9 Mean
But the people did not listen

- The nation as a whole refused to heed God’s clear warnings (2 Kings 21:8) and the prophetic calls returning them to covenant faithfulness (Jeremiah 25:4–7).

- This willful deafness echoes Israel’s earlier hard-heartedness in the wilderness (Psalm 106:24–26) and during the days of the Judges (Judges 2:17).

- Persistent refusal to listen is always presented as moral rebellion, never as mere misunderstanding (Proverbs 28:9; Zechariah 7:11-12).

and Manasseh led them astray

- Leadership carries real spiritual weight; when the king embraced idolatry (2 Kings 21:3–7), the people followed. Compare Jeroboam’s precedent in 1 Kings 14:16 and Ahab’s in 1 Kings 21:25-26.

- The phrase underscores culpability on both sides: Manasseh as a stumbling block (Matthew 18:6) and the people for choosing to follow (Isaiah 9:16).

- God’s design is that rulers shepherd toward righteousness (Deuteronomy 17:18-20), but here the shepherd drives the flock over a cliff (Hosea 4:9).

so that they did greater evil than the nations

- Israel was meant to be “a kingdom of priests” (Exodus 19:6), yet their apostasy surpassed the Canaanites’ original depravity (Leviticus 18:24–30).

- The comparison exposes sin’s downward spiral: unchecked rebellion grows worse (Romans 1:24–32).

- Greater privilege brings greater accountability (Luke 12:48); therefore their greater evil is especially grievous.

that the LORD had destroyed before the Israelites

- God had earlier judged the Canaanite nations to cleanse the land (Deuteronomy 9:4-5; Joshua 23:9-13).

- By matching—and exceeding—those sins, Judah invited the same judgment (2 Kings 23:26-27).

- The historical reference affirms God’s consistency: He judges sin impartially, whether in pagans or in His covenant people (Acts 10:34; 1 Corinthians 10:1-11).

summary

2 Kings 21:9 shows a tragic chain reaction: the people’s refusal to listen, Manasseh’s corrupt leadership, the nation’s escalating wickedness, and the inevitable comparison to the earlier cultures God had expelled. The verse warns that turning a deaf ear to God’s Word and following ungodly leadership will always plunge a society deeper into sin and place it under the same righteous judgment previously poured out on pagan nations.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(9) Seduced them.--Led them astray. Chronicles renders the same verb made them to err.

To do more evil.--To do the evil more . . . The LXX. adds: "in the eyes of Jehovah." The idolatry of Judah was worse than that of the Canaanites, because they worshipped only their national gods, whereas Judah forsook its own God and was ready to adopt almost any foreign cultus with which it was brought into contact (Jeremiah 2:11).

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 9. - But they hearkened not. The people, and not Manasseh alone, were disobedient. Had they remained faithful, Manasseh's sin would not have affected their future. And Manasseh seduced them. The influence of a young and gay king, always great, is in the East immense. When such a king succeeds one of strict and rind principles, he easily carries away the multitude with him, and leads them on to any excess of profligacy and irreligion. The beginnings of sin are delightful, and the votaries of pleasure, readily beguiled into evil courses, know not where to stop. Manasseh seduced them, we are told, to do more evil than did the nations whom the Lord destroyed before the children of Israel; that is, than the Hivites, Hittites, Amorites, Canaanites, Perizzites, Gergashites, and Jebusites (Deuteronomy 7:1, etc.). The sin of Israel exceeded that of the Canaanitish nations, not so much in any outward and tangible features, as in the fact that it was committed against light, in spite of the Law, and against all the warnings and denunciations of the prophets (comp. 2 Kings 17:13, 14).
