And Ahaz rested with his fathers
This phrase indicates the death of King Ahaz, who was the 12th king of Judah. The expression "rested with his fathers" is a common biblical euphemism for death, suggesting a joining with ancestors in the afterlife. Ahaz's reign was marked by idolatry and alliances with foreign powers, which were contrary to God's commands. His death signifies the end of a tumultuous period for Judah, characterized by spiritual decline and political instability.

and was buried with them in the City of David
The City of David refers to Jerusalem, specifically the area that was the original settlement of King David. Being buried in the City of David was an honor, indicating royal status and lineage. However, 2 Chronicles 28:27 notes that Ahaz was not buried in the tombs of the kings, suggesting a lack of honor due to his unfaithfulness to God. This burial location underscores the importance of Jerusalem as the political and spiritual center of Judah.

and his son Hezekiah reigned in his place
Hezekiah's ascension to the throne marks a significant turning point for Judah. Unlike his father, Hezekiah is remembered as a righteous king who sought to restore the worship of Yahweh and purify the temple (2 Kings 18:1-6). His reign is characterized by religious reforms and a return to covenant faithfulness. Hezekiah's leadership is often seen as a foreshadowing of Christ, as he acts as a savior figure for Judah, delivering them from Assyrian threats and leading a spiritual revival.

Ahaz's reign serves as a warning about the dangers of turning away from God and relying on worldly alliances.

The Hope of Renewal
Hezekiah's succession represents the possibility of renewal and reform, even after a period of spiritual decline.

The Importance of Legacy
The transition from Ahaz to Hezekiah highlights the impact of a leader's faithfulness (or lack thereof) on future generations.

Despite Ahaz's failures, God's plan continued through Hezekiah, demonstrating His control over history.

What Does 2 Kings 16:20 Mean
And Ahaz rested with his fathers

– The phrase signals the close of King Ahaz’s earthly life, echoing earlier notices of monarchs passing away (1 Kings 2:10; 14:31).

– “Rested” reminds us that death is not annihilation but a transition; the body ceases, yet the soul continues, awaiting God’s final judgment (Hebrews 9:27; Luke 16:22-23).

– By linking Ahaz with his “fathers,” Scripture underlines the continuity of Israel’s history—each generation hands down a spiritual legacy, for good or ill (Judges 2:10).

– Though Ahaz practiced idolatry (2 Kings 16:3-4), God still records his death in the same respectful formula used for other kings, showing the Lord’s impartial accuracy in recording human deeds.

and was buried with them in the City of David

– The City of David (2 Samuel 5:7) housed royal tombs; burial there affirmed Ahaz’s legitimate place in the Davidic line, despite his failures (2 Chronicles 28:27).

– Royal burial sites served as national reminders of God’s covenant with David (2 Samuel 7:12-16). Even an unfaithful king could not nullify that promise (Psalm 89:30-37).

– The interment location also underscores that earthly honor can outlast personal righteousness—an implicit warning that God’s ultimate verdict matters more than human ceremony (Matthew 23:27-28).

and his son Hezekiah reigned in his place

– The throne passes smoothly to Hezekiah, illustrating God’s providence in preserving a Davidic successor (2 Kings 18:1; 2 Chronicles 29:1).

– A striking contrast unfolds: Ahaz led Judah into deeper idolatry, while Hezekiah “did what was right in the eyes of the LORD” (2 Kings 18:3).

– God often raises reformers after seasons of decline—renewing worship (2 Chronicles 29:3-11), cleansing the temple (2 Chronicles 29:15-17), and restoring covenant faithfulness (2 Kings 18:4-6).

– The seamless accession stresses divine sovereignty: human kings come and go, but the Lord’s redemptive plan moves forward (Daniel 2:21).

summary

2 Kings 16:20 closes the record of a troubled reign and opens the door to renewal. Ahaz dies and is buried in royal dignity, yet his life warns of the emptiness of compromise. The burial in the City of David reaffirms God’s unbroken covenant line. Hezekiah’s rise showcases the Lord’s ability to bring fresh obedience from the same lineage, proving that even after seasons of spiritual darkness, God’s faithfulness endures and His purposes prevail.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 20. - And Ahaz slept with his fathers, and was buried with his fathers in the city of David. This must be taken in the same sense, and with the same limitations, as the same phrase in 2 Kings 12:21. The writer of Chronicles (2 Chronicles 28:27) says, "And Ahaz slept with his fathers, and they buried him in the city, even in Jerusalem: but they brought him not into the sepulchers of the kings." Like Uzziah, he was not thought worthy of sepulture in the royal catacomb (see the comment on 2 Kings 12:21).
