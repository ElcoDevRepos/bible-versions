Study BibleBook ◦ Chapter 
Elisha turned away and paced back and forth across the room.
This phrase highlights Elisha's persistence and dedication in seeking God's intervention. The act of pacing can be seen as a form of intense prayer or meditation, reflecting a deep reliance on God. In biblical context, prophets often engaged in physical acts as part of their intercessory efforts (e.g., Elijah on Mount Carmel in 1 Kings 18:42-44). The room is likely the upper chamber prepared by the Shunammite woman, symbolizing a place set apart for divine encounters.

Then he got on the bed and stretched himself out over the boy again,
Elisha's actions mirror those of his mentor, Elijah, who performed a similar miracle in 1 Kings 17:21. This physical act of stretching out over the boy signifies a transfer of life and power, symbolizing the prophet's role as a mediator of God's life-giving power. It also prefigures the concept of resurrection, a theme that finds its ultimate fulfillment in Jesus Christ, who is the resurrection and the life (John 11:25).

and the boy sneezed seven times and opened his eyes.
The sneezing seven times is significant, as the number seven often represents completeness or perfection in the Bible (Genesis 2:2-3, Revelation 1:20). This act signifies the complete restoration of life to the boy. The opening of the eyes symbolizes awakening and new life, akin to spiritual rebirth. This miracle foreshadows the resurrection power of Christ, who opens the eyes of the spiritually blind and brings life to the dead (John 9:39, Ephesians 2:5).

Elisha's actions demonstrate unwavering faith in God's ability to perform miracles. Believers are encouraged to trust in God's power, even in seemingly hopeless situations.

The miracle reflects God's compassion for the Shunammite woman and her family. Believers can find comfort in knowing that God cares deeply for their personal struggles and needs.

The boy sneezed seven times, a number often associated with completeness and perfection in the Bible. This signifies the complete restoration of life and health.

What Does 2 Kings 4:35 Mean
Elisha turned away

Elisha had already stretched himself over the child once (v. 34). Turning away shows deliberate pause rather than defeat.

• A moment to refocus on the Lord’s power, not his own effort (Psalm 62:5).

• Prophets sometimes stepped aside to seek fresh direction—compare Elijah on Mount Carmel in 1 Kings 18:42.

• Jesus likewise withdrew briefly before mighty works (Mark 7:33).

and paced back and forth across the room

The pacing pictures fervent, persistent intercession (James 5:16).

• Walking can aid focused prayer, a bodily echo of wrestling in the Spirit (Colossians 4:12).

• Similar urgency is seen when Hezekiah turned and prayed for healing (Isaiah 38:2–5).

• Elisha’s movement also keeps him near the child, ready for God’s timing (Habakkuk 2:3).

Then he got on the bed

Returning to the boy signals confidence that God has answered (1 John 5:14-15).

• Positioning himself on the same level as the lifeless body acknowledges human helplessness (Psalm 103:14).

• Elijah used the same posture when God raised the widow’s son (1 Kings 17:21-22), hinting at continuity in God’s methods.

• In Acts 20:10 Paul lay on Eutychus, showing the pattern continues into the New Testament.

and stretched himself out over the boy again

Contact underscored that life would flow from God through His prophet (2 Kings 13:21).

• Touch often accompanies divine healing: Jesus touched the leper (Matthew 8:3) and the coffin at Nain (Luke 7:14).

• Repetition (“again”) confirms perseverance until the answer manifests (Luke 18:1-8).

and the boy sneezed seven times

Sneezing proves breath restored (Genesis 2:7).

• Seven points to completeness (Joshua 6:15; Revelation 1:4). The child is fully, not partially, revived.

• The sound of sneezing breaks the silence of death—an audible testimony to everyone present (Psalm 118:17).

and opened his eyes

Opening eyes seals the miracle with unmistakable evidence of life (Luke 8:55).

• God, “who opens the eyes of the blind,” now opens eyes that had been closed in death (Psalm 146:8).

• This foreshadows the spiritual awakening Christ brings (Ephesians 2:5).

summary

Every movement in 2 Kings 4:35 showcases determined faith and God’s sovereign power. Elisha’s turning, pacing, returning, and stretching reveal persistent, expectant prayer. The boy’s seven sneezes and opened eyes display complete restoration, fulfilling God’s promise to the Shunammite mother. The passage assures believers that the Lord who literally raised this child still hears fervent prayer and possesses absolute authority over life and death.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(35) He returned.--From off the bed.

Walked in the house to and fro.--Or, in the chamber. Elisha's walking to and fro is an index of intense excitement. He was earnestly expecting the fulfilment of his prayer. Cornelius a Lapide thinks the prophet walked "ut ambulando excitaret majorem calorem quem puero communicaret" (!)

The child sneezed.--The verb occurs here only. It denotes a faint rather than a loud sneeze. (Heb., 'atish?h; Job 41:10.) It is omitted by the LXX., which has, "and he bowed himself over the boy until seven times." The repeated sneezing was a sign of restored respiration. (Comp. Luke 7:15.)

Keil supposes that whereas Elijah raised the widow's son at once, his successor only restored the Shunammite's son by degrees; and that this betokens an inferiority on the part of Elisha. But the narrative in 1Kings 17:17 seq. is plainly abridged.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 35. - Then he returned, and walked in the house to and fro; literally, once and once fro; took, i.e., a single turn up and down the large room adjoining his bed-chamber - scarcely with any remedial object, but as men do when they are in distress and doubt. And went up, and stretched himself upon him - i.e. repeated his former act, laying himself upon the child, and warming it - and the child sneezed seven times - showing the recovery of suspended respiration - and the child opened his eyes; i.e. came to himself.
