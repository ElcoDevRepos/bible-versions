And Jezebel’s body will lie like dung in the field
This phrase signifies the ultimate disgrace and humiliation for Jezebel, a queen known for her idolatry and persecution of God's prophets. In ancient Israel, a proper burial was a sign of honor, while being left unburied was a curse and a sign of divine judgment. The comparison to dung emphasizes the contempt and worthlessness attributed to her due to her actions. This fulfills the prophecy given by Elijah in 1 Kings 21:23, where it was foretold that dogs would devour Jezebel by the wall of Jezreel.

on the plot of ground at Jezreel
Jezreel was a significant location in Israel, serving as a royal city and a site of many pivotal events. It was here that Naboth's vineyard was unjustly taken by Ahab and Jezebel, leading to their downfall. The mention of Jezreel ties back to the injustices committed by Jezebel and Ahab, highlighting the theme of divine retribution. The plot of ground is a reminder of the specific prophecy against Jezebel and the fulfillment of God's word.

so that no one can say: This is Jezebel.’”
This phrase underscores the complete erasure of Jezebel's legacy and identity. In the cultural context of the time, a person's name and memory were crucial to their honor and legacy. By ensuring that no one could identify her remains, the prophecy indicates that Jezebel's influence and memory would be obliterated. This serves as a stark warning against idolatry and rebellion against God, illustrating the severe consequences of such actions. The anonymity of her remains contrasts with the eternal remembrance of the righteous, as seen in scriptures like Proverbs 10:7, which states, "The memory of the righteous is a blessing, but the name of the wicked will rot."

What Does 2 Kings 9:37 Mean
And Jezebel’s body

“Dogs will eat Jezebel within the boundaries of Jezreel.” (2 Kings 9:10)

• The prophecy against Jezebel reaches all the way back to 1 Kings 21:23, underscoring God’s unwavering commitment to fulfill His word.

• Jezebel, who led Israel into Baal worship (1 Kings 16:31) and murdered God’s prophets (1 Kings 18:4), now faces personal judgment—her own body is the object of divine justice.

• Like Pharaoh (Exodus 14:30), Goliath (1 Samuel 17:46), and Herod (Acts 12:23), Jezebel’s fall shows that no ruler, however powerful, can defy the LORD and escape accountability.

will lie like dung in the field

• Dung is waste—worthless, despised, trampled underfoot (Deuteronomy 28:26; Jeremiah 8:2).

• God’s sentence strips Jezebel of every shred of dignity; her corpse receives no burial, no monument, no mourning (Psalm 79:2-3).

• The graphic language drives home the seriousness of idolatry and bloodshed. What seems shocking is meant to shock us back to the fear of the LORD (Hebrews 10:31).

on the plot of ground at Jezreel

• This exact field once belonged to Naboth, a righteous man Jezebel helped murder (1 Kings 21:15-16).

• By choosing that very spot, God turns Jezebel’s stolen gain into her grave. The “poetic justice” here mirrors Haman hanging on his own gallows (Esther 7:9-10) and King Saul falling on the same battlefield where he once shed innocent blood (1 Samuel 31:4; 2 Samuel 21:1).

• Every detail affirms the LORD as both Witness and Judge—He remembers every injustice and repays it perfectly (Romans 12:19).

so that no one can say: “This is Jezebel.”

• When Jehu’s men searched for her, they found “only the skull, the feet, and the palms of her hands” (2 Kings 9:35). The rest had been consumed by dogs, exactly as foretold.

• Without a recognizable body, Jezebel receives no honor in death, no chance for admirers to build a shrine or celebrate her legacy (Proverbs 10:7; Psalm 37:10).

• The scene also warns future generations: if even a queen can disappear into oblivion for defying God, how much more should ordinary people humble themselves before Him (1 Peter 5:5-6).

summary

2 Kings 9:37 is God’s final word on Jezebel. Every phrase underscores His sovereignty, justice, and faithfulness to prophecy. Her body—once adorned for manipulation and pride—becomes dung in the very field she seized through murder. Stripped of burial and memorial, Jezebel’s name fades, while God’s word stands forever. The verse is a sober reminder that sin pays wages, judgment is certain, and the Lord vindicates righteousness in His perfect time.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(37) And the carcase of Jezebel.--This continuation of the prophecy is not given in 1Kings 21:23. It is probably original; not "a free expansion" by Jehu, as Keil asserts.

Shall be.--It is questionable whether the Hebrew text is to be read as a rare ancient form weh?y?th); or simply as an instance of defective writing (weh?yeth?). We prefer the second view.

As dung.--Comp. Psalm 83:10.

So that they shall not say.--Comp. Genesis 11:7 for the construction. The sense is, So that men will no longer be able to recognise her mangled remains.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 37. - And the carcass of Jezebel shall be as dung upon the face of the field (comp. Psalm 83:10; Zephaniah 1:17; Jeremiah 9:22; Jeremiah 16:4, etc.). The expression was proverbial. In the portion of Jezreel (see the comment on the preceding verse); so that they shall not say, This is Jezebel. The fragments of the body were so scattered that there could be no collective tomb, no place whereat admirers could congregate and say, "Here lies the great queen - here lies Jezebel." To rest in no tomb was viewed as a shame and a disgrace.
