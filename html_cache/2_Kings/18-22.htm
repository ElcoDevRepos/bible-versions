But if you say to me, ‘We trust in the LORD our God,’
This phrase is part of a speech by the Assyrian field commander, the Rabshakeh, during the siege of Jerusalem under King Hezekiah. The Assyrians were known for their military prowess and psychological warfare, often attempting to undermine the confidence of their enemies. The Rabshakeh challenges the faith of the Israelites, questioning their reliance on God. This reflects a common biblical theme where faith is tested in the face of overwhelming odds, similar to the challenges faced by figures like David against Goliath (1 Samuel 17).

is He not the One whose high places and altars Hezekiah has removed,
Hezekiah's religious reforms included the removal of high places and altars throughout Judah (2 Kings 18:4). These high places were often associated with idolatrous practices, even though some were used for worshiping Yahweh. The Rabshakeh's statement reveals a misunderstanding or misrepresentation of Hezekiah's reforms, suggesting that removing these sites was an affront to God. In reality, Hezekiah's actions were in obedience to God's command to centralize worship in Jerusalem, aligning with Deuteronomy 12:5-14.

saying to Judah and Jerusalem:
Hezekiah's reforms were directed at both the people of Judah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, emphasizing the importance of unified worship. This reflects the centralization of religious practices in the capital, which was not only a political move but also a spiritual one, aiming to purify the worship of Yahweh and eliminate syncretism.

‘You must worship before this altar in Jerusalem’?
The altar in Jerusalem refers to the altar in the Temple, the designated place for sacrifices and worship according to the Mosaic Law. This centralization of worship was a fulfillment of God's instructions in Deuteronomy 12:11, where God chose Jerusalem as the place for His name to dwell. The Rabshakeh's challenge highlights the tension between political power and religious obedience, a recurring theme in the Old Testament. This altar also foreshadows the ultimate sacrifice of Jesus Christ, who is seen as the fulfillment of the sacrificial system, offering Himself once for all (Hebrews 10:10).

Persons / Places / Events
1. Hezekiah

The southern kingdom and its capital, where Hezekiah centralized worship according to God's command.

3. Rabshakeh
The Assyrian official who delivered a message of intimidation to the people of Judah, questioning their trust in God.

Sites of worship that were often used for idolatrous practices, which Hezekiah removed to purify the worship of Yahweh.

Teaching Points
Trust in God Amidst Challenges
Hezekiah's reforms were a demonstration of trust in God, even when facing external threats. Believers today are called to trust God, especially when their faith is challenged.

Purity of Worship
Removing high places and altars signifies the importance of pure worship. Christians are encouraged to examine and remove any idols or distractions in their own lives that hinder true worship.

Centrality of God's Commandments
Hezekiah's actions reflect obedience to God's commandments. Believers should prioritize God's Word and align their lives with His instructions.

Facing Intimidation with Faith
The Assyrian threat was a test of faith for Judah. Christians can learn to face intimidation and fear with faith, relying on God's promises and power.

Leadership and Reform
Hezekiah's leadership in reforming worship serves as a model for Christian leaders to guide their communities towards faithfulness to God.

What Does 2 Kings 18:22 Mean
But if you say to me,

• The Assyrian field commander, Rabshakeh, is challenging Hezekiah’s representatives (see 2 Kings 18:19).

• His opening “But if” casts doubt, setting up a taunt that Judah’s stated confidence is misguided (compare 2 Chronicles 32:10–11).

• The statement shows the enemy’s strategy: undermine faith before fighting a battle.

‘We trust in the LORD our God,’

• Judah’s hope is declared openly, echoing the repeated biblical call to rely on the LORD alone (Psalm 20:7; Proverbs 3:5; Isaiah 26:3–4).

• Trust here is not vague optimism; it rests on God’s covenant faithfulness (2 Samuel 7:23–24).

• The enemy mocks that trust, implying it is naïve when political and military pressures mount (Psalm 25:2).

is He not the One whose high places and altars Hezekiah has removed,

• Hezekiah’s reforms tore down illicit worship sites across the land (2 Kings 18:4; 2 Chronicles 31:1).

• Rabshakeh twists those faithful actions into evidence that Hezekiah angered God, suggesting Judah has lost divine favor.

• The accusation reveals spiritual blindness: obedience to God’s Word is misread as rebellion (Isaiah 5:20).

• The incident illustrates how the world often misunderstands genuine reform and holiness.

saying to Judah and Jerusalem: ‘You must worship before this altar in Jerusalem’?

• Hezekiah required worship in the temple, fulfilling God’s command to centralize sacrifice (Deuteronomy 12:5–6, 13–14; 1 Kings 8:29).

• Rabshakeh mocks the exclusivity of temple worship, not recognizing that God Himself ordained it.

• The enemy implies that restricting worship to one altar limits divine power, yet Scripture teaches God’s presence dwells where He chooses (Psalm 132:13–14).

• This earthly ridicule of God’s appointed means echoes later scorn toward Christ, the true temple (John 2:19).

summary

Rabshakeh’s taunt in 2 Kings 18:22 distorts Hezekiah’s faithful reforms, aiming to shake Judah’s confidence in the LORD. He portrays obedience as offense, hoping to sever the people’s trust. Yet the verse highlights the very opposite: true reliance on God stands firm when worldly voices misinterpret righteousness. Hezekiah’s removal of high places was obedience, not apostasy, and worship at the Jerusalem altar aligned Judah with God’s revealed will. The passage calls believers to trust unflinchingly in the LORD’s covenant promises, even when opponents misconstrue fidelity as folly.

Ellicott's Commentary for English Readers
(22) But if ye say.--The address seems to turn abruptly from Hezekiah to his ministers, and to the garrison of Jerusalem in general. But the LXX., Syriac, Arabic, and Isaiah 36:7 have the singular, "But if thou say," which is probably original. (Hezekiah is presently mentioned in the third person, to avoid ambiguity.)

In the Lord our God.--The emphatic words of the clause.

Whose high places and whose altars Hezekiah hath taken away.--This is just the construction which a heathen would naturally put on Hezekiah's abolition of the local sanctuaries. (2Kings 18:4; 2Chronicles 31:1.) The Assyrians would appear to have heard of Hezekiah's reformation, As he was a vassal of the great king, no doubt his proceedings were watched with jealous interest.

Ye shall worship . . . in Jerusalem?--Literally, Before this altar shall ye worship, at Jerusalem. The great altar of burnt offering was to be the one altar, and Jerusalem the one city, where Jehovah might be worshipped.

Pulpit Commentary
Verse 22. - But if ye say unto me, We trust in the Lord our God. Sennacherib had also heard of this second ground of trust, which Hezekiah had certainly put forward with great openness (2 Chronicles 32:8). No doubt he thought it purely fantastical and illusory. But he was not unaware that it might inspire a determined resistance. He therefore condescended to argue against reliance on it. Is not that he, whose high places and whose altars Hezekiah hath taken away? His counselors have suggested to Sennacherib a specious argument - How can Hezekiah confidently rely on the protection of the God of the land, Jehovah, when he has been employing himself for years in the destruction of this very God's high places and altars? Surely the God will not favor one who has been pulling down his places of worship! Putting out of sight the special requirements of the Jewish Law, the argument might well seem unanswerable. At any rate, it was calculated to have a certain effect on the minds of those who were attached to the high-place worship, and desired its continuance. And hath said to Judah and Jerusalem, Ye shall worship before this altar in Jerusalem. A weak argument, if addressed to Jews of Jerusalem only, but likely to have weight with the country Jews, if, as is probable, they had crowded into the city when the invasion began.
